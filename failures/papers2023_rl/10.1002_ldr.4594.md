# The direct and legacy effects of drying‐rewetting cycles on active and relatively resistant soil carbon decomposition

## Abstract

Global climate change is expected to increase the frequency of drought and heavy precipitation, which could create more frequent drying-rewetting cycles (DWC) in the soils. Although the DWC effects on soil organic carbon (SOC) decomposition have been widely studied, the effect of DWC and the subsequent legacy effect on the decomposition of different SOC pools is still unclear. We conducted a 128-d laboratory incubation to investigate the DWC effects by using soils from old-field for 15 years (OF, representing active SOC), bare-fallow (BF)for 15 years, and BF for 23 years plus an extra 815-d incubation (BF+, representing relatively resistant SOC). The experiment included nine 10-d DWC of three treatments: (1) one mean constant-moisture at 60% water-holding capacity (WHC), (2) a mild DWC with 10-d drying to 40% WHC and immediately rewetting to 80% WHC, and (3) a strong DWC with 10-d drying to 20% WHC and immediately rewetting to 100% WHC. Following the DWC period (0–90 d), there was a 10-d stabilization period (adjusting all treatments to 60% WHC), and then a 28-d extended incubation under the constant moisture of 60% WHC. During the DWC period, the strong DWC had a strong effect on CO<sub>2</sub> release compared with the constant-moisture control, reducing the SOC decomposition of OF by 8% and BF by 10%, while increasing the SOC decomposition of BF+ by 16%. In addition, during the extended period, both mild and strong DWC significantly increased SOC mineralization of OF, but decreased that of BF and BF+. This legacy effect induced by DWC compensated for the changes in CO<sub>2</sub> release during the DWC period, resulting in the minor response of SOC decomposition of OF and BF+ to the DWC during the entire incubation. Together, DWC could create both direct and legacy effects, and these effects vary with DWC intensity and SOC pools.

## 1 INTRODUCTION

Global climate models predict increases in atmospheric carbon dioxide (CO<sub>2</sub>) concentration and surface temperature (IPCC, 2013), which will alter global hydrologic cycle, including an increase in the number and severity of droughts and heavier precipitation between drought periods (Dai, 2013; Donat et al., 2016). These events impact soil moisture, which is a key factor controlling the microbial decomposition of soil organic carbon (SOC; Moyano et al., 2013). When soil is dry, microbial activity and substrate diffusion are restricted, and soil respiration is reduced (Schimel, 2018). The rapid precipitation after a drought usually increases mineralization of SOC, creating a large pulse of CO<sub>2</sub> release (Birch, 1958; Kim et al., 2012). Blazewicz et al. (2014) suggested that this CO<sub>2</sub> pulse is due to the rapid increase in microbial activity, in which dead microorganisms provide available substrates for resuscitated dormant microorganisms after wet-up. In addition, the physically protected carbon (C) will be available to microorganisms upon rewetting due to the drying-induced aggregate breakdown (Denef et al., 2001; Najera et al., 2020; Schimel et al., 2011). This rewetting-driven CO<sub>2</sub> pulse can be sustained for more than 20 days (Canarini et al., 2017) and elevated by as much as 475% relative to a constant-moisture soil (Fierer & Schimel, 2003). Therefore, soil drying-rewetting cycles (DWC) are considered an essential environmental factor regulating the C cycle in terrestrial ecosystems (Borken & Matzner, 2009; Zhu & Cheng, 2013).

Although a large number of studies have examined the effects of DWC on soil C decomposition, DWC increased (Butterly et al., 2009; Miller et al., 2005; Xiang et al., 2008), decreased (Li et al., 2018; Shi & Marschner, 2014), or did not change (Guo et al., 2012; Yemadje et al., 2017) cumulative C decomposition relative to constant-moisture control in previous studies. Zhang et al. (2020) indicated that the rewetting-driven CO<sub>2</sub> pulse can fully compensate for the reduced CO<sub>2</sub> emission during the drying phase, and the changes in cumulative C loss depend on DWC intensity in a recent meta-analysis. Intensified drought usually triggers a stronger CO<sub>2</sub> pulse after rewetting (Barnard et al., 2015; Li et al., 2018), because a more severe drought may lead to a higher frequency of microbial death and accumulate more compatible solutes, which can contribute largely to the CO<sub>2</sub> pulse (Barnard et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2012). Moreover, there may be a drought threshold, beyond which the normally inaccessible C will be accessed, thereby contributing to the subsequent CO<sub>2</sub> pulse (Canarini et al., 2017; Homyak et al., 2018). Moreover, DWC can promote the release of old C that has been occluded in soils for more than 600 years (Schimel et al., 2011). However, due to the difficulties in separating the decomposition of SOC pools with different turnover times, few studies have evaluated the vulnerability of different SOC pools to DWC. Therefore, due to the large proportion of stable C in the total soil C pool, clarifying its response to climate change is essential for predicting future changes in the global C cycle.

SOC can be divided into three pools with different physiochemical characteristics and turnover times (Davidson & Janssens, 2006; Lin et al., 2015). The most active fraction is the annually cycling active SOC, which mainly consists of microbial biomass and plant detritus and has a fast turnover time from weeks to years, contributing to approximately 0% to 5% of the total SOC pool (Parton et al., 1987; Semenov et al., 2018). The most inert fraction is millennial cycling, passive SOC, which is mainly composed of black C or humic substances, accounting for about 10% to 40% of the total SOC pool (Schmidt et al., 2011). The most critical pool is the decadally cycling, relatively resistant (intermediate, slow) SOC, which is the dominant component of the total SOC pool, accounting for around 60% to 80% of the total SOC pool. It is generally believed that different microbial communities use different soil substrates (Berg & McClaugherty, 2008; Xu et al., 2015), and bacteria are considered more responsible for consuming readily available substrates (Moore-Kucera & Dick, 2008), while fungi are believed to have the ability to decompose more stable SOC. Fungi display an overall higher resistance to drying and subsequent rewetting processes than bacteria (Barnard et al., 2015; Yuste et al., 2011). As a consequence, relatively resistant SOC may be more susceptible to DWC due to the higher resistance of fungi to drought and thus the smaller reduction in C decomposition during the drying process (de Vries et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2020).

Furthermore, previous conditions can affect current processes, which is called the legacy effect (Monger et al., 2015). The DWC-driven legacy effect can compensate for 14% of the decrease in cumulative C decomposition during the DWC period (Li et al., 2018). However, the legacy effect of DWC on the decomposition of different SOC pools remains unclear. Thus, the direct and legacy effects of DWC on SOC pools with different turnover times remain uncertain and require further investigation.

To fill this knowledge gap, we use soils from a long-term experiment to explore the direct and legacy effects of DWC on active and relatively resistant soil C decomposition. In our study, the direct effect represents the effect of DWC on C decomposition in the DWC period, and the legacy effect denotes the effect of previous DWC on C decomposition in the post-DWC incubation period with constant moisture. We conducted a laboratory incubation experiment of 128 days, combining three water regimes—nine 10-d DWC (in a row), and one 28-d extended period of constant moisture—to investigate the direct and legacy effects of DWC on active and relatively resistant soil C decomposition. We hypothesized that: (i) compared to the constant-moisture control, the DWC with intensified drought would stimulate more C emission during the entire drying-rewetting period due to a greater CO<sub>2</sub> pulse after rewetting; (ii) the relatively resistant SOC (the bare-fallow (BF) and the BF+incubation soils) would be more vulnerable to DWC than the active SOC (the old-field soil) due to the higher resistance of fungi in the relatively resistant SOC to DWC; and (iii) the DWC could create a legacy effect on soil C decomposition due to the changes in microbial biomass and labile substrates.

## 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

### 2.1 Site description and soil sampling

Soils were sampled from a long-term experimental field located in the Shenyang Station of the Chinese Academy of Sciences, Liaoning Province, northeastern China (41°32′ N, 122°23′ E). The climate is a typical continental monsoon, with a mean annual temperature of 7.9°C and a mean annual precipitation of 604 mm (1981–2018, https://power.larc.nasa.gov). The long-term old-field (OF) and BF treatments were established in 2003 and were paddy fields before treatments. The OF plot received fresh C input for 15 years (2003–2018) with the naturally growing dominant vegetation *Phragmites australis* but no anthropogenic interference. The BF plot was kept free of C input for 15 years (2003–2018) by frequent hand weeding. Soils from these OF and BF treatments were sampled in 2018. Another BF plot had been kept free of organic input for 23 years (1990–2013) until soil sampling for this study (Zhang et al., 2021). None of the plots were fertilized. Soils were collected from the plow layer (0–20 cm depth) in the long-term experimental field. Within each plot, soils were sub-sampled at five randomly selected sites and then homogenized into one sample. Prior to the incubation experiment, the soil samples were air-dried, sieved to 2 mm, and homogenized thoroughly. Visible roots, plant detritus, and stones were removed carefully by handpicking.

### 2.2 Experimental design and soil incubation

The soil from the 15-year OF treatment represents the active SOC. The soils from the BF treatment for 15 years and the BF treatment for 23 years plus an extra 815 days of laboratory incubation (BF+, the additional incubation is to further consume active organic C) represent relatively resistant SOC (Zhang et al., 2017). Therefore, we refer to the OF SOC as active C, the BF and BF+ SOC as relatively resistant C in this study. Although all three soils may still contain various organic C components with different turnover times and protection mechanisms, here we operationally used the three soils to represent active and relatively resistant SOC (Conant et al., 2008; Townsend et al., 1997) and compared the relative vulnerability of these SOC pools to the direct and legacy effects of drying-wetting cycles for two reasons. First, we measured the proportion of active C (dissolved organic C/total C, DOC/TC) and found that the proportion of active C in the OF soil was the highest, and that in the BF+ soil was the lowest (Table 1). Second, we measured the chemical composition of SOC of these three soils by Fourier-transform infrared (FTIR) and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) analyses and found that the molecular index of SOC stability (or resistance) in the OF soil was the lowest, and that in the BF+ soil was the highest. Third, we constrained a two-pool model using observed cumulative soil respiration data and found that the SOC stability of the three soils followed the same order as the FTIR and NMR analyses (Figure S3). The basic properties of these three soils can be found in Table 1 and in the work of Zhang et al. (2017).

The experiment consisted of a pre-incubation period, a drying-rewetting period, and an extended period (Figure 1). For each soil, a subsample of 60 g (dry weight basis) was placed in a 1-L incubation jar at 60% water-holding capacity (WHC) with deionized water, and the soils were pre-incubated at 22°C for 10 days in the dark. The soil WHC describes the ability of soil to hold water against the gravity force. A WHC of approximately 60% is considered the most suitable water content for microbial activity (Iovieno & Bååth, 2008), but a WHC exceeding 100% may leave the soil in an anaerobic state. Anaerobic conditions were not considered in this study, and it is relatively easy to tell if the soil is near anaerobic using WHC, so we set the average moisture content (constant-moisture control) at 60% WHC. To achieve soil drying, 45- and 20-g silica gel were placed in the incubation jars for 100% to 20% WHC and 80% to 40% WHC treatments, respectively (Figure S1). These two weights were chosen because our pre-experimental data showed that these two weights of silica gel could accelerate the loss of soil moisture to the expected levels (i.e., 20% and 40% WHC) in 10 days (Zhang et al., unpublished data). Inside each jar, 10 ml 0.5 M NaOH solution was used to trap CO<sub>2</sub> from the SOC decomposition. At the end of each drying period, the NaOH solution and silica gel were replaced, and the soil samples were rapidly rewetted by adding deionized water with a syringe. The trapped CO<sub>2</sub>–C in the NaOH solutions was immediately measured in the form of total inorganic C using a Lotix combustion TOC analyzer (Teledyne Tekmar, USA), and the silica gel was dried in an oven at 105°C overnight to regenerate it. After replacing the silica gel and NaOH solution, the jars were sealed immediately with gas-tight lids and kept at 22°C in the dark. A previous study proved that silica gel does not absorb CO<sub>2</sub>(Harrison-Kirk et al., 2013). Three blanks without soil were incubated at the same condition to quantify the amount of C in the air and the initial NaOH solution. Prior to measuring the total inorganic C, the NaOH solutions in the DWC treatments were adjusted back to 10 ml with deionized water.

After the 90-d DWC period, all soils were adjusted to a constant moisture content of 60% WHC and maintained for 10 days (stabilization period, 90–100-d). We did not measure respiration for the stabilization period. On day 100, we carried out destructive soil sampling. Specifically, 8-g subsamples were used to determine dissolved organic carbon (DOC) and microbial biomass carbon (MBC), 10-g subsamples were stored at −80°C until the measurement of microbial biomass, and 10-g subsamples were incubated for another 28-d (extended period, 100–128 d) in which the CO<sub>2</sub>–C in the NaOH solutions was measured on days 103, 107, 114, 121, and 128.

In total, we incubated 30 jars ((3 × 3 + 1) × 3) consisting of three soils (OF, BF, and BF+ soils), two DWC treatments (100%–20% and 80%–40% WHC), one constant-moisture control treatment (60% WHC), one blank, and three replicates for each treatment.

### 2.3 Soil property analyses

The soil total C and N (nitrogen) contents were measured using an elemental analyzer (EA 3000, Euro Vector, Milan, Italy). The pH was measured in a 1:2.5 soil–water ratio with a pH meter (PHS-3C, LEICI, China). The particle size distribution was examined using the hydrometer method (Bouyoucos, 1962). The WHC was measured by placing a 100-cm<sup>3</sup> cutting ring filled with thoroughly wet soil for 24 hr on another cutting ring filled with corresponding air-dried soil and allowing it to drain for 8 hr.

The soil MBC content was measured using the chloroform fumigation extraction method (Vance et al., 1987). Briefly, a soil sample was divided into two sets (4 g per set); one set was fumigated with chloroform for 24 h in the dark, and the other set was unfumigated. Then, the fumigated and unfumigated samples were extracted with 20-ml 0.5 M K<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> in a 1:5 ratio. The extracts were filtered through a 0.45 μm syringe filter (Siyan Biotechnology, China) and determined in the form of total organic C using a Lotix combustion TOC analyzer (Teledyne Tekmar, Mason, OH, USA). The soil MBC was calculated from the K<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>-extractable organic C between the fumigated and unfumigated samples with a conversion factor of 0.45. The total organic C in unfumigated samples represents DOC.

### 2.4 Molecular indices analyses

The molecular composition of SOC was characterized by FTIR and NMR spectroscopies. FTIR was measured using a spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific Nicolet iS10 spectrometer, USA). Briefly, 2 mg air-dried ground soil samples (<0.147 mm) were pressed into 100 mg potassium bromide (KBr) pellets (64 scans within the spectral range 4000–400 cm<sup>−1</sup> at a resolution of 4 cm<sup>−1</sup> were averaged). Band maxima at wavenumbers around 2930, 2850, 1635, 1430, and 1040 cm<sup>−1</sup> were selected and assigned to a range of organic functional groups (with limited mineral interference). The peak areas of these five bands were integrated using a tangential baseline from the onset to the offset of each peak, and the relative peak areas of each band were calculated to evaluate the assignments of soil C functional groups (Table S1). The ratio of the peak areas at 1635 and 2930 cm<sup>−1</sup> (rA1635/rA2930 ratio) was defined as the resistance index and taken as an indicator of SOC stability (Demyan et al., 2012; Ernakovich et al., 2015; Hou et al., 2019).

The soil samples were treated with hydrofluoric acid (HF) to remove paramagnetic compounds and minerals. Then, the molecular composition of SOC was analyzed on a solid-state <sup>13</sup>C-NMR at 14.01 T using a JNM-ECZ600R spectrometer (JEOL RESONANCE Inc., Japan) operating at a 1H resonance frequency of 599.7 MHz. The NMR spectrum was quantified in the following chemical shift regions (Table S2): alkyl C (0–50 ppm), O–alkyl-C (50–110 ppm), aromatic C (110–160 ppm), and carbonyl C (160–220 ppm). The signal intensity of the alkyl-C/O–alkyl-C ratio (aliphaticity), aromatic-C/O–alkyl-C ratio (aromaticity), and (aromatic-C + alkyl–C)/O–alkyl-C ratio (combined index) were calculated to assess the SOC stability (Hou et al., 2019).

### 2.5 Polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification

The copy numbers of fungi and bacteria were measured by PCR amplification following the procedure of Xia et al. (2011). DNA was extracted from 0.5 g soil using the NucleoSpin Soil DNA extraction kit (Macherey-Nagel, Duren, Germany). DNA extracts were stored at −20°C. The 16 s rRNA gene, a molecular marker for bacteria, was amplified using 515F/907R (forward 5′-GTGCCAGCMGCCGCGG-3′; reverse 5′-CCGTCAATTCMTTTRAGT-3′). PCRs were performed in 20 μl containing 1 μl of TB Green Fast qPCR Mix, 2.5 μl of each primer (10 μM), 1 μl of DNA template, and 20 μl of molecular biology quality water. The amplifications were performed in a CFX-96 (Bio-Rad) following the thermal program: (1) 95°C for 5 min; (2) 40 cycles at 95°C for 45 s, 51°C for 45 s, and 72°C for 60 s; and (3) 72°C for 5 min.

The ITS gene, a molecular marker for fungi, was amplified using ITS1F/ITS2R (forward 5′-CTTGGTCATTTAGAGGAAGTAA-3′; reverse 5′-GCTGCGTTCTTCATCGATGC-3′). PCRs were performed in 20 μl containing 1 μl of TB Green Fast qPCR Mix, 2.5 μl of each primer (10 μM), 1 μl of DNA template, and 20 μl of molecular biology quality water. The amplifications were performed in a CFX-96 (Bio-Rad) following the thermal program: (1) 95°C for 5 min; (2) 40 cycles at 95°C for 45 s, 55°C for 45 s, 72°C for 60 s; and (3) 72°C for 5 min. The F:B values were calculated using the ratio of the fungal-to-bacterial gene copy numbers. qPCR cannot provide an estimation of the F:B biomass ratio because different taxa contain an unknown number of copies of the rDNA operon in their genomes. However, it provides information about the differences in the relative abundance of fungi and bacteria across soil samples (Fierer et al., 2005).

### 2.6 Statistical analyses

For all response variables, significant differences (*p* < 0.05) among water regimes in each soil and significant differences among soils in each water regime were assessed by one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA). The effects of water regimes (one constant-moisture control treatment at 60% WHC and two DWC treatments of 100% to 20% and 80%–40% WHC), soil treatments (OF, BF, and BF+) and their interactions on C decomposition, fungal-to-bacterial ratio (F:B), MBC, and DOC were assessed by linear mixed models (LMM). For the LMM, soil treatment and water regime were considered fixed factors, and sample identity was regarded as a random factor. All statistical analyses were performed using IBM SPSS Statistics 20 (IBM SPSS Inc.), and graphs were drawn using SIGMAPLOT 14.0 (Systat Software Inc.).

## 3 RESULTS

### 3.1 Soil properties

The soil C, N, and clay contents of the BF and BF+ soils were much lower than those of the OF soil, but there was no significant difference in the C/N ratio among the three soils (Table 1). The DOC content was highest in the OF soil, followed by the BF soil, and finally the BF+ soil. The DOC/TC ratio in BF+ soil was significantly lower than that in OF soil, indicating a lower active C (DOC) content in the BF and BF+ soils due to the long-term removal of plant inputs. Among the three soils, the differences in soil pH did not exceed 0.3 units, and the differences in soil WHC did not exceed 1%.

### 3.2 Molecular index of SOC stability

Fourier–transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy of three soils generally featured common peaks but showed different intensities (Figure S2a). The rA1635 of BF+ soil was significantly lower than that of OF soil, and the order of the rA2930 of the three soils was OF > BF > BF+ (*p* < 0.05, Figure 2a,b and Table S1). The order of the resistance index (ratio of rA1635/rA2930) of the three soils was BF+ > BF > OF (*p* < 0.05, Figure 2c). The trend of the resistance index (rA1635/rA2930) indicated that the molecular index of SOC stability (or resistance) was the highest in BF+ soil, followed by BF soil, and finally OF soil.

The aliphaticity and the combined index of aliphaticity and aromaticity were highest in BF+ soil, followed by BF and OF soils (Figures 2d,f), but the aromaticity showed no such trend in all three soils (Figure 2e).

### 3.3 Dynamics of soil carbon decomposition

During the DWC period, soil C mineralization rates (per unit initial SOC) declined rapidly after the first cycle (0–10 d) for all water regimes and then decreased slowly with the increasing number of DWC (Figure 3). For OF soils, cumulative soil C mineralization in the 60%, 80% to 40%, and 100% to 20% WHC treatments was 28.0, 27.1, and 25.7 mg CO<sub>2</sub>–C g<sup>−1</sup> SOC, respectively. For BF soils, cumulative soil C mineralization in the 60%, 80% to 40%, and 100%–20% WHC treatments was 24.0, 22.3, and 21.6 mg CO<sub>2</sub>–C g<sup>−1</sup> SOC, respectively. For BF+ soils, cumulative soil C mineralization in the 60%, 80% to 40%, and 100% to 20% WHC treatments was 10.8, 11.9, and 12.5 mg CO<sub>2</sub>–C g<sup>−1</sup> SOC, respectively (Figure 4a). Compared to the constant-moisture control treatment (60% WHC), the 100%–20% WHC treatment decreased the cumulative C mineralization of OF and BF soils by 8% and 10%, respectively, but increased that of BF+ soil by 16%. The 80% to 40% WHC treatment did not significantly change the cumulative C mineralization of all three soils relative to the constant-moisture control treatment (Figure 4a and Table S2). There was also an interactive effect of water regime and soil treatment on cumulative C mineralization during the DWC period (*p* = 0.001, Table 2).

Specifically, the 80% to 40% WHC treatment increased C mineralization by 18% in OF soil and decreased it by 35% and 59% in BF and BF+ soils, respectively. Meanwhile, the 100% to 20% WHC treatment increased C mineralization by 16% in OF soil and decreased it by 31% and 74% in BF and BF+ soils, respectively (Figure 4b and Table S3). There was also an interactive effect of water regime and soil treatment on cumulative C mineralization during the extended period (*p* = 0.001, Table 2).

During the entire incubation period (0–90 d and 100–128 d), the 80% to 40% and 100% to 20% WHC treatments decreased the cumulative C mineralization of BF soil by 12% and 14%, respectively, but did not significantly change the cumulative C mineralization of OF and BF+ soils compared with the constant-moisture control treatment (Figure 4c and Table S3).

## 4 DISCUSSION

Our results indicated that DWC had contrasting effects on the decomposition of different SOC pools. The strong DWC (100%–20% WHC) increased C release from the BF+ soil, but decreased C release from the OF and BF soils. The DWC-driven legacy effect on the C release can last for at least 38 d. Both the amount of microbial biomass and labile substrates contributed to this legacy effect. When considering both the extended and the DWC periods, the 80% to 40% and 100% to 20% WHC treatments did not have a significant effect on the SOC decomposition of OF and BF+ soils, but decreased the SOC decomposition of BF soil by 12% and 14%, respectively, compared with the constant-moisture control treatment. Taken together, these results demonstrate the importance of DWC in modulating SOC dynamics and show the varying degrees of vulnerability of different SOC pools to DWC.

### 4.1 Direct effects of DWC on soil carbon mineralization

As we hypothesized, the strong DWC with intensified drought had a stronger impact on C emission; compared to the constant-moisture control treatment (60% WHC), the 100% to 20% WHC treatment decreased cumulative C mineralization during the DWC period in OF and BF soils and increased it in BF+ soil, while the 80% to 40% WHC treatment did not significantly change the C emission of all three soils (Figure 4a). This is because the 80% to 40% WHC treatment likely did not cause as much osmotic shock and cell lyses as the strong DWC treatment (100%–20% WHC in this study) during the drying period (Guo et al., 2012). Moreover, the 80% to 40% WHC treatment might not have reached the drought threshold, and the accessible C from physical and physiological mechanisms after the mild drought (to 40% WHC) may not have contributed much to the subsequent CO<sub>2</sub> pulse (Barnard et al., 2020; Slessarev et al., 2020).

Although the 100% to 20% WHC treatment decreased cumulative C mineralization during the entire DWC period (0–90 d) in OF and BF soils, this reduction in the C emission of the 100% to 20% WHC treatment only occurred in two cycles and did not significantly affect C mineralization in other cycles (Table S3). This result was consistent with the meta-analysis of Zhang et al. (2020), which showed that the rewetting-driven CO<sub>2</sub> pulse can fully compensate for the reduced CO<sub>2</sub> emission during the drying period.

For BF+ soil, the 100% to 20% WHC treatment increased cumulative C mineralization by 16% relative to the mean constant-moisture control treatment (Table S3). Since little active SOC in the BF+ soil was left after the 25-year BF treatment and 815-d laboratory incubation, the microbial community in the BF+ soil was more dominated by fungi (Figure 5c). Fungi are more resistant to drought and osmotic stress than bacteria owing to their multicellular hyphal networks allowing remote access to water in the soil (Barnard et al., 2013). Therefore, the reduction in C mineralization of the soil with more fungi (BF+ soil) during the drying period may be smaller, and the cumulative C mineralization may be higher during the whole DWC period compared to the soil dominated by bacteria (OF soil). Moreover, DWC can result in a microbial composition shift toward more desiccation tolerant. For example, sensitive microorganisms die or become inactive during drought and are difficult to recover, thereby supplying substrates for more drought-tolerant surviving microorganisms (Meisner et al., 2021).

### 4.2 Legacy effects of DWC on soil carbon mineralization

Both 80% to 40% and 100% to 20% WHC treatments increased C mineralization in OF soil but decreased it in BF and BF+ soils compared to the mean constant-moisture control treatment during the extended period (Figure 4b), indicating that the repeated DWC could cause a legacy effect on soil C mineralization that lasts for at least 38-d (including the 10-d stabilization period and the 28-d extended period). Butterly et al. (2011) proposed that this legacy effect can last for more than 35-d, and Li et al. (2018) believed that after four times of DWC, this legacy effect can last for at least 60-d.

Compared to the mean constant-moisture treatment (60% WHC), the MBC content at the start of the extended period (100-d) was higher in the 100% to 20% WHC treatment for OF soil (Figure 5a), while it was lower in both the 80% to 40% and 100% to 20% WHC treatments for BF and BF+ soils (Figure 5a). Moreover, there was a positive correlation between MBC and cumulative C mineralization during the extended period across the three soils (Figure 6a). These results suggested that the changes in microbial biomass affected the legacy effect of DWC on soil C mineralization. In addition, DOC was also positively correlated with cumulative C mineralization during the extended period across the three soils (Figure 6b), indicating that the amount of microbial biomass and labile substrates (i.e., DOC) both contributed to the legacy effect of DWC on soil C mineralization. Notably, the fact that both MBC and DOC were positively correlated with cumulative C mineralization during the extended period is likely due to the positive linear relationship between MBC and DOC (Figure S5). Moreover, the DWC-driven changes in soil microbial community composition may also contribute to this legacy during the extended period (Meisner et al., 2021). Together, these results are consistent with the finding of Li et al. (2018), who showed that the legacy effect during the extended period is attributed to the amount of plant litter (as substrate) and microbial biomass.

Taking together, both mild and strong DWC treatments had a minor effect on the SOC decomposition in OF and BF+ soils, respectively, but decreased SOC decomposition in BF soil by 12% and 14%, respectively (Figure 4c). However, it should be noted that these results need to be interpreted with caution, because we did not measure the C release during the stabilization period (90–100-d) and the extended period (100–128-d, 28 days) may not be long enough. Future studies can use a longer extended period and measure microbial community composition for a more mechanistic understanding of the legacy effect of DWC on soil C release.

## 5 CONCLUSIONS

This study explored the direct and legacy effects of DWC on active and relatively resistant SOC decomposition by using three soils from a long-term experimental field as well as an incubation experiment. Compared to the mean constant-moisture treatment (60% WHC), the strong DWC treatment (100%–20% WHC) had minor effects on the SOC mineralization of OF soil and BF soil in most of the cycles, but stimulated the SOC mineralization of BF+ soil. This result suggests that the relatively resistant SOC is more vulnerable to DWC than the active SOC during the DWC period (0–90-d). Moreover, the DWC had a legacy effect on SOC decomposition during the extended period (100–128-d), being positive for active SOC but negative for relatively resistant SOC. Both MBC and DOC may have contributed to this legacy effect. However, when both direct effect and legacy effect were considered, the strong DWC did not significantly affect the cumulative SOC decomposition of OF and BF+ soils, but had a negative effect on that of BF soil during the entire incubation period. Taken together, our study demonstrates that DWC could create both direct and legacy effects on SOC decomposition, which vary with DWC intensity and SOC pools. These findings also suggest that more frequent alternating droughts and rapid precipitation in the future may have a minor or even negative effect on soil C loss.
