# Decreasing temperature sensitivity of spring phenology decelerates the advance of spring phenology in northern temperate and boreal forests

## Abstract

The start of the growing season (SOS) plays a pivotal role in the intricate interplay between climate and terrestrial ecosystems. Since the late 1990 s, a slowdown in SOS advancing trend has been documented across numerous regions in the Northern Hemisphere. However, it remains uncertain whether a commensurate slowdown in the SOS advancing trend has manifested in northern temperate and boreal forests. Therefore, our effort is dedicated to unraveling the change in SOS both before and after the late 1990 s and to elucidate the underlying mechanisms causing this phenomenon. Our findings show that despite an overall increase in preseason temperature in northern temperate and boreal forests, an unexpected slowdown in the SOS advancing trend has occurred between 1999 and 2015, primarily due to a decrease in SOS temperature sensitivity. Furthermore, we have identified two distinct effects of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity. As preseason precipitation increases, SOS temperature sensitivity experiences an increment of 0.34 days/°C∙10 mm, up to a preseason precipitation threshold of 70 mm, after which it undergoes a reduction at a rate of 0.12 days/°C∙10 mm. Our results provide valuable insights into understanding how SOS responds to ongoing climate warming in northern temperate and boreal forests. In addition, we have, for the first time, identified two distinct regulatory mechanisms governing SOS temperature sensitivity in relation to preseason precipitation before and after the 70 mm threshold, opening new avenues for the development of more accurate phenological models.

## Introduction

Northern temperate and boreal forests play a critical role in regulating terrestrial ecosystems' carbon, energy, and water cycles across the growing season, employing a variety of physiological and ecological mechanisms, including photosynthesis, respiration, and evapotranspiration (Fan et al., 2023; Hänninen, 2016; Lian et al., 2022; Reich et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021). The start of the growing season (SOS) is crucial for influencing the length of the forest growing season and for regulating interactions between the atmosphere and terrestrial ecosystems (Li et al., 2023; Lian et al., 2020). Therefore, analyzing and assessing changes in the SOS of northern temperate and boreal forests and their climate sensitivity is necessary to enhance our understanding of how terrestrial ecosystems respond to climate change (Fu et al., 2015; Li et al., 2023).

Numerous studies have examined the variations of SOS in different regions of the Northern Hemisphere, using both ground-based and space-based observations (Fu et al., 2014a; Jeong et al., 2011; Menzel et al., 2006). The development of satellite remote sensing has enabled the observation of SOS on a large, even global scale (Ma et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2015a). However, due to limitations in meteorological conditions and the spatiotemporal resolution of satellites, satellite remote sensing encounters challenges in directly observing specific phenological events, such as leaf unfolding and flowering (Fu et al., 2014a). Therefore, scholars mainly analyze changes in vegetation indices and use specific thresholds, such as the time when vegetation indices change most rapidly, to extract SOS (Piao et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2023). In the context of climate warming, the SOS in northern temperate and boreal forests was reported to exhibit an advancing trend in the 1980 s and 1990 s (Jeong et al., 2011; Park et al., 2018). However, since the late 1990 s, a slowdown in the SOS advancing trend has been observed in some parts of the northern temperate and boreal forests, including Siberian boreal forests (Park et al., 2018), European forests (Fu et al., 2015), and Chinese temperate forests (Wu and Liu, 2013). To the best of our knowledge, there is currently no conclusive evidence regarding whether the northern temperate and boreal forests have experienced an overall slowdown in the SOS advancing trend since the late 1990 s. Therefore, it is essential to elucidate the changes in SOS and explore their potential causes. However, two critical concerns need to be addressed in response to this topic.

The first concern pertains to changes in external temperature. SOS is highly sensitive to climate change, especially temperature, making it an important indicator of climate warming (Piao et al., 2019). Global air temperature is reported to have experienced a notable rise during the 1980 s and 1990 s, followed by a decline in the rate of warming from the late 1990 s to 2015 (Hu and Fedorov, 2017; Medhaug et al., 2017). It's important to note that the slowdown in the rise of global surface temperatures doesn't necessarily lead to a similar shift in preseason temperatures (Ying et al., 2015), defined as the average temperature most strongly correlated with the SOS during the months preceding the climatological SOS (Shen et al., 2015a). For instance, air temperature in Central Europe significantly increased in winter and decreased from late winter to early spring, thereby accelerating the SOS of early-unfolding plants and delaying the SOS of late-unfolding plants (Fu et al., 2014a). Therefore, an accurate assessment of changes in preseason temperature in northern temperate and boreal forests is required.

The second question concerns the effect of SOS temperature sensitivity, defined as the change in SOS per unit change in preseason temperature. SOS temperature sensitivity is considered crucial for predicting phenology's response to climate change (Shen et al., 2015a; Wang et al., 2015a). SOS temperature sensitivity varies with environmental conditions (Gao et al., 2020), and even when preseason temperature changes are consistent, different SOS temperature sensitivities can lead to varying SOS trends. For instance, in European forests, SOS temperature sensitivity declined from 4.0 ± 1.8 days per °C in 1980–1994 to 2.3 ± 1.6 days per °C in 1999–2013, resulting in a slowdown in SOS advancing trends despite a sustained rise in spring temperature (Fu et al., 2015). Existing studies have identified several factors that can trigger changes in SOS temperature sensitivity, including precipitation (Shen et al., 2015b), photoperiod (Meng et al., 2021), and frost (Liu et al., 2018b). However, previous studies on the effect of precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity are limited and have been concentrated only in specific regions in northern temperate and boreal forests. For instance, in Chinese temperate and montane forests, it was observed that SOS temperature sensitivity tends to increase with higher preseason precipitation (Cong et al., 2013; Du et al., 2019). To the best of our knowledge, there is no conclusive evidence that the positive influence of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity applies universally to all northern temperate and boreal forests. Therefore, a more thorough understanding of the changes in SOS temperature sensitivity and the influence of preseason precipitation is essential to elucidate the factors driving the shifts in SOS advancing trends in northern temperate and boreal forests.

In this study, we first extracted the SOS from the NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) time series of northern temperate and boreal forests during 1982–2015 using four widely used phenology extraction models. Second, we separately calculated the changes in SOS, preseason temperature and SOS temperature sensitivity for the periods of global rapid warming (1982–1998) and global warming hiatus (1999–2015). Finally, we analyzed the variations in SOS temperature sensitivity across different preseason precipitation gradients. The objectives of this study were to (1) determine whether the SOS advancing trend in northern temperate and boreal forests slowed down during the global warming hiatus period, (2) reveal the changes in preseason temperature and SOS temperature sensitivity, and (3) explore the mechanisms of how preseason precipitation affects SOS temperature sensitivity.

## Materials and methods

### Datasets

#### NDVI dataset

We used the NDVI dataset produced by the GIMMS3g (Global Inventory Modeling and Mapping Studies 3rd generation) to extract the SOS of northern temperate and boreal forests. The latest 15-day GIMMS3g NDVI dataset is from July 1981 to December 2015, with a spatial resolution of 1/12 degrees. Compared with other AVHRR (Advanced Very High Resolution Radiometer)-based NDVI datasets (e.g., LTDR (Land Long Term Data Record) NDVI, VIP (Vegetation Index and Phenology) NDVI), the GIMMS NDVI3g dataset has been shown to have the best temporal consistency and has been widely used to study the SOS (Tian et al., 2015; Wu and Liu, 2013; Zhang et al., 2020).

#### Land cover dataset

The extent of northern temperate and boreal forests is determined using the land cover classification dataset provided by the University of Maryland Department of Geographical Sciences (UMD) (Park et al., 2015) (Fig. S1). We selected four forest types: woodlands, mixed forests, deciduous broadleaf forests, and deciduous needleleaf forests. Evergreen needleleaf forests were excluded due to their lack of distinct seasonal changes (Wang et al., 2015b).

#### Climate dataset

We used monthly temperature and precipitation data from the CRU-TS (Climatic Research Unit gridded Time Series) 4.07 dataset, spanning the period 1982–2015, with a spatial resolution of 0.5° × 0.5°. This dataset has been widely applied to study vegetation dynamics in response to climate change (Wang et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2018). To avoid potential errors due to climate data downscaling, we resampled the extracted SOS data to the resolution of the climate data (Gao et al., 2020; Xiong et al., 2023).

### Methods

To study the variation of SOS, preseason temperature and SOS temperature sensitivity in northern temperate and boreal forests from 1982 to 2015, as well as the effect of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity, this study consists of two main steps (Fig. 1):
1)
Data preparation (sections 2.2.1 and 2.2.2). First, we extracted the SOS of northern temperate and boreal forests from 1982 to 2015 using four phenological extraction models. Then, we combined the SOS derived from these four phenological extraction models along with their mean values to calculate preseason temperature and precipitation.
2)
Data analysis (section 2.2.3). First, we compared the changes in SOS, preseason temperature, and SOS temperature sensitivity between 1982 and 1998 and 1999–2015. We then conducted an analysis of the effect of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity.

#### Spring phenology extraction

The SOS data were extracted from the NDVI time series. Since the NDVI time series is susceptible to snow cover and clouds (Peng et al., 2019), we preprocessed the raw NDVI data before extracting the SOS. First, we obtained the median annual minimum positive NDVI time series for each pixel, and then replaced the pixels smaller than this value to mitigate the influence of snow cover (Xiong et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2007). We then used the Savitzky-Golay filter to smooth out abrupt decreases in NDVI time series caused by clouds (Peng et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019). Currently, there are several models for phenological extraction, but these models still have significant uncertainties, mainly due to differences in fitting functions and phenological thresholds (Zeng et al., 2020). To minimize the uncertainties associated with phenological extraction models, it is recommended to use multiple models to extract phenological parameters (Huang et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2021). After preprocessing, we extracted the SOS using four widely adopted phenological extraction models: the double logistics method (Wu et al., 2017a), the asymmetric Gaussian method (Fu et al., 2021), the HANTS (Harmonic Analysis of Time Series) method (Liu et al., 2016), and the Spline method (Cong et al., 2013) (Table S1). To reduce the influence of different phenological extraction models on the acquired SOS, our analysis mainly relies on the average SOS calculated from these four models (Fu et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2017).

#### Preseason climate calculation

It has been reported that the climate influencing SOS in the Northern Hemisphere primarily occurs within 90 days before the multiyear average SOS (Gao et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2015b). Therefore, we calculated temperature and precipitation data in 1-month increments for the 3 months prior to the multiyear average SOS, and the preseason length was defined as the month with the highest absolute value of the partial correlation coefficient between climate factors and the SOS (Liu et al., 2016) (Fig. S2).

#### Data analysis

Given the potential correlation between preseason temperature and precipitation (Liu et al., 2016), we used ridge regression to calculate SOS temperature sensitivity. Ridge regression has been shown to be an effective method for mitigating covariance among independent variables and obtaining robust estimates of regression coefficients (Wu et al., 2017b; Zhong et al., 2023). We then utilized linear regression to determine changes in SOS, preseason temperature, and SOS temperature sensitivity between 1982 and 1998 and 1999–2015. Finally, we applied linear and piecewise linear regression to examine the effect of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity.

## Results

### Changes in SOS over northern temperate and boreal forests

The SOS was dominated by advancing trends over northern temperate and boreal forests in both periods, but both the proportion and mean value of advancing trends in the SOS decreased during the global warming hiatus period (Fig. 2). During the global rapid warming period, SOS advancing trends occupied 81.28 % (19.64 %) of the study area (represented in parentheses by the proportion occupied by significant pixels, p < 0.05), mainly in northwestern and southeastern North America, the Mediterranean region, and southern Siberia, and SOS delayed trends occupied 18.72 % (0.78 %) of the study area, mainly in northern Siberia and the Great Lakes region of North America. During the global warming hiatus period, SOS advancing trends accounted for 63.59 % (7.31 %) of the study area, mainly in northern Siberia and southeastern North America, and SOS delayed trends occupied 36.41 % (1.29 %) of the study area, mainly in northern North America and the Mediterranean region. Our results further showed that the SOS advancing trend was 0.39 days/year during the global rapid warming period and 0.12 days/year during the global warming hiatus period.

### Changes in preseason temperature and SOS temperature sensitivity in northern temperate and boreal forests

Preseason temperature predominantly showed increasing trends over northern temperate and boreal forests in both periods, and both the proportion and mean of increasing trends in preseason temperature increased during the global warming hiatus period (Fig. 3 a1-c1). During the global rapid warming period, increasing trends in preseason temperature were observed in 66.23 % (11.37 %) of the study area, mainly in the eastern part of North America, Siberia, and the Mediterranean region. Conversely, decreasing trends in preseason temperature occupied 33.77 % (0.04 %) of the study area, mainly in the northwestern part of North America and Eastern Europe. During the global warming hiatus period, increasing trends in preseason temperature occupied 75.29 % (16.39 %) of the study area, mainly in the eastern and northwestern parts of North America, and decreasing trends in preseason temperature occupied 24.71 % (1.14 %) of the study area, mainly in the north-central part of North America, southern Siberia, and Mediterranean region. Our results further showed an overall increase in preseason temperature of 0.03 °C/year during the global rapid warming period, and a higher increase of 0.05 °C/year during the global warming hiatus period.

Negative SOS temperature sensitivity dominated most of the study area during both periods, but decreased during the global warming hiatus period (Fig. 3a2-c2). During the global rapid warming period, negative SOS temperature sensitivity covered 98.74 % (81.69 %) of the study area, while positive SOS temperature sensitivity accounted for 1.26 % (2.35 %), primarily in southeastern North America. During the global warming hiatus period, negative SOS temperature sensitivity extended to 99.02 % (78.82 %) of the study area, while positive SOS temperature sensitivity was found in 0.98 % (81.69 %) of the study area, primarily in the southeastern North America and the Mongolian Plateau. Our findings further indicate that the SOS temperature sensitivity was 5.12 days/°C during the global rapid warming and decreased to 4.71 days/°C during the global warming hiatus period.

### Effects of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity in northern temperate and boreal forests

The spatial distribution of the 34-year mean preseason precipitation is showed in Fig. 4 a. We found that preseason precipitation was particularly high in mid-latitude regions, particularly in coastal areas, with most areas exceeding 90 mm. Conversely, in high-latitude regions such as Alaska and eastern Siberia, preseason precipitation was relatively low, with most areas receiving less than 30 mm. To further illustrate the effect of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity, we analyzed the changes in SOS temperature sensitivity along preseason precipitation gradients, as shown in Fig. 4b. We found that there was no significant linear relationship between SOS temperature sensitivity and preseason precipitation across the entire precipitation gradients. Interestingly, using piecewise linear regression, we discovered that before reaching approximately 70 mm of preseason precipitation, SOS temperature sensitivity gradually increased with increasing preseason precipitation (0.34 days/℃∙10 mm). However, after reaching 70 mm, SOS temperature sensitivity gradually decreased with further increases in preseason precipitation (0.12 days/℃∙10 mm). To confirm these two opposing effects of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity and the robustness of the thresholds obtained, we compared the results obtained by four separate methods. We found that all four methods identified these two opposing effects of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity, with the thresholds consistently around 70 mm. However, the slopes before and after the thresholds were not consistent (Fig. S3).

Comparing the differences in preseason precipitation between the two periods (Fig. 5 a), we observed that 62.16 % of the study area exhibited an increasing trend in preseason precipitation. The most significant increase was concentrated in the Great Lakes region of North America, with an increase of more than 15 mm. Conversely, in the region where preseason precipitation decreased, the most significant decrease was found in the southwestern region of North America, with a decrease of more than 15 mm.

To further investigate the spatial distribution of the effect of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity (Fig. 5b), we applied a 70 mm preseason precipitation threshold, which revealed three distinct patterns. First, when preseason precipitation had a positive effect on SOS temperature sensitivity (with average preseason precipitation above 70 mm in both periods), a decrease in preseason precipitation led to a reduction in SOS temperature sensitivity, affecting 12.67 % of the study area, primarily in the southwestern region of North America. Conversely, an increase in preseason precipitation led to an increase in SOS temperature sensitivity, affecting 10.69 % of the study area, primarily in the northwestern region of North America. Second, when preseason precipitation had a negative effect on SOS temperature sensitivity (with average preseason precipitation less than 70 mm in both periods), a decrease in preseason precipitation resulted in increased SOS temperature sensitivity, affecting 24.22 % of the study area, mainly located in northeastern North America, Eastern Europe, and southern East Siberia. Conversely, an area with decreased SOS temperature sensitivity due to increased preseason precipitation occupied 48.76 % of the study area, primarily in northern Siberia, around Hudson Bay in North America, and northeastern Canada. Third, preseason precipitation had opposite effects on SOS temperature sensitivity in the two periods (with mean preseason precipitation values distributed on each side of 70 mm), covering 3.66 % of the study area, primarily in North America's Great Lakes region.

## Discussion

### Slowdown in SOS advancing trends and its driving factors in northern temperate and boreal forests

Compared to the global rapid warming period, the SOS advancing trend in northern temperate and boreal forests decreased by two-thirds during the global warming hiatus period. We observed a reduction in the SOS advancing trend from 0.39 days/°C during the global rapid warming period to 0.12 days/°C during the global warming hiatus period. This is consistent with the documented slowdown in the SOS advancing trend in Chinese temperate forests (Cong et al., 2013), boreal forests (Park et al., 2018), and European forests (Fu et al., 2014a) since the late 1990 s. However, it's important to note that the reasons for the decline in the SOS advancing trend in different regions of the Northern Hemisphere were not consistent. For instance, in European forests, SOS temperature sensitivity decreased from 4.0 ± 1.8 days/°C during 1980–1994 to 2.3 ± 1.6 days/°C during 1999–2013, leading to a slowdown in the SOS advancing trend (Fu et al., 2015). In contrast, in boreal forests, the SOS advancing trend also slowed down in the first decade of the 21st century, primarily due to external climatic factors, particularly a decrease in cumulative temperature around the SOS (Park et al., 2018). To date, there has been no research that conclusively identifies the causes for the declining SOS advancing trend in the entire northern temperate and boreal forests since the late 1990 s. Therefore, we further analyzed the changes in preseason temperature and SOS temperature sensitivity to determine the cause of the declining SOS advancing trend.

For the entire northern temperate and boreal forests, the slowdown in the SOS advancing trend during the global warming hiatus period was primarily driven by the decrease in SOS temperature sensitivity rather than the changes in preseason temperature. Our results demonstrated that, compared to the period of global rapid warming, there was a 0.02 °C per year increase in the overall preseason temperature trend during the global warming hiatus period, while SOS temperature sensitivity decreased by 0.41 days/°C. Consequently, the decline in SOS temperature sensitivity stands as the key factor responsible for the reduction in the overall SOS advancing trend in northern temperate and boreal forests. There is currently no conclusive evidence regarding the specific factors causing the decline in SOS temperature sensitivity across the entire northern temperate and boreal forests (Fu et al., 2015; Park et al., 2018). Given previous studies suggesting that reduced preseason precipitation can decrease SOS temperature sensitivity in temperate forests (Cong et al., 2013; Du et al., 2019), we further validated the applicability of this mechanism across the entire northern temperate and boreal forests.

### Influences of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity in northern temperate and boreal forests

We initially confirmed the discovery made by Cong et al. (2013) that SOS temperature sensitivity increases with more preseason precipitation. The rate of change we observed (0.34 days/°C ∙ 10 mm) aligns closely with their findings (0.36 days/°C ∙ 10 mm). However, we extended our investigation and revealed that this pattern was confined to areas with preseason precipitation less than 70 mm. In contrast, in other regions, SOS temperature sensitivity decreased with increasing precipitation, at a rate of 0.12 days/°C ∙ 10 mm. Our observation suggests that preseason precipitation plays two distinct roles in regulating SOS temperature sensitivity in northern temperate and boreal forests. The potential reason for this discrepancy is that in areas with low preseason precipitation (<70 mm), more preseason precipitation can effectively alleviate the constraints on vegetation green-up caused by insufficient soil moisture, consequently enhancing SOS temperature sensitivity (Shen et al., 2015b). However, as preseason precipitation continues to increase, soil moisture ceases to be the primary limiting factor for forest green-up. Instead, more preseason precipitation may lead to wetter soils, resulting in cooler-than-expected soil conditions, which would necessitate more heat for forest green-up (Fu et al., 2014b). In addition, more precipitation often means more cloudy days, which can reduce the amount of solar radiation absorbed by forests (Yun et al., 2018). As a result, more preseason precipitation can alleviate the advance of SOS triggered by warming and reduce SOS temperature sensitivity (Meng et al., 2021). Through a deeper examination of changes in preseason precipitation between these two periods (Fig. 5), we identified that the increase in precipitation in temperate forests in eastern North America, southern Europe, and southern Asia, coupled with the reduced preseason precipitation in boreal forests, served as the primary driver for the overall decline in SOS temperature sensitivity in northern temperate and boreal forests.

In summary, we considered that there was a complex interaction between the SOS and preseason climate in northern temperate and boreal forests. Northern temperate and boreal forests can maintain a dynamic balance between maximizing the benefit of climatic conditions and minimizing the influence of limiting factors (Shen et al., 2015b; Cong et al., 2017). It's worth noting that the same climate factor can play opposite roles under different conditions (Wu et al., 2023).

### Limitations and future perspectives

Although different phenological extraction models all demonstrated two opposite effects of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity, with the obtained thresholds consistently around 70 mm, the slopes before and after these thresholds differed among different methods. This indicates that there is still significant uncertainty regarding the magnitude of the effect of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity when relying on satellite remote sensing (Fu et al., 2014a). In future studies, it is crucial to conduct more ground control experiments to accurately determine the extent of the effect of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity.

Furthermore, to the best of our knowledge, existing SOS models primarily rely on temperature alone or a combination of temperature and photoperiod (Liu et al., 2018a) and they do not explicitly consider the effects of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity. Our study introduces a potential approach to improve the precision of SOS models by incorporating the dual effects of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity. This, in turn, can contribute to a more accurate simulation of the carbon and water cycles in forest ecosystems.

## Conclusions

In this study, we aimed to investigate whether the SOS advancing trend in northern temperate and boreal forests slowed down during the global warming hiatus period and its driving factors. We first applied four commonly used phenological extraction models to derive SOS, preseason temperatures, SOS temperature sensitivity and preseason precipitation data. We then analyzed the changes in SOS, preseason temperature and SOS temperature sensitivity during the periods of global rapid warming and global warming hiatus. Finally, we explored the effect of preseason precipitation on SOS temperature sensitivity. The main conclusions are as follows.
(1)
During the global warming hiatus period, the overall SOS advancing trend in northern temperate and boreal forests exhibited a decline, primarily due to a reduction in SOS temperature sensitivity rather than changes in preseason temperature. Compared to the period of global rapid warming, the SOS advancing trend decreased by two-thirds with a 0.02 °C per year increase in preseason temperature trend and a 0.41 days/°C decrease in SOS temperature sensitivity.
(2)
Preseason precipitation has two distinct effects on SOS temperature sensitivity in northern temperate and boreal forests. Up to approximately 70 mm of preseason precipitation, SOS temperature sensitivity gradually increases with more preseason precipitation (0.34 days/°C∙10 mm), after which SOS temperature sensitivity gradually decreases with more preseason precipitation (0.12 days/°C∙10 mm).
