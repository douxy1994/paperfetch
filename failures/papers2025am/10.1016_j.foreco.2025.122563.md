# Density-dependent selection effect of dominant species rather than species diversity increased aboveground biomass accumulation in a temperate oak forest

## Abstract

Forest aboveground biomass (AGB) exerts a critical role in mediating the global carbon cycle. The impacts of multiple abiotic and biotic factors on AGB have been extensively discussed, research on artificially assembled ecosystems has consistently shown that AGB is predominantly influenced by the prevalence of dominant species. However, it is unclear whether forest AGB is also regulated by the varying proportions of dominant species within a community. Based on nine relative density gradients (10 % - 90 %) of *Quercus aliena* var. *acutiserrata* populations, this study explored the spatial pattern in forest AGB and the underpinning drivers using generalized linear mixed models (GLMM) and structural equation modeling (SEM). Our results showed that as relative density of the dominant oak species increases, the community AGB, soil nutrients, intraspecific competition intensity and evolution diversity significantly increased, whereas tree species diversity, intraspecific and interspecific diameter at breast height (DBH) variation and interspecific competition intensity all significantly decreased. Soil nutrients could exert an important positive effect on forest AGB accumulation by mediating tree species diversity (Simpson index) and interspecific exclusion stress (interspecific DBH variation and competition intensity). Species diversity and interspecific DBH variation had negative impact whereas the interspecific competition intensity exerted positive contribution on forest AGB. We found that the density-dependent selection effect may be the fundamental cause of biodiversity loss and aboveground biomass accumulation, which highlights the significant influence of dominant species density on community development. Our study provides a valuable insight into the crucial role of dominant species populations in regional forest carbon sinks predication and forest ecosystem management.

## Introduction

Forests are the most substantial terrestrial carbon sinks, and majority of forest carbon is found in the living biomass (Mo et al., 2023). Forest aboveground biomass (AGB), representing approximately 35 % of the global forest carbon stock (FAO, 2020), is a focal point in the study of carbon sequestration within forest ecosystems (Hubau et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023). Over the past several decades, mounting researches have focused on aboveground biomass accumulation (Pan et al., 2011; Cardinale et al., 2012; Yuan et al., 2019; Sha et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2024) and elucidated the main drivers on forest AGB as biodiversity, stand structure complexity, and soil nutrients (Ali and Mattsson, 2017a, 2017b; Ali et al., 2019; Georgi et al., 2021; Michalet et al., 2023).

Species diversity is one essential element for the operation and maintenance of ecosystems (Lasky et al., 2014; Cardinale et al., 2012). Many researches have demonstrated a positive correlation between forest aboveground biomass and biodiversity (Tilman et al., 2014; Vargas-Larreta et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2023). While there are also some contrary studies showing that certain forests, characterized by lower biodiversity, can attain higher AGB (Michalet et al., 2023). It was nonconclusive regarding the relationship between species diversity and forest AGB (Yuan et al., 2019). Species diversity reflects the variety of species in a community, while phylogenetic aspects provide insights into the evolutionary relationships among these species (Staab et al., 2021). The integration of both perspectives offers a comprehensive understanding of community structure and function (Srivastava et al., 2012; Hähn et al., 2024). Some researchers propose that interpreting variations in AGB is more appropriately accomplished by incorporating the phylogenetic relationships among species (Mittelbach et al., 2007; Weis et al., 2007). Evolutionary diversity constitutes another pivotal determinant of AGB (Norden et al., 2012; Sol, 2020; Huang et al., 2020), as phylogenetic distance offers an overall measure of species discrepancy (Kunwar et al., 2021). It was suggested that an optimal combination of species diversity and phylogenetic species clustering (PSC) may be a crucial factor for enhancing estimations of AGB (Potter and Woodall, 2014; Orman et al., 2021).

Stand structural complexity is also an important biotic factor significantly influencing forest AGB, wherein elements such as diameter at breast height (DBH) variation and competition intensity, could exert significant impacts on biomass accumulation (Huang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021; Trinder et al., 2021). It have been demonstrated that a significant disparity in tree DBH within a community can enhance the accumulation of AGB (Crockett et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023). Intra- and interspecific DBH variation serves as critical components of tree size diversity (Lian et al., 2022), which could result in disparity of resources and niche differentiation. Overlapping resource capture among trees in close proximity often provokes intense inter-individual competition (Michalet et al., 2023), and competition effect has a long-standing role in the process of community assembly (Grime, 1973). Interspecific competition is a key driver of primary productivity and species diversity loss (Tilman, 2020), which often leads the extinction of local subordinate species and increased biomass in forests with dominant species (West and Ratkowsky, 2022). It was concluded that competition usually reflect interactions with the nearest neighbors, but they were actually unable to account for the size differences of all trees within the community (Ali and Mattsson, 2017a, 2017b; Li et al., 2018; Badali and Zilman, 2020; Liu et al., 2022). Therefore, it is essential to conduct further discussions on how stand structural variations influence AGB across intraspecific and interspecific scales.

Soil nutrients are also regarded as a major abiotic determinant of AGB and serve as an indicator for community resource accessibility (Xu et al., 2013). Enriched soil nutrients promote plant growth and subsequently improve AGB (Marschner and Rengel, 2023). Nutrient availability directly impacts AGB has been widely documented (Quadros and Zimmer, 2017; Xue et al., 2018; Augusto and Boča, 2022), while its indirect effects, such as altering soil microbial composition and activity, are often underestimated (Melito et al., 2018). Additional findings demonstrated that large trees could accumulate more biomass with higher competition ability for more soil and other resources (Weigel et al., 2019; Rog et al., 2022).

Changes in the relative density of dominant and subordinate species significantly affect forest biomass (Avolio et al., 2019; Lisner et al., 2023). The more abundance of dominant species in communities (the degree of dominance) normally associates with the higher resources and AGB for dominant species (Akatov et al., 2023). This phenomenon is recognized as density-dependent selection effect, which can counterbalance ecological constraints, such as resource competition or biodiversity loss, by allowing dominant species to maximize their competitive advantage (Lack, 1954; Travis et al., 2023). It usually seen as major drivers of dominance of high-yield species across communities succession and biomass accumulation (van der Plas, 2019). This select effect varies from negative to positive in different regions, depending on whether the species with below- or above-average biomass dominate in the community (Heijden et al., 1999).

Focused on *Q. aliena* dominated communities, this study discusses the density-dependent selection drivers of forest AGB along with the relative density of this oak population (from 10 % to 90 %). This study concentrated on a oak-dominated temperate deciduous forest. Its diverse climate and terrain promote the mixing of northern and southern Chinese flora. Research on species coexistence in this ecological zone is vital for advancing knowledge of species distribution and coexistence, essential for biodiversity conservation efforts and better prediction of forest carbon sequestration potential. The primary question is:(1) Whether it exhibit density-dependent selection effects in subtropical-warm temperate forests in China? (2) How do multiple abiotic and biotic factors jointly mediate variations in AGB along the relative density gradient of the dominant species?

## Materials and methods

### Study area

The study area located in the subtropical-warm temperate transition zone along the southern slope of Funiu Mountains (110°30′-113°05′E, 32°45′-34°00′N,
Fig. 1), central China (Peng et al., 2024). At higher altitudes, the primary vegetation zones in this region are characterized by a broad-leaf dominant species as *Q. aliena* (Yu et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2018). The soil in this forest region is identified as brown earth. This region has a mean annual temperature of 15.1°C and the mean annual precipitation of 886 mm with a continental monsoon climate (Ma et al., 2002).

### Plot selecting and vegetation survey

We established nine relative density gradients (10 % - 90 %) of *Q. aliena* populations and selected four plots (20 m × 20 m) for each gradient. A total 36 forest plots (20 m×20 m) was established in the Baotianman National Nature Reserve according to CTFS protocols (Condit, 1998). Each woody individual (DBH≥1 cm) in plots was tagged, measured and recorded. Plot vegetation characteristics are presented in Fig. 2.

### Soil sampling and analyses

Based on the five-point sampling method (Hamid et al., 2021), a total of 540 soil samples (each approximately 500 g) were collected across the nine relative density gradients. The litter layer, shrubs, and herbs were removed, and soil samples were collected at depths of 0–10, 10–20, and 20–30 cm. We mixed the soil samples from each of the three depths, sieved it to 2 mm, and stored it in zip locked bags for further soil chemical property analysis.

Soil samples were air-dried and grounded in the laboratory for further measuring organic matter (SOM), total nitrogen (TN), available potassium (AK), and available phosphorus (AP). TN was determined using the semi-micro Kjeldahl method, while SOM was measured via the K2Cr2O7 heating method (Addo-Fordjour and Rahmad, 2015). AK was quantified through ammonium acetate extraction flame photometry, and AP through alkaline hydrolysis NaHCO3-extraction Mo–Sb–Vc colorimetry (Merunková and Chytrý, 2012). Principal component analysis (PCA) was conducted on soil nutrients using the 'vegan' R package (version R-4.4.1; Greenacre et al., 2022). The first two principal components (PC1 and PC2) were used in statistical analyses to address complexity and multicollinearity (Ali and Yan, 2017). PC1 described over 70.95 % of the soil variation across most forest plots and was used to map soil nutrients (Appendix S1: Fig. S1).

### Measuring aboveground biomass and biotic variables

Aboveground biomass (AGB) were calculated using specific allometric equations for different species from previous researches (Peng et al., 2016). Some explanatory variables such as soil nutrients, evolutionary diversity, species diversity, DBH structure and individual competition were used to investigate their effects on AGB along with the relative density of dominant species *Quercus aliena* var. *acuteserrata* in forest community.

The Simpson index (SP) was chosen to indicate the level of community species diversity (Addo-Fordjour and Rahmad, 2015). Simpson index were conducted using the 'vegan' R package (Oksanen et al., 2022). Phylogenetic species clustering (PSC) is a basic measure of biodiversity based on evolutionary diversity (Faith, 1992). PSC quantifies branch-tip clustering on a phylogenetic tree, with a value of 1 indicating less related species and 0 denoting highly related species at the tree's tip (Helmus et al., 2007; Potter and Woodall, 2014; Ali and Yan, 2018). It was calculated by the *'*picante R package (Kembel et al., 2010).

The coefficient of variation for tree DBH within each tree species ($α$
*-*DCV) is calculated by dividing the species standard deviation of DBH measurements by its mean DBH within all plots, and the result is then expressed as a percentage (Eq. (1)).

(1)

$$
α - DC{V}_{i} = (\frac{{σ}_{i}}{{μ}_{i}}) \times 100%
$$

where${σ}_{i}$is the standard deviation of all tree DBH measurements within the $i$th species, and${μ}_{i}$is the mean DBH of the *i*th species i.e.${σ}_{i} = \sqrt{\frac{∑_{k = 1}^{{n}_{i}}{({D}_{ik} - {μ}_{i})}^{2}}{{n}_{i}}}$
*,*
${μ}_{i} = \frac{∑_{k - 1}^{{n}_{i}}{D}_{ik}}{{n}_{i}}$,${D}_{ik}$represents the DBH of the$k$th tree within the$i$th species, and ${n}_{i}$is the number of trees measured within that species.

The coefficient of variation for tree DBH among species ($β$
*-*DCV) is calculated by dividing the overall standard deviation of DBH measurements by the overall mean DBH across the species, and the result is then expressed as a percentage (Eq. (2)).

(2)

$$
β - DCV = (\frac{{σ}_{all}}{{μ}_{all}}) \times 100%
$$

where${σ}_{all}$ is the overall standard deviation of DBH measurements across all species and plots and${μ}_{all}$ is the overall mean DBH across all species, i.e.${σ}_{all} = \sqrt{\frac{∑_{i = 1}^{N}∑_{k = 1}^{{n}_{i}}{({D}_{ik} - {μ}_{all})}^{2}}{{N}_{total}}}$
*,*
${μ}_{all} = \frac{∑_{i = 1}^{N}∑_{k = 1}^{{n}_{i}}{D}_{ik}}{{N}_{total}}$
*,*
$N$is the total number of species, and ${N}_{total}$is the total number of trees measured across all species.

The coefficient of intraspecific competition intensity ($α$-CI) for a tree $i$ measures the competitive pressure exerted by other individuals of the same species within a defined plot, and the result is then expressed as a percentage (Eq. (3)).

(3)

$$
α - C{I}_{i} = ∑_{j = i}\frac{{D}_{j}}{{D}_{i}{L}_{ij}}
$$

where ${D}_{j}$is the breast diameter of the competing tree *j* of the same species as tree *i*, ${D}_{i}$ is the DBH of competing tree *i*, and ${L}_{ij}$is the distance between tree *i* and competing tree *j* This index measures the competitive effects exerted by all neighboring trees of the same species on tree *i*, providing insights into intraspecific dynamics.

The coefficient of interspecific competition intensity ($β$-CI) for a tree *i* measures the competitive interactions between individuals of the different species, and the result is then expressed as a percentage (Eq. (4)).

(4)

$$
β - C{I}_{i} = ∑_{j≠i}\frac{{D}_{j}}{{D}_{i}{L}_{ij}}
$$

where ${D}_{j}$is the DBH of competing tree *j* from a different species than tree *i*, ${D}_{i}$ is the DBH of competing tree *i* and ${L}_{ij}$is the distance between trees *i* and *j*.

### Statistical analyses

This study employed linear regression analysis and one-way ANOVA with Tukey’s test to examine the distribution pattern and significant differences (Zieliński, 1994). Generalized linear mixed models (GLMM) was applied to investigate the effects of multiple predictors on aboveground biomass. This analysis was conducted utilizing the 'MuMIn' R package (Rosseel, 2012a, 2012b), and the relative contributions of each factor were calculated using the 'glmm.hp' R package (Lai et al., 2022, 2023). Structural equation model (SEM) was used to determine the direct and indirect effects of multiple abiotic and biotic factors on AGB at the community level (Figs. 5 and 6). Owing to the strong multicollinearity among predictors within each biotic factor group, we selected a single variable per group to represent it in SEM. To simplify the model and to focus on the predominant abiotic factors, we utilized the first principal component of soil properties (soil PCA), which serves as a proxy for the soil nutrient gradient (Appendix S1: Fig. S1). In the process of selecting the best-fitting SEMs, a range of tests were applied to assess the model's goodness of fit (Malaeb et al., 2000). These included the Chi-square (${χ}^{2}$) test, goodness-of-fit index (GFI), comparative fit index (CFI), standardized root mean square residual (SRMR), akaike information criterion (AIC) and root mean square error ofapproximation (RMSEA). Finally, from the optimal model, we identified the most influential predictor for each group, defined by the highest standardized effect size (*β*) on the variable of interest (Ali and Mattsson, 2017a, 2017b; Grace et al., 2016). The implementation of SEM was carried out using the R package *'*lavaan*'* (Rosseel, 2012a, 2012b).

## Results

### AGB patterns in plots with different relative densities of *Q. aliena* var. *acuteserrata*

The mean aboveground biomass (AGB) of all plots (Figs. 2 and 3a) is 111.583 ± 28.98 kg/hm<sup>2</sup>, wherein the AGB of the dominant species account for 78.4 %, and the of subordinate account for 21.6 %. At the community level, mean DBH was lower than the dominants but higher than the subordinates. The abundance of the dominants and the subordinates were less than community, while the AGB of dominants was significantly higher than the subordinates (Fig. 2).

### Variations of abiotic and biotic factors along with the relative density gradient of *Q. aliena* var. *acutiserrata*

Linear regressions revealed that all soil variables showed consistently and significantly increasing trend along with increasing relative density of *Q. aliena* in our study region (Fig. 3h–i). In the communities with a high proportion of *Q. aliena* (70 %–90 %) reached their maximum values, and the average values of soil organic matter (SOM), total nitrogen (TN), available potassium (AK), and available phosphorus (AP) are approximately about 73.54 ± 28.33 g/kg, 2.22 ± 0.65 g/kg, 137.07 ± 22.25 mg/kg and 10.32 ± 3.63 mg/kg, respectively.

As the relative density of *Q. aliena* increases, some biotic factors show different variation trends (Fig. 3b–g). Among these, species diversity (SP), intraspecific (*α*-DCV) and interspecific (*β*-DCV) DBH variations and interspecific competition intensity (*β*-CI) all significantly decreased (*p* < 0.01), whereas intraspecific competition intensity (*α*-CI) and evolution diversity (PSC) significantly increased with the increase in relative density of *Q. aliena* (*p* < 0.01).

## Discussion

### Soil nutrients indirectly enhanced forest AGB

Soil nutrients were the most critical indirect abiotic factors which contribute to the forest AGB accumulation by exerting negative impact on species diversity, DBH variations and competition intensity (Table 2,
Figs. 5 and 6). These relationships are visually demonstrated in Fig. 6, where soil nutrients significantly impact AGB both directly and indirectly through their effects on other predictors, such as competition intensity and evolutionary diversity. Previous studies concluded that soil vitality serves as a key driver of forest biomass dynamics in temperate forest ecosystems (Yuan et al., 2019). During ecological succession, communities transition from early colonizers to late-successional species dominated by larger individuals (Han et al., 2023). *Q. aliena* is a dominant species with large deciduous broadleaves in the Funiu Mountains, they could absorb more light resources (Jorge et al., 2023) and enhance their competitive edge over other species (Yuan et al., 2018). Larger trees with broader leaves contribute more litter and soil fertility (Liu et al., 2023; Moldovan et al., 2022), which further facilitate tree growth and AGB. Big individuals increase community biomass via their higher competition ability, which simplifies the DBH class structure (DBH variation; Table 2, Fig. 6). This process reduces the number of smaller trees, driven by self-thinning through intraspecific competition and thinning by interspecific competition (Medina-Vega et al., 2022; Woods et al., 2021). Our results confirmed that as the relative density increases, the number of large-diameter *Q. aliena* and AGB of the community increases (Figs. 2 and 3a). It gave a clue that the community biomass increases due to the soil nutrients indirect effect on AGB.

### Biodiversity decreased forest AGB

While a positive correlation between biodiversity and AGB is commonly accepted, our study indicates that a negative correlation will occur under specific conditions (Figs. 4 and 5). With the relative density of the dominant species increasing, species diversity in community decrease and the competition ability from dominant species increase (Wandrag et al., 2019). Such asymmetric competition will limit the development of subordinate species and potentially diminish the positive impacts of biodiversity on ecosystem AGB accumulation (Herbert et al., 2004). In a relative stable environment, competitive exclusion from dominant species could be detrimental to species diversity which subsequently have a negative impact on ecosystem productivity in a short term. However, this competitive effect may enhance biomass in a long term through the better adaptation of the dominant species to environmental changes (Baeza and Santana, 2015; Bezabih and Gebäck, 2010).

Differ from other studies that evolution diversity enhancing AGB, our study suggests that evolution diversity exerts unclear effect on AGB accumulation. Evolutionary diversity is often seen as an good surrogate for functional trait diversity to study its influence on AGB (Kunwar et al., 2021; Ali and Yan, 2018; Cadotte et al., 2009). When construct our models to discuss the influence of evolution diversity on AGB, we employed various indices of evolutionary diversity and found only PSC enabled models to converge. Although we have yet to clarify the intrinsic relationship between phylogenetic relatedness and aboveground biomass in this study, some previous studies indicated that species with close genetic relationship tend to share habitats and resource requirements, which can lead to reduced interspecific interactions over time (Chen et al., 2018). Our regression analysis reveals that forests with phylogenetically distant species exhibit higher AGB (Fig. 3g). It may give a clue that the asymmetric competition and niche displacement among exploitative species and less advantaged species in our study area are facilitated by the ample nutrient availability (DeMalach et al., 2016). These conclusions need further validation through extensive modeling in subtropical forests, to provide directions for future research.

### Density-dependent selection effect of dominant species increased AGB

The interspecific exclusion stress (sum of$β$-DCV and $β$-CI) could be defined as density-dependent selection effect of dominant species, which had the largest effect on forest AGB in this (Table 2, Fig. 6). As expected, our study confirms that the density-dependent selection effect of dominant species rather than species diversity increased AGB accumulation (Fig. 5). In our study, this effect is manifested through inter- and intraspecific exclusion stress, while previous research indicated that the impact of intra- and interspecific DBH variation on AGB differs from that of intra- and interspecific competition (Rasmussen and Weiner, 2017). Intraspecific competition intensifies as density increase, likely due to homogeneity in resource acquisition strategies among larger trees within a community (Osada and Hiura, 2019; Ku et al., 2023). While interspecific exclusion stress is often seen as a kind of selection effect (Wang, 2017), as it often exert an important influence on AGB by its mediatorial role. During community succession processing, community composition tends to shift from species characterized by those being advantageous for resource acquisition to those increasingly dominated by species with conservative life history strategies (Liancourt and Dolezal, 2021).

The identity and traits of dominant species are the main drivers of community biomass accumulation (Lv et al., 2020), as the dominant species could foster forest structure with less variation in tree sizes (DVC), and maintain biomass production after substantial species loss (Lisner et al., 2023). As little-size individuals of subordinate species increasingly struggle with resource acquisition, due to its heightened asymmetric competition, diminished living spaces and reduced nutrient availability (Bartha et al., 2014). As a result, the competitive capabilities discrepancy of species drive the interspecific exclusion (Saito et al., 2018). In a stable communities with less DBH variation (DCV), species redundancy (i.e., functionally similar species) often prevail to significantly enhance AGB along with reduced DBH variation and diminished competition between dominant and subordinate species (Quadros and Zimmer, 2017). This result is consistent with Tilman’s (1982) that high-biomass communities are driven by dominant species in the absence of environmental conditions limiting biomass (Michalet et al., 2023; Tilman, 2020). In the selective process driven by competition, *Q. aliena* gradually assumes a dominant position along with its relative density increasing. When asymmetric competition occurs among population or different species, interspecific competition diminishes. At last it leads to a decrease in species richness and DBH variation, which in turn results in an increase in aboveground biomass (Yang et al., 2019; Schorn et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Michalet et al., 2023).

## Conclusions

Based on plot investigation and model analyses on natural forests along a relative density gradient of *Quercus aliena* var. *acutiserrata*, our study discussed the forest AGB and its underpinning drivers in the subtropical-warm temperate transitional zone in central China. Our findings indicate that the degree of density-dependent selection exerted by dominant species significantly modulates aboveground biomass. It was found that species diversity generally had a significant negative impact on the AGB, whereas evolution diversity was too weak to affect the AGB in this zone. Our research highlights that despite the multiple abiotic and biotic factors jointly mediated forest AGB, which also indirectly regulated by soil nutrients through the density-dependent selection effects of dominant species. This study could improve the understanding of density-dependent selection effect and help predicting how AGB will respond to its dominant drivers in different forest types.
