# Increased atmospheric water demand reduces ecosystem water use efficiency

## Abstract

Revealing the drivers and underlying mechanisms of ecosystem water use efficiency are crucial for forecasting the impact of future climatic change on ecosystem carbon and water dynamics. Recent decades have been characterized by rising temperature worldwide, leading to a rapid increase in vapor pressure deficit. However, how elevated vapor pressure deficit would modulate the water use efficiency and the underlying mechanisms remain not fully understood. In this study, the spatial responses of water use efficiency to vapor pressure deficit were assessed through observations from 109 eddy covariance flux towers. Spatially, the negative sensitivity coefficients of water use efficiency to vapor pressure deficit significantly decreased with increasing aridity index, indicating that higher decreasing rate in water use efficiency with increased vapor pressure deficit in wet regions than that in dry regions. Compared to other environmental factors, vapor pressure deficit was the dominant driver regulating the water use efficiency variations, accounting to 19.93 %. More importantly, water use efficiency in response to vapor pressure deficit was dominated by more significantly negative gross primary productivity than the evapotranspiration. By the end of this century, vapor pressure deficit will increase by approximate 1.00 kPa, resulting in decreases of the average water use efficiency by 0.57–1.65 g C/kg H2O under the highest emission scenario. This finding improves our understanding of future climate change on ecosystem carbon and water dynamics and provides valuable insights into predicting vegetation growth and managing ecosystems under a more extreme climate.

## Introduction

Water use efficiency, defined as the ratio of the gross primary productivity to the evapotranspiration, indicates the trade-off of carbon uptake and water loss in terrestrial ecosystem. It is very important to investigate the changes of water use efficiency because water use efficiency is crucial to our predictive capability of future climate change (Huang et al., 2021; Knauer et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2015), water availability (Donohue et al., 2017; Frank et al., 2015), food production (Ai et al., 2020; Kang et al., 2021; Lenka et al., 2021) and the ability to manage the biosphere for climate mitigation and adaptation (Bai et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2021). Numerous studies have reported the substantial changes of water use efficiency responding to climate change and atmospheric CO2 concentrations rising (Cheng et al., 2017; De Kauwe et al., 2013; Huang et al., 2015; Sun et al., 2018; Xiao et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2019). However, there are still large uncertainties in the environmental regulations of water use efficiency and its varying trend, and these studies have not consistently aligned with the assertion that increased water use efficiency should necessarily correlate with increased carbon assimilation and/or reduced water losses in different biomes.

Complicate and inconsistent conclusions above imply a large knowledge gap about the environmental regulations of water use efficiency. In addition to rising atmospheric CO2 concentration, the atmospheric water demand is another important dominator of water use efficiency, which shows the substantial long-term change globally with climate warming. A synthesis of four global climate datasets reveals a sharp increase of atmospheric water demand after the late 1990s (Yuan et al., 2019). In response, the rising gross primary productivity trend was evident before the late 1990s, was subsequently stalled or reversed. More importantly, the negative effects of vapor pressure deficit on gross primary productivity offsets the atmospheric CO2 fertilization effects to gross primary productivity (Wang et al., 2020; Yuan et al., 2019). Regarding evapotranspiration, the high vapor pressure deficit can have a dual effect: it may suppress evapotranspiration by reducing stomatal conductance (Talsma et al., 2018; Xiao et al., 2022); conversely, it can also enhance evapotranspiration by amplifying the atmospheric water demand from the land surface (Li et al., 2019a; Wu et al., 2019). It is more complicated that the effects of vapor pressure deficit on evapotranspiration highly differ among ecosystem types and vegetation types. Therefore, how changing vapor pressure deficit impacts water use efficiency depends on the response of gross primary productivity and evapotranspiration to vapor pressure deficit.

Here we investigate the sensitivities of water use efficiency to various possible drivers based on eddy covariance measurements, including the potential roles of vapor pressure deficit. In particular, a machine learning method was adopted to separate the impacts of climate variables and atmospheric CO2 concentration, and quantify their respective contributions to varying water use efficiency. The analysis includes the measurements at 109 eddy covariance sites globally, including various vegetation types and geographics regions. This study also projected the magnitude of vapor pressure deficit −driven effects on water use efficiency into the future. We hypothesize that 1) vapor pressure deficit plays a dominant role in regulating the water use efficiency variations in different ecosystem types and vegetation types; and 2) water use efficiency in response to vapor pressure deficit depends on the response of gross primary productivity rather than evapotranspiration. A comprehensive comprehension of how various vegetation types respond to elevated CO2 levels in conjunction with a warmer and drier climate is imperative and urgently required to enhance the accuracy of predicting vegetation dynamics and terrestrial atmospheric interactions.

## Methods

### Study sites

Half hourly/hourly observations from eddy covariance flux towers, including the gross ecosystem productivity, vapor pressure deficit, air temperature, atmospheric CO2 concentration, precipitation, wind speed, incoming shortwave radiation, latent heat flux, and sensible heat flux were obtained from FLUXNET2015 dataset (http://fluxnet.fluxdata.org/data/fluxnet2015-dataset/). The half hourly/hourly data were transformed to the daily scale based on the following criteria: 1) Only daytime data were used since productivity would be inactive at night, also the half hourly/hourly missing values of daytime data were less than 20 %. 2) The daily ratio of the sum of latent heat flux and sensible heat flux to incoming shortwave radiation was higher than 50 % considering the energy balance. 3) Data from rain events and 1 days after a rain event were excluded. 4) Only data for the summer months (June, July and August for the Northern Hemisphere; December of previous year, January and February of current year for the Southern Hemisphere) were selected for the analysis, in which the number of daily data were more than 46. In total, 109 sites were selected in analysis, including five vegetation types of evergreen needle forest, deciduous broadleaf forest, grassland, savanna and shrubland (Tab. S1).

### Climate data

Daily climatic water deficit (CWD) was used to assess changes in soil moisture condition that is closely related to vegetation growth (Huang et al., 2021).

(1)

$$
CWD = Pre - PET
$$

Where Pre is precipitation, and PET is daily potential evapotranspiration, which is calculated from the temperature and the latitude of the site based on the Thornthwaite equation (Thornthwaite, 1948).

Aridity index data with a spatial resolution of 0.5° was obtained from the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (https://data.apps.fao.org). The mean annual aridity index is the mean annual precipitation over the potential evapotranspiration. Higher aridity index represents wetter condition.

In order to predict the future variations of water use efficiency induced by vapor pressure deficit, we obtained monthly data on air temperature and specific humidity for the study sites from 4 General Circulation Models under the Shared Socioeconomic Pathway 5 and Representative Concentration Pathway 8.5 (SSP5-RCP8.5) scenario for the period of 1980–2100 (https://esgf-node.llnl.gov/search/cmip6/, Tab. S2). The SSP5-RCP8.5 scenario exhibits very high levels of fossil fuel use, up to a doubling of global food demand, and up to a tripling of greenhouse gas emissions over the course of the century, marking the upper end of the emission scenario literature (Hurtt et al., 2020). We calculated the monthly vapor pressure deficit (VPD) based on monthly air temperature and specific humidity through the Magnus equation (eqn. (2) (Murray, 1967). Monthly data of June, July, and August were averaged to calculate the yearly vapor pressure deficit.

(2)

$$
VPD = e_{s} - e_{a}
$$

where ea is the actual vapor pressure (kPa) and es is the saturation vapor pressure (kPa). The ea is calculated using the equation (3):

(3)

$$
e_{a} = \frac{qP}{(0.62 + 0.38q)}
$$

where q is the specific humidity (kg/kg), and P is the air pressure (1.01 kPa).

The Clausius-Clapeyron equation was applied to calculate the es (eqn. (4):

(4)

$$
e_{s} = 6.11exp(\frac{17.27Tem}{Tem + 237.3})
$$

where Tem is the air temperature (℃).

### Definition of water use efficiency

The ecosystem water use efficiency (WUE) can be calculated as the ratio between gross primary productivity (GPP) and evapotranspiration (ET) as follows:

(5)

$$
WUE = \frac{GPP}{ET}
$$

Here, the unit of WUE is gC·m<sup>−2</sup>·mm<sup>−1</sup>, the unit of GPP is gC·m<sup>−2</sup>·d<sup>−1</sup>. the unit of ET is mm·d<sup>−1</sup>. ET was converted from latent heat flux (LE) based on the formula as follows:

(6)

$$
ET = \frac{LE}{λ}
$$

Where λ is 2.45 kJ·g<sup>−1</sup>.

### Statistical analyses

Random forest was used to simulate the effects of climate factors and atmospheric CO2 concentration on water use efficiency, since the prediction of random forest is not affected by multicollinearity. The random forest was performed in the package of *randomForest* in the R environment. The drivers included in the random forest model were vapor pressure deficit, temperature, atmospheric CO2 concentration, climatic water deficit and wind speed. At each site, the training data account for 80 % of available daily data in a year, the remaining 20 % of data was used for testing. There was one random forest model simulating water use efficiency, which was driven by all variables changing over time (RFALL). The number of trees and the number of variables tried at each split were set to the default values of 500 and 3, respectively. The model was run 10 times, the simulated water use efficiency were the mean values of all 10 times simulations. Correlation analysis between the simulated water use efficiency of RFALL and the testing data was performed to verify the random forest. A p-value of less than 0.05 for each year at each site indicated the simulated water use efficiency of RFALL match well. The increase in mean squared error (%IncMSE) output of random forest model was used to quantify the relative importance of factors influencing water use efficiency. The higher value of %IncMSE was, the more important the variable was in explaining the water use efficiency in this study. Then climate change simulation of water use efficiency was produced by holding climate factors constant at its average level of the year while allowing the atmospheric CO2 concentration to change with time (RFCLI0). Similarly, CO2 fertilization simulation of water use efficiency was produced by holding atmospheric CO2 concentration constant at its average level of the year while allowing the climate factors to change with time (RFca0). The differences of two simulation results were used to estimate the yearly sensitivity of water use efficiency to climate factors (kVPD, kTem, kCWD, and kWS) and Ca (kCa) with regression as equation (7) and (8):

(7)

$$
{ΔWUE}_{({RF}_{ALL} - {RF}_{CLI0})i} = k_{VPD} \times {ΔVPD}_{({RF}_{ALL} - {RF}_{CLI0})i} + k_{Tem} \times Δ{Tem}_{({RF}_{ALL} - {RF}_{CLI0})i} + k_{CWD} \times Δ{CWD}_{({RF}_{ALL} - {RF}_{CLI0})i} + k_{WS} \times Δ{WS}_{({RF}_{ALL} - {RF}_{CLI0})i} + b_{1}
$$

Where ΔWUEi *,* ΔVPDi, ΔTemi, ΔCWDi, and ΔWSi represent the differences of water use efficiency, vapor pressure deficit, temperature, climatic water deficit, and wind speed of two simulations (RFALL and RFCLI0) in each year, respectively. *b*<sub>1</sub> is the intercept.

(8)

$$
{ΔWUE}_{({RF}_{ALL} - {RF}_{Ca0})i} = k_{Ca} \times {ΔCa}_{({RF}_{ALL} - {RF}_{Ca0})i} + b_{2}
$$

Where ΔWUEi and ΔCai represent the differences of water use efficiency and atmospheric CO2 concentration of two simulations (RFALL and RFCa0) in each year, respectively. *b*<sub>2</sub> is the intercept.

The p-value of F-test was computed to examine the significance of the regression. A p-value of a year in each site of less than 0.05 indicated that the regression was reliable. The SWUE-VPD, SWUE-Tem, SWUE-CWD, SWUE-WS and SWUE-Ca were averaged from kVPD, kTem, kCWD, kWS, and kCa across all years with both two above p-values less than 0.05. The negative sensitivity coefficients (e.g. SWUE-VPD) mean water use efficiency decrease with the environmental factors, while high absolute values indicate high decreasing rates. The SWUE-VPD of each site was assumed to be constant to nearly estimate the variations of water use efficiency induced by future elevated vapor pressure deficit, since the values of SWUE-VPD in different years of long-term sites differed (Fig. S1).

The same method above was performed to estimate the sensitivity of gross primary productivity and evapotranspiration to vapor pressure deficit (SGPP-VPD and SET-VPD). Noticeably, gross primary productivity and evapotranspiration were divided by incoming shortwave radiation to eliminate the effect of radiation before the random forest operation (Lu et al., 2022). Differences in values of SWUE-VPD among four vegetation types were tested for significance by one-way analysis of variance (post hoc least significant difference test). The SGPP-VPD, SET-VPD, and SWUE-VPD were used to determine whether the gross primary productivity or evapotranspiration controls the water use efficiency in response to vapor pressure deficit. ‘GPP controlled’ refers to that the SGPP-VPD, SET-VPD, and SWUE-VPD keep consistent sign (all positive or negative). ‘ET controlled’ represents that SWUE-VPD is positive, while SGPP-VPD and SET-VPD are negative; or SWUE-VPD is negative, while SGPP-VPD and SET-VPD are positive. ‘Co-regulated’ means that SWUE-VPD and SGPP-VPD are positive, while SET-VPD is negative; or SWUE-VPD and SGPP-VPD are negative, while SET-VPD is positive (Huang et al., 2021).

## Results

Over 61 sites out of all 109 sites (56 %), water use efficiency shows the negative sensitivity coefficients to vapor pressure deficit (SWUE-VPD), which implies the increasing vapor pressure deficit will significantly decrease water use efficiency at most sites. Moreover, SWUE-VPD significantly decreased with increasing aridity index spatially, indicating higher decrease rate in water use efficiency along with increasing vapor pressure deficit in wet regions than dry regions (Fig. 1 a). Additionally, the mean SWUE-VPD in evergreen needle forest was higher than that in deciduous broadleaf forest and grassland, though there was no significant difference among the five vegetation types (Fig. 1 b). We also filtered the sites with SWUE-Tem, SWUE-Ca, SWUE-CWD, and SWUE-WS. Negative sensitivity coefficients are observed at some sites, whereas positive sensitivity coefficients are observed at others. In addition, SWUE-Tem, SWUE-Ca, SWUE-CWD, and SWUE-WS in five vegetation types show no significant trend along the increasing aridity index spatially (Fig. S2).

Different colors with the circles indicate different vegetation types, lines are the linear fits, different letters among four climate zones indicate statistical significance (p < 0.05).

The results further demonstrate vapor pressure deficit plays the most important role in regulating the water use efficiency variations compared with rising atmospheric CO2 concentration and other climate variables (Fig. 2). Machine learning model can quantify the importance of all variables to water use efficiency variations, and the results show that the importance of vapor pressure deficit is 16.95 %, which is quite higher than those of CO2 concentration (5.18 %) and other climate variables (2.16 %-8.70 %). Vapor pressure deficit was the most important factor regulating the water use efficiency in five vegetation types, with the average importance of 15.13 %, 19.88 %, 15.81 %, and 17.24 % in evergreen needle forest, deciduous broadleaf forest, grassland, savanna and shrubland, respectively (Fig. S3).

As water use efficiency is the ratio of gross primary productivity and evapotranspiration, with the increased vapor pressure deficit, both decreased gross primary productivity and increased evapotranspiration will lead to a lower water use efficiency. To determine the roles of gross primary productivity and evapotranspiration to water use efficiency, we perform the same method (i.e., machine learning method) to quantify the sensitivities of gross primary productivity and evapotranspiration to atmospheric CO2 concentration and climate variables. Then, the sensitivity coefficients of gross primary productivity, evapotranspiration and water use efficiency to vapor pressure deficit (SGPP-VPD, SET-VPD, and SWUE-VPD) were investigated to determine whether gross primary productivity or evapotranspiration plays dominant role for water use efficiency response to vapor pressure deficit. The results suggest that SGPP-VPD notably decreased with increasing aridity index spatially, while there was no obvious pattern between SET-VPD and aridity index (Fig. 3 a b). Moreover, gross primary productivity dominates water use efficiency response to vapor pressure deficit at about 80 % sites out of all sites, which evergreen needle forest, deciduous broadleaf forest, grassland, savanna and shrubland account for 35.71 %, 28.57 %, 10.71 %, and 7.14 %, respectively. By contrast, evapotranspiration only plays the dominate role at about 7.14 % sites including 3.57 % in evergreen needle forest and 3.57 % in deciduous broadleaf forest. Evapotranspiration and gross primary productivity jointly determine water use efficiency responses at 10.71 % sites, which evergreen needle forest and grassland account for 7.14 % and 3.57 %, respectively (Fig. 3 c, Fig. S4). In addition, vapor pressure deficit was also the most important factor regulating the gross primary productivity and evapotranspiration in all vegetation types, with the average importance of 22.79 % and 13.25 %, respectively (Fig. S5).

Future increases in vapor pressure deficit are projected to decrease water use efficiency, since the dominant roles of vapor pressure deficit on water use efficiency. An extreme condition (SSP5-RCP8.5 scenario) was considered in the future in the study (Fig. 4). Results of changes in vapor pressure deficit and water use efficiency vary substantially from one site to the next (Fig. S6, S7). By the end of the century, future increases in vapor pressure deficit are projected to 0.96, 1.19, 1.16, and 0.96 kPa under SSP5-RCP8.5 scenario, decreasing water use efficiency by 1.15, 1.65, 1.12, and 0.57 g C/kg H2O by the end of the century, for evergreen needle forest, deciduous broadleaf forest, grassland, savanna and shrubland, respectively.

## Discussion

### **Vapor pressure deficit dominates the variation of** water **use efficiency**

The variations of vapor pressure deficit had a dominant negative impact on water use efficiency, overriding other drivers. Vapor pressure deficit significantly influence the stomatal conductance (Grossiord et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2023; Novick et al., 2024), which in turn affects both photosynthetic rate and transpiration rate. Therefore, vapor pressure deficit is closely related to water use efficiency. The effects of vapor pressure deficit on water use efficiency are interactively modulated by other climatic and biological properties (Li et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2021), which is consistent with previous regional studies (de Carcer et al., 2018; Ding et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2022), even though the impact of elevated vapor pressure deficit on gross primary productivity and evapotranspiration varies across different bioclimatic regions (Wu et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2022).

Negative effect of vapor pressure deficit on water use efficiency was attributed to the decrease in gross primary productivity (Xiao et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022). Increased vapor pressure deficit can result in a reduction of the stomatal conductance of vegetation, leading to a decrease in the photosynthetic rate. Global gross primary productivity has persistently and extensively declined after the late 1990s due to the elevated vapor pressure deficit, which counteracts the positive CO2 fertilization effect (Yuan et al., 2019). Furthermore, gross primary productivity is more sensitive to vapor pressure deficit changes than evapotranspiration, leading to a more negative impact of increased vapor pressure deficit on water use efficiency under humid conditions (Li et al., 2018).

Vapor pressure deficit’s effect on evapotranspiration is very complex. Evapotranspiration was comprised by plant transpiration, canopy interception, and evaporation from bare soil. Plant transpiration is a physiological process regulated by stomatal conductance and community structure (Frank et al., 2015). Evaporation is a physical process influenced by climate factors such as vapor pressure deficit and wind speed (Talsma et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2019). The high vapor pressure deficit values act as an ‘airborne water pump’ that draws moisture from land surface. The increased vapor pressure deficit is supposed to increase soil evaporation, and reduce plant transpiration (Lemordant et al., 2018; Roby et al., 2020). Interestingly, a recent global study had revealed that elevated vapor pressure deficit can increase transpiration via increasing the vapor pressure inside the stomata (Li et al., 2023).

Changes in response of water use efficiency to vapor pressure deficit vary with different vegetation types. This variability is likely influenced by the stomatal and xylem structural characteristics of the vegetation. For example, the round shape and larger diameter of stomata in evergreen needle forests exhibit heightened sensitivity to changes in vapor pressure deficit, leading to significant fluctuations in stomatal conductance. The xylem structure of evergreen needle forest was non-porous wood, which enables forests to maintain effective water transport under high vapor pressure deficit conditions (Wu et al., 2022). Although the results showed that there was no significant difference in response of water use efficiency to vapor pressure deficit among the four vegetation types, the mean value of SWUE_VPD in evergreen needle forest was lower than that in grassland and deciduous broadleaf forest (Fig. 1 b).

In addition to vegetation types, the different response of water use efficiency to vapor pressure deficit may be related to climate conditions. The results showed that SWUE-VPD remarkably decreased with increasing aridity gradient spatially, which was accompanied by enhanced stomatal regulation of water loss in dry sites (Li et al., 2023; Rodriguez-Caton et al., 2021; Thomas et al., 2018). In arid ecosystems, plants demonstrate an increased responsiveness of stomata to decreases in soil-to-leaf hydraulic conductance. This heightened sensitivity may serve as a protective mechanism by hydraulically isolating the vascular system from the drying soil, thereby retarding the advancement towards hydraulic failure and dehydration (Li et al., 2019b). Recent findings suggest that while complete stomatal closure may lead to a decrease in gas exchange and carbon assimilation, it precedes significant embolism formation (Martin-StPaul et al., 2017).

### Other factors affecting water use efficiency

Other factors such as atmospheric CO2, temperature, soil moisture and wind speed could also affect water use efficiency. A primary response of plants to increasing CO2 is to increase photosynthesis and is often accompanied by reducing diffusive stomatal conductance to minimize transpiration water loss. As a result, water use efficiency is generally known to increase with rising atmospheric CO2 (Liu et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2016). However, a prolonged exposure to higher CO2 concentration is also expected to enhance the effective evaporation area of the leaf and surface, thereby increasing the total ecosystem-level evaporation (Lemordant et al., 2018). In areas with abundant water resources and high CO2 concentrations, the impact on water transpiration from elevated CO2 levels may exceed that of CO2 assimilation (Jiao et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2021). Warming directly accelerates the photosynthetic rates (Flanagan and Syed, 2011), while the evapotranspiration rates tend to remain constant due to stomatal regulation. As a result, the increased gross primary productivity compared to evapotranspiration leads to positive response of water use efficiency to warming (Serrat-Capdevila et al., 2011). The negative effects of warming on water use efficiency included increased water stress and enhanced evapotranspiration from vegetation (Huang et al., 2015; Zhu et al., 2020). Decreasing soil moisture levels pose a hindrance to the movement of water towards sites of evaporation, impacting surface conductance to water vapor and ultimately reducing evapotranspiration rates. This disruption in the water cycle can have significant implications for the overall water balance within ecosystems, affecting plant transpiration and water availability for various biological processes. In addition, the importance of climatic water deficit in grassland, savanna, and shrubland tended to be higher than in forests (Fig. S3), which may highlight the importance of forests reliance on stored water or deep rooting systems (Fan et al., 2017). The wind velocity is anticipated to impact the ecosystem water use efficiency through evapotranspiration (Montaldo and Oren, 2016; Wu et al., 2019). Increasing wind speed predominantly exerts a positive influence on changes in evapotranspiration, whereas decreases in evapotranspiration are related to decreasing wind speed (Li et al., 2021).

### Implications

Vapor pressure deficit has been acknowledged as a primary driver limiting ecosystem productivity and accelerator of large-scale vegetation mortality (Bauman et al., 2022; de Carcer et al., 2018; Trotsiuk et al., 2021), and increasing vapor pressure deficit has been identified as a primary constraint to vegetation growth (Babst et al., 2019; Grossiord et al., 2020; Restaino et al., 2016; Yuan et al., 2019). The underlying physiological processes driving vegetation growth changes are likely linked to reduced or suspended photosynthesis under high vapor pressure deficit due to closed stomata, compromised phloem transport hindering the supply of carbon to sink tissues, and xylem cavitation induced by water tension surpassing critical levels in the xylem under high vapor pressure deficit (Duursma et al., 2019; Grossiord et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022; Zweifel et al., 2021).

More water was needed to maintain the same level of photosynthetic rate due to the increasing vapor pressure deficit. Water use efficiency is a measure of the amount of carbon absorbed per unit of water consumed, reflecting the level of water consumption during carbon assimilation (Hatfield and Dold, 2019). Average vapor pressure deficit is expected to increase by approximate 1.00 kPa by the end of the century, resulting in decrease of the average water use efficiency by 0.57–1.65 g C/kg H2O under the highest emission scenario. The decline in water use efficiency necessitates a higher proportion of water consumption to maintain the same level of vegetation carbon sequestration, which will affect the water cycle profoundly (Ficklin and Novick, 2017). In conjunction with persistent and significant warming, numerous regions are likely to undergo a shift towards a novel hydro-climatological regime. This is especially important for wet regions, as remarkably negative impacts of vapor pressure deficit on water use efficiency and a larger reduction in water use efficiency.

## Conclusion

Our examination of the relative impacts of various environmental drivers underscored the significance of elevated vapor pressure deficit in regulating ecosystem water use efficiency among different vegetation types, especially, ecosystem water use efficiency in response to vapor pressure deficit was dominated by more significantly negative gross primary productivity than the evapotranspiration. Our findings highlight the environmental mechanisms regulating ecosystem water use efficiency and provide valuable insights into predicting vegetation growth and managing ecosystems under a more extreme climate.
