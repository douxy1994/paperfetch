# Field Warming‐Based Observations Reveal Constrained Apparent Temperature Sensitivities of Cropland N<sub>2</sub> O Emissions

## Abstract

The response of nitrous oxide (N<sub>2</sub>O) emissions to warming is critical for accurately projecting nitrogen-climate feedback. Compelling evidences have shown that N<sub>2</sub>O emissions are temperature dependent, offering insights into their intrinsic temperature sensitivity. However, when extrapolating from laboratory-controlled to field-ambient conditions, it remains unclear how this temperature sensitivity varied across cropland fields subject to diverse environmental and anthropogenic constraints. We performed a comprehensive meta-analysis of 144 field-warming observations worldwide, aiming to address the magnitude, heterogeneity, and drivers of such apparent temperature sensitivity. The results showed that mean growing-season field-scale N<sub>2</sub>O emissions were not strongly temperature dependent with low averaged activation energy (0.32 eV) compared with that of laboratory-scale observations (0.62 eV). At site levels, the spatial variation in such temperature sensitivity was primarily driven by the interactions of nitrogen fertilization, edaphic, and climatic variables. This analysis also identified a crucial yet frequently overlooked role of soil pH in nonlinearly modulating such temperature sensitivity. Our findings provide evidence for understanding nitrogen and climate feedback by capturing the soil-climate-management constrained warming response and highlight the importance of incorporating these constraints into models to improve prediction accuracy.

## Plain Language Summary

In cropland areas, nitrogen fertilization contributes to substantial nitrous oxide (N<sub>2</sub>O) emissions into the atmosphere. Future warming was expected to increase emissions of this stratospheric ozone-depleting greenhouse gas and utimately results in climate warming. Currently, understanding this N<sub>2</sub>O-climate positive feedback is limited, and one central challenge is regarding responses of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions to temperature change. Previous laboratory-controlled studies showed that the N<sub>2</sub>O emissions were strongly temperature dependent, though this dependency carries substantial uncertainties across field-ambient croplands. Constraining this temperature sensitivity to accurately capture the warming-induced N<sub>2</sub>O emissions from diverse cropland fields, however, is challenging for current models. In this paper, we quantified the magnitude and variability of temperature sensitivities of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions using global warming-based and field-specific observations. These findings provide evidence for the remarkable constraints on warming-induced N<sub>2</sub>O emissions from cropland fields and emphasize the local soil-climate-management driven variability in warming reponse.

## 1 Introduction

Nitrous oxide (N<sub>2</sub>O) is a long-lived stratospheric ozone-depleting greenhouse gas and has risen steadily since the mid-twentieth century (Ravishankara et al., 2009). The key reason for this rise is that human activities have greatly increased the reactive nitrogen (N) in the environment (Qiu et al., 2024). For example, agricultural N fertilization dominated the human-induced N<sub>2</sub>O emissions, making croplands being the largest anthropogenic source of atmospheric N<sub>2</sub>O over the past decades (Cui et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2020). Global warming has been evidenced to accelerate soil nitrification and denitrification processes in which N<sub>2</sub>O is an intermediate product and a byproduct, respectively (Dai et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022). This will consequently stimulate N<sub>2</sub>O emissions, creating a positive feedback loop that further exacerbates climate warming (Harris et al., 2022; Parn et al., 2018; Song et al., 2018). This feedback is particularly important in croplands due to the considerable amounts of N fertilization (Song et al., 2018). Currently, there are substantial uncertainties in understanding the positive feedback within cropland ecosystems, and one central challenge is that the sensitivity of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions to temperature and its dependency on climate, soil, and management factors is subjected to much debate (Cui et al., 2016, 2023; Wu et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2013). Thus, accurately quantifying the temperature sensitivity of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions is vital for projecting the positive nitrogen-climate feedback.

Temperature sensitivity represents a key parameter to evaluate warming effects on reaction rates in processed-based models (Huang & Gerber, 2015; Xu Ri & Prentice, 2008). Previous estimates regarding cropland temperature sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions were primarily conducted under laboratory setting, featuring ample substrate availability, and controlled, near-optimal soil moisture (Blagodatskaya et al., 2014; Niraula et al., 2018). This reflected the intrinsic temperature sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions, indicating the kinetic properties of microbial N<sub>2</sub>O productions (Zhou et al., 2009). Under natural environmental gradients, factors such as climatic variations or agronomic practices may either individually or interactively obscure such intrinsic temperature sensitivity (Elrys et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023). When considering these confounding effects, the constrained sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emission to temperature change was defined as the apparent temperature sensitivity (Wu, Liu, et al., 2023). The existing difference between the apparent and intrinsic temperature sensitivity involved in a complex of underlying environmental constraints and is critical for evaluating warming effects. A robust understanding of such constrained temperature sensitivities is essential to parameterize current process-based models (Giltrap et al., 2010; Wu et al., 2021), thereby improving predictive accuracy for nitrogen cycling in response to future warming (Abdalla et al., 2010; Grant & Pattey, 2008; Gu et al., 2016; Zheng et al., 2024).

Activation energy (*E*<sub>a</sub>) represents the lowest energy amount required to trigger a reaction and has been primarily applied to describe the temperature sensitivity of greenhouse gas emissions (Chen et al., 2021). The majority of previous studies focused on quantifying the intrinsic temperature sensitivity of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions at local scales (Castaldi, 2000; Hatch et al., 2005; Niraula et al., 2018; Saenger et al., 2011; Xing et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2020), presenting a site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> ranged from −1.09 to 1.81 eV (1 eV = 96 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) across American croplands (Chatterjee et al., 2020) and from −0.01 to 0.97 eV across Chinese croplands (Cui et al., 2023). As for the apparent temperature sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions, there are only three studies to our knowledge in which the site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> was quantified with the values of 0.30–1.04 eV in forest (Heinzle et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2019) and 0.30 eV in cropland soils (Alam et al., 2019). Despite experimental differences between these laboratory-controlled and field-ambient studies (Table S1 in Supporting Information S1), the extent to which external environmental factors and human interventions constrain N<sub>2</sub>O temperature sensitivity remains uncertain particularly at seasonal scales in croplands. The limited research and the narrow spatial and temporal scope of existing studies leave significant gaps in understanding the apparent temperature sensitivity. Therefore, it is imperative to evaluate apparent temperature sensitivities of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions at growing-season scales from diverse cropland fields representing diverse climatic conditions and human interventions.

In this study, we advanced our understanding by quantifying the apparent temperature sensitivity of mean growing-season N<sub>2</sub>O emissions and linked this temperature sensitivity to climate, soil, and fertilization based on the retrievable literatures comprised 144 field-warming observations from 23 sites worldwide. Throughout the previous meta-analysis studies (Gao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2022), the warming impact on cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions has been well documented, and the underlying changes in their temperature sensitivity and the key drivers remain poorly investigated. Therefore, we conducted the present meta-analysis for providing an empirical perspective to reveal the magnitude, heterogeneity, and key drivers of such temperature sensitivity. In this article, we aimed to address three key questions. First, what is the magnitude of the apparent temperature sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions from cropland fields? Second, what is the heterogeneity of the site-level temperature sensitivity across cropland sites? Third, how strong do different drivers impact such site-level temperature sensitivity?

## 2 Data and Methods

### 2.1 Data Compilation

To compile data on temperature responses of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions, we searched for all peer-reviewed relevant literature published before 1 July 2023 using the Web of Science, Google Scholar, and China National Knowledge Infrastructure Databases. We used the following keywords (“warm” or “temperature” or “climat”) AND (“N2O” or “nitrous oxide” or “greenhouse gas” or “GHG” or “nitrogen”) AND (“farmland” or “arable” or “agriculture” or “crop” or “rice” or “paddy” or “wheat” or “maize” or “vegetable”).

We used three criteria to screen the literature: (a) studies must simultaneously contain ambient and warming treatments with at least 3 replicates in the open field or greenhouse, and the laboratory experiments were excluded; (b) measurements of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions should persist for at least one growing season with cumulative N<sub>2</sub>O emissions and growing period recorded; (c) experiments with different crops in different years were treated as individual observations. Site-specific data were directly collected from the tables or extracted from the figures using Get Data v.2.22 Digitizer software (http://getdata-graph-digitizer.com).

For the missing data, SOC was supplemented by the conversion of soil organic matter via dividing 1.72 and adjusted pH values measured in water instead of CaCl<sub>2</sub> by minus 0.75 (Maaz et al., 2021). The stilling missing soil properties were supplemented by Harmonized World Soil Database (HWSD v.1.2) provided by the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) (http://www.fao.org/soils-portal/soil-survey/soil-maps-and-data bases), while climatic conditions (MAT and MAP) from the China Meteorological Administration (CMA) (http://data.cma.cn) and National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI) (https://www.ncei.noaa.gov/). Importantly, the missing air temperatures of warming treatments were the sum of ambient air temperature and warming-induced temperature increase.

Our search yielded 72 pairs (ambient vs. warming) of field measurements from 27 publications. Specifically, this data set includes 144 observations of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions from 23 experimental sites spanning 7 countries with the majority located in China. Note that China contributes largely to the global N<sub>2</sub>O emissions over most recent decades (Tian et al., 2020), but this could generate somewhat potential biases in our estimates of temperature sensitivities. For each record, the crop types include 12 observations for no crops, 38 for rice, 26 for soybean, 54 for winter wheat, and 14 for the other crops, whereas the fertilizer type includes 22 observations for no fertilizer, 94 for urea, 18 for compound, and 10 for other fertilizers. Although the soil hydrological conditions and irrigations were important factors (Dobbie & Smith, 2001; Liang et al., 2016), the data were generally missing and thus impossible to extract in our meta-analyses.

### 2.2 Temperature Sensitivity

We used *E*<sub>a</sub>(activation energy) to assess the apparent temperature sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions in cropland fields. Aiming to evaluate the overall and site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions, we adopted two approaches. First, we used the whole 144 observations to generate the overall *E*<sub>a</sub> by mixed-effects modeling. Second, we estimated the site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> by the Arrhenius function on the basis of each paired observations (ambient and warming treatments). Our quantification of cropland *E*<sub>a</sub> was performed at growing-season timescale, specifically, using seasonally averaged N<sub>2</sub>O fluxes and corresponding air temperatures (Yvon-Durocher et al., 2012). Since only 12 of 27 studies reported continuous soil temperatures, we relied on air temperature for consistency (Table S2 in Supporting Information S1). Available soil and air temperatures in our data set were strongly correlated (Figure S1b in Supporting Information S1; Slope = 1.04, *R*<sup>2</sup><sub>*adj*</sub> = 0.97) and responded similarly to warming (Figure S1a in Supporting Information S1). Therefore, air temperature was adopted as a proxy in the following Arrhenius function when calculating temperature sensitivities.

The overall Ea of N2O emissions was performed using linear mixed-effects modeling on the basis of a Boltzmann-Arrhenius function (Li et al., 2022):

**Equation 1.**

$$
lnF_{i}(T) = (\overset{‾}{E_{a}} + ε_{E})(\frac{1}{{kT}_{C}} - \frac{1}{kT}) + \overset{‾}{lnF(T_{C})} + ε_{i}
$$

where $\overset{‾}{E_{a}}$ represents the average apparent activation energy among sites; ln Fi(T) is the natural logarithm of the mean N2O fluxes at absolute air temperature T (K) for arbitrary sites; TC is the average air temperature among sites, and $\overset{‾}{lnF(T_{C})}$ corresponds to the mean N2O fluxes among sites at TC; $ε_{E}$ and $ε_{i}$ are the specific deviations of $\overset{‾}{E_{a}}$ and $\overset{‾}{lnF(T_{C})}$, respectively.

The site-level Ea was calculated based on the Arrhenius function (Abdalla et al., 2009):

**Equation 2.**

$$
E_{a} = R\frac{lnk_{1} - lnk_{2}}{1 / T_{2} - 1 / T_{1}}
$$

where k1 and k2 represent the mean growing-season N2O fluxes (μg N m−2 h−1), alongside T1 and T2 indicate the corresponding air temperatures (K) under ambient and warming treatments, respectively; R is the ideal gas constant with a value of approximately 8.314 J mol−1 K−1;

To compare the temperature sensitivity results between Ea and Q10 in our and previous studies, we used the following equations to transform the Q10 into Ea (J mol−1) based on the Arrhenius function:

**Equation 3.**

$$
F = Ae^{[\frac{- E_{a}}{RT}]}
$$

**Equation 4.**

$$
Q_{10} = {(\frac{F_{2}}{F_{1}})}^{[\frac{10}{T_{2} - T_{1}}]} = {(Ae^{[\frac{- E_{a}}{RT_{2}}]} / Ae^{[\frac{- E_{a}}{RT_{1}}]})}^{[\frac{10}{T_{2} - T_{1}}]}
$$

**Equation 5.**

$$
E_{a} = \frac{R}{10}lnQ_{10}T_{1}T_{2}
$$

where T1 (K) and T2 (K) are the air temperatures under ambient and warming treatments, respectively; R is the constant of approximately 8.314 J mol−1 K−1;

Assuming that 1 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup> = 0.01037 eV (Yvon-Durocher et al., 2012), we transformed the *E*<sub>a</sub> unit from J mol<sup>−1</sup> to eV by dividing 1,000 and multiplying by 0.01037.

### 2.3 Relative Importance

We performed two data-driven approaches, random forest modeling (Luo et al., 2023), and multi-model inference (Cui et al., 2021) to recognize the most important predictor variables in driving patterns of cropland *E*<sub>a</sub> at the global scale.

Random forest modeling is a nonparametric and ensemble learning algorithm originating from classification and regression trees (Breiman, 2001). Given better performances of the random forest to provide the relative importance of each predictor in determining response variable (Liao et al., 2023), we used this approach to quantify the contributions of edaphic features, climatic conditions, fertilizer types/rates, and crop types to the cropland *E*<sub>a</sub>. The model was performed in *R* software using the “ *randomForest*” package (Liaw & Wiener, 2002) with default settings, while *mtry* (the number of randomly selected predictor variables at each node) = 3 and *ntree* (the number of trees to grow in the forest) = 500. The visualized relative importance of each predictor is estimated from the “*%IncMSE*” metric in the modeling results.

The multi-model inference surpasses the single best AIC model and accounts for uncertainties both in the parameter estimate and the model (Grueber et al., 2011). In the current study, we generated a full submodels based on linear-mixed effects modeling in the *R* software and then used the *model. avg* function in *R* package “ *MuMIn*” (Bartoń, 2023) to estimate the model parameters on basis of the top models. The relative importance was estimated by summing the Akaike weights across all models. The visualized relative importance of each predictor was calculated via dividing the Akaike weight of each variable by their sum.

### 2.4 Statistical Analyses

The warming effects on N<sub>2</sub>O emissions and increase in air temperature were evaluated using the natural log of response ratios as effect size (Yang, Jia, et al., 2022), and the bias-corrected 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were calculated in MetaWin 2.1 using a categorical random effects model and a bootstrapping procedure with 4,999 iterations (Fan et al., 2022). The warming effects were considered significantly different if their 95% CIs did not overlap with zero.

The linear mixed-effects ANOVA was conducted to test the effects of heating facility, increase of air temperature, soil pH, BD (bulk density), MAT (mean annual temperature), MAP (mean annual precipitation), crop type, fertilizer type, and rates on site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> with the site as a random factor. This analysis was performed using the “ *nlme*” package in *R* software. Linear and nonlinear regressions were used to explore the relationship between site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> and edaphic features, climatic conditions, fertilizer rates, respectively. Correlations among the variables were determined by Pearson's correlation analysis performed using Origin software. To meet the assumption of normal distribution for certain analyses, all data were natural logarithm transformed when necessary. A *t*-test was used to compare site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> values partitioned using the mean and optimal values of soil pH, BD, and MAP, whereas only the mean value used for MAT. Noted that we removed 7 outliners of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions in paired observations due to the absurd negative and extremely high values. All statistical analysis was carried out using *R* (version 4.3.0) (R Core Team, 2023), and graphs were drawn using OriginPro 9.8.0.200.

## 3 Results

The mean growing-season cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions were temperature dependent as expected with an exponential positive relationship (*P* < 0.001; Figure S2 in Supporting Information S1). Given this correlation, the overall temperature sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions was estimated as the apparent activation energy of 0.32 eV (95% CI: 0.17–0.46 eV; 1 eV = 96 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) (Figures 1a and 1b). At site-level scale, the frequency distribution of the temperature sensitivity could be characterized by a Gaussian distribution across all sites, spanning a wide range of −5.1 to 6.9 eV (Figure 1c). This indicated a noteworthy spatial heterogeneity of site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> despite the limited sample size of our data set (Figure S3a in Supporting Information S1). When we considered their differences among climate zones, however, we failed to capture the significance (*P* = 0.34; Figure S3b in Supporting Information S1). Relative to the apparent temperature sensitivity, we analyzed the previously reported intrinsic temperature sensitivity of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions, yielding a markedly higher activation energy of 0.62 ± 0.08 eV (mean ± 95% CI) (Table S3 in Supporting Information S1).

Warming treatments (per 1°C increase of air temperature) increased cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions by 12.2% (95% CI: 10.4%–14.0%) (Figure S4 in Supporting Information S1) but did not remarkably impact their temperature sensitivity. Specifically, the overall temperature sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions was 0.31 eV (95% CI: 0.09–0.53 eV) under ambient conditions and 0.37 eV (95% CI: 0.19–0.55 eV) under warming treatments (Figures 2a and 2b). We observed nonsignificant differences of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions and their temperature sensitivities among different heating facilities and warming-induced temperature increase (Figure S4 in Supporting Information S1 and Figure 2c).

By aggregating all impact factors (Figure 3a), both the random forest modeling (RF) and multi-model inference (MM) results identified edaphic variables as the most important driver of site-level temperature sensitivity of mean growing-season N<sub>2</sub>O emissions with the climatic variables followed (Figure 3b). Specifically, edaphic variables were responsible for 66.4% and 54.4% of their variation in the RF and MM approaches, respectively, whereas climatic variables only contributed 28.6% and 24.5%. The relative importance of fertilization (fertilizer type and rate) to site-level temperature sensitivity was relative minor, that is, 4.9% and 14.7% in RF and MM approaches, respectively.

The field sites cover soil pH ranging from 4.4 to 8.6, with an average value of 6.8. We identified a weak but statistically nonlinear relationship between soil pH and site-level *E*<sub>a</sub>(*R*<sup>2</sup><sub>*adj*</sub> = 0.15; *P* < 0.01). With the increase in soil pH from acid to alkaline conditions, the site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> increased first and then decreased demonstrating a peak at pH = 5.63 (Figure 4a). Similar patterns for nonlinear effects on site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> were also found in BD (Figure 4b) and MAP (Figure 4d), whereas the MAT showed a linear correlation with site-level *E*<sub>a</sub>(Figure 4c). When using optimal values of soil pH, BD, and MAP derived from these nonlinear relationships as breakpoints, site-level *E*<sub>a</sub> did not differ significantly between the two partitions (Figure S6 in Supporting Information S1). These significant but weak relationships were also supported by the results from the Pearson correlation analysis (Figure 4e) and the best fit of the linear mixed-effect regression models (Table S4; Figure S5 in Supporting Information S1). As shown in Figure 5b and Table S4 of Supporting Information S1, rice paddies exhibited relatively higher temperature sensitivities possibly due to the flooding-induced alleviation of moisture and oxygen constraints. It is worth noting that soil pH explained the highest variation (Figure 4a) and also showed the highest relative importances in site-level temperature sensitivity (Figure 3a). Additionally, the fertilizer rate was not correlated with the site-level temperature sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions (*P* = 0.81; Figure 3e), and this lack of effects remains consistent uniformly across varying levels of fertilizer rates (*P* > 0.05; Figure 5c).

## 4 Disccustion

In this study, we provided a perspective for quantifying the apparent temperature sensitivity of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions based on extensive field-warming observations within cropland ecosystems (Figure 1). Our findings revealed a indisputable disparity between this apparent and previously reported intrinsic temperature sensitivity. We also highlighted the spatial variation in apparent temperature sensitivities and their driven linkage to soil, climate, and fertlizations with a particular interest in soil pH (Figures 4 and 5). This study offers novel insights for unrevailing nitrogen and climate feedback by considering the environmentally constrained response of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions to global warming within cropland ecosystems.

Our findings revealed the impact of environmental constraints on warming response of mean growing-season N<sub>2</sub>O emissions across cropland fields, presenting an averaged apparent temperature sensitivity of 0.32 eV (Figure 1b). Although relative studies are scarce, the existing one still suggested an apparent temperature sensitivity of 0.30 eV (*Q*<sub>10</sub> = 1.5) in croplands aligning closely with ours (Alam et al., 2019). As expected, this apparent temperature sensitivity was relative weak regardless of its site-level variation. In comparison, the intrinsic temperature sensitivities of biological reaction rates are generally acknowledged to span 0.51–0.81 eV, which corresponds to *Q*<sub>10</sub> values of 2–3 (Haaf et al., 2021; Li, Pei, et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2017; van ’t Hoff, 1898). Our study accordingly demonstrated laboratory-derived intrinsic temperature sensitivities of similar magnitude yet spatially varied across heterogeneous soils with an average of 0.62 eV (Table S3 in Supporting Information S1). These results evidenced that field-scale constraints obscured intrinsic temperature sensitivities, causing lower observed apparent values, with very few exceptions. Despite uncertainties in spatial heterogeneity, the extent of this suppression was striking, reaching approximately 50%. We recognize that experimental differences exist among previous laboratory studies as well as between these laboratory and our field studies particularly in averaged temporal ranges and source of temperature variation, all of which could contribute to bias in this suppression. Although our further analyses showed pronounced uniformity in experimental designs and conditions across previous laboratory studies (Table S3 in Supporting Information S1), methodological disparities between laboratory and field settings introduced inherent uncertainties (Table S1 in Supporting Information S1) necessitating caution in interpreting this suppression. As these uncertainties may partly explain the observed discrepancy, we argue that it may largely stem from substrate limitations under field conditions, which suppressed enzyme expressions of N<sub>2</sub>O production at molecular levels (Sihi et al., 2020). Specifically, field periodical fertilizations provided only weeks of increased substrate availability which, when averaged over the full growing season, cannot compare with adequate N supplies in a laboratory setting (Wu, Liang, et al., 2023).

The range of site-level apparent temperature sensitivities is high (Figure 1c; Figure S4a in Supporting Information S1), and we attributed this spatial pattern to heterogeneity of soil properties, climate conditions, and management practices across sites (Davidson & Janssens, 2006). Among these factors, we initially identified N fertilizers as a primary driver, given their roles in enhancing substrate availabilities and N<sub>2</sub>O emissions, which in turn contributes to higher temperature sensitivities (Alam et al., 2019; Song et al., 2018). However, our analyses of the growing-season data revealed nonsignificant effects of N fertilizer rates or types on site-level temperature sensitivities (Figures 5b and 5c). One possible reason is that the positive impact of fertilization-driven substrate increase was somehow underestimated because it is often short-lived. Specifically, fertilizations trigger short-term positive pulses in substrate availability and N<sub>2</sub>O emissions particularly immediately after application compared to the rest of the growing season (Deng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). Capturing these transient, periodic post-fertilization effects on temperature sensitivities is challenging, particularly when the majority of fertilizers in our study is urea-based, which can be readily utilized or lost at a rapid rate (Ding et al., 2015). Our growing-season analyses may not fully reflect this dynamic process, potential underestimating the role of fertilization and substrate availability in driving apparent temperature sensitivities. As a result, fertilization may not emerge as a primary driver in our study (Sihi et al., 2020).

Among soil properties, our study identified soil pH and BD as key drivers in nonlinearly affecting spatial patterns of site-level temperature sensitivities (Figures 3a and 4a). Extensive literature has demonstrated that soil acidification inhibits N<sub>2</sub>O reductase, thereby establishing a negative correlation between soil pH and the N<sub>2</sub>O/(N<sub>2</sub>O + N<sub>2</sub>) emission ratio (Qu et al., 2014; Russenes et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2018; Wu, Liang, et al., 2023). As a result, declining soil pH is generally associated with higher temperature sensitivities of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions, a pattern also observed in our findings (Figure 4e) and a prior local study (Zhang et al., 2021). However, beyond the well-documented negative relationship, our results indicate that temperature sensitivities tend to peak in moderately acidic soils (Figure 4a), while continued acidification may suppress N<sub>2</sub>O temperature sensitivities. This could be attributed to substrate limitations on denitrification-derived N<sub>2</sub>O production, as decreasing soil pH constrains nitrification and subsequently reduces nitrate availability (Elrys et al., 2021). Evidence for this explanation comes from previous studies demonstrating that nitrifying archaea lose their ammonia oxidation activity when soil pH drops below 5.5–6.0 (Jiang et al., 2015; Kim et al., 2012). Furthermore, a recent study verified the highest N<sub>2</sub>O emissions and N utilization in moderate acid soils (pH = 5.6–6.0) (Qiu et al., 2024) providing an empirical support for our results (Figure 4a). Compare with soil pH, the BD showed significant but minor influences on temperature sensitivities (Figure 4). Soil BD influenced the availability of oxygen for nitrifiers and denitrifiers with highest N<sub>2</sub>O emissions observed generally under medium BD conditions (Yang, Reijneveld, et al., 2022). Specifically, loose soils with ample pore spaces and great oxygen availability tend to hinder denitrification N<sub>2</sub>O production, whereas overly dense soils create strictly anaerobic microsites, promoting the reduction of accumulated N<sub>2</sub>O to N<sub>2</sub> via the denitrification process (Bessou et al., 2010; Liu et al., 2021).

Additionally, climatic variables are also critical in driving temperature sensitivities of microbial reactions (Liu et al., 2024). In our study, we observed higher temperature sensitivities of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions in response to increased MAP and MAT conditions (Figure 5a). Generally, higher MAP has been found to increased soil water-filled pore space, triggering a short-term positive pulse in N<sub>2</sub>O emission due to enhanced denitrification (Ding et al., 2019; Li, Zheng, et al., 2020; Xue et al., 2013), which consequently results in higher temperature sensitivities of N<sub>2</sub>O emissions. Similarly, higher MAT has also been reported to increase microbial activities of nitrifiers and denitrifiers, thereby improving substrate utilization efficiency (Yang, Jia, et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). Furthermore, a previous study also reported the linkage between rising MAT and greater soil inorganic N supplies expanding substrate availability for N<sub>2</sub>O production (Yan et al., 2022).

Given the co-variation between MAT and temperature sensitivities, the response of N<sub>2</sub>O emission to experimental warming should be positive. Previous meta-analyses showed that experimental warming reduced N fixation and plant N uptake, whereas enhancing soil net N mineralization, nitrification, and denitrification with N leaching and immobilization remaining largely unaffected (Bai et al., 2013; Yan et al., 2022). Although these processes exerted differing influences on soil N substrates and N<sub>2</sub>O production, their interplay ultimately shifts the balance toward a greater supply of reactive N, thereby amplifying N<sub>2</sub>O emissions. However, under field conditions, our findings suggested that this warming response is variable rather than uniformly positive, a pattern also observed in earlier research (Wang et al., 2022). Although some sites exhibited increased emissions, others reported suppressive warming effects on N<sub>2</sub>O fluxes, yielding negative values of site-specific temperature sensitivities (Figure S3a in Supporting Information S1). Furthermore, these suppressive impacts offset the aforementioned positive warming responses, leading to observed similarities in temperature sensitivities between ambient and warming conditions (Figure 2a). With respect to the suppressive warming impacts, the primary cause might be the warming-induced reduction in soil moisture, which hampered denitrification N<sub>2</sub>O production (Bai et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2023; Martins et al., 2017). Additionally, soil carbon (C) limitation could be another impact factor. Warming-accelerated decomposition reduced soil C (Davidson & Janssens, 2006), which limited the availability of C-derived available electrons for denitrifiers (Canfield et al., 2010). As a result, the denitrification N<sub>2</sub>O emissions were constrained under C-limited conditions, yielding low temperature sensitivities. This is partially supported by our results, showing that negative values of temperature sensitivities were predominantly observed in soils of low SOC levels (Table S5 in Supporting Information S1).

Overall, our findings revealed a fundamental yet neglected gap between temperature sensitivities of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions observed under field versus laboratory conditions. As expected, the averaged apparent temperature sensitivities are lower than intrinsic values; however, this attenuation reaches nearly 50% in magnitude. This demonstrated a robust constraint on warming-induced N<sub>2</sub>O emissions under practical context and thus suggesting a potentially lower magnitude of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions in a future warmer world. Supported by currently retrievable ensemble of warming-based and site-specific estimates, our findings highlighted the spatial variability of apparent temperature sensitivities with acceptable uncertainties across global cropland fields. We explain this pattern with the complex interplay of soil, fertilization, and climate factors. Our results further pointed out the importance of considering local soil conditions within the context of climate-management impacts for constraining warming-induced N<sub>2</sub>O emissions. These findings provide empirical evidence to improve the understanding of predicting cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions under future warming. For example, soil acidification has occurred in a large portion of agricultural soils in China, US and Europe because of long-term N fertilization, resulting in divergently decreased soil pH locally (Bowman et al., 2008; Chen et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2010). In this context, our analyses revealed that the predicted increase in cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions under future warming would progressively intensify until soil pH reaching a certain threshold (∼5.6), after which the rate of increase slows. However, these controls are not captured in current models, and future work is needed to assess the nitrogen-climate cycle from the perspective of spatially explicit temperature sensitivity from local to global scales.
