# Density dependence of tree growth varies with temperature gradient and mycorrhizal type

## Abstract

Conspecific negative density dependence (CNDD) plays a key role in maintaining species coexistence and theoretically contributes to large-scale diversity patterns. However, the effect of CNDD on large-scale tree diversity patterns remains debated, particularly for long-lived tree species.
In this study, we collected decadal dynamic tree growth data from 50 forest plots across eastern China. We evaluated density-dependent tree growth responses to conspecific versus heterospecific neighbours along the temperature gradient. Specifically, we compared the stabilizing CNDD effect that was calculated as the difference between conspecific and heterospecific density effects on tree growth between arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) and ectomycorrhizal (EM) tree species.
Our results revealed that the strength of both CNDD and stabilizing CNDD in AM tree species became more negative with increasing temperature, while remaining constant in EM tree species along the temperature gradient. The observed CNDD patterns correspond well with climatic patterns of local tree species richness, where the increase in species richness along the temperature gradient is mainly attributed to AM tree species. Furthermore, we found stronger conspecific limitation on tree growth in AM trees compared to EM trees in warm forests.
*Synthesis.* Our results support a close link between the strength of CNDD and local tree species richness patterns along the temperature gradient. These findings highlight the critical role of mycorrhizal symbiosis in mediating CNDD processes and shaping large-scale tree diversity patterns.

## 1 INTRODUCTION

Understanding the mechanisms behind biodiversity patterns remains a fundamental goal of ecology (Gaston, 2000). Climate, especially temperature, plays a critical role in shaping global patterns of local tree species richness, but it is still a challenge to explain the exceptionally high tree diversity in tropical regions (Liang et al., 2022). A central mechanism for maintaining high species richness is conspecific negative density dependence (CNDD), whereby plant fitness declines when surrounded by more conspecific neighbours (Comita et al., 2010; Wright, 2002). CNDD can be caused by the accumulation of specialized enemies near dense patches of their hosts, as proposed by the Janzen–Connell hypothesis (Connell, 1971; Janzen, 1970), as well as by autotoxicity and intense conspecific competition due to niche overlap (Hülsmann et al., 2021). Crucially, CNDD implies stronger negative impacts on tree performance by conspecific versus heterospecific neighbours, thereby limiting the population growth of common species while sustaining rare species (Hülsmann et al., 2021). In addition, a general hypothesis is that biotic interactions may be stronger in the tropics than out of the tropics (Schemske et al., 2009). Therefore, variation in CNDD strength is considered a potential driver of large-scale diversity patterns (Hülsmann et al., 2024; LaManna et al., 2017, 2022).

Empirical studies have reported the widespread existence of CNDD in natural forests (Hülsmann et al., 2024; Johnson et al., 2012, 2014; Kalyuzhny et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2015). However, the role of CNDD in shaping species coexistence and diversity patterns remains debated (Hülsmann et al., 2021). Most research has focused on seeds and seedlings, which may experience stronger CNDD due to vulnerability in early life stages (Comita & Hubbell, 2009; Zhu et al., 2018). However, for long-lived tree species, fitness is difficult to measure directly, and the influence of survival differences in early life stages on population growth may be diluted over time (Broekman et al., 2019; Hülsmann et al., 2021). Considering different fitness components such as growth, survival, and recruitment at different life stages can deepen our understanding of the integrated effects of CNDD on the entire life history and species coexistence (LaManna et al., 2021). Moreover, the evidence regarding the role of CNDD in shaping tree diversity patterns is inconsistent (Hülsmann et al., 2024; LaManna et al., 2017). Several studies using large-scale forest survey data have reported latitudinal patterns of CNDD associated with tree diversity (Johnson et al., 2012; LaManna et al., 2017). However, these patterns were attributed to statistical artefacts arising from the use of static data (Chisholm & Fung, 2018; Detto et al., 2019; Hülsmann & Hartig, 2018). Therefore, there is still no consensus about the pattern of CNDD and its role in shaping large-scale patterns of tree diversity (Hülsmann et al., 2021, 2024).

CNDD in forest communities is predominantly caused by soil-borne pathogens (Mangan et al., 2010), which can also be influenced by mycorrhizal fungi (Bennett et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2019). Mycorrhizal symbioses are very common in natural forests, with nearly 90% of tree species forming either ectomycorrhizal (EM) or arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) associations (Steidinger et al., 2019). On the one hand, EM fungi can produce antibiotic compounds and provide physical protection for trees by covering the root tips, leading to reduced pathogen colonization (Chen et al., 2019; Genre et al., 2020). AM trees exhibit stronger conspecific limitation than EM trees (Bennett et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2020), which may promote the coexistence of diverse AM tree species. On the other hand, trees benefit from common mycorrhizal networks through mechanisms such as belowground signalling and nutrient transfer among plants (Klein et al., 2016; Tedersoo et al., 2020). When plants are surrounded by individuals of the same mycorrhizal type, AM tree species may benefit more than EM tree species due to the relatively lower host specificity of AM fungi (Delavaux et al., 2023). The advantage of shared mycorrhizal networks among AM tree species may also contribute to their coexistence.

Recent studies suggest that mycorrhizal symbioses shape tree diversity (Jiang et al., 2025). One possible mechanism is that mycorrhizal symbioses influence the patterns of density dependence of species fitness that shape tree diversity patterns (Jiang et al., 2021; Teste et al., 2017). However, there is still a lack of evidence about how mycorrhizal symbioses mediate the large-scale patterns of CNDD. Here, we collected decadal forest inventory dynamics data from 50 permanent forest plots across eastern China. Density dependence was estimated using long-term dynamic data to avoid statistical artefacts associated with the use of static data (Detto et al., 2019). In our dataset, tree species richness rarefied by abundance increased along the temperature gradient, primarily due to an increase in AM tree species richness (Figure 1). We evaluated the density dependence of tree growth and calculated the stabilizing CNDD effect as the difference between conspecific and heterospecific density effects (Hülsmann et al., 2024; LaManna et al., 2022). By examining the influence of temperature and mycorrhizal type on these effects, we tested three hypotheses concerning tree diversity patterns. First, stabilizing CNDD becomes more negative with increasing temperature (H1), shaping observed tree diversity patterns along the temperature gradient. To explain high local diversity of AM tree species in warm forests, we hypothesized that AM trees experience stronger CNDD than EM trees in warm forests (H2), thereby preventing any single AM tree species from dominating the community. In addition, heterospecific neighbourhoods with the same mycorrhizal type may provide greater benefits to AM trees in warm forests (H3), thus facilitating the coexistence of abundant AM tree species.

## 2 METHODS

### 2.1 Forest inventory data

Tree growth data were collected from 50 permanent forest plots located in six mountains in eastern China. Most plots were 900 or 1000 m<sup>2</sup> in area (Table S1). All free-standing woody stems (hereafter trees) with a diameter at breast height (DBH; 1.3 m above the ground) ≥5 cm within each plot were identified, measured and mapped. Field-made steel dendrometer bands were installed at a height of 1.3 m on these trees, approximately 30–50 cm above any significant damage (due to fluting, deformation, or disease) (Chi et al., 2015). Changes in the windows of the dendrometer bands, which correspond to changes in tree circumference, were measured with digital callipers (precision ±0.01 mm) in April and October of each year. The annual basal area growth rate for each stem was calculated as the difference in basal area between two consecutive Octobers. Growth data were collected annually from 2010 to 2021. A total of 9211 stems from 290 species were measured, with the time series of tree growth ranging from 8 to 11 years.

### 2.2 Environment data

Climate data were extracted for each plot based on geographic coordinates from WorldClim version 2.1 (Fick & Hijmans, 2017), with a spatial resolution of 30 arc-seconds (~1 km<sup>2</sup>). We extracted mean annual temperature and annual precipitation for the period 1970–2000. Additionally, soil phosphorus (P) density was extracted from Chinese datasets on nitrogen and phosphorus density and concentration, with a spatial resolution of 30 seconds (~1 km<sup>2</sup>) (Zhang et al., 2021).

### 2.3 Mycorrhizal type

Mycorrhizal types were assigned to tree species based on the FungalRoot Database, a global database that records the intensity and frequency of colonization by different mycorrhizal fungi (Soudzilovskaia et al., 2022). When the mycorrhizal type was undetermined or unknown at the species level, we also referred to the recommended mycorrhizal symbioses at the genus level because mycorrhizal types are generally phylogenetically conserved within genera (Brundrett & Tedersoo, 2018; Soudzilovskaia et al., 2020). Of all tree species identified in the forest inventory, 213 (73.4%) were AM, 54 (18.6%) were EM, and 18 (6.2%) were ericoid mycorrhizal tree species. In addition, five species from *Populus* and *Salix* can be colonized by both AM fungi and EM fungi (AM-EM) and thus were not categorized as either AM or EM tree species.

### 2.4 Growth data and neighbourhood density

Tree growth is defined as the annual increase in basal area. The distribution of growth data is left-skewed. Growth data may contain negative values due to water deficit fluctuations and systematic measurement errors, and removing these negative values would bias growth rates upwards. We applied power transformation and maintained negative values as follows (Equation 1) (Figure S1):

**Equation 1.**

$$
T = {\begin{matrix} g^{k}g \geq 0 \\ - [{(- g)}^{k}]g < 0 \end{matrix}
$$

where g is the basal area increment and T is the growth data after transformation. Power transformation is a commonly used method for normalizing data, with the key advantage of accommodating negative values (Kenfack et al., 2014). We examined the distribution of T across the same range of k values (between 0.3 and 0.5) as in Kenfack et al. (2014) and found that the skewness of growth data can be most effectively reduced when k = 0.4 (Figure S1).

Neighbourhood density was calculated as the total basal area at the neighbourhood scale. Due to constraints on plot size, defining the neighbourhood scale as a fixed radius (e.g. 7.5 m) around the focal tree would result in too few individuals in the central areas of plots. Instead, we initially divided the plots into subplots (5 × 5 m) at 5-m intervals. The neighbourhood density for each focal stem was calculated by summing the basal area of all neighbouring stems with a DBH ≥5 cm within a 15 × 15 m area centred on the 5 × 5 m subplot containing the focal tree (Coomes & Allen, 2007; Zhang et al., 2024). Trees within a 5-m distance from the plot boundary were excluded as focal trees to avoid incomplete neighbourhoods. We considered only the growth rates of trees that survived throughout the survey period in the analysis, and neighbourhood density greater than 3.15 m<sup>2</sup> was excluded as outliers (with the maximum value of the remaining data being 2.47 m<sup>2</sup>) (Figure S2). Finally, basal area growth of 4130 stems from 227 species was included as focal trees in the analysis. Neighbourhood density was then divided into conspecific (Con) and heterospecific (Het) density. Heterospecific density was further divided into con-mycorrhizal (Conmy) and hetero-mycorrhizal (Hetmy) heterospecific density.

### 2.5 Measuring density dependence of tree growth

To measure the density dependence of tree growth along the temperature gradient, we fitted the basal area growth (T) with Bayesian hierarchical generalized linear mixed models. The model (Equation 2 in program R ‘lmer’ notation) for growth was fitted separately for AM, EM and all tree species.

**Equation 2.**

$$
\begin{matrix} T~1 + Year + Year^{2} + DBH + DBH^{2} + MAT + Con + Het \\ + Con: MAT + Het: MAT + (1| Plot) + (1| Plot \times Year) \\ + (1| Species \times Year) + (1| ID) + (1 + Con + Het| Species) \\ + (1 + Con + Het| Plot \times Species) \end{matrix}
$$

*DBH* and *Year* were included to account for size- and age-dependent growth variation, and quadratic terms were included based on residual analysis. *MAT* was the mean annual temperature of the plots. We considered random intercepts of species, plot, plot-species combinations, plot-year (census) combinations and species-year (census) combinations, as well as *ID* (individuals) to account for autocorrelation. By comparing different random factors with the Leave-One-Out Cross-Validation method (Table S2), models including random slopes for species and plot-species combinations on conspecific (*Con*) and heterospecific (*Het*) density showed better performance, reflecting species-specific density effects on tree growth.

We also fitted models (Equation 3 in program R ‘lmer’ notation) including con-mycorrhizal (Conmy) and hetero-mycorrhizal (Hetmy) heterospecific density to evaluate heterospecific effects from different mycorrhizal types on tree growth for AM and EM tree species.

**Equation 3.**

$$
\begin{matrix} T~1 + Year + Year^{2} + DBH + DBH^{2} + MAT + Con \\ + Conmy + Hetmy + Con: MAT + Conmy: MAT \\ + Hetmy: MAT + (1| Plot) + (1| Plot \times Year) + (1| Species \times Year) \\ + (1| ID) + (1 + Con + Conmy + Hetmy| Species) \\ + (1 + Con + Conmy + Hetmy| Plot \times Species) \end{matrix}
$$

These models were fitted with R package ‘brms’ (Bürkner, 2017). The posterior distribution was sampled with five Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) chains, 500 burn-in and 4000 post-burn-in iterations, thinning interval of 10. The residual distribution was set as Student's *t*-distribution. The correlation of independent variables in these models was very weak (Figure S3) and the MCMC convergence of the models was satisfactory, as the potential scale reduction factors (Rhat) of the model parameters were <1.01. These models fit the data reasonably well based on the Bayesian residual plots (Figures S4–S8) from the R package ‘DHARMa’ (Hartig, 2022). CNDD and heterospecific negative density dependence (HNDD) were evaluated by predicting the changes in annual tree growth with an increase of one unit of conspecific and heterospecific density with the posterior samples generated from Bayesian models, separately. Stabilizing CNDD was calculated as the difference between the effect of CNDD and HNDD on tree growth, where negative values indicate stronger CNDD relative to HNDD. Effects of con-mycorrhizal heterospecific density and hetero-mycorrhizal heterospecific density on tree growth were also predicted by increasing one unit of density with the posterior samples generated from Bayesian models.

Apart from temperature, previous studies have found that the strength of plant–soil feedbacks may also be influenced by other environmental factors, such as precipitation (Comita et al., 2014) and soil phosphorus (Laliberté et al., 2015). In our dataset, precipitation and soil phosphorus density are highly correlated with temperature (Figure S9). We further conducted supplementary analyses to examine how the density dependence of tree growth varied along gradients of precipitation and soil phosphorus density. The patterns of CNDD mediated by mycorrhizal associations along the precipitation (Figure S10) and soil phosphorus density (Figure S11) were consistent with the results observed along the temperature gradient.

In addition, based on information on tree death, we performed supplementary analyses to measure density dependence of tree survival. However, these models showed limited power to explain adult tree mortality variation (Table S6). Consequently, we only considered the density dependence on tree growth. See supplemental information for more details (Supporting Information S1).

All analyses were conducted in R version 4.5 (R Core Team, 2025).

## 3 RESULTS

We examined how mean annual temperature modulates the strength of density dependence of tree growth. For all trees (Figure 2a–c), the average strength of CNDD exhibited a significant negative trend with increasing temperature, whereas the strength of HNDD showed a negative but non-significant trend. There was no strong evidence that the average strength of stabilizing CNDD on tree growth became more negative along the temperature gradient across all species (*p* (*β* <0) = 0.89).

The conspecific effect and heterospecific effect are predicted with posterior samples as the change in tree growth by increasing one unit of conspecific and heterospecific density, separately. Stabilizing CNDD is evaluated with posterior samples as the difference between the conspecific and heterospecific effect on tree growth. Points represent the average predictive values for species-by-plot combinations and colours indicate mycorrhizal types. Lines represent the average predictive values from all posterior samples at different temperatures with 95% credible intervals. Slopes with >95% probability of being negative are displayed as solid lines.

However, mycorrhizal symbiosis mediated the climatic patterns of stabilizing CNDD along the temperature gradient. For AM trees (Figure 2d–f), the effects of both CNDD and HNDD on tree growth became increasingly negative with rising temperatures. As a result, there was a distinct climatic pattern of stabilizing CNDD that was neutral to negative under warm temperatures and increased towards cold temperatures (*p* (*β* < 0) = 0.96). For EM trees (Figure 2g–i), there was no difference between CNDD and HNDD in their effects on tree growth, and the strength of stabilizing CNDD showed no trend along the temperature gradient (*p* (*β* < 0) = 0.77).

Different types of density dependence of tree growth varied under different temperature conditions (Figure 3). Under warm temperatures, CNDD effects were more negative for AM trees compared with EM trees. In addition, we found a more positive CNDD effect for AM trees compared with EM trees in cold forests. No significant positive effect of con-mycorrhizal heterospecific density on tree growth was observed in warm forests (Figure 3). In fact, classifying the heterospecific density into different mycorrhizal types did not improve the model performance (Table S3). For different temperature conditions and different mycorrhizal types, the effect of con-mycorrhizal heterospecific and hetero-mycorrhizal heterospecific density showed relatively small differences in tree growth.

## 4 DISCUSSION

CNDD represents a key mechanism for maintaining local tree species diversity (Wright, 2002). Strong CNDD can reduce the relative abundance of common species and stabilize the population dynamics of rare species, leading to the persistence of greater numbers of rare species in a community (Comita et al., 2010; Johnson et al., 2012). The effects of CNDD at different stages of life history may vary, and they jointly contribute to population demography and species fitness (O'Brien et al., 2022). Considerable evidence demonstrates the key role of CNDD in early life stages such as seeds and saplings (Comita et al., 2010; Zhu et al., 2018), while adult stages remain understudied (Hülsmann et al., 2021). Our results provide evidence of density dependence in the growth of adult trees, which is a key component of plant fitness. Density dependence of tree growth provides valuable insights into the role of CNDD in the maintenance of diversity for long-lived tree species (LaManna et al., 2021, 2022).

We found that the strength of density dependence on tree growth is mediated by environmental conditions and mycorrhizal symbioses. The density dependence of AM tree growth appears more sensitive to environmental conditions. The effect of CNDD as well as stabilizing CNDD, that is, the difference between CNDD and HNDD, on AM trees became increasingly negative under warm, moist and phosphorus-limited conditions. There was also evidence that CNDD tends to be stronger in warm (Song et al., 2018) and moist years (Lebrija-Trejos et al., 2023). Such environmental mediation of CNDD strength provides a more mechanistic understanding of the latitudinal (LaManna et al., 2017) and elevational (LaManna et al., 2022) patterns of CNDD. Specialized enemies (e.g. insects and pathogens) are major drivers of CNDD processes (Bagchi et al., 2014). The composition of natural enemies along with their impact on host species can be regulated by environmental conditions (Liu & He, 2019). For instance, the host specificity of herbivorous insects in tropical forests is greater than that in temperate forests (Dyer et al., 2007; Forister et al., 2015). Climate warming may increase the relative abundance of soil pathogens (Delgado-Baquerizo et al., 2020), potentially intensifying soil pathogen negative feedback and leading to stronger CNDD effects on tree performance (Liu & He, 2021). Increased moisture could extend the range of phytopathogen transmission and enhance the host specificity of phytopathogens, thereby intensifying the CNDD effect (Milici et al., 2020). Moreover, there may be a trade-off between efficient phosphorus acquisition and resistance to soil-borne pathogens (Newsham et al., 1995). In regions with low phosphorus availability, plants tend to invest more in short-lived and poorly defended fine roots that are more efficient at acquiring poorly mobile nutrients such as phosphorus but less capable of defending against soil-borne pathogens (Laliberté et al., 2015). Consequently, the mean strength of CNDD and stabilizing CNDD for AM trees becomes more negative under warm, moist and phosphorus-limited environments.

However, EM trees maintained constant CNDD along the environmental gradients and experienced relatively weaker conspecific limitation compared with AM trees in warm forests. Several studies have revealed a weaker CNDD effect for EM tree species than for AM tree species (Bennett et al., 2017; Delavaux et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2020, 2021). EM fungi can protect plants from soil pathogens through several mechanisms (Marx, 1972). First, EM fungi form a pseudoparenchymatous tissue known as the ‘sheathing mantle’, which covers the entire root tip and provides physical protection to roots (Genre et al., 2020). Second, EM fungi produce specific antibiotic compounds that offer chemical protection to plants (Lambers et al., 2018). In addition, EM fungi generally have high host specificity and are prone to form mycorrhizal networks associated with conspecific adults that benefit the growth of conspecifics (Liang et al., 2020; Simard et al., 2012). Overall, the pathogen-defence function (Chen et al., 2019) and positive plant–soil feedbacks (Liang et al., 2020) of EM fungi may mitigate or offset the CNDD effect on EM tree growth, resulting in weak and constant CNDD across the temperature gradient. However, we also found that the growth limitations on AM trees by conspecific density were weaker than those of EM trees under cold temperatures, consistent with a case study in temperate forests (Jevon et al., 2022). This unexpected pattern implies that mycorrhiza-induced plant–soil feedbacks cannot capture all variation in CNDD (Veresoglou & Lambers, 2025). For example, AM-associated trees might be superior colonizers of forest gaps and have a narrower ecological niche, leading to patchy distribution (Wulantuya et al., 2020). Forests under cold temperatures are dominated by EM tree species (Figure S12), and habitat preferences may simultaneously lead to patchy distribution and rapid growth of AM trees. The potential mechanisms underlying mycorrhizal mediated CNDD require further investigation (Veresoglou & Lambers, 2025).

The climatic patterns of local tree species richness are mainly attributed to AM tree species (Figure 1), which is consistent with evidence at the global scale (Li et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2021). These diversity patterns of AM and EM trees correspond well with the climatic patterns of stabilizing CNDD. Under warm, moist, and phosphorus-limited conditions, the stronger CNDD experienced by AM trees may promote greater AM tree species richness, while the constant CNDD of EM trees is likely to contribute little to EM species diversity (Figure 4). Our results also support stronger CNDD on AM tree growth than on EM tree growth in warm forests (H2). This may help explain why a higher proportion of EM trees can reduce tree species diversity in local communities, especially at low latitudes and in moist conditions (Jiang et al., 2025). The strong CNDD of AM trees in warm forests could theoretically facilitate both AM and EM species within the community, but empirical observations reveal disproportionate advantages gained by AM tree species (Figure 1). One hypothesis involves positive con-mycorrhizal density dependence that AM trees can benefit from shared mycorrhizal networks due to their relatively lower host specificity (Delavaux et al., 2023). However, our results do not support this hypothesis (H3). Heterospecific density effects across mycorrhizal types on tree growth are relatively consistent. Another potential explanation is that the forest mycorrhizal dominance depends on climate conditions (Steidinger et al., 2019). There is a bimodal pattern of AM versus EM tree relative abundances at the global scale, with EM associations dominating forests at high altitudes with cold and dry climates, and AM associations dominating warm tropical forests (Barceló et al., 2019). Our data confirm that the relative abundance of AM trees increases along the temperature gradient (Figure S12). AM fungi are highly efficient in the uptake of inorganic nutrients such as mineral phosphorus, which confers competitive advantages for their hosts in warm and humid environments with highly weathered soil (Deng et al., 2023; van der Heijden et al., 2015). EM fungi are more advantageous in cold temperatures with slow decomposition due to their capacity to acquire nutrients directly from organic matter (Shah et al., 2016; Steidinger et al., 2019). Overall, the differential nutrient acquisition strategies of AM and EM fungi give AM associations greater opportunities to establish within warm forests. The stronger CNDD for AM trees in warm forests partly explains why warm forests are composed of high-diversity AM species, rather than a few abundant AM species.

**Figure 4.** Conceptual summary of the environmental patterns of both conspecific negative density dependence (CNDD) and diversity for different mycorrhizal associations based on the findings of the present study. We found that the effect of stabilizing CNDD on arbuscular mycorrhizal tree growth is stronger under warm, moist and phosphorus-limited conditions. However, the strength of stabilizing CNDD on ectomycorrhizal tree growth remains stable along environmental gradients.
