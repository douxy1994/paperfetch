# Urban heat island analysis based on high resolution measurement data: A case study in Beijing

## Abstract

Urban heat island (UHI) is a widely concerned urban climate which impacts billions of residents worldwide. Nevertheless, inadequate rigorous air temperature (Tair) measurement has become an obstacle to UHI research and mitigation, especially in megacities like Beijing. In this study, we applied a 1-km hourly Tair dataset interpolated from records of 521 stations in Beijing. The dataset enables us to analyze UHI patterns on a block scale and the impacts of landscapes combined with the local climate zones (LCZ) scheme. Our results indicate that: (1) Tair and its temporal variation in each LCZ vary significantly, which are highly relevant to their morphology and thermal-radiative properties. (2) LCZ 1 and LCZ 2 have the highest nocturnal UHI intensities (UHII). Discrepancies among LCZs also cause various temporal variations in their UHII. (3) The spatial pattern of LCZs affects regional temperature. Area ratio and aggregation extent of LCZ 1&2 show significant positive correlations with regional temperature, while LCZ A&D&G have the opposite regularity. (4) Distinct mitigation strategies are put forward for each subarea in Beijing with various endowments of population and the LCZ structure. The study provides insight into Beijing UHI and plausible mitigation advice in megacities like Beijing.

## Introduction

Urban heat island (UHI), manifested as higher temperatures in urban areas than in surrounding rural areas, is one of the most widespread human-induced local climates (Oke, 1982; Peng et al., 2012). It can cause several negative impacts, including exacerbating the intensities and impacts of heat waves (Yang & Zhao, 2024), increasing the energy consumption for cooling (Li et al., 2019), and reducing air quality by accelerating formation or reducing dispersion of certain pollutants (Ulpiani, 2021). UHI can be attributed to less evapotranspiration on impervious surfaces, lower albedo of constructive material, more anthropogenic heat emission, less convection efficiency between urban surface and atmospheric boundary layer, and more stored heat by building materials than rural land surface (Taha, 1997; Owen, Carlson, & Gillies, 1998; Yuan & Bauer, 2007; Mahmood et al., 2014; Zhao et al., 2014).

Previous UHI research were mainly based on observed air temperature (Tair) within the urban canopy (the canopy UHI, CUHI) (Meier et al., 2017; Wei et al., 2022), or land surface temperature (LST) inversed from remote sensing images (the surface UHI, SUHI) (Hou et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Yang & Zhao, 2024). Despite SUHI and CUHI show similarities in spatial patterns (Wang et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2022), a handful of comparative analyses have found differences in spatiotemporal patterns and correlation with natural and urban factors (Ho et al., 2016; Hu et al., 2019; Du et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2022; Pena Acosta et al., 2023). Notably, SUHI intensities were greatly overestimated compared to CUHI which raised concerns in its application in urban heat risk assessment (Venter et al., 2021). In contrast, CUHI is more relevant to public health as Tair is a critical element in calculating indices for urban heat stress and human comfort evaluation (Zhao et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022).

CUHI research depends on stable, accurate, and high-resolution measurements. The complexity of urban surface and morphology gives rise to substantial variations in UHI, emphasizing the need for detailed spatial and temporal resolutions in such studies (Kim & Brown, 2021; Kianmehr et al., 2023). Previous CUHI research mainly gained Tair from four sources: self-set weather stations (Ribeiro et al., 2023), crowdsourcing data (Muller et al., 2015a), numerical urban models (Yang et al., 2023), and observation network (Muller et al., 2015b). Most of the data sources can provide high temporal resolution measurements while only few of them can offer high quality and spatial resolution measurements. The number of self-set stations is strictly limited due to the excessive cost (Yang et al., 2018), crowdsourcing data faces the challenges of rigorous data quality control (Napoly et al., 2018; Chapman et al., 2023), and numerical models are computational costly and still require validation with observation data (De Ridder et al., 2015). In contrast, observation network can provide both high quality and fine resolution measurements which are normally low-costly or free to access (Skarbit et al., 2017; Manalo et al., 2022). High resolution urban meteorological networks (UMN) have been applied in CUHI research and proved to be efficient in evaluating the heterogeneous heat environment in urban area (Azevedo et al., 2016).

Beijing Meteorological Bureau (BMB) has been building the UMN since the 1970s (Zhang et al., 2009), and the records have been widely applied in CUHI study since the 2010s (Liu et al., 2006; Miao et al., 2009; Dou et al., 2015; Cui et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2017). Prior applications primarily concentrated on the temporal variations of CUHI for individual stations, with limited attention given to the spatial patterns of CUHI on block scale. On the other hand, with the popularity of the local climate zones (LCZ) scheme in recent years, it has been applied in UHI analysis worldwide (Stewart et al., 2014; Leconte et al., 2015; Yang et al., 2019, 2021; Zhou et al., 2021). LCZ is an emerging classification system of urban-rural landscapes according to their morphological, radiative, and thermal properties (Stewart & Oke, 2012). Despite several researches that have combined LCZ with LST and simulated data in Beijing UHI analysis (Zhang et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), few of them are concerned about combining it with high spatial resolution measurements to put forward mitigation strategies in urban planning.

In this study, we applied a 1-km hourly observational dataset interpolated from measurements of 521 weather stations in the Beijing urban meteorological network to analyze the temporal-spatial characteristics of CUHI in Beijing. We combined gridded Tair data with gridded LCZ data to analyze the effects of urban structure on local and regional temperatures. The results will help to design plausible mitigation strategies for urban heat stress in Beijing.

## Data and methods

### Study area

Beijing, the capital of China, is located in the northern part of the North China Plain (39°26′N-41°03′N, 115°25′E-117°30′E). The total area of Beijing is 16,410 km<sup>2</sup>, with a population of over 20 million. Mountains encircle Beijing on the north and west sides, and their average elevation reaches 1 km (He et al., 2015). Most of the urban area locates on the south-central plains, where the altitude between 40 and 60 m. As of 2021, the whole built-up area in Beijing reached 3,517 km<sup>2</sup>, accounting for 21.43% of the whole city (Zhou et al., 2021).

Beijing has a typical continental monsoon climate, classified as Dwa according to the Köppen climatic classification (Li et al., 2023). Its winter is cold and dry, while summer is quite hot and humid. Because of the rapid urbanization in the past two decades, Beijing has suffered an increasingly serious UHI effect, with the strongest intensity in winter and the weakest in summer (Meng et al., 2018). Also, it showed a diurnal variation of the strong intensity at night and the weak one at noon (Miao et al., 2009). The study area includes the metropolis of Beijing and surrounding suburbs, with a range of 39.53°N-40.53°N and 115.78°E-116.87°E (Fig. 1). We filter out mountainous regions with altitude over 100 m to minimize the terrain effect on CUHI.

### LCZ data

The open-access LCZ data was provided by the World Urban Database and Access Portal Tools (WUDAPT) (Bechtel et al., 2015; Ching et al., 2018; Demuzere et al., 2021). The data was uploaded by Weibo Liu in January 2017 with a spatial resolution of 100 m (Liu, 2017). It classified Beijing landscapes in 2015 into 13 types, including 8 built types (LCZ 1-6, LCZ 8, and LCZ 10) and 5 land cover types (LCZ A, LCZ B, LCZ D, LCZ E, and LCZ G) (Fig. 1). We chose this data because it could reflect a general pattern of the landscapes during 2009-2020, regardless of land use change or urbanization.

### High resolution air temperature dataset

We collected an hourly Tair dataset ranging from 2009 to 2020 which is available on the website of the BMB (Beijing Meteorological Bureau, 2020). It incorporates records from 521 meteorological stations, including 20 manual stations and hundreds of automatic stations. All stations were set and maintained by BMB and provide measurements of Tair, precipitation, wind speed, wind direction, air pressure, and relative humidity. Most of the stations were set in green belts in urban area and were functioning well (Wang et al., 2017). The field investigation of stations was added to this research to ensure the station measurements could represent the surrounding temperature pattern and the built environment (Supplementary Material A).

We kept all stations available and processed rigorous quality control and weather selection to screen out valid Tair data with ideal weather. We followed the quality control method proposed by Yang Ping to filter out missing and biased observations from the raw data (Table 1) (Yang et al., 2011). To avoid the disturbance of unfavorable weather on the CUHI (Morris et al., 2001), we filtered out days with the maximum wind speed over 4 m/s (Giannaros & Melas, 2012), the cloud amount over 4 Oktas (Yang et al., 2016), recorded precipitation, and fog or haze weather. Over 14000 hours of reliable measurements were selected after the preprocessing.

| --- | --- | --- |
| Data missing | Station lacks observations for more than 10 h in one day | Remove all data of the station on that day |

| Abnormal diurnal variation | The daily range of Tair exceeds 24 °C | Discard all data of the site on that day |

We used ANUSPLIN, a professional spatial interpolation software developed by the Australian National University (Hutchinson & Xu, 2013), to rasterize Tair data. It can perform thin-plate smooth spline interpolation according to the location of meteorological stations and has been widely used in national and global surface climate background interpolation (Price et al., 2000; Hijmans et al., 2005). ANUSPLIN introduces altitude as a covariate to better deal with the terrain impact and applies a smooth function to ensure smoother interpolation results (Liu et al., 2008). The spatial resolution of output data was set to 1 km as most of the stations were separated by a few kilometers. Considering that LCZ performed best on block scale (Stewart & Oke, 2012), we downscaled the interpolated data to 100 m by assigning the Tair to each LCZ grid based on the nearest 1 km temperature grid (nearest neighbor method).

We selected one station for each LCZ to validate interpolation accuracy, the remaining sites in and out of the study area were used for interpolation (Fig. 3). There isn't any interpolation station in the 1 km range around each sample station so that the validation result won't be disturbed by adjacent stations. We chose 4 days from each year as the testing samples, representing four seasons, respectively (Supplementary Material B). We calculated the bias ($T_{interpolated} - T_{obs}$), mean-error (ME), and mean-absolute-error (MAE) between measurements and interpolated data on sample sites and plot them in Fig. 4.

The validation result suggests that 43.9%±6.7% of the bias is in the range of [-0.5 °C, 0.5 °C], 70.4%±7.5% of the bias is in the range of [-1.0 °C, 1.0 °C] and less than 8.1%±7.2% of the bias exceeds the range of [-2.0 °C, 2.0 °C]. The ME values of all sample sites fit in range of [-1.0 °C, 1.0 °C] and only A1023 (-0.69 °C), A1235 (-0.64 °C), A1087(-0.83 °C), and A1247(-0.60 °C) exceed the range of [-0.5 °C, 0.5 °C]. The MAE values of most stations are in range of [0.6 °C, 1.0 °C] except A1014 (1.28 °C), A1235 (1.38 °C), and A1087 (1.34 °C). Generally, the bias of interpolation is acceptable compared to the 1-3 °C temperature difference between LCZ types.

### Evaluation of UHII

We used the formula (1) proposed by Stewart and Oke (2012) to evaluate UHII as the difference between two LCZ types.

(1)

$$
UHII = T_{LCZX} - T_{LCZY}
$$

LCZ Y refers to the base type and LCZ X refers to other types. In this study, we selected the low plants (LCZ D) as the base type because it is widely distributed in the rural areas and performs well in the previous research (Stewart et al., 2014).

## Results

### Spatial-temporal characteristics of air temperature

Our interpolated Tair data performs well in reflecting the spatial pattern of UHI in the study area (Fig. 5). In both summer and winter nights, a huge heat island appears in the center of the study area, with up to 5 °C warmer than the surrounding areas. Moreover, the temperature gradient between urban and suburban areas is greater in winter nights, indicating a more significant UHI phenomenon in winter than in summer. In contrast, the structure of the heat island dissipates as the urban-rural temperature differences shrink to 0.5–1.5 °C on summer and winter days. The general variations of the CUHI pattern are consistent with previous research (Liu et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2016; Cui et al., 2017). The interpolated data also captures local atmospheric circulation impacts on the UHI pattern (Miao et al., 2015). For instance, oriented by the northwesterly prevailing wind in winter, the heat core of UHI expands southeasterly compared to summer, and a stronger sea-breeze development in summer causes rural areas in the north to be warmer than in winter.

Fig. 6(a) shows the daily pattern of Tair for each LCZ in summer (Jun-Aug) and winter (Dec-Feb). LCZ 1 (Compact high-rise) and LCZ 2 (Compact mid-rise) are the warmest in both summer and winter night, with 1 °C and 2 °C warmer than all the other types. LCZ A (Dense trees) is the coolest type in both winter and summer, with 0.5–2 °C cooler than other types. LCZ 5 (Open mid-rise) and LCZ 8 (Large low-rise) are 1–1.5 °C cooler than LCZ 1 and LCZ 2 but still 0.2–0.5 °C warmer than other open or low-rise built types. LCZ 3 (Compact low-rise), LCZ 4 (Open high-rise), and LCZ 6 (Open low-rise) are the coolest built types. LCZ 10 (Heavy industry) varies most during the day and night, which is the warmest during summer and winter noon as well as the coolest during summer and winter morning and night. LCZ D (Low plants) is 0.2–0.7 °C warmer than LCZ A while still cooler than most of the other types. LCZ B (Scattered trees) is surprisingly warm during the winter night, even warmer than most of the built types. This may be caused by the foehn weather on the east slope of the Taihang Mountains (Wang et al., 2012). The warm and dry wind creates a warm area in the southwest part of the study area where most LCZ B is located (Fig. 5) and also leads to an abnormal warming at 5:00-6:00 LT in the winter (Luo et al., 2020). Because of the nonnegligible terrain effect on LCZ B temperature, its UHII won't be discussed in the following article.

Daily pattern of Tair for each LCZ. (a) hourly temperature of each LCZ in summer (Jun-Aug) and winter (Dec-Feb). (b-c) hourly temperature variation (°C) of each LCZ in summer and winter, respectively

Fig. 6(b and c) illustrates the hourly temperature variations for each LCZ during summer and winter, respectively. Notably, LCZ 1 and LCZ 2, identified as the warmest in Fig. 6(a), exhibit relatively low rates of warming after sunrise and cooling after sunset. Specifically, LCZ 1 displays the lowest warming and cooling rates in both winter (-0.50 °C/h and 0.99 °C/h) and summer (-0.48 °C/h and 1.15 °C/h). The sluggish warming is primarily attributed to shading effects of buildings and heat storage of artificial materials (Christen & Vogt, 2004; Wang et al., 2017). The subdued cooling rates result from the release of stored heat and massive anthropogenic heat emissions in densely populated areas (Donateo et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2024). In contrast, LCZs characterized by open or low-rise buildings, such as LCZ 3-6 and LCZ 8, demonstrate much higher warming and cooling rates due to their larger sky view factors (SVF) (Stewart & Oke, 2012). Notably, LCZ 10 exhibits the highest rates of warming and cooling in winter (1.23 °C/h and -0.65 °C/h), attributed to its high SVF and substantial anthropogenic heat emissions during the daytime. While LCZ A and LCZ D share a similar SVF with LCZ 3-6, they store less heat during the day, resulting in slightly higher warming rates compared to open or low-rise buildings. Additionally, their cooling rates are slightly elevated relative to LCZ 3-6 due to increased latent heat flux through evapotranspiration. Furthermore, LCZ G displays relatively low warming and cooling rates, attributed to the high specific heat capacity of water. These findings suggest that variations in the morphology and thermal-radiative properties of LCZs contribute to diverse temporal temperature patterns, further influencing the UHI pattern.

### Diurnal and seasonal characteristics of UHII

Fig. 7 presents the annual average hourly UHII patterns for each LCZ. In ideal weather conditions, LCZ 1 and LCZ 2 have the strongest UHII compared to other LCZs. Their hourly UHII keep positive in most of the time and can reach over 4 °C. All the other built types also have positive UHII at night, ranging from 0.5 to 2 °C. The UHII of most LCZs with positive values have similar variation patterns: they usually increase around the sunset and decline at about 1–1.5 h after sunrise. The 1-hour lag decrease in UHII may be due to the increasing anthropogenic heat produced in the morning and the continued release of stored heat overnight. In contrast, LCZ 10 has the opposite variation on its UHII. It often strengthens after sunrise and weakens around sunset, which is consistent with the working hours of factories. This suggests that the anthropogenic heat is a prominent factor for the UHII of LCZ 10. LCZ E and LCZ G only perform a weakly positive UHII during the nighttime and a slightly negative UHII during the daytime while LCZ A presents a significant negative UHII during the whole year.

Annual average hourly patterns of UHII (°C) for each LCZ. Blank patches refer to missing data after preprocessing. Red line at the top refers to the time of sunset, green line in the middle refers to the time of noon, blue line at the bottom refers to the time of sunrise.

Fig. 8 depicts the nocturnal (0:00–6:00 LT) UHII for each LCZ. LCZ 1 and LCZ 2 consistently exhibit the highest nocturnal UHII values in both winter, summer, and the annual average. LCZ 5 and LCZ 8 also stand out with nocturnal UHII exceeding 0.8 °C in winter and the annual average. Other built types display comparatively lower nocturnal UHII, with an annual average ranging between 0.4 and 0.6 °C. LCZ A is unique in having the sole negative nocturnal UHII among all types, with winter, summer, and annual averages all surpassing -0.5 °C. In contrast, LCZ E and LCZ G only register positive UHII values below 0.5 °C. Most LCZs exhibit higher UHII values during winter compared to summer. LCZ E, however, displays a notably stronger UHII in summer than in winter, possibly attributed to seasonal changes in LCZ D. LCZ D predominantly represents cropland in the study area, with its growing season occurring in the summer and harvest taking place in the fall. Consequently, LCZ D and LCZ E share similar surface properties in winter, while LCZ E exhibits heightened evapotranspiration in summer.

### Relationship between LCZ spatial pattern and regional temperature

We have confirmed that Tair varies among LCZs, but how does LCZ patterns affect urban Tair on a regional scale? To answer the question, we apply sliding window method to build a statistical model between the LCZ distribution and regional temperature. We design three different sizes of windows (3 * 3 km<sup>2</sup>, 5 * 5 km<sup>2</sup>, 7 * 7 km<sup>2</sup>) to test the scale effect. The window temperature (Twin) refers to the average Tair for grids in a window. We measure the LCZ patterns from two aspects: their areas in the region and aggregation extent. Each aspect is evaluated with the area ratios (AR) and division index (DI), respectively:

(2)

$$
AR = \frac{AreaofeachLCZ}{Areaofwindow}
$$

(3)

$$
DI = 1 - ∑{(\frac{Areaofpatch}{Areaofwindow})}^{2}
$$

AR ranges from 0 to 1 and the LCZ occupies a larger area when its AR gets close to 1. DI also ranges from 0 to 1 and the LCZ distribution gets more dispersed when the value is close to 1. To simplify the analysis, we categorize LCZs into three groups: LCZ 1&2 (G1), LCZ 3-6&8 (G2), and LCZ A&D&G (G3). Each LCZ group has similar UHII and thermal properties. LCZ 10 and LCZ E are excluded because they don't fit any groups and their distribution in the study area is too small (< 1%) to form a new group.

Fig. 9 shows all the linear regression models between spatial pattern indicators of each LCZ group and their Twin. To minimize the impact of other LCZ groups, only windows where the analyzed LCZ groups constitute the majority types (AR > 0.5) were selected as input data. Spots in each plot refer to the average Twin of windows with the same AR or DI. Fig. 9(a–c) illustrates the linear regression models between the AR of each LCZ group and their Twin. In all window sizes, there exists a significant positive correlation between the AR of G1 and G2 with Twin. Notably, G1 has higher R<sup>2</sup> values (0.28-0.32) compared to G2 (0.16-0.19), underscoring the stronger warming impact of G1 relative to their area percentage compared to G2. Conversely, the AR of G3 demonstrates a significant negative correlation with Twin, resulting in a cooling effect. As the window size increases, the slope of G1 decreases from 1.03 to 0.73, whereas the slope of G2 increases from 0.63 to 1.03. This signifies that a larger G1 area manifests a more pronounced warming effect at the local scale, while G2 demonstrates a stronger warming effect on a broader scale. Despite minimal variation in the slope of G3 across the three window sizes, its R<sup>2</sup> decreases from 0.32 to 0.15 with the expanding window sizes. This suggests that area of G3 contributes more significantly to the local-scale temperature cooling as opposed to its impact on a larger scale.

![Fig. 9](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S2210670724002178-gr9_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

Regression results between (a-c) AR and Twin; (d-f) DI and Twin. G1 is shown in red, G2 is shown in blue, and G3 is shown in green. s refers to the slope, *** refers to the slope being significant under 99% level, R<sup>2</sup> refers to the R-squared value of the model.

Fig. 9(d–f) shows the regression models between the DI of each LCZ group and their Twin. Across all window sizes, DI of G1 and G2 exhibit a significant negative correlation with Twin. This implies that when being aggregated, these zones contribute to a significant warming effect on Twin. The R<sup>2</sup> values for G1 (0.18–0.35) remain considerably higher than those for G2 (0.06-0.12), highlighting the stronger impact of G1 aggregation on regional temperature compared to G2. In contrast, the DI of G3 displays a positive correlation with Twin, suggesting that the aggregated effect of these zones leads to a cooling trend. G1 shows a lower slope on 5 km (-0.46) and 7 km (-0.47) scales and higher R<sup>2</sup> (0.34, 0.35) compared to 3 km scale (s=-0.55, R<sup>2</sup>=0.18), indicating a stronger effect on the local scale and a more significant contribution to regional temperature variation on a broader scale. With the window size increases, both the slope and R<sup>2</sup> of G2 rise, suggesting that their aggregation has stronger effects and contributes more to regional temperature variations on a broader scale. In contrast, G3 exhibits an insignificant scale effect across various window sizes.

## Discussion

### Evaluation of the high-resolution measurements

Our research on CUHI aligns with prior studies. Our findings reveal positive UHII for all built types LCZ, whereas land cover types exhibit weak positive or negative UHII (Zhang et al., 2023). LCZ 1 and LCZ 2 display a much stronger UHI effect than all the open or low-rise buildings (Chen et al., 2022). LCZs exhibit notable interclass variations in Tair, with the most significant differences observed in winter nights and the lowest in summer days (Ren et al., 2023). Additionally, the percentage of area and aggregation of built types significantly contribute to regional temperature warming effects (Zhang et al., 2022).

Our study benefits from high-resolution UMN data, offering advantages over previous Beijing CUHI research relying on a limited number of automatic weather stations (Cui et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2022). The detailed spatial variation of Tair in our dataset facilitates the analysis of spatial patterns and LCZ effects on CUHI at the block scale. Compared to SUHI research in Beijing, UMN data provides higher temporal resolution and detailed atmospheric information, aiding in controlling wind effects and analyzing hourly variations of CUHI (Zhang et al., 2021).

While our study effectively captures spatial patterns of CUHI at the block scale, the limited number of stations poses challenges in analyzing CUHI within blocks or even at the meter scale. For higher-resolution CUHI research, we recommend incorporating temperature data from numerical simulations and on-site measurements (Unal Cilek & Cilek, 2021; Ren et al., 2023).

### An ideal experiment

Previous research proved that reallocating LCZ is an efficient mitigation strategy for UHI (Yi et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023). According to the Results, decreasing G1 and increasing G3 seems to be a one-fit-all method for CUHI mitigation. However, this will also decrease the accommodation supply. Despite the urgency of heat stress, maintaining city functions for development is still the key premise of city planning. In such conditions, distinct mitigation measures should be applied to regions with specific endowments of population and LCZ structure.

To put forward more feasible advice for urban planning, we conduct an ideal experiment by leveraging the conclusions in Results 3.3. We select all 5 * 5 km<sup>2</sup> windows consisting of four LCZ groups and calculate their summer Twin with mean Tair from June to August as the heat stress is the most threatening in summer in Beijing (Miao et al., 2022).

Fig. 10(b) shows the mean summer Twin for windows with identical LCZ composition. Black patches indicate missing data and shaded patches refer to estimated Twin calculated with regression models in Results 3.3 (Supplementary Material C). The bottom-axis represents the AR of G2, the left-axis represents the AR of G1 and the right-axis represents the AR of G3. We categorize all windows into four areas according to their LCZ composition: areas where G1, G2, or G3 occupy over 50% (A1, A2, and A3), and areas where all LCZ groups occupy less than 50% (A4). Fig. 10(a) offers an instruction for (b), and (c) shows the spatial pattern of each subarea.

![Fig. 10](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S2210670724002178-gr10_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

Results of the ideal experiment. (a) a brief instruction for ternary plot, (b) ternary plot of Twin for windows consist of various proportions of G1, G2, and G3, (c) spatial pattern for each area defined in (b) in the study area.

A1 is the hottest and most densely populated, which mainly distributes in the central urban area in Beijing. For this area, replacing G1 with G3 can cause a significant cooling effect of up to 1.2 °C, while it will also seriously decrease the population carrying capacity. To maintain the accommodation supply, replacing one unit of G1 with a minimum of two units of G2 is imperative, given the negligible cooling effect (for example, Twin of windows with 80% G1 and 4% G2 is 28.18 °C, Twin of windows with 72% G1 and 20% G2 is 27.80 °C, Twin of windows with 64% G1 and 36% G2 is 28.18 °C). In such circumstance, A1 faces a dilemma between huge population pressure and severe heat stress.

A2 has middle density of buildings and population, which mainly allocate in the suburban area. Despite G2 being the majority types, G1 and G3 still greatly influence Twin in A2. For windows with 80% G2, those who have 20% G1 and 0% G3 are 0.7 °C warmer than those who have 0% G1 and 20% G3. This also explains why R<sup>2</sup> of G2 in Results 4.3 is less significant than that of G1 and G3.

A3, mainly represents the rural area, has the lowest Twin among all four areas. High coverage of G3 causes A3 1-2 °C cooler than A1 and 0.5-1 °C cooler than A2. In condition of G3 taking over 70% of the area, the Twin keeps under 27 °C regardless of the construction area. When the built types occupy over 30% of the area, Twin will rise 0.4-0.7 °C with the decrease of AR of G3.

A4 is a hybrid area which mainly located in the junction of other areas. Due to the complex composition of LCZs in this area, it is hard to evaluate the missing temperature and reveal a significant regularity among AR of each LCZ groups and Twin. Based on limited Twin data, we suggest that increasing G1 or decreasing G3 may cause 0.2–0.6 °C warming on Twin.

### Distinct mitigation strategies

In accordance to the results of the ideal experiment, we confirm that taking distinct mitigation strategies in areas with specific endowment for population and LCZ structure is necessary and efficient. We conclude several suggestions as follow:
(1)
In densely populated central urban areas (A1), addressing the challenge of balancing the significant demand for accommodation with the mitigation of severe heat stress during LCZ reallocation presents a considerable dilemma. To tackle this issue, we propose to build city parks and planting trees along streets to curb the aggregation of buildings. It is advisable to concentrate green infrastructure rather than dispersing it (Jang et al., 2024), with an emphasis on the cultivation of taller trees for greater efficiency (Huang et al., 2021). Additionally, we advocate for the adoption of cooling roofs and the substitution of building materials to enhance surface albedo and reduce heat retention (Chen et al., 2022). However, we advise against the widespread implementation of green roofs due to factors such as inadequate rainfall and a shortage of irrigation water in the Beijing area.
(2)
In suburban areas characterized by moderate population and building density (A2), a reduction in green spaces and an increase in high-density communities could significantly exacerbate heat conditions. Preserving adequate green areas (over 10%) and regulating the expansion of high-density buildings (less than 40%) by replacing them with low-density or low-rise communities is recommended to alleviate UHI. Opting for extensive coverage of open spaces or low-rise buildings not only provides ample accommodation but also helps minimize heat stress within the area.
(3)
In rural areas (A3), the construction of new towns or satellite cities offers a viable solution to enhance population carrying capacity while effectively managing heat stress levels within acceptable limits. However, it is crucial to recognize that the cooling effect provided by vegetation and water in rural areas is not unlimited. Excessive construction of densely packed buildings in new towns or satellite cities may exacerbate urban heat stress. Hence, city planners must prioritize the consideration of urban heat stress when designing and developing new towns (You et al., 2023). By carefully integrating green spaces, water features, and appropriate building densities, planners can create sustainable environments that optimize both population growth and thermal comfort.
(4)
In hybrid areas characterized by a mix of buildings and landscapes (A4), it is anticipated that they may transition to either A1 or A2 classifications during the urbanization process. To effectively mitigate urban heat stress in the future, we recommend the conversion of A4 areas to A2 by prioritizing the construction of additional open spaces or low-rise communities. This transition offers greater benefits in terms of heat stress alleviation and ensures sustainable urban development.

### Limitations and future studies

Our research exhibits several limitations. Firstly, in comparison to numerical simulations and self-set measurements, the UMN dataset employed in our study lacks the granularity to provide insights into CUHI spatial patterns below the block scale. Additionally, a more comprehensive statistical and thermodynamic explanation regarding how LCZ patterns influence regional temperature is necessary. Despite our efforts to minimize terrain effects by excluding high-altitude areas, further studies should investigate the impact of terrain on CUHI in Beijing. Moreover, for a more thorough exploration of landscape effects on regional temperature, apart from the area ratio and aggregation of landscapes, additional factors should be considered, such as the compositions and configurations of urban green spaces (Zheng et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022), urban ventilation (Yang et al., 2019), and so on. Our future work will prioritize elucidating the thermodynamic mechanisms through which urban landscape patterns affect CUHI on the block scale, leveraging UMN data and numerical simulations.

## Conclusion

In this study, we utilized 1-km resolution interpolated Tair data combined with the LCZ scheme to investigate the characteristics of CUHI in Beijing and its influencing factors. Here are the conclusions:
1.
The gridded Tair data derived from the Beijing UMN measurements effectively capture the spatial and temporal dynamics of the UHI phenomenon at a block level, enabling a detailed analysis of its characteristics.
2.
The diurnal and seasonal variations in Tair across different LCZs highlight the diverse thermal behaviors of urban environments. The distinct morphological, radiative, and thermal properties of LCZs result in varying cooling and warming rates throughout the day.
3.
LCZ 1 and LCZ 2 demonstrate the most pronounced UHII, with temperature differentials of 1.5-2 °C, while other built environments exhibit moderate UHII ranging from 0.5-1 °C. LCZ E and LCZ G show a weak UHI effect (0-0.5 °C), while LCZ A displays a negative UHII (-0.5 °C). Notably, LCZ 10 displays an interesting pattern with higher UHII during the daytime and lower UHII at night, indicative of the significant influence of anthropogenic heat emissions.
4.
The spatial distribution of LCZs exerts a substantial impact on regional temperature patterns. The area ratio and aggregation extent for LCZ 1&2, as well as LCZ 3-6&8, contribute to significant warming effects, while for those of LCZs A&D&G exhibit a cooling effect. Furthermore, the magnitude of these effects varies with spatial scale, with the warming influence of LCZ 1&2 weakening and that of LCZs 3-6&8 strengthening as the analysis area expands. LCZ A&D&G, however, do not demonstrate a significant scale effect.
5.
Tailored mitigation strategies are devised for different subareas categorized by LCZ patterns. By comprehending the nuanced interplay between urban form and temperature dynamics, targeted interventions can be developed to mitigate the UHI effect while ensuring adequate accommodation (Fig. 2).
