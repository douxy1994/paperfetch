# Historical and contemporary climate jointly determine angiosperm plant diversity patterns across east Eurasia

## Abstract

Mechanisms underlying large-scale spatial patterns of species richness are one of the central issues in ecology. Although contemporary climate, evolutionary history, and historical climate change have been proposed as drivers of species richness patterns, variation in the relative importance of different factors remains a major challenge. Here, using newly compiled distribution data with a spatial resolution of 100 × 100 km for 43 023 angiosperms plant species in east Eurasia, we mapped species richness patterns for plants with different growth forms (i.e. woody versus herbaceous) and range sizes (i.e. wide-ranged versus narrow-ranged species), and compared the relative importance of the four hypotheses in explaining these patterns, i.e. freezing tolerance hypothesis, historical climate change hypothesis, Janzen's hypothesis (predicting that climate seasonality and topography determine species richness patterns), and diversification rate hypothesis. We found that species richness of all angiosperm plants presented a clear latitudinal gradient and was highest in southwestern China and Central Asian mountains. Notably, species richness patterns and their dominant drivers differed between species groups. Historical climate change was the dominant driver for richness patterns of all and herbaceous species. The freezing tolerance hypothesis dominated the drivers for all woody species, while Janzen's hypothesis dominated narrow-ranged woody and herbaceous species. Our study suggests that different hypotheses contribute to large-scale plant richness patterns via their effects on different plant groups. Our results did not support the diversification rate hypothesis, but demonstrated the high importance of historical climate change to plant diversity in east Eurasia, providing new perspectives on the mechanisms of plant diversity patterns in this continent.

## Introduction

Global biodiversity is facing rapid loss due to anthropogenic threats, and effective conservation of biodiversity has become a global challenge. Revealing large-scale patterns of species richness and the underlying mechanisms provides data and scientific support for the spatial prioritization of biodiversity conservation, and hence has increasingly intrigued ecologists over the past decades (Hawkins and Diniz-Filho 2004, Ricklefs 2004, Wang et al. 2011, Pimm et al. 2014, McCauley et al. 2015). Previous studies have widely investigated large-scale patterns in species richness of terrestrial vertebrates, especially amphibians (Buckley and Jetz 2007, Jenkins et al. 2013), birds (Davies et al. 2007, Hawkins et al. 2007, Jetz et al. 2012), and mammals (Schipper et al. 2008, Karanth et al. 2009, Buckley et al. 2010). However, despite the important role of angiosperms in terrestrial ecosystems, the patterns of angiosperm species richness remain much less studied in east Eurasia due to the scarcity of high-resolution distribution data (Kreft and Jetz 2007, Jenkins et al. 2013).

In order to comprehend the mechanisms underlying spatial patterns of species richness, several theoretical hypotheses based on the effects of the contemporary environment, diversification rates, and historical climate change have been proposed and widely discussed (Jetz and Rahbek 2002, Willig et al. 2003, Currie et al. 2004, Ricklefs 2006, Svenning and Skov 2007, Wang et al. 2012). Many studies have identified contemporary climate as a crucial driver of patterns in species richness (O'Brien 1993, Wang et al. 2009). Among the contemporary climate hypotheses, the freezing tolerance hypothesis has been recognized as a key mechanism for explaining the effect of contemporary climate on latitudinal gradients in plant species richness across northern temperate regions (Latham and Ricklefs 1993, Wang et al. 2011, Xu et al. 2016, Su et al. 2020).

The assumption under the freezing tolerance hypothesis is evolutionary niche conservatism (Latham and Ricklefs 1993, Zanne et al. 2014). This hypothesis suggests that most clades originated in tropical climates, and struggled to evolve adaptations to cold climates at high latitudes due to evolutionary niche conservatism, which led to the decrease in species richness from tropical to temperate regions. Based on this hypothesis, recent freezing temperatures are suggested to be the dominant factor for geographic patterns in species richness. This hypothesis has been employed to explain woody species richness in east Eurasia (Wang et al. 2011, Su et al. 2020). For example, the mean temperature of the coldest quarter was found to be the most influential factor for patterns of woody plant richness in China and of woody families with tropical affinities in east Eurasia (Wang et al. 2011, Su et al. 2020).

Another widely discussed hypothesis concerning the influence of contemporary climate on patterns of species richness is Janzen's hypothesis (Ghalambor et al. 2006, Gill et al. 2016, Liu et al. 2017, Lyu et al. 2018, Smith 2018). Janzen (1967) found that tropical mountains have very low temperature seasonality, while temperate mountains have large temperature seasonality. The large temperature seasonality in temperate mountains requires that temperate species evolve broad ranges of temperature tolerances to adapt to large seasonal climatic variations. In contrast, tropical organisms could evolve narrow temperature tolerances due to the less seasonal nature of the tropical climate. Therefore, Janzen (1967) postulated that tropical mountain passes pose more formidable obstacles to species dispersal than temperate mountain passes with similar elevations, which leads to higher speciation rates and species richness in tropical than temperate mountains. Thus, areas with small seasonal changes in temperature and high habitat heterogeneity generally have higher species richness. Several recent studies provided support for Janzen's hypothesis. For instance, Polato et al. (2018) revealed that tropical insect species have narrower temperature tolerance and higher speciation rates than temperate ones. Janzen's hypothesis also explains well the *Rhododendron* species richness pattern in China (Shrestha et al. 2018).

In addition to the effects of the contemporary environment, recent studies suggest that evolutionary history also exerts a crucial role in shaping geographical species richness patterns (Stevens 2006, 2011). Specifically, the diversification rate hypothesis argues that high species richness in certain regions is a result of high diversification rates (Mittelbach et al. 2007, Svenning et al. 2008, Tietje et al. 2022). Previous studies have provided evidence in favor of the diversification rate hypothesis. For example, studies have shown that diversification rate serves as the most influential predictor of the latitudinal gradient of species richness for amphibians (Pyron and Wiens 2013), mammals (Rolland et al. 2014), and flowering plants (Jansson and Davies 2008). However, recent studies have found contrasting results and indicated that regions with high diversification rates did not necessarily have high species richness (Igea and Tanentzap 2020, Cerezer et al. 2022, Dimitrov et al. 2023). Whether the species richness pattern of plants in east Eurasia supports the diversification rate hypothesis remains to be tested.

Recent studies have found that modern species richness may have also been influenced by historical climate change. Since the last glacial maximum (LGM, ca 21 000–18 000 years ago), high latitudes of the Northern Hemisphere were covered by massive glaciers. The presence of glaciers had dramatic impacts on plant distributions (Svenning and Skov 2005, Montoya et al. 2007, Davies et al. 2009, Sandel et al. 2011), resulting in the migration of a large number of plant species to the south or extinction during the LGM. After the glaciers retreated, species re-colonized the northern latitudes. However, many species did not spread northward to areas where the climate was suitable because of dispersal constraints (Svenning and Skov 2005, Normand et al. 2011). For example, recent studies showed that climate change since the LGM was the dominant factor of species richness for trees, amphibians, reptiles, and dung beetles in Europe (Svenning and Skov 2005, Araújo et al. 2008, Hortal et al. 2011, Feng et al. 2019), for woody plants in Central Asia (Liu et al. 2019), and trees in the Northern Hemisphere (Feng et al. 2019), and played an important role in shaping mammal, bird, and amphibian richness patterns in Africa (Pinkert et al. 2020).

Although these hypotheses have been supported in different studies, their relative importance in determining species richness patterns remains uncertain, which has hindered the development of general theories on mechanisms of species richness patterns. Moreover, previous studies suggested that east Eurasia has been much less affected by climate change since the LGM than Europe and North America (Qian 2002, Qian and Ricklefs 2004). Whether climate change since the LGM is a weaker predictor of plant richness patterns in east Eurasia than contemporary climate and diversification rates remains to be tested.

Woody and herbaceous growth forms have different responses to climate changes owing to their different life history strategies (Steinberg et al. 1990, Pretzsch 2002, Massad 2013, Luo et al. 2023). Moreover, species with different range sizes (i.e. narrow-ranged versus wide-ranged species) may also respond differently to climate changes (Svenning and Skov 2007). Previous studies suggest that narrow-ranged species tend to have constrained dispersal abilities and cannot spread to all suitable habitats, hence their species richness patterns are more susceptible to local evolutionary processes (Dynesius and Jansson 2000, Jansson 2003, Liu et al. 2017). In contrast, wide-ranged species are more able to cope with climate variations, and their richness patterns tend to be more strongly associated with environmental factors (Liu et al. 2017). Understanding variations in drivers underlying species richness patterns among woody and herbaceous growth forms, and among species with different range sizes, would improve our capability to predict the impacts of global changes on communities dominated by different species (Zanne et al. 2018, Luo et al. 2023). Nonetheless, previous studies of plant species richness patterns have been strongly biased towards woody plants, while richness patterns of herbaceous species are much less studied due to a lack of data. Freezing tolerance has been frequently considered to be an important factor influencing species richness patterns of woody plants (Oommen and Shanker 2005, Wang et al. 2011, Su et al. 2020). Compared with woody plants, herbaceous plants are considered to be less sensitive to freezing temperatures due to their shorter lifespan, higher rate of niche evolution, and weaker niche conservation (Smith and Beaulieu 2009, Zanne et al. 2018). However, whether woody and herbaceous species richness is similarly dominated by the freezing tolerance hypotheses remains to be tested.

East Eurasia has high plant diversity, preserves high proportions of endemic and relict plant species, and contains several global biodiversity hotspots (i.e. Central Asian mountains, Himalayas, southwest China mountains) (Myers et al. 2000). Previous studies indicated that plant species richness in east Eurasia is significantly greater than that observed at equivalent latitudes in Europe and North America due to evolutionary and ecological mechanisms (Qian 2002). Therefore, understanding species richness patterns in this region is important not only for gaining insights into the evolutionary development of floras in the Northern Hemisphere (Xu et al. 2013, Chen et al. 2018), but also for biodiversity conservation planning. However, the paucity of data on plant distributions in east Eurasia compared to Europe and North America severely limits our understanding of plant species richness patterns and their drivers in this region (Hughes et al. 2021).

In earlier investigations, Wang et al. (2011) and Su et al. (2020) established a database on the distribution of woody plants across China and east Eurasia, providing a foundational resource for exploring plant richness in the region. However, data on the distribution of herbaceous plants in this region are still scarce. Here, we compiled a comprehensive database of angiosperm plant distributions in east Eurasia and mapped the species richness patterns for all species, and for different species groups separately (i.e. woody, herbaceous, wide-ranged, and narrow-ranged species). Then we explored the determinants of species richness patterns for different species groups. Our objective is to evaluate the following three hypotheses. 1) Climate change since the LGM has weaker effects on species richness patterns in east Eurasia than contemporary climate and diversification rates. 2) The dominant hypothesis for species richness patterns differs between growth forms and between different range size groups. Specifically, we expect that woody and herbaceous species richness will be dominated by the cold tolerance hypothesis and climate change since the LGM, respectively. Wide-ranged woody and herbaceous richness is dominated by the contemporary climate, while narrow-ranged woody and herbaceous richness is dominated by climate change since the LGM.

## Material and methods

### Species distribution data

The Database of Angiosperm Plant Distribution in east Eurasia was newly compiled, containing geographic distributions with a spatial resolution of 100 × 100 km for 43 023 angiosperm plant species (from 4479 genera and 316 families). The species distributions were compiled from more than one hundred published regional and local floras and checklists for China, Central Asia (Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Turkmenistan, Uzbekistan), Mongolia, and Asian Russia. Occurrence data for these countries were also obtained from online databases, including the ‘National Specimen Information Infrastructure' (NSII, < www.nsii.org.cn>, accessed in May 2019), ‘the Global Biodiversity Information Facility' (GBIF, < http://www.gbif.org>), and the ‘Virtual Guide to the Flora of Mongolia' (< http://greif.uni-greifswald.de/floragreif>). The complete list of data sources is available in Supporting information.

The raw species distribution data in the data sources were normally recorded in the following formats: 1) spatial polygons (e.g. species distribution ranges), 2) spatial points (e.g. occurrences of specimen collections), and 3) administrative (e.g. villages, towns, counties) and geographic units (e.g. mountains and nature reserves) where species occurrences were recorded. The distribution data in the first two formats from published floras and checklists were manually digitized using ArcGIS 10.2 (ESRI 2013). In contrast, distribution data in the third format were georeferenced using GeoNames (containing over 25 million geographical names, http://www.geonames.org), Global Administrative Areas Database (https://gadm.org), and World Geographical Scheme for Recording Plant Distributions (https://www.tdwg.org/standards/wgsrpd). To enhance the precision of species distributions, we only kept distribution records of administrative/geographic units at the county level or smaller so that the records can be precisely transformed into 1–2 grid cells. Additionally, the coordinates of specimen occurrences available in the online databases were downloaded.

We used the cylindrical equal area projection for the compilation of species distribution data and for all analyses. Specifically, the raw distribution data were transformed into gridded distributions using a grid of 100 × 100 km with the cylindrical equal area projection. A grid cell was considered as presence for a species when it contained at least one point of specimen locations, or more than 25% of its area was covered by the species' distribution range comprising polygons and administrative/geographic units. We also recalculated species richness using a threshold of 0.25% (i.e. when more than 0.25% of a grid cell was covered by a species distribution range, the species was considered to be present in the grid cell), and the results were consistent with those based on the threshold of 25% (Supporting information). The nomenclature of species names from different sources was merged following the Catalogue of Life (http://www.catalogueoflife.org). After finishing the database, we carefully examined the species distribution data manually to enhance data quality. The records of cultivated and exotic species were removed and intraspecific units (i.e. subspecies and varieties) were regarded as the same species. We removed the data in the Caspian Sea, the Korean Peninsula and a small part of China (e.g. Taiwan) because of the low quality of distribution data in these regions. It is noteworthy that the number of data sources for compiling distribution data varies across different regions. Compared to China and Russia, the number of data sources in Mongolia and Central Asian countries is relatively low. However, our database has collected all available data that could be obtained, and it is currently one of the most comprehensive plant species distribution databases for east Eurasia.

### Environmental data

To assess the impact of the freezing tolerance hypothesis on species richness patterns, mean temperature of the coldest quarter (MTCQ, °C. The coldest quarter is defined as the period from December through January–February) was used following prior research (Wang et al. 2011, Liu et al. 2017, Su et al. 2020). We selected temperature seasonality (TSN, standard deviation of monthly mean temperature within one year) and elevational range within a grid cell (TOPO_RANGE) to represent Janzen's hypothesis. MTCQ and TSN were obtained from the WorldClim database (http://www.worldclim.org, ver. 2) with a spatial resolution of 30 arc seconds (∼ 1 km at the equator). Then, we calculated the average of MTCQ and TSN in each grid cell (100 × 100 km). TOPO_RANGE with each grid cell was determined by calculating the difference between the maximum and minimum elevations, using GTOPO data at a spatial resolution of 1 × 1 km (https://www.usgs.gov). We also calculated the 5–95% elevational range within each grid cell and found that the 5–95% elevational range was highly correlated with the elevational range (Supporting information). We also analyzed the relationship between species richness and other environmental factors (Supporting information).

Paleoclimate change variables included temperature and precipitation changes since the LGM (MAT anomaly and MAP anomaly, respectively), which were computed as the absolute differences between the LGM and contemporary temperature and precipitation values, and were used to evaluate the historical climate change hypothesis. The temperature and precipitation data in the LGM were reconstructed by the MPI-ESM-P model (Watanabe et al. 2011) and were obtained from the WorldClim website, with a spatial resolution of 1 arc minute.

### Diversification rate

Net diversification rate (DR) was used to evaluate the diversification rate hypothesis. DR was estimated following Jetz et al. (2012). This method treated the inverse of species' mean equal splits as the species-level lineage diversification rate for each individual species. Here, we used the dated phylogeny containing 353 185 seed plant species generated by Smith and Brown (2018) (i.e. ALLOTB), which has gained substantial traction in macroecological and biogeographical studies (Sandel et al. 2020, Weigelt et al. 2020, Luo et al. 2023, 2024). This phylogeny was constructed by placing the molecular phylogenies (containing 79 855 species in total) for major clades (i.e. orders) in the backbone of the Open Tree of Life (Smith and Brown 2018), and inserting species without molecular data as polytomies in their families. To generate a fully resolved phylogeny, we conducted a polytomy resolving analysis using a birth–death (Yule) bifurcation process (Lu et al. 2018). In particular, for each genus that included species represented as polytomies, we employed the Yule bifurcation process to randomly resolve these polytomies. This process resulted in random relationships among the polytomy species within genera. To evaluate whether the polytomy resolving process may influence our results, we generated 100 resolved phylogenies by repeating this process 100 times and repeated our analysis using all these 100 resolved phylogenies.

### Statistical analyses

Species richness was quantified as the count of species within each grid cell. We mapped species richness patterns in the whole study area using ArcGIS 10. To better demonstrate species richness variations at high latitudes, we showed the zoomed-in maps of Mongolia and Asian Russia, separately (Supporting information). To explore the differences in species richness patterns between growth forms, we divided all species into woody and herbaceous growth forms. Woody species were defined following Zanne et al. (2014) (i.e. species having a prominent aboveground stem that is persistent in changing environmental conditions) and included trees, shrubs, and lianas (Luo et al. 2023). Among all species, 15 207 were recorded as woody. We also divided all species into wide-ranged and narrow-ranged species. We ranked species in descending order of range size, and then categorized the upper 25% species (area of occupancy) as wide-ranged species and the lower 25% species as narrow-ranged species (Supporting information) following Araújo et al. (2008). Considering that the distribution ranges of some species extend beyond the study area, narrow-ranged species were also defined as the east Eurasian species whose global distribution ranges are among the top 50% of the species with the smallest distribution ranges across the world. Global species distribution data were obtained from Luo et al. (2023). We found that the richness patterns of wide-ranged woody and herbaceous species were similar to those of all woody and herbaceous species (Supporting information; Lennon et al. 2004). Moreover, the dominant hypotheses explaining species richness patterns were also the same for wide-ranged and all woody and herbaceous species. Thus, we show the results for wide-ranged herbaceous and woody species only in the Supporting information. We also calculated weighted species endemism patterns, which are similar to those of narrow-range species (Supporting information).

To compare the explanatory power of different hypotheses on species richness patterns, we first fitted multiple generalized linear models (GLMs) with quasi-Poisson residuals between species richness and different environmental variables. To assess the relationship of single environment variables to species richness, we set the other variables to fixed values (i.e. their median values) (Breheny and Burchett 2017). To account for the effect of spatial autocorrelations on model significance, we used a modified t test to test the relationships between the observed and GLM-predicted richness patterns. The GLM was considered significant when the p value of the modified t-test was < 0.05. (Clifford et al. 1989). Second, we conducted random forest models to estimate the relative importance of each hypothesis in explaining species richness patterns. Specifically, we log-transformed species richness and constructed a baseline random forest model with MTCQ, TSN, TOPO_RANGE, MAT anomaly, and MAP anomaly as predictors. Specifically, we randomly selected 70% of all data as the training data to build the random forest model, and used the other 30% as the testing data to evaluate the MSE of the model. For the randomized models, we randomized the values of the variables representing each hypothesis in the training data, and the random process of each variable was independent. For example, to quantify the relative importance of climate change since the LGM, we randomized MAT anomaly and MAP anomaly. Using the randomized data, we rebuilt the random forest model, which was then used on the testing data to estimate the MSE. This process was iterated 1000 times to provide a robust assessment of the relative importance of each hypothesis. For comparison, hierarchical partitioning analysis was also applied to estimate the independent and joint importance of each hypothesis, and the results were consistent with those based on random forest (Supporting information).

Due to the significant negative correlation between species richness and DR, which is in contrast to the prediction of the diversification rate hypothesis, we did not include DR in the random forest and hierarchical partitioning analysis. All analyses were conducted in R ver. 4.0.3 with ‘randomForest', ‘ggplot2', ‘picante', and ‘ape' packages (http://www.Rproject.org).

## Results

### Species richness patterns in east Eurasia

The species richness for all species, the two growth forms (i.e. woody and herbaceous species), and the two range-size groups (i.e. wide-ranged and narrow-ranged) decreased with increasing latitude (Fig. 1–2). Species richness hotspots differed between growth forms (Fig. 2a, b) and between wide-ranged and narrow-ranged species (Fig. 2c, d, Supporting information). Specifically, woody species richness peaked in southern China (e.g. Yunnan–Guizhou Plateau) (Fig. 2a), while herbaceous species richness peaked in the Hengduan Mountains and the mountains of central Asia (Fig. 2b). The richness of narrow-ranged woody and herbaceous species peaked in southwestern China (Fig. 2c, d).

### Determinants of species richness patterns

Multiple GLMs showed that the richness patterns of all and narrow-ranged species were positively correlated with MTCQ, MAT anomaly, and TOPO_RANGE, but negatively correlated with TSN (Fig. 3). In other words, regions with high MTCQ and TOPO_RANGE, low MAT anomaly, and TSN tend to have high species richness. For woody species, the species richness was significantly positively correlated with MTCQ and MAT anomaly, and negatively correlated with TSN. For herbaceous species, the species richness was significantly positively correlated with MAT anomaly and TOPO_RANGE. These results are consistent with predictions of the freezing tolerance hypothesis, the hypothesis of climate change since the LGM, and Janzen's hypothesis. However, the species richness of all species groups was negatively correlated with DR, suggesting that regions with high diversification rates do not have high species richness. This result contradicts the diversification rate hypothesis, which predicts that high species richness is due to high diversification rate.

Both random forest and hierarchical partitioning analyses showed consistent results with those of univariate GLMs (Fig. 4, Supporting information). The relative importance of different mechanisms (the freezing tolerance hypothesis, the hypothesis of climate change since the LGM and Janzen's hypothesis) on species richness patterns differ between growth forms and range size groups. For all species and herbaceous species, climate change since the LGM had the highest importance on species richness patterns (Fig. 4, Supporting information). In contrast, the freezing tolerance hypothesis and Janzen's hypothesis had the highest importance to woody and narrow-ranged species richness, respectively (Fig. 4, Supporting information).

## Discussion

The species richness gradients observed across east Eurasia, resembling well-documented latitudinal trends, exhibited nuanced variations among different species groups (i.e. all species, woody species, herbaceous species, narrow-ranged species, and wide-ranged species). These differences in richness patterns across species groups are attributed primarily to the diverse evolutionary trajectories, life history strategies, and environmental sensitivities of each group (Hawkins et al. 2007, Svenning and Skov 2007, Buckley et al. 2010, Normand et al. 2011, Zanne et al. 2018). Our analysis underscores the importance of considering these factors in elucidating the spatial distribution of species richness. Moreover, our results highlight the unique relationships between species richness and environmental factors for each species group, indicating that distinct mechanisms likely govern the observed species richness patterns across various taxa.

### Climate change since the LGM dominated plant richness patterns in east Eurasia

Early studies generally suggested that east Eurasia had been less affected by climate change since the LGM (Qian 2002, Qian and Ricklefs 2004) in comparison with the effects of contemporary climate and long-term diversification rate. However, our results did not support this traditional view, but rather indicated that climate change since the LGM dominated species richness patterns of angiosperms in east Eurasia (Fig. 3). Specifically, the richness of all angiosperm species was significantly negatively correlated with the rates of climate change since the LGM. In other words, species richness was lower at sites with stronger temperature and precipitation changes since the LGM, but was higher in regions with a more stable climate. A possible reason for the inconsistency between our study and previous studies may be that our study included more herbaceous species in east Eurasia and a wider study area at high latitudes, which led to a much broader environmental gradient than previous studies. Including high latitudes that had experienced more dramatic historical climate changes may increase the importance of climate change since the LGM on species richness patterns (Svenning and Skov 2005, Feng et al. 2019). The strong impact of climate change since the LGM on species richness patterns has also been demonstrated for other taxa and in other continents. For example, studies on species richness of trees (Svenning and Skov 2007), reptiles, and amphibians in Europe (Araújo et al. 2008), consistently highlighted climate change during the LGM as a significant driver shaping species richness patterns. However, whether the relative importance of historical climate change and contemporary environment remains consistent across different scales requires further evaluation.

Previous studies suggested that climate change since the LGM may influence contemporary species richness patterns via its effects on species dispersal and/or species ranges. The strong temperature and precipitation changes since the LGM may have significantly affected the northern regions, which might have caused a large number of species to migrate to the south and/or mountainous regions. After the LGM, many species have been found to have difficulties in recolonizing all areas with suitable climates in the north (Sandel et al. 2011, Wang et al. 2012, Liu et al. 2017). The Central Asian mountains and southern China had experienced relatively weak climate change since the LGM and hence had become refuges for species (López-Pujol et al. 2011a, b 2011b, Sandel et al. 2011, Tang et al. 2018). Their topographic diversity may have provided sufficient ecological stability to buffer the effects of extreme climatic fluctuations (Sandel et al. 2011).

### The influence of the diversification rate hypothesis on species richness patterns

Notably, our results revealed that species richness patterns were negatively correlated with the geographic patterns of average diversification rate, which does not support the diversification rate hypothesis (Mittelbach et al. 2007, Svenning et al. 2008, Tietje et al. 2022). In recent studies, the importance of net diversification rate to large-scale species richness patterns remained uncertain. Previous studies of the species richness of some flower plant groups, such as palms (Arecaceae; Svenning et al. 2008), Zygophyllaceae (Wang et al. 2018), and Rhododendrons (Shrestha et al. 2018), indicated that net diversification rate was strongly positively correlated with species richness, and hence supported the diversification rate hypothesis. In contrast, Jansson and Davies (2008) and Igea and Tanentzap (2020) found that the species richness decline with latitude cannot be explained by latitudinal changes in diversification rate, which is consistent with our findings. One explanation for the debate on the diversification rate hypothesis is that temperate lineages have fewer species and their unoccupied ecological niche spaces may be large, thus increasing the chances of speciation (Simpson 1953, Schluter 2016). Although the average diversification rate was low in the tropics, the variation in diversification rate across species may be relatively large in the tropics due to larger species pools in these regions. How diversification rate variation across species may influence species richness patterns deserves further research. In addition to diversification rate, species richness may also be influenced by the accumulation time of speciation (Mittelbach et al. 2007, Etienne et al. 2019). Compared with the accumulation time of temperate taxa in temperate regions, taxa with tropical origins have occupied tropical regions for a much longer time and had a longer time to diversify (Stephens and Wiens 2003, Li and Wiens 2019).

### Difference in determinants of woody and herbaceous species richness patterns

Our results indicated that the dominant hypothesis for geographic patterns of species richness differed between woody and herbaceous growth forms. Specifically, the species richness pattern of woody plants is dominated by winter temperature. This finding provides more evidence supporting the freezing tolerance hypothesis (Francis and Currie 1998, Wang et al. 2011, Zhao et al. 2020). In China, mean temperature of the coldest quarter was the dominant factor driving the pattern of woody plant richness (Wang et al. 2011, Su et al. 2020). One of the possible explanations is that China's woody flora contains more tropical and subtropical floristic components (Fang and Yoda 1989), and most of these tropical species are very sensitive to low temperatures in winter, and thus cannot widely spread to the north (Wang et al. 2011). This finding on Chinese woody plants, together with our results at a larger spatial scale, suggests that freezing tolerance dominates the species richness patterns of woody plants across east Eurasia.

Our results indicated much weaker effects of freezing tolerance on herbaceous species richness than on woody species richness. This might be related to differences in functional traits and evolutionary niche conservatism between the two growth forms (Hawkins et al. 2011, Zanne et al. 2014, 2018, Engemann et al. 2016). Above-ground parts of herbaceous species can die in winter, with shoots located near or even below ground to avoid cold stress, while woody plants cannot (Zanne et al. 2014, 2018, Engemann et al. 2016). Moreover, herbaceous species tend to have shorter generation time, higher evolutionary rate, and weaker phylogenetic niche conservatism (Smith and Donoghue 2008, Qi et al. 2014), and hence tend to be less sensitive to freezing temperature than woody species (Smith and Beaulieu 2009).

Climate change since the LGM had a weaker impact on woody species richness than on herbaceous species richness. Herbaceous species could better adapt to environmental changes since the LGM due to lower phylogenetic niche conservatism and hence may track climate change more quickly than woody plants (Albuquerque et al. 2011, Liu et al. 2019). In addition, the proportion of herbaceous richness in floras is also higher in temperate regions experiencing greater climate change during the LGM compared to the southern mountainous regions (Luo et al. 2023).

### Determinants of narrow-ranged species richness pattern

The dominant factors for the richness patterns of narrow-ranged woody and herbaceous species differed from those for the richness patterns of all woody and herbaceous species. Compared with other hypotheses, Janzen's hypothesis (suggesting that low temperature seasonality in the tropics tends to lead to narrow thermal tolerances of species, which in turn leads to higher speciation and species diversity in tropical mountains) had the highest explanatory power for the richness patterns of narrow-ranged woody and herbaceous species (Fig. 4). Similarly, the high explanatory power of Janzen's hypothesis on species richness patterns has also been found in several taxa such as Gesneriaceae and gymnosperms (Liu et al. 2017, Lyu et al. 2018). These studies consistently suggested that areas with low temperature seasonality and high topographic heterogeneity have higher narrow-ranged species diversity. Climate stability preserves more genotypes adapted to specific environments, and these genotypes often have low dispersal ability, and hence are more likely to have narrow distributions (Dynesius and Jansson 2000, Jansson 2003, Liu et al. 2017). In addition, regions with high topographic heterogeneity can provide a richer variety of habitat types, thus providing more ecological niches for more organisms to survive (Currie 1991, Fjeldså et al. 2012). Since the mid-Cenozoic, due to diverse habitats and climatic stability, the mountains of southwestern China have been a refuge for narrow-ranged species, preserving many narrow-ranged plant and vertebrate species (López-Pujol et al. 2011a, Sandel et al. 2011, Xing and Ree 2017). However, the mountainous areas in southwestern China are undergoing rapid climate warming, surpassing the rate observed in lowland regions (Pepin et al. 2015). Furthermore, future air temperatures in these mountains are anticipated to be even higher than current levels (Nogués-Bravo et al. 2007). Strong climatic changes may have aggravated the extinction risk of the narrow-ranged plants in these mountains (Moritz et al. 2008, Menéndez et al. 2014, Rowe et al. 2015).

## Conclusion

Based on newly compiled distribution data for 43 023 angiosperm species, we demonstrated species richness patterns in east Eurasia, and found that species richness was highest in southwestern China and the Central Asian mountains. Species richness patterns differed between growth forms (woody and herbaceous) and species with different range sizes. Climate change since the LGM dominated the richness patterns of all and herbaceous angiosperms, while winter coldness dominated richness patterns for woody species. Janzen's hypothesis provided the best explanation for the richness patterns of narrow-ranged species (both woody and herbaceous). Our study did not support the diversification rate hypothesis. We demonstrated different hypotheses underlying plant species richness patterns of different species groups, enhancing our knowledge of biodiversity dynamics. Our findings reveal the role of historical climate change in driving plant diversity in east Eurasia. They provide insights into species richness patterns and their environmental determinants (especially historical and contemporary climate) in east Eurasia and provide support for future conservation and ecosystem management efforts in the context of climate change.
