# Increased crossing of thermal stress thresholds of vegetation under global warming

**Abstract.** Temperature extremes exert a significant influence on terrestrial ecosystems, but the precise levels at which these extremes trigger adverse shifts in vegetation productivity have remained elusive. In this study, we have derived two critical thresholds, using standard deviations (SDs) of growing‐season temperature and satellite‐based vegetation productivity as key indicators. Our findings reveal that, on average, vegetation productivity experiences rapid suppression when confronted with temperature anomalies exceeding 1.45 SD above the mean temperature during 2001–2018. Furthermore, at temperatures exceeding 2.98 SD above the mean, we observe the maximum level of suppression, particularly in response to the most extreme high‐temperature events. When Earth System Models are driven by a future medium emission scenario, they project that mean temperatures will routinely surpass both of these critical thresholds by approximately the years 2050 and 2070, respectively. However, it is important to note that the timing of these threshold crossings exhibits spatial variation and will appear much earlier in tropical regions. Our finding highlights that restricting global warming to just 1.5°C can increase safe areas for vegetation growth by 13% compared to allowing warming to reach 2°C above preindustrial levels. This mitigation strategy helps avoid exposure to detrimental extreme temperatures that breach these thresholds. Our study underscores the pivotal role of climate mitigation policies in fostering the sustainable development of terrestrial ecosystems in a warming world.

## **Abstract**

Temperature extremes exert a significant influence on terrestrial ecosystems, but the precise levels at which these extremes trigger adverse shifts in vegetation productivity have remained elusive. In this study, we have derived two critical thresholds, using standard deviations (SDs) of growing- season temperature and satellite- based vegetation productivity as key indicators. Our findings reveal that, on average, vegetation productivity experiences rapid suppression when confronted with temperature anomalies exceeding 1.45 SD above the mean temperature during 2001–2018. Furthermore, at temperatures exceeding 2.98 SD above the mean, we observe the maximum level of suppression, particularly in response to the most extreme hightemperature events. When Earth System Models are driven by a future medium emission scenario, they project that mean temperatures will routinely surpass both of these critical thresholds by approximately the years 2050 and 2070, respectively. However, it is important to note that the timing of these threshold crossings exhibits spatial variation and will appear much earlier in tropical regions. Our finding highlights that restricting global warming to just 1.5°C can increase safe areas for vegetation growth by 13% compared to allowing warming to reach 2°C above preindustrial levels. This mitigation strategy helps avoid exposure to detrimental extreme temperatures that breach these thresholds. Our study underscores the pivotal role of climate mitigation policies in fostering the sustainable development of terrestrial ecosystems in a warming world.

## **1** | **INTRODUCTION**

Temperature extremes are of great concern due to their widespread and profound impacts on human health, the socioeconomic system, and ecosystem functioning (Bastos et al., 2020; von Buttlar et al., 2018). As the burning of fossil fuel continues, hot extreme events are projected to increase in frequency and intensity

(IPCC, 2021), with unprecedented extreme events already becoming more commonplace (Bartusek et al., 2022; Fischer et al., 2021). Although declining cold extremes under increasing levels of global warming will reduce their damage to future ecosystems (Fischer et al., 2013), more frequent expected hot extremes pose a risk of decreasing vegetation productivity and thus suppressing terrestrial carbon uptake (King, 2017; Wehrli et al., 2020; Zscheischler

et al., 2020). Ultimately much more intense temperature anomalies are expected to trigger the failure of land ecosystems (Reyer et al., 2015), especially in tropical latitudes, which show earlier exposure to frequent temperature extremes (Harrington et al., 2016). Such earlier exposure is likely due to natural temperature variability being much smaller in the tropics, lowering historical exposure of vegetation to extremes. Yet geographically variable but globally applicable parameters of temperature extremes that define ecosystem suppression have not yet been derived. This lack of understanding hampers determining the emergence time when contemporary extreme temperatures, verified as detrimental to terrestrial ecosystems, will become routine. Filling this knowledge gap to inform policymakers of the global warming levels above which local ecosystem productivity will have a high risk of suffering severe suppression is therefore important.

The compounding effects of water limitations can alter the response of vegetation productivity by modulating the stressors of temperature extremes. Through land- atmospheric feedback, hightemperature extremes combined with major soil moisture (SM) deficits exacerbate the dry and hot conditions, substantially exceeding the negative impacts of separate individual extreme temperature or moisture levels on vegetation productivity (Alizadeh et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2019; Zscheischler et al., 2020). This combined dry and heat stress is more likely to surpass the tolerance range of vegetation (Wolf & Paul- Limoges, 2023), leading to enhanced suppression of vegetation productivity. Understanding simultaneous high temperature and arid conditions is important. Under global warming, it may be expected that droughts become more intense (Trenberth et al., 2014), and there is some evidence that land- atmosphere feedback in a changing climate may cause an enhancement of the highest extreme temperature events (Berg et al., 2014). Additionally, compounded wet extremes are also suggested to amplify the damage of cold extremes to vegetation growth in boreal regions relative to individual low- temperature events over the recent decades (Yang et al., 2023). Considering that the temperature extremes often occur compounded by the water extremes (Hammond et al., 2022; Van der Woude et al., 2023; Wigneron et al., 2020), it is of particular importance to improve our understanding of how compounding effects of water extremes modify the vegetation response to temperature extremes.

This study first parameterized two key thresholds of temperature extremes for vegetation productivity by the parallel analysis of growing- season temperature and the near- infrared reflectance of terrestrial vegetation data (NIRv; Badgley et al., 2017). We detected and constrained thresholds that define the critical points that trigger both the start of major vegetation suppression and when ecosystems are fully suppressed. These thresholds were then available to project different exposure risks for vegetation to temperature extremes for a range of global warming levels in the future (see Section 2). Thus, in combination with projections from Earth System Models (ESMs, Meinshausen et al., 2020; O'Neill et al., 2016), we estimated locations where future mean temperatures would become routinely higher (i.e., “the norm”) than such thresholds (see Section 2). This information is further used to assess the effect of the Paris climate

targets for 1.5 and 2°C global mean warming in avoiding the potential risk to land ecosystems of local high growth temperatures. We also assessed the compound impacts of SM (Muñoz- Sabater et al., 2021) deficit during temperature extremes.

## 2.1.1 | ERA5- Land data

The temperature of the air at 2 m above the surface and data for total soil moisture (SM) content used in this study for 2001–2018 were derived from an enhanced global data set for the terrestrial component of the fifth generation of European ReAnalysis (ERA5- Land). ERA5- Land is a state- of- the- art global reanalysis dataset combining model data with observations, produced by the European Centre for Medium- Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) (Muñoz- Sabater et al., 2021). The original resolution of the data set was 0.1 × 0.1°, and the data were at monthly intervals. The total SM content was calculated using the first (SM1, 0–7 cm), second (SM2, 7–28 cm), third (SM3, 28–100 cm), and fourth (SM4, 100–289 cm) volumetric soil layers (unit: m[−3] /m[3]). The equation was SM = SM1 × 70 + SM2 × (280 – 7 0) + SM3 × (1000 – 280) + SM4 × (2890 – 1000) by converting the unit to kg/m[2]. ERA5- Land data are available at https:// cds. clima te. coper nicus. eu/ cdsapp#!/ datas et/ reana lysis - era5- land- month ly- means? tab= overview.

Given the higher uncertainties associated with SM compared to temperature in the ERA5- Land dataset, we assessed the performance of historical SM estimates from that source. We found that there is high coherence between growing- season SM from ERA5Land and satellite- based SM from the Global Land Evaporation Amsterdam Model (GLEAM, Martens et al., 2017) dataset during 2001–2018 (_R_[2] values >.5 in approximately 61% of pixels, _p_ <.05 in Figure S1). We consider this dataset sufficient in estimating SM anomalies to understand compounded thermal and water events (see details in Section 2.2).

## 2.1.2 | MODIS NIRv

We used the near- infrared reflectance of terrestrial vegetation (NIRv) as a proxy for vegetation productivity (Badgley et al., 2017). The NIRv data (16- day and 0.05 × 0.05°) for 2001–2018 were derived from MODIS 16- day NDVI and NIR reflectance (NDVI × NIR) of the MCD43C4 Vegetation Index Product (available at https:// lpdaac. usgs. gov/ produ cts/ mcd43 c4v006/). NIRv, calculated in terms of a renormalized NDVI multiplied by reflected NIR, is closely linked to gross primary productivity (GPP) due to the fact that this index can minimize the influence of soil background in the total NIR reflectance of the pixel and tracking the impacts of diurnal and seasonal variation in light use efficiency on GPP (Baldocchi et al., 2020;

Hence, we applied NIRv as a valid proxy of vegetation productivity, noting the compelling evidence that NIRv has strong correlations with eddy covariance GPP at monthly timescale (see details in figure 3 in Badgley et al., 2017), and furthermore that NIRv can accurately predict photosynthesis at in situ flux sites at finer time resolutions and across space (Badgley et al., 2019). Throughout our analysis, we computed the growing- season mean values of temperature, SM, and NIRv. That is, for each year over the entire study period, we generated growing- season values by averaging monthly data but only for months within the vegetation's growing period (Figures 1 and 2). The local growing season for each grid cell was defined following the global map of growing seasons created by Zhu et al. (2016). Subsequently, we expressed the severity of temperature extremes as a dimensionless “number of standard deviations [SDs].” This value is calculated as the deviation (in units of SD) from the averaged growing- season values of climate or vegetation variables during 2001–2018.

## 2.2.1 | Threshold detection

We detected the thresholds for extreme high and low temperatures that determined the main shifts in simultaneous vegetation response in the years of extreme temperatures during 2001–2018. To overcome the short- term record of satellite vegetation observations and find a compromise between fully local and global thresholding, we first used the same method as described by Li et al. (2023) to extract the features for global land using a principal component analysis (PCA). The five variables associated with the background climates and vegetation dynamics used for the PCA were mean annual temperature and precipitation, the interannual variability of vaporpressure deficit calculated from the ERA5- Land data, the fraction of tree cover (Song et al., 2018) and the interannual variability of NIRv (Badgley et al., 2017). We clustered grid cells characterized by similarities in climate and vegetation in the three- dimensional space of the principal components (PCs) of the PCA (87% of accumulative variance explained) based on the first three PC scores. We then statistically analyzed a special grid cell using the climatic and vegetation data from the cell and its neighboring cells in the PC space.

We next identified coincidences between the occurrence of temperature extremes and the response of vegetation to the changing severities of temperature anomalies. Specifically, we quantified the probability of NIRv anomalies <−1.0 SD per year of extreme temperatures when the absolute value of the severity of a temperature anomaly was equal to or greater than the temperature threshold. _χ_ thres was calculated as:

We identified two points, _χ_ 1 and _χ_ 2, that characterized the main shift in the response of vegetation (Figure 2a). Theoretically, the first threshold, _χ_ 1, is the inflection point at which further increased temperature changes initiate stress that causes a substantial loss of vegetation functioning, and the second threshold, _χ_ 2, is when an ecosystem completely loses the ability to cope with temperature extremes at this level or above. Mathematically, _χ_ 1 is an inflection point of the response curve, which identifies a point of an abrupt change in the response of suppressed vegetation productivity to increasing temperature stress (i.e., the critical point separating _Phase A_ and _Phase B_). _χ_ 1 is obtained by piecewise linear regression following the method applied by Li et al. (2023). _χ_ 2 is the critical point (after passing _χ_ 1) when the rate of increase in _χ_ thres decreases to zero or very close to zero as the temperature threshold _χ_ thres increases (i.e., the critical point separating _Phase B_ and _Phase C_). Threshold _χ_ 2 by definition, however, will appear at high levels of temperature stress so may not be detected within the range of observations (<3.0 SD). We therefore quantified _χ_ 2 based on the second derivatives of the fitted polynomial curve. Note that the precise _χ_ 2 values we derived may depend on the specific data- led curve- fitting algorithm we adopted and may thereby be one source of uncertainty in this threshold estimation.

Given that this two- threshold framework captures the link between a driver (here extreme temperature events) and a response (i.e., vegetation suppression), _χ_ 1 and _χ_ 2 capture all connections between the two, including the vegetation- climate feedbacks. Hence, although temperature extremes drive suppression of vegetation, there may be an alternative feedback where moderately high temperatures trigger fires, and the resultant new land cover may act to further alter near- surface temperatures and SM. All location- specific possibilities should be contained, implicitly, in parameters _χ_ 1 and _χ_ 2.

## 2.2.2 | The routine occurrence of current extremes

We defined the future norms of the temperature extremes as the future temperature consistently higher than the threshold _χ_ 1- and _χ_ 2- inferred high temperatures for 2001–2018. ## **3.1** | **Fewer extremes of vegetation than temperatures**

The growing- season temperature extremes widely exceeded +1 SD (Figure 1a) or were below −1 SD (Figure 1b) during the years 2001–2018 (Figure 1a,b). For a normal distribution, in general, it is expected that these thresholds are crossed at an occurrence of ~16%, but our spatial patterns exhibit substantial spatial variability. The conditional probabilities of an extreme NIRv value (i.e., ≤−1.0 SD) per temperature event revealed that vegetation extremes did not necessarily occur when temperature extremes occurred. ## **3.3** | **Future norm of temperature extremes**

Vegetation may be extensively damaged if the adaptive evolution of vegetation fails to keep pace with the rapid rates of warming caused by the human burning of fossil fuel. Vegetation suppression might become much more prevalent if contemporary rare high- temperature events become normal under future climate change. Hence, we used ESMs to determine the year when the decadal- mean temperature of a particular year and the preceding 9 years first becomes higher than _χ_ 1high- or _χ_ 2high- inferred temperatures in Figure 2. We named these years that become the norm to cross thresholds as the time of emergence of _χ_ 1high or _χ_ 2high temperature thresholds (abbreviated to the Emergent time, EMt, of _χ_ 1high or _χ_ 2high) (Figure 3; Figure S9). The ESMs we used to derived EMt values are from CMIP6 ensemble and in configurations corresponding to two future scenarios of GHGs concentrations, SSP24.5 and SSP5- 8.5 (Section 2).

Where thresholds have been identified, we find that the EMt of _χ_ 1high will appear, on average, in the year 2046 under SSP2- 4.5 and 5 years earlier under SSP5- 8.5 (Figure 3a,b). By 2050, 66% (SSP2- 4.5) and 76% (SSP5- 8.5) of areas will reach or pass the EMt of _χ_ 1high. The average EMt of _χ_ 2high is predicted to appear after 2069 (SSP2- 4.5) and 2060 (SSP5- 8.5) (Figure 3c,d). Higher emissions (i.e., SSP5- 8.5), as expected, will accelerate extremes as the future norms, and so >90% of areas where _χ_ 2high is detected will have reached the EMt of that threshold by 2080. Some areas, especially tropical areas, are notably likely to attain the EMt of _χ_ 1high, and even the EMt of _χ_ 2high, in the next one or two decades, providing a major near- term challenge to the resilience of vegetation (Figure 3a–d). Much of the policy for mitigating climate change is based on determining pathways that decrease fossil- fuel emissions sufficiently to stabilize mean global warming at (or below) either 1.5 or 2°C above pre- industrial levels (Aadhar & Mishra, 2020; King et al., 2017). ## **4** | **DISCUSSION**

In this study, we detected two thresholds of temperature extremes merging with satellite- derived vegetation observations, based on theoretical understanding and following a framework for threshold detection (Figure 2a). This approach, which was well applied in previous studies on drought thresholds (Li et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024), show good performance in estimating extreme temperature thresholds that impact vegetation productivity (Figure 2; Figure S4). Although we eliminated the impacts of uncertainty as much as possible, some uncertainties inevitably exist in processing of observations, estimating threshold values, and projecting future risk based on climate models.

This practice can lead to overestimating threshold values for simultaneous vegetation response if vegetation shows a longer response lag with strong tolerance to thermal stress (Teskey et al., 2015). On the other hand, the legacy effects may lead to the severe suppression of vegetation greenness in a temperature event persisting into the next year even if for that following year, no temperature events occur or only less severe temperature events occur.

Second, it is noticeable in the examples of Figure S4, that we are unable to constrain the upper parameter _χ_ 2 for tree vegetation types. We speculate that the dynamics of understory microclimate in the forest ecosystem may, to some extent, mitigate the negative impacts of temperature extremes on vegetation productivity (Zellweger et al., 2020). A site- level study has indicated that tree canopies act as buffers, protecting forest floors from both high and low macroclimatic temperatures, leading to very local tree- level temperatures becoming less extreme (De Frenne et al., 2019).

Additionally, this uncertainty could result from systematic errors, including the potential mismatch of satellite NIRv data and the hydrometeorological data from different sources. Of note is that the SM from ERA5- Land exhibits higher differences with satellitederived values for many arid regions (Figure S1), potentially influencing the threshold values of temperature extremes compounded by droughts or wet extremes. Another source of uncertainty comes from the performance of NIRv data in representing vegetation productivity. We applied NIRv as a surrogate of vegetation productivity based on the strong correlation between NIRv and GPP values relying on the research of Badgley et al. (2017, 2019). Hence, we expect that the thresholds at which temperatures start to suppress vegetation, as observed in NIRv and expressed in units of SD away from the temperature mean, will correspond to identical thresholds in GPP. However, there may be some residual errors, if environmental stress effects observable by structural changes and as detected by remotely sensed NIRv occur at slightly different stress levels to those impacting (Yin et al., 2020). A further caveat is that NIRv data may also saturate at high values. Using NIRv as an approximation of the proportion of reflected photons that directly interact with vegetation, which is estimated by both NDVI and NIR components, the saturation problem remains, particularly for evergreen forests (Camps- Valls et al., 2021; Mallick et al., 2024). To avoid these caveats, we suggest that a future piece of research is to study directly existing and new FLUXNET measurements in the context of our thresholds. That is, for each FLUXNET location with simultaneous CO2 exchange and meteorological measurements, verify thresholds _χ_ 1 and _χ_ 2 exist when presenting GPP as a function of temperature.

While we consider our findings to be a good indicator of expected future changes to vegetation, there may be some offsetting factors that bring partial resilience during further high- temperature extremes. Such factors that support productivity in a warmer climate (and related extremes) include vegetation adaptation and acclimation to climate change, and CO2 fertilization effects. Furthermore, rising atmospheric CO2 concentration may partly ameliorate the impacts of future heat stress by increasing water- use efficiency (Good et al., 2013; Smith et al., 2020) and thus saving water loss through photosynthesis.

Unfortunately, the magnitude or timing of effects that may protect vegetation is uncertain. The rate of vegetation acclimation and adaptation will likely lag behind the pace of rapid climate change (Huang et al., 2017), and the magnitude and persistence of the CO2 fertilization effect has remained controversial so far (Peñuelas et al., 2017). In addition, higher water- use efficiency could also reduce transpiration rates and increase temperature damage to the leaf (Doughty et al., 2023), offsetting the gains of decreased stomatal conductance. Moreover, we may underestimate the exposure risk of vegetation productivity in places where high- temperature extremes are compounded by an acceleration of drought affecting photosynthesis. Understanding these influencing factors is a high research priority, noting recent progress, for instance, to include acclimation effects into a land surface model designed for operation within an ESM (Mercado et al., 2018). A particular advantage of our characterization of the effects of hightemperature climate extremes on vegetation productivity by two simple and intuitive threshold parameters is that as new process understanding is added to land surface models, their impact can be determined from how they cause our estimates of such parameters to change.

As a final note, our focus in this study is solely on the adverse effects of temperature extremes on vegetation productivity within the framework for threshold detection. However, temperature events can also lead to positive extremes in vegetation productivity. For instance, high- temperature events might enhance vegetation

productivity rather than cause damage, particularly in regions limited by temperature (Piao et al., 2024). The enhanced vegetation growth, if frequently occurring in certain areas, can compensate for the carbon loss caused by high- temperature extremes or droughts under future climates (Jiang et al., 2019). Therefore, in future analyses, we anticipate employing this framework in reverse to examine positive vegetation extremes. Such an evaluation could offer insights into enhancing terrestrial carbon storage in regions where these positive vegetation responses might become routine under future climate conditions.

## **5** | **CONCLUSION**

We have established two thresholds for temperature extremes that trigger shifts in land ecosystem productivity and maximize temperature- related damage. Our analysis utilizes simultaneous historical temperature records and satellite- based vegetation measurements, revealing spatial variations in these thresholds. Notably, we have identified lower temperature thresholds in eastern Australia, northeastern China, and specific parts of Europe and North America. Furthermore, these thresholds are further reduced in the presence of conditional SM deficit, indicating additional negative impacts from concurrent droughts on vegetation.

When we integrate our thresholds with ESM projections, we anticipate significant exposure of vegetation productivity to hightemperature extremes that are currently considered extreme but are projected to become the norm. Even if global warming is constrained to a 2°C increase above preindustrial levels, more than a tenth of the Earth's land cover will regularly experience growing- season temperatures exceeding these two key thresholds. This scenario underscores the challenge that future warming poses to the current global sink of CO2 in the path toward eventual tipping points in terrestrial ecosystems. The suppression of vegetation productivity under a climate with more extreme high temperatures has implications for whether the contemporary terrestrial carbon sink, offsetting CO2 emissions, will be sustained in the future (Peñuelas, 2023; Peñuelas et al., 2017). Ultimately, our data- derived thresholds represent a substantial advancement in accurately assessing changes in vegetation productivity resulting from temperature extremes. These thresholds provide valuable benchmarks for calibrating and testing ecological sub- models within ESMs, thereby enhancing the reliability of projections regarding the future evolution of the global carbon cycle.
