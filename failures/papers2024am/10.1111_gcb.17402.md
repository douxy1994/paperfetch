# On the adjusted <scp>N<sub>2</sub> O</scp> emission factor in dry subhumid, semiarid, and arid regions

## On the adjusted N<sub>2</sub>O emission factor in dry subhumid, semiarid, and arid regions

In our recent paper (Shang et al., 2024), we estimated the contribution of cropland fallow-period nitrous oxide (N2O) emissions to annual totals (Rfallow) and adjusted the global-scale N2O emission factor (EF) based on the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Tier 1 method. Cardinael et al. (2024) list three concerns regarding the adjusted N2O EF: (i) the extrapolation of IPCC Tier 1 EF based on Rfallow, (ii) the adjusted EF for countries with dry fallow periods, (iii) the representativeness of observations. We address these concerns below.

For the extrapolation of IPCC EF defaults, we directly converted fertilizer-induced emission using *R*<sub>fallow</sub> for nitrogen (N) treatment (Equation SE1), because the emissions for N treatment and control, which should be converted separately using *R*<sub>fallow</sub> for N treatment and control (Supporting Text 1), were unavailable from IPCC Tier 1. We agree that *R*<sub>fallow</sub> for N treatment may differ from that for control (without N), which could introduce some bias. However, the simplified and original EFs were almost identical (Slope = 0.99 and *R*<sup>2</sup> =.89; Figure S1) for collected observations (Table S1), suggesting our approach is robust.

We agree that fallow-period precipitation (FP) is an important factor influencing *R*<sub>fallow</sub>. However, due to the correlations with other variables, it does not show a significant partial correlation with *R*<sub>fallow</sub>(Table S7 in Shang et al., 2024). Additionally, models with FP have larger AICs than our model (section 2.2 in Shang et al., 2024; Table S2), so including FP cannot improve model performance and may lead to overfitting. These results likely reflect that the influence of FP is largely represented by cropping system type in our model. Our adjusted EF was based on IPCC Tier 1 EF defaults, recommended for countries without country-specific EFs (IPCC, 2006, 2019). The default EF for upland crops is 1%, already much greater than those from the field measurements (<0.3% and 0.41%) mentioned in Cardinael et al. (2024).

We discussed the representativeness of observations in sections 2.1 and 3.5 of Shang et al. (2024). Nonetheless, we appreciate the additional studies from the under-represented regions proffered by Cardinael et al. (2024). These were missing in our search as the related keywords, title, and abstract do not include our search terms. After including the additional data (Table S3) and excluding the data from Dick et al. (2008), the model performance (Figures 1a and S2), the global magnitude and variation of *R*<sub>fallow</sub> and its dominant drivers (Table S4, Figures 1 and S3, S4) remain broadly consistent. The updated adjusted EFs are slightly lower in some areas, but global EF still almost doubled to 1.6%. This is because our original dataset already covered major global variations, but we agree on the importance of studies in under-represented zones, as they may further improve the global estimates.

In summary, while Cardinael et al. (2024) bring up some valid concerns, they do not affect the main findings of Shang et al. (2024). We fully agree with Cardinael et al. (2024) on the need for more observations of cropland N<sub>2</sub>O emissions during the fallow period, especially in the under-represented regions, and we thank them for a fruitful discussion.
