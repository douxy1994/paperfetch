# Sampling origins and directions affect the minimum sampling area in forest plots

## Abstract

The minimum sampling area (minimum area) is the smallest space that reflects species composition and characteristics of a plant community. The quantitative concept of minimum area is often estimated using species–area relationships (SARs) and has become the classical foundation for managing protected areas. However, sampling designs to determine the minimum area in different forest types have not been systematically evaluated.
China.
We used tree census data from three forest dynamic plots, each with a size of 25–60 ha, in different climatic zones in China to determine the minimum areas of woody plants and to analyze the effects of species richness and topographic heterogeneity on the minimum areas by changing sampling origin and direction.
We found that mainly sampling design affects the estimation of woody plant species richness and required minimum area in different forest types. The estimated size of the minimum areas required was several hectares and varied significantly with sampling origin and direction, and showed a difference of approximately 1.5–2 times in the forest plots. Topographic heterogeneity significantly affected the minimum area through changes in species composition.
Sampling origin and direction should be considered when using SARs to estimate the minimum area and species diversity in communities. Such a comprehensive approach of sampling can contribute to a better understanding of vegetation characteristics and the minimum area required for a conservation census in heterogeneous environments.

## 1 INTRODUCTION

Estimation of community diversity relies on field investigations of species composition within a finite area (Chase et al., 2020). The minimum sampling area represents the smallest space that adequately reflects the species composition and characteristics of a plant community (Ellenberg & Mueller-Dombois, 1974), and modeling the relationship between species richness and sampling area is essential for determining the optimal sampling effort and design for biodiversity research (Chase et al., 2018; Kunin et al., 2018; McGlinn et al., 2019). The minimum sampling area can also inform conservation decisions, such as identifying biodiversity hotspots, calculating the minimum protected area to maintain species diversity, and assessing biodiversity loss due to habitat fragmentation (Guilhaumon et al., 2008; Rybicki & Hanski, 2013; Drira et al., 2019; Rapacciuolo et al., 2019).

The concept of minimal sampling area was originally developed by phytosociologist and has been introduced and subsequently spread to various fields within ecology, conservation biology, and biogeography, despite a lack of solid theoretical foundation. Various methodological definitions have been proposed to quantify the minimum sampling area, such as the curves of species presence, abundance, or the vegetation homogeneity index, but no objective definition has been widely recognized (Moravec, 1973; Dietvorst et al., 1982). Pragmatically and for convenience, ecologists adopted this concept to conduct vegetation surveys. A simple and commonly used method to estimate the minimal sampling area is based on the species–area relationship (SAR). Although visual estimation of the minimal area from accumulation curves is mathematically biased, many ecologists often define the minimum sampling area as the area where the curve begins to flatten or where the expected number of species is found to occur (Fisher et al., 1943; Barkman, 1989). The expected number of species was the survey target, that is, an artificial proportion of the total species richness in the region investigated. As the sampling area continues to increase, species richness increases gradually.

The formula and parameters of SARs are largely influenced by the sampling design (Drakare et al., 2006). For example, the slope of the SAR curve obtained by the nested sampling design is smaller than that based on the random sampling design (Reinhard & Drossel, 2021). The form of the SAR curve also depends on the sample grain size and intensity as the sampling scale increases (Schrader et al., 2019; Bueno et al., 2020). Several studies constructed SAR curves using a series of quadrats or transects with a single sampling origin and direction (Schrader et al., 2019; Dengler et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020). For instance, each plot is strictly nested within the next larger plot and then these plots build SARs (Type I curves, Scheiner, 2003; Type A.a.i, Dengler, 2009) from a corner of the research area and then double the sampling area in a fixed direction (He et al., 1996; Keeley & Fotheringham, 2003; Seabloom et al., 2021). However, this method generally does not account for the effects of sampling origin and direction, which might bias the SARs and minimum sampling area estimates. These sampling efforts may over- or underestimate species richness and distort the true species composition and characteristics of plant communities.

In addition to the sampling design, the spatial heterogeneity of species diversity and the environment may also affect SARs (He & Legendre, 2002; Tjørve et al., 2008). The spatial and abundance distributions of species are key factors that influence SARs theoretically (He & Legendre, 2002). Habitats filter plant species, resulting in a heterogeneous distribution pattern of species in forests (Shen et al., 2009; Liu et al., 2020). For example, topography and soil properties explain the spatial distribution of different plant life forms and regulate their SARs in forest plots (He et al., 2022). Therefore, understanding the roles of species diversity patterns and environmental heterogeneity is crucial for determining the necessary sampling effort in forest studies.

In this study, we collected the individual distribution data of woody plants from three large forest dynamic plots across different climatic zones in China, namely Mount Wanglang in Sichuan Province (Wanglang Plot), Mount Badagongshan in Hunan Province (Badagongshan Plot), and Mount Jianfengling in Hainan Province (Jianfengling Plot) (Table 1). We used power-law models to fit plot-scale species–area relationship (SAR) curves of woody plants and estimated the minimum sampling areas under pre-assigned survey targets of plant richness, representing a proportion of the total species richness. In particular, we explored how different sampling designs affected the minimum sampling area. We examined the relationship between topographic heterogeneity, species diversity, and the minimum sampling area and investigated whether species diversity and environmental heterogeneity explained the variation. Specifically, we tested the following hypotheses. First, the SAR forms, their model parameters, and minimum sampling area estimates depend on the sampling origin and direction. Second, these differences may be attributed to environmental heterogeneity, as this shapes the spatial distribution of species with further heterogeneity of species diversity in the forest plot. In general, higher topographic heterogeneity might result in a more heterogeneous species composition and distribution in the plot and a larger minimum sampling area is needed.

## 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

### 2.1 Study sites

In this study, we used woody plant inventory data (Table 1) from three forest dynamics plots located on Mt. Wanglang in Sichuan, Mt. Badagongshan in Hunan, and Mt. Jianfengling in Hainan Province. These plots spanned different climatic zones from north to south, with a mean annual temperature and precipitation of 2.9°C and 866.5 mm for the Wanglang Plot, 11.5°C and 2105.4 mm for the Badagongshan Plot, and 19.8°C and 2683.6 mm for the Jianfengling Plot, respectively. The vegetation types were subalpine dark coniferous forest, evergreen and deciduous broad-leaved mixed forest, and tropical montane rainforest, respectively. The overall coverage of the plots ranged from 25 to 60 ha. Each plot was further divided into 20 m<sup>2</sup> × 20 m<sup>2</sup> quadrats. All woody plant individuals with diameters at breast height (DBHs) above 1 cm in the plots were mapped, tagged, and identified. We constructed the SAR curves based on the quadrats. The total woody richness was highest in the Jianfengling Plot (273 species), followed by the Badagongshan (248 species) and Wanglang Plots (48 species). Similarly, at the quadrat level, species richness was highest in the Jianfengling Plot (78.1 species/400 m<sup>2</sup>), followed by the Badagongshan (44.6 species/400 m<sup>2</sup>) and the Wanglang Plot (16.2 species/400 m<sup>2</sup>).

### 2.2 Sampling design

To unify the study scale, we set the maximum sampling area to a 25-ha area from the plot origin for each of the three plots (360 × 700 m<sup>2</sup> for the Wanglang Plot, 500 × 500 m<sup>2</sup> for the Badagongshan Plot and Jianfengling Plot). Nested sampling is a more common practice in forest vegetation surveys. Two types of SAR curves were constructed using different nested sampling designs (Figure 1). Sampling Design A (Type A.a.i, Dengler, 2009) constructed the SAR curves of woody plants based on nested subplots from different origins and directions, starting with a randomly selected quadrat and then adding adjacent quadrats in a clockwise direction. We then summarized the species richness in the sampling area after each quadrat was added to obtain the relationship between species richness and sampling area. For each plot, we constructed multiple nested SARs to characterize species accumulation from different origins and along different directions (Figure 1). Sampling design B constructed a SAR curve by calculating the mean species richness at each gradient of the sampling area, which averaged the effect of the sampling design (Type A.b.i., Dengler, 2009). Each forest plot had a mean SAR curve that revealed the overall characteristics of woody plants (Figure 1).

### 2.3 Calculation of minimum sampling area

We applied the power-law model to fit the SAR curves, because of its superior fitness to SAR curves and its quantitative representation of SAR curve variations through model parameters:

**Equation 1.**

$$
S = c \times A^{z}
$$

where S represents species richness, A represents the sampling area (m2), and c and z are estimated model parameters. To display the results more explicitly, we converted the sampling area from square meters (m2) to hectares (ha) on the axis. A transformed power-law function was applied to calculate the minimum sampling area ($A^{′}$) required to satisfy the corresponding woody plant species survey target (Ɵ):

**Equation 2.**

$$
A^{′} = e^{\frac{log(\frac{ƟS}{c})}{z}}
$$

We calculated the difference between the observed sampling area and the model-predicted value (mean standardized error), and the determination coefficient (*R*<sup>2</sup>) to evaluate the fitness of the power-law models. When species survey targets increased from 50% to 90% of the total richness of the plot, the fitness error approached zero (Appendix S1). Hence, we focused on the 90% species survey target; that is, we defined the minimum sampling area as the area where sampled richness reached 90% of the total richness of the plot. We used the total number of species censused within a 25-ha plot as the total richness for each plot, while there are other methods and models to estimate the total richness in the plot. We chose the species richness censused to predict the corresponding minimal sampling area due to its significance as a fundamental biodiversity metric within a plant community. The minimum sampling areas were estimated using sampling designs A and B. Variations were further analyzed in each plot.

### 2.4 Topographic heterogeneity

We extracted the elevation (m), slope, and aspect of each quadrat (20 × 20 m<sup>2</sup>) based on the digital elevation model (DEM) of each forest plot (Appendix S2). To measure the topographic heterogeneity within a forest plot, we calculated the standard deviation of the topographic factors between quadrats. We calculated topographic heterogeneity within each minimum area to explore its relationship with the minimum sampling area size. We also performed correlation analyses of topographic heterogeneity to assess the strength of the correlations between these variables (Appendix S2).

### 2.5 Species diversity

To reveal the relationship between species diversity and the minimum sampling area under different sampling origins and directions, we calculated the α-diversity and β-diversity among the quadrats of the forest dynamic plots (Appendices S3 and S4). Our study is primarily an empirical analysis. Hence, we chose to used α-diversity and β-diversity to describe these patterns because they are widely recognized in ecological studies and convey the essence of our findings effectively. The α-diversity was defined as the species richness at the first sampling quadrat (or the sampling origin), while the β-diversity was calculated based on the Jaccard dissimilarity index (Crist & Veech, 2006; Chao et al., 2016):

**Equation 3.**

$$
β - diversity = \frac{∑Jaccard_{n}}{n}
$$

**Equation 4.**

$$
Jaccard_{n} = 1 - \frac{a}{b + c - a} = 1 - \frac{S_{n}}{S}(n \geq 1)
$$

where b represents the number of species in one assemblage, c represents the number of species in another, and a represents the number of species that co-occurred in both assemblages. Thus, $S_{n}$ is the species richness within n quadrats sampled and S is the total species richness in the plot (Equation 4). We calculated the Jaccard dissimilarity index for each sampling gradient under a sampling process (Appendix S5). We then averaged the Jaccard dissimilarity indices to obtain the β-diversity (Equation 3), which reflects the rate of species accumulation along the sampling direction. The specific diversity depended on the origin and direction of sampling.

### 2.6 Data analysis

We used univariate linear regression to analyze the relationships between topographic heterogeneity, species diversity, and the minimum sampling area (Appendices S3, S4, S6). To better illustrate the linear trends of these relationships, we classified the independent variables (topographic heterogeneity and species diversity) into five quantile groups. The values between the minimum and 20th percentiles were grouped as 1, and the values between the 80th percentile and maximum were grouped as 5. We then used the *p-*values of the regression coefficients and model *R*<sup>2</sup> to assess the significance and strength of the correlations.

We further used the structural equation model to analyze the impact of topographic heterogeneity (elevation, slope, and aspect heterogeneity of the quadrats) and species diversity (*α*- and *β*-diversity) on the minimum sampling area in a forest plot. Species richness in the quadrats differed between quadrats, and the defined *β*-diversity changed with sampling origin and direction. All variables of topographic heterogeneity and species diversity were normalized to compare the magnitudes of the causal effects. The model assumed that the minimum sampling area ($A^{′}$) as the dependent variable was directly influenced by *α-* and *β*-diversity, and the topographic heterogeneity along the sampling design affected the *α-* and *β*-diversity, which in turn influenced the minimum sampling area ($A^{′}$). The priori full model included the causal effect of three possible topographic heterogeneities on species diversity, as well as the causal effect of all factors (topographic heterogeneity and species diversity) on the minimum sampling area. We filtered out the causal relationships with non-significant effects (*p* > 0.05) and selected the best-performing model using the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), Fisher index, and *p*-values. Model construction and analysis used the R packages *piecewiseSEM* and *nlme*. All data analyses were performed using R 4.0.5 (R Core Team, 2022).

## 3 RESULTS

### 3.1 Characteristics and variations of SAR curves

The *R*<sup>2</sup>-values of the power-law models were relatively high, suggesting that the power-law model fit the SAR curves well. The mean standardized error decreased with the species survey targets (Appendix S1). For sampling design B, the *z*-value was highest in the Badagongshan Plot (0.19) and lowest in the Jianfengling Plot (0.10). The *c*-value was the highest in the Jianfengling Plot (78.65) and the lowest in the Wanglang Plot (11.20) (Figure 2, Appendix S8).

### 3.2 Variations in minimum sampling area with sampling design

Under the 90% species survey targets ($Ɵ = 0.9$), the average minimum sampling area required based on the mean SAR curve was largest for the Badagongshan Plot (13.22 ha), followed by the Wanglang Plot (9.03 ha), and the Jianfengling Plot (8.30 ha). The minimum sampling area determined from different sampling origins and directions varied approximately 1.5–2 times in different plots (Figure 4, Appendix S9); it was largest in the Jianfengling Plot (2.5th quantile: 5.78; 97.5th quantile: 11.19 ha), followed by the Wanglang Plot (2.5th quantile: 6.35 ha; 97.5th quantile: 11.52 ha) and the Badagongshan Plot (2.5th quantile: 10.89; 97.5th quantile: 15.20 ha). The minimum-area frequency did not follow a normal distribution. For lower species survey targets, the value of the minimum sampling area decreased, but the range of variation was 1.5–10 times in different plots (Appendices S7, S9).

### 3.3 Impact of species diversity and topographic heterogeneity on the minimum sampling area

The pattern of species diversity in the plots showed that the minimum sampling area increased with *β*-diversity within the forest plot but did not change significantly with *α*-diversity (Appendices S3, S4). Topographic heterogeneity, mainly elevation heterogeneity, explained the variation in the minimum sampling area between the different sampling designs (Appendix S6). The minimum sampling area increased with elevation heterogeneity in the Wanglang (*R*<sup>2</sup> = 0.30) and Jianfengling (*R*<sup>2</sup> = 0.68) Plots but showed a unimodal pattern with elevation heterogeneity in the Badagongshan Plot (*R*<sup>2</sup> = 0.15). Finally, SEM analysis showed that topographic heterogeneity indirectly affected the change in the minimum sampling area by species diversity (Figure 5).

## 4 DISCUSSION

### 4.1 Influence of sampling origins and directions

Consistent with our first hypothesis, the SAR model parameters varied among the different sampling origins and directions (Figure 3), resulting in a significant change in the minimum sampling area (Figure 4 and Appendix S7). The variation was at least 1.5–2 times in the different plots. These results indicate that traditional SAR methods, such as those built by nested quadrats or transects under a single sampling origin and direction, may be insufficient to accurately estimate the minimum sampling area for forests. Despite this, most studies neglect to consider sampling design (e.g., sampling origin and direction) when estimating the minimum sampling area to investigate forest communities and estimate species diversity. For example, previous studies used a simplified power-law SAR to estimate the minimum protected area based on local and regional species richness data without constructing a true SAR curve (Desmet & Cowling, 2004). Although this approach has been widely applied in biodiversity conservation (Foster et al., 2013; Karenyi et al., 2016; Davis et al., 2017), the estimated minimum area may differ significantly from the results of many other SAR models (Drira et al., 2019).

Our study highlights the importance of considering *β*-diversity and its variation among different sampling directions rather than relying solely on species richness at the sampling origin (Figure 5). Therefore, we suggest to take the effects of the sampling origin and direction into consideration when estimating the minimum sampling areas and their ranges in a forest. Considering the field sampling efforts, it is difficult to choose a sampling method with comprehensive coverage to fully solve the problem of sampling effects (McGlinn et al., 2019). This poses a challenge for vegetation surveys in determining the minimum sampling area.

### 4.2 Impacts of beta-diversity and topographic heterogeneity

The SEM analysis revealed that topographic heterogeneity indirectly affected the minimum sampling area by *β*-diversity in the forest plot, while *α*-diversity had little effect (Figure 5), which are consistent with our second hypothesis. Environmental heterogeneity and biological factors influence the spatial distribution of individuals and the species composition of local plant assemblages in forests (Punchi-Manage et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2018). These factors are intertwined and have cascading effects on the dynamics of plant communities. Among them, topographic heterogeneity could be an essential factor that influences the species diversity. For instance, tree species have large compositional differences between valley and ridge quadrats in Amazonian forests (Valencia et al., 2004). The dissimilarity in tree species composition among the plot quadrats might increase with topographic heterogeneity within the forest plot (De Cáceres et al., 2012). Similarly, we found that topographic heterogeneity within forest plots explained a high proportion of *β*-diversity (species accumulation) variation. Although there were some differences in the intensity and direction of specific effects (Figure 5), such as elevation, heterogeneity positively affected *β*-diversity in the Wanglang (0.38), Jianfengling (0.50), and Badagongshan Plots (0.60).

In general, a more heterogeneous plant assemblage pattern requires a larger minimum sampling area to capture most species in the plot. The *α*-diversity indicated species richness at the quadrat level, which was important but not decisive for the variation in the minimum sampling area at the plot scale. In contrast, a higher *β*-diversity indicated a lower species accumulation rate throughout the sampling process (Appendix S5), which reflected the heterogeneity of the plant assemblages distributed in the plot. We found that the *β*-diversity was higher in the Badagongshan Plot than in the Jianfengling and Wanglang Plots (Appendix S4), which indicated the complexity of the spatial pattern of plant assemblages in the Badagongshan Plot. Owing to the slow species accumulation during the sampling process, a large minimum area is required. Hence, the *β*-diversity was positively correlated with the minimum sampling area in the forest plot. The minimum sampling area for forest tended to be larger in the Badagongshan Plot. Although there might be commonly known spatial autocorrelations within the forest plot that may influence the intensity of correlations, they do not necessarily generate bias (Legendre, 1993; Diniz-Filho et al., 2003). We compared our linear regression model with spatial regression models, finding that the significance of the topographic influences remained consistent (Appendix S10). Moreover, it was necessary to control for other plot conditions to isolate the effects of the sampling design from the spatial attribute itself. Hence, our study suggests that topographic heterogeneity should be considered in the layout of sampling quadrats and the construction of SARs, which may help investigate richer species diversity of vegetation with more reasonable sampling efforts in forests.

### 4.3 A larger minimum sampling area may be needed in vegetation studies

Generally, within a community, the more sampling quadrats, the richer the species richness discovered in the covered communities and habitats. Conversely, small sampling areas may underestimate the species diversity of a community (Cam et al., 2002; Azovsky, 2011). The size of the plot is related to vegetation and habitat type. Historically, the practical sampling area to determine the characteristics and species diversity of vegetation at the community level was often determined by experience and ranged from a few to hundreds of square meters (Hopkins, 1955; Kilburn, 1966; Otypková & Chytry, 2006). However, for forest communities, the minimum sampling area ranges from 200 m<sup>2</sup> to 900 m<sup>2</sup> in most studies, which is relatively small and may underestimate forest diversity (Barkman, 1989). This may not reflect forest richness. Based on three 25-ha forest dynamic plots located in different climatic zones in China, we found that the traditional minimum sampling area was insufficient to estimate even 50% woody plant richness in the forests (Appendix S7). To achieve 80%–90% species survey targets for a forest, a minimum sampling area of at least several hectares may be required (Figure 4).

Based on our results, we recommend that a larger minimum sampling area is necessary to obtain more complete biodiversity information regarding the species composition and distribution of the forest communities. A larger sampling area also helps deal with sampling challenges posed by habitat topography. A habitat with complex topography may require more sampling effort than a homogeneous habitat. For instance, in vegetation habitats with complex topography, it may be advisable to increase the number of quadrats surveyed. Moreover, constructing multiple SAR models with different sampling origins and directions, and obtaining the range of the parameters and estimation values can help assess and predict species diversity in a forest community more accurately.

## 5 CONCLUSIONS

We found that sampling origins and directions affected the determination of the minimum sampling area, resulting in a variation of approximately 1.5–2 times in different plots. Environmental factors, represented by topographical heterogeneity, influence the minimum area through changes in species composition. The dissimilarity in species accumulation along the sampling direction largely explained the variation in the minimum area, whereas species richness had a weak influence. We suggest that it is necessary to carefully design the sampling origins and directions to estimate large variations in the minimum sampling areas in a vegetation survey. Taking the topographic heterogeneity into consideration may be of help to investigate richer species diversity of vegetation with more reasonable sampling efforts in forests. Our study highlights the importance of tailoring sampling designs to the specific topographic characteristics of the forest. These findings contribute to a better understanding of the vegetation characteristics and the minimum area required for a census in heterogeneous habitats.
