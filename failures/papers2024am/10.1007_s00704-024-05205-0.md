## **Abstract**

Precipitation prediction is crucial for various sectors, including agriculture, water resource management, and transportation. This paper addresses the gap in rainfall prediction studies that seldom separate spatial and temporal scales. The spatial distribution of rainfall in Zhejiang Province in 2015 is analyzed using multiple interpolation methods: Inverse Distance to a Power (IDP), Kriging Interpolation, Minimum Curvature Interpolation (MC), Modified Shepard’s Method (MSM), Nearest Neighbour Interpolation (NeaN), and Natural Neighbor(NN). Additionally, daily and monthly rainfall are simulated using a 30-year historical precipitation dataset (1990-2020) of an observation station from Zhejiang Province, employing Radial Basis Function Network (RBFN) and Back Propagation (BP) neural networks.Our results confirm the accuracy of these methods in both simulation and interpolation. The benefits of Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) over traditional interpolation techniques in predicting rainfall are underscored, while the challenges ANNs face, such as nonlinear rainfall patterns, training time, computational complexity, and the configuration of meteorological stations are also acknowledged. Notably, RBFN outperformed the BP model in simulating rainfall, especially for longer forecast periods.In conclusion, the potential applications and future directions of ANNs in rainfall prediction are discussed, their utility across different spatial and temporal scales are emphasized. By comparing ANNs with classical interpolation methods, their respective strengths are highlighted, providing scientific insights for future precipitation forecasting at provincial administrative levels.

Precipitation is a fundamental parameter for evaluating hydrology, ecosystems, agriculture, and water resources (Xiaoping et al. 2020). Variations in precipitation can lead to extreme weather events such as droughts and floods, significantly impacting ecosystems, water resources, and agricultural productivity. These changes have profound implications for human life and the national economy (Yen et al. 2019). Accurate rainfall forecasting is vital for planning disaster prevention measures

Historically, meteorologists have relied on personal experience and weather maps–knowledge garnered through years of observation and grounding in weather theory–to forecast rainfall (Darji et al. 2015). The inadequacies and inaccuracies of these traditional methods were primary catalysts for the development of numerical weather prediction and machine learning algorithms for weather forecasting. Contemporary rainfall prediction techniques can be categorized into three main groups: data-driven prediction methods employing Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), time series and statistical techniques, and numerical prediction methods based on numerical weather models. Traditional time series modeling approaches, such as Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) and Seasonal ARIMA (SARIMA), have been employed for monthly rainfall prediction (Delleur and Kavvas 1978). However, these models are fundamentally linear and, although applicable to the steady state of rainfall series, they are limited in their ability to capture the highly nonlinear features of rainfall, which is influenced by various meteorological and mathematical conditions. Moreover, according to Nourani et al. (2009), these models exhibit limited capacity for monthly precipitation forecasting and are unsuitable for long lead time predictions.

Many researchers posit that ANN techniques offer significantadvantagesovertraditionalstatisticalforecastingmodels due to their ability to map any nonlinear function without requiring knowledge of physics or assumptions inherent in conventional methods (Wu et al. 2015). Consequently, ANNs possess highly flexible, nonlinear, and data-driven learning capabilities, enabling them to predict precipitation with greater accuracy than traditional techniques (Basha et al. 2020). For instance, to forecast cyclone intensity for the next 12, 24, 36, 48, 60, and 72 hours, multiple linear regression methods were combined with a Back Propagation (BP) neural network (Baik and Hwang 1998). The results demonstrated that the BP neural network model’s prediction errors were 10% to 16% lower than those of the regression model, indicating the BP neural network’s potential for predicting tropical cyclone intensity. In comparison to the Australian Community Climate and Earth System Simulator Seasonal

Prediction System (ACCESS), the method proposed by Haidar and Verma, which employs Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), produced superior quantitative metrics for monthly precipitation predictions at specific locations in eastern Australia (Haidar and Verma 2018). Furthermore, to illustrate the advancements in rainfall prediction, Poornima and Pushpalatha (2019) proposed a Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) based on Reinforced Long Short-Term Memory (Reinforced LSTM) and compared its results with those of Holt-Winters, Extreme Learning Machine (ELM), ARIMA, Recurrent Neural Network, and Long Short-Term Memory models. Additionally, small samples of rainfall data were utilized for precipitation classification to explore the application of the BP neural network in weather prediction (Pirone et al. 2023).

Most of the existing research employing artificial neural networks for precipitation forecasting has not extensively addressed the anticipated values of rainfall across various time scales (e.g., annual, monthly, and daily) or the variability in expected rainfall at regional scales. However, Guhathakurta (2008) introduced the concept of scaling in monsoon rainfall forecasting using ANNs by analyzing monthly rainfall time series data from 36 meteorological subdivisions. This approach improved the accuracy of seasonal precipitation predictions in independent cycles. Precisely incorporating parameters from multiple time scales and spatial scales into rainfall prediction models would enable the accurate determination of sequence values and distribution trends of rainfall predictions within independent cycles and designated locations.

This study conducts a simulation and analysis of rainfall prediction for Zhejiang Province in China on both daily and monthly bases, utilizing the Radial Basis Function Network (RBFN) model and the Back Propagation (BP) model, two traditional artificial neural network approaches. The analyses’ results are compared, and their accuracy is validated. The parameters used include the spatial scale of provincial areas and historical datasets spanning 30 years of an observation station in Zhejiang Province. To simulate rainfall forecasts and comparative study, several classical interpolation techniques, including Inverse Distance to a Power(IDP),Kriging, Minimum Curvature(MC), Modified Shepard’s Method(MSM), Nearest Neighbor(NeaN), and Natural Neighbour(NN), are utilized. The validation data for this study are derived from 2015 rainfall observations at various points in Zhejiang Province. Several precision indexes are then validated and subsequently summarized and discussed. This study explores the potential integration of conventional interpolation techniques with artificial neural networks for rainfall and precipitation prediction.

## **2.2.2 Time scale: 1990-2020 precipitation dataset for an observation site in Zhejiang province**

Rainfall is a complex meteorological process influenced by numerous factors, including wind speed, air temperature, humidity, topography, air pressure, and the presence of water bodies such as lakes and rivers. Among these, humidity, air temperature, and wind speed are the primary determinants of rainfall. High humidity increases the water vapor content in the atmosphere; elevated air temperatures enhance the evaporation rate of water vapor, further increasing atmospheric moisture; and wind speed facilitates the evaporation andtransportofwatervapor,allofwhichcontributetotheformationofrainfall(Wangetal. 2021).Thedailymaximumand minimum temperatures are critical drivers of rainfall prediction, with their scientific significance rooted in their influence on atmospheric stability, water vapor content, and precipitation mechanisms. These temperature indices collectively determine the occurrence and development of precipitation through the interaction of complex physical processes.

Therefore, on the temporal scale, four variables were selected as driving factors: relative humidity (%), maximum air temperature (°C), minimum air temperature (°C), and wind speed (m/s), with precipitation(mm) as the output variable. The RBFN and BP models were employed for training. The spatial resolution of the humidity, air temperature, and wind speed data is 30 meters. ## **2.3 Interpolation methods**

Interpolation calculations are among the most widely utilized spatial analysis techniques in Geographic Information Systems (GIS), with a broad range of applications. Spatial interpolation, a statistical method, is primarily employed to transform scattered measurement data into a continuous data

surface, facilitating comparison with the distribution patterns of other geographical phenomena. In practical research, the selection of an appropriate interpolation method should be based on a comprehensive analysis of the application requirements, the quality of experimental results, and the spatial scale of the input data. While classical and stable interpolation methods are frequently employed, they may exhibit drawbacks in various aspects when generating results. To enhance the precision and reliability of rainfall precipitation forecasts, numerous researchers have adopted traditional interpolation techniques such as Kriging interpolation and inverse distance interpolation.

To enhance the precision and reliability of rainfall predictions, a spatial relational model was developed by integrating the temporal component and utilizing measured data from meteorological stations (Chutsagulprom et al. 2022). Uyyala (2022) employed the inverse distance interpolation method for rainfall forecasting. This approach improved forecast accuracy and reliability by calculating the distance between unknown and known locations, assigning various weights, creating a relational model between rainfall and distance, and combining it with an artificial neural network. To predict rainfall in mountainous areas, Guhathakurta (2008) collected measured data from weather stations and remote sensing data. They compared the effectiveness of kriging interpolation, inverse distance interpolation, and other methods, subsequently evaluating the precision and dependability of these various approaches.

In this study, SURFER software was utilized to analyze the trend and distribution pattern of rainfall in Zhejiang Province. Initially, a Grid file was generated by interpolating precipitation data from the foundational points, specifying x (longitude), y (latitude), and z (rainfall observation), and selecting an appropriate interpolation method (IDP, Kriging, MC, MSM, NeaN, NN) along with an output path. To evaluate the accuracy of the interpolation results, the interpolated values from the Grid file were extracted into ground point precipitation data through point sampling.

IDP interpolation method is widely employed, using the distance between the interpolation point and the sample point as the weight for a weighted average. The closer a sample point is to the interpolation point, the greater the weight assigned to it. However, IDP has limited capability to handle complex data distributions and is prone to abrupt data changes. The Kriging interpolation method, initially proposed by D. Matheron, a South African mining engineer, in 1951 while searching for gold ores, was later formalized and systematized by G. Matheron, a renowned French statistician. It is a classical spatial interpolation method used in geostatistics. MC method seeks to balance the data points and the interpolated surface, minimizing the curvature of the interpolated surface. MSM employs distance inverse weighted least squares with corresponding improvements.

## **4 Conclusion and discussion**

The main findings of the present study highlight the effectiveness of different interpolation methods and artificial neural networks (ANNs) in predicting precipitation. By interpolating rainfall data from 1,900 observation stations across Zhejiang Province, China, using six classical methods–Inverse Distance Weighting (IDP), Kriging, Multiple Covariates (MC), Moving Average (MSM), Nearest Neighbor (NeaN), and Neural Network (NN)-we assessed their efficacy through grid visualizations and numerical precision analyses. This initial phase provided valuable insights into how well each method captures the overall rainfall distribution trend for the year 2015.

Our findings are consistent with other studies that emphasize the advantages of ANNs over traditional interpolation techniques. For instance, research by Basha et al. (2020) has shown that ANNs, especially Radial Basis Function Networks (RBFN), generally outperform conventional methods in terms of accuracy for longer time scales. This study corroborates these findings by demonstrating that the RBFN model significantly improves prediction accuracy for monthly data compared to the Backpropagation (BP) model and classical interpolation methods. The BP model’s lower accuracy on daily data, attributed to limited training data, further supports the notion that ANNs excel in handling non-linear characteristics of rainfall data.

The implications of these findings are substantial for improving precipitation forecasting. The ability of ANNs to

integrate various environmental factors, such as temperature, relative humidity, and wind speed, leads to more accurate and reliable predictions. This advancement enhances our understandingofrainfallpatternsandoffersarobusttoolforsectors dependent on accurate precipitation forecasts, such as agriculture and water resources management.

However, the study also acknowledges several limitations. The RBFN model’s increased computational complexity and longertrainingtimescanbeaconstraint,particularlyforlarge datasets or real-time applications. Additionally, the accuracy of ANN predictions is sensitive to the quality of meteorological data, which can be compromised by uneven distribution or insufficient station density. Simulation errors were more pronounced on daily scales, reflecting the inherent difficulty in modeling short-term rainfall dynamics.

In conclusion, this study confirms that ANNs, especially the RBFN model, offer significant advantages over traditional interpolation methods for predicting precipitation on monthly and longer time scales. While ANNs provide a superior tool for modeling non-linear rainfall characteristics, their application is limited by computational demands and data quality issues.

Future research should focus on employing advanced machine learning techniques, such as deep learning models, to enhance prediction accuracy and manage computational requirements. Additionally, improving the configuration and density of meteorological stations will contribute to better data quality and model performance. Incorporating additional environmental variables, such as terrain and soil conditions, could further refine prediction models and extend their applicability across various forecasting scenarios.
