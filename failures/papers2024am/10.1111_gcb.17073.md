# Recent decline in tropical temperature sensitivity of atmospheric <scp>CO<sub>2</sub></scp> growth rate variability

## Abstract

A two-fold enhancement in the sensitivity of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> growth rate (CGR) to tropical temperature interannual variability ($Γ_{CGR}^{T}$) till early 2000s has been reported, which suggests a drought-induced shift in terrestrial carbon cycle responding temperature fluctuations, thereby accelerating global warming. However, using six decades long atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> observations, we show that $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ has significantly declined in the last two decades, to the level during the 1960s. The $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ decline begs the question of whether the sensitivity of ecosystem carbon cycle to temperature variations at local scale has largely decreased. With state-of-the-art dynamic global vegetation models, we further find that the recent $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ decline is barely attributed to ecosystem carbon cycle response to temperature fluctuations at local scale, which instead results from a decrease in spatial coherence in tropical temperature variability and land use change. Our results suggest that the recently reported loss of rainforest resilience has not shown marked influence on the temperature sensitivity of ecosystem carbon cycle. Nevertheless, the increasing extent of land use change as well as more frequent and intensive drought events are likely to modulate the responses of ecosystem carbon cycle to temperature variations in the future. Therefore, our study highlights the priority to continuously monitor the temperature sensitivity of CGR variability and improve Earth system model representation on land use change, in order to predict the carbon–climate feedback.

## Recent decline in tropical temperature sensitivity of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> growth rate variability

## 1 INTRODUCTION

Terrestrial carbon cycle feedback to climate warming is one of major contributors to uncertainties in future climate projections (Arora et al., 2020; Bodman et al., 2013; Booth et al., 2012). Interannual variability (IAV) in net land–atmosphere carbon exchange (net ecosystem exchange [NEE]) provides a natural testbed for exploring the mechanisms of terrestrial carbon cycle dynamics (Keeling et al., 1995; Poulter et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2022). It is well known that tropical ecosystems dominate the IAV in global NEE and in atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> growth rate (CGR) (Piao et al., 2020). Moreover, the IAV of tropical land carbon cycle is mainly driven by temperature and water variations (Humphrey et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022). A recent study has shown an increasing water control on the tropical land carbon cycle since the 1960s (Liu et al., 2023), whereas the changes in temperature effects on the tropical land carbon uptake have been not fully understood.

The responses of tropical land carbon cycle to temperature fluctuations strongly relate to the positive feedback of carbon cycle to climate systems (Cox et al., 2013; Padrón et al., 2022; Piao et al., 2020). Previous studies found that the tropical temperature sensitivity of CGR ($Γ_{CGR}^{T}$) had doubled from the 1960s to the early 2000s (Rödenbeck et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2014), which raises a concern that tropical ecosystems have shifted to be more sensitive and vulnerable under warming and increasing drought conditions (Balch, 2014; Piao et al., 2019). However, when extending atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> observations to the year of 2021, we could find a dramatic decline of $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ during the past two decades (Luo & Keenan, 2022; Rödenbeck et al., 2018), to the level in the 1960s (Figure 1a). It is unclear whether the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ decline, found at regional scale, indicates a weakening impact of temperature variability on tropical ecosystem carbon cycle at local scale, or results from other driving processes. Given the recent loss of rainforest resilience (Boulton et al., 2022; Forzieri et al., 2022) and the decrease in tropical forest carbon sink (Brienen et al., 2015; Hubau et al., 2020), understanding the mechanisms of the recent $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ decline is imperative to project the future evolution of the terrestrial carbon cycle.

The primary object of this study is to investigate the causes of the recent decline in $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$. To do this, we developed a statistical decomposition separating the impacts of three potential drivers: change in local temperature sensitivity of NEE ($γ_{NEE}^{T}$), change in local temperature variations, and change in spatial covariations of tropical land temperature (see Section 2.6). With state-of-the-art dynamic global vegetation models (DGVMs), we compared the contributions of the three drivers to the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ changes in different periods. In addition, we also explored the effects of land use change on the recent decline in $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$.

## 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

### 2.1 CGR data

We used annual CGR data for years 1959–2021 from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration Earth System Research Laboratory (NOAA/ESRL) (https://gml.noaa.gov/ccgg/trends/gr.html). The CGR data used in this study are based on observed CO<sub>2</sub> mole fraction at Mauna Loa, which was obtained from NOAA/ESRL and Scripps Institution of Oceanography (Keeling et al., 1976) (https://scrippsco2.ucsd.edu/). CGR in a given year was calculated as the difference between the 4-month average of CO<sub>2</sub> mole fraction centered on the January 1 of the next year and the 4-month average of CO<sub>2</sub> mole fraction centered on the January 1 of this year.

### 2.2 Climate dataset

Monthly gridded air temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover data for years 1959–2021 with a spatial resolution of 0.5° × 0.5° are obtained from the Climatic Research Unit (CRU TS4.06) (Harris et al., 2020). The gridded shortwave radiation data with a spatial resolution of 0.5° × 0.5° are obtained from the 6-h reanalysis dataset Climatic Research Unit and Japanese Reanalysis (CRUJRA v2.3) (University of East Anglia Climatic Research Unit & Harris, 2022). We also used a reconstruction dataset of terrestrial water storage (TWS) changes, which was generated by a data-driven statistical model and based on the Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment (GRACE) satellite observations (Humphrey et al., 2018). The statistical model relates daily precipitation and temperature anomalies to monthly TWS anomalies so that it can be used to reconstruct TWS changes over the past few decades.

### 2.3 NEE estimates from DGVMs

The NEE for years 1959–2019 is mainly estimated by DGVMs from TRENDYv9 project (Friedlingstein et al., 2020). We used the net biome productivity output with the opposite sign in simulation S2, where DGVMs are forced by varying CO<sub>2</sub> and climate change. Ten models were chosen following the two criteria: (1) the modeled temperature sensitivity of tropical NEE variability is positive, that is, warmer conditions are related to less net carbon uptake (Wang et al., 2013); (2) models could reproduce the changes (first increase and then decrease) in temperature sensitivity of tropical NEE variability (Figure 1). The 10 models are CLASSIC (Melton et al., 2020), DLEM (Tian et al., 2015), ISAM (Meiyappan et al., 2015), ISBA-CTRIP (Delire et al., 2020), LPJ (Poulter et al., 2011), LPJ-GUESS (Smith et al., 2014), LPX-Bern (Lienert & Joos, 2018), OCN (Zaehle & Friend, 2010), ORCHIDEEv3 (Vuichard et al., 2019), and YIBs (Yue & Unger, 2015). Monthly NEE from DGVMs is remapped to a spatial resolution of 0.5° × 0.5° using the nearest-neighbor method and then used to calculated the annual NEE.

### 2.4 NEE estimates from atmospheric inversions

To test the robustness of our analyses, we also used NEE estimates for the time period of 1980–2019 from two long-term atmospheric inversions (CAMS and Jena CarboScope) assimilating surface measurements of atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub>. The CAMS (version 20r1) provides monthly NEE with a spatial resolution of 1.9° latitude × 3.75° longitude (Chevallier et al., 2005). The Jena CarboScope (version s76oc_v2020) provides daily NEE with a spatial resolution of 4° latitude × 5° longitude (Rödenbeck et al., 2003). Before we calculated the annual NEE, we remapped the NEE output from two atmospheric inversions into a spatial resolution of 1° × 1° using the nearest-neighbor method.

### 2.5 Temperature sensitivity of the IAV in NEE

To estimate the sensitivity of NEE IAV to temperature variations, we removed the linear trends of annual NEE, temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover at each grid cell and used the detrended anomalies in NEE, temperature (T), precipitation (P), and cloud cover (Cld). In this study, the years 1991 and 1992 are excluded in order to minimizing the effects of the eruption of Mt. Pinatubo on CGR (Humphrey et al., 2018; Luo & Keenan, 2022). Then the local temperature sensitivity of NEE IAV ($γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T}$) at a grid cell (i) was calculated by a linear multiple regression:

**Equation 1.**

$$
\begin{matrix} NEE_{i} = γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T} \cdot T_{i} + γ_{NEE_{i}}^{P} \cdot P_{i} + γ_{NEE_{i}}^{Cld} \cdot Cld_{i} + ε \\ ≈γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T} \cdot T_{i} + γ_{NEE_{i}}^{P} \cdot P_{i} + γ_{NEE_{i}}^{Cld} \cdot Cld_{i}, \end{matrix}
$$

where $NEE_{i}$, $T_{i}$, $P_{i}$, and $Cld_{i}$ are anomalies in annual NEE (unit: PgC year−1), temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover at a grid cell (i), respectively. $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T}$, $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{P}$, and $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{Cld}$ indicate the sensitivities of NEE IAV to temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover at a grid cell (i), respectively. ɛ is a residual term. Similarly, the temperature sensitivity of tropical NEE IAV ($Γ_{NEE}^{T}$) was estimated by a linear multiple regression:

**Equation 2.**

$$
NEE = Γ_{NEE}^{T} \cdot T + Γ_{NEE}^{P} \cdot P + Γ_{NEE}^{Cld} \cdot Cld + ε,
$$

where NEE, T, P, and Cld are detrended anomalies in tropical annual NEE (unit: PgC year−1), temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover, respectively. $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$, $Γ_{NEE}^{P}$, and $Γ_{NEE}^{Cld}$ indicate the sensitivities of tropical NEE IAV to temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover, respectively. ɛ is a residual term.

The temperature sensitivity of CGR ($Γ_{CGR}^{T}$) was also calculated by the linear multiple regression (similar with Equation 2) considering the impacts of temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover:

**Equation 3.**

$$
\begin{matrix} CGR = Γ_{CGR}^{T} \cdot T + Γ_{CGR}^{P} \cdot P + Γ_{CGR}^{Cld} \cdot Cld + ε, \end{matrix}
$$

where CGR, $Γ_{CGR}^{P}$, and $Γ_{CGR}^{Cld}$ indicate detrended CGR anomalies, the sensitivities of CGR IAV to precipitation and cloud cover, respectively. Given that the IAV of CGR also strongly relates to the IAV of TWS (Humphrey et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022), we additionally used reconstructed TWS instead of precipitation in the Equation (3) to confirm the significantly recent decline of $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ (Figure S1). Given that the shortwave radiation and 6-month lagged precipitation could have larger influence on CGR IAV than cloud cover and mean annual precipitation, respectively, we also replaced the cloud cover with mean shortwave radiation and replaced mean annual precipitation with 6-month lagged precipitation in the regression models (Figure S2). In addition, we included the interaction term of temperature and precipitation in the regression model to test whether the interactions of temperature and precipitation affect the changes of $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ (Figure S2). Results from these regression models agree with the decadal changes (first increased and then decreased) in the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$, implying that the recent decline in the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ is robust. In this study, we calculated the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ in 19-, 20-, 21-, and 23-years time windows and found that the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ increased during the first half of the whole period and then decreased. Therefore, we also divided the past six decades into three parts: the first 20 years (1959–1978), the middle 20 years (1979–1998), and the latest 21 years (1999–2019), to estimate the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ changes between two time periods. We suggested that different divisions of time periods could slightly affect the magnitude of the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ decline but would not change the finding of the recent decline in $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$.

### 2.6 Factors affecting changes in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$

We investigated the link between $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ and $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T}$ to obtain the factors affecting changes in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$. According to Equation (2), the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ can be expressed as the partial derivative of NEE with respect to T:

**Equation 4.**

$$
\begin{matrix} Γ_{NEE}^{T} = \frac{∂(NEE)}{∂(T)} = \frac{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}NEE_{i})}{∂(T)}, \end{matrix}
$$

where $NEE = \overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}NEE_{i}$, unit: PgC year−1. The partial derivative $∂(NEE) / ∂(T)$ indicates the temperature sensitivity of NEE calculated by linear multiple regression (Equation 2), which considers the impacts of precipitation and cloud cover on NEE. The tropical land temperature (T) is calculated as area-weighted mean temperature over all grid cells in tropical vegetated areas:

**Equation 5.**

$$
\begin{matrix} T = \overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i}, \end{matrix}
$$

where $ω_{i}$ is the weight of the temperature variations at a gird cell i ($T_{i}$), which is the ratio of area at the gird cell i to the area of all tropical vegetated regions.

According to Equations (1), (4), and (5), the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ can be decomposed as:

**Equation 6.**

$$
\begin{matrix} Γ_{NEE}^{T} = \frac{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}NEE_{i})}{∂(T)}≈\frac{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}(γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T} \cdot T_{i} + γ_{NEE_{i}}^{P} \cdot P_{i} + γ_{NEE_{i}}^{Cld} \cdot Cld_{i}))}{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i})} \\ = \frac{∂(∑_{i = 1}^{n}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T} \cdot T_{i} + \overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{P} \cdot P_{i} + \overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{Cld} \cdot Cld_{i})}{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i})} \\ = \frac{∂(∑_{i = 1}^{n}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T} \cdot T_{i})}{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i})} + \frac{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{P} \cdot P_{i})}{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i})} + \frac{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{Cld} \cdot Cld_{i})}{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i})}, \end{matrix}
$$

In Equation (6), the $∑_{i = 1}^{n}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T} \cdot T_{i}$ is temperature-induced NEE IAV. Similarly, the $\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{P} \cdot P_{i}$ and $\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{Cld} \cdot Cld_{i}$ are precipitation-induced NEE IAV and cloud cover-induced NEE IAV, respectively. Hence, the changes in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ are theoretically determined by three factors: the sensitivity of temperature-induced NEE IAV to regional temperature variations $(\frac{∂(∑_{i = 1}^{n}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T} \cdot T_{i})}{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i})})$, the sensitivity of precipitation-induced NEE IAV to regional temperature variations $(\frac{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{P} \cdot P_{i})}{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i})})$, and the sensitivity of cloud cover-induced NEE IAV to regional temperature variations $(\frac{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{Cld} \cdot Cld_{i})}{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i})})$. Moreover, the effect of the first factor is expected to be much stronger than other two factors. That is, the changes in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ are mainly attributed to changes in the sensitivity of temperature-induced NEE IAV to regional temperature variations $(\frac{∂(∑_{i = 1}^{n}γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T} \cdot T_{i})}{∂(\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}T_{i} \cdot ω_{i})})$. Given that the $ω_{i}$ does not vary with time, the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ change mainly results from changes in local temperature sensitivity ($γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T}$) and local temperature variability ($T_{i}$). Furthermore, our analyses indicate that the influence of $T_{i}$ on $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ is mainly from the influence of the spatial covariations in tropical land temperature (Figure 2).

To isolate the effects of $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T}$, $T_{i}$, and the spatial covariations in tropical land temperature on the changes in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$, we first calculated local sensitivities of NEE to climatic drivers ($γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T}$, $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{P}$, and $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{Cld}$) following Equation (1) using outputs of DGVMs for three periods of 1959–1978, 1979–1998, and 1999–2019, and then designed four scenarios (T0–T3) (Table 1). In each scenario, we calculated the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ following Equation (2) for the periods of 1959–1978 and 1979–1998. According to Table 1, for the periods of 1959–1978 and 1979–1998, difference in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ between T1 and T0 indicates the effect of changes in $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T}$. Similarly, difference in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ between T2 and T0 indicates the effect of changes in $T_{i}$, and difference in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ between T3 and T0 indicates the effect of changes in spatial covariations of tropical land temperature. It should be noted that the sum of the effect of changes in $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T}$ and $T_{i}$ does not add up to the changes in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ (Figure 2), because the synergy between the change in $γ_{NEE_{i}}^{T}$ and the change in $T_{i}$ is not accounted for. Nevertheless, our analysis is robust to compare the relative importance of different factors in the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ changes.

### 2.7 Spatial covariations in tropical land temperature

To characterize the spatial coherence of temperature IAV, we conducted *K*-means clustering analysis (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007). This statistical method allows us to partition all land grid cells into several clusters and outliers according to the similarity of local temperature IAV (Yang et al., 2019). Grid cells where temperature IAVs are significantly positively correlated are assigned into the same cluster.

Specifically, we first assumed that the number of cluster *K* is greater than 2 but less than 20. For each of potential *K* value, we computed distances (one minus the correlation coefficient) from each grid cell to each of the centroids and each grid cell was assigned to its closest centroid. Then the new centroid for each of cluster was obtained by calculating the mean of all the points within the cluster. We repeated the processes that distances from each grid cell to each of the centroids were computed and the new centroids were obtained until the assignments no longer change. With these procedures, we assigned the land grid cells into *K* clusters. To get more robust results, these procedures were replicated 100 times. The optimal results can be obtained according to the sum of distances of each gird cell to its cluster centroid. Next, for a given *K* value, the within-cluster sum of point-to-centroid distances (“sum1”) was calculated and the sum of the squares of “sum1” was computed (“sum2”). Then, we could find a decrease in “sum2” with the increase in *K* value. The point where the rate of “sum2” decrease abruptly slows down thus corresponds to the optimal *K* value. It should be noted that some grid cells were not assigned to any cluster and regarded as outliers because the distances of them to their own cluster centroids were larger than the 75th quantile of distances within their own clusters (Yang et al., 2019). In this study, land grid cells except outliers were partitioned into nine clusters.

To verify the shift of spatial coherence of tropical temperature IAV, we also calculated the correlation coefficients between CGR IAV at Mauna Loa and local temperature IAV at each land grid cell in the tropics. Widespread decrease in the correlation coefficients would imply the decline in spatial coherence of tropical temperature IAV.

### 2.8 Contributions of land use change to changes in the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$

We included two datasets in our study to estimate the influence of land use change on changes in the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$. The first dataset is the global forest change data derived from Landsat time-series imagery (Hansen et al., 2013). This dataset provides the year of gross forest cover loss event at a spatial resolution of 1 arc-second per pixel during the period 2000–2021, so that we could illustrate the continuous increase in forest cover loss. For display purposes, we remapped the results to a 0.5° geographic grid.

To estimate the influence of land use change more directly, we used an improved version of ORCHIDEE DGVM to calculate the carbon emissions due to land use change (Yue et al., 2020). This model has advantages in calculating carbon fluxes over managed land and intact land separately at a sub-grid scale and has been validated with satellite observations, chronosequence observations, and regional forest inventory data. Moreover, a previous study using this model has demonstrated the important role of land use change in the temperature sensitivity of global NEE IAV (Yue et al., 2020). In this study, we used this model to calculate the correlation coefficients between NEE IAV over lands undergoing land use change and that over intact lands within 20- and 25-years moving windows.

## 3 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

### 3.1 Changes in $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ over the past six decades

First, using anomalies of CGR, tropical annual temperature, precipitation, and cloud cover without linear trend, we examined the changes in the sensitivity of CGR variability to tropical mean temperature $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ over the past six decades (see Section 2.5). Our results indicate that $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ has largely increased in the first three decades, and subsequently decreased in the recent three decades (Figure 1a). Specifically, $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ has more than doubled from 1959–1978 to 1979–1998, while during 1999–2019, $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ decreases and gets close to that during 1959–1978 (Figure 1b). Additional analyses using other regression models which include the effects of shortwave radiation, water, and interactions of temperature and water confirm the long-term changes in $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$, especially the recent $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ decline (Figures S1 and S2). Moreover, our results of $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ changes calculated with different time window sizes are consistent (Figure S2). The recent decline in $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ could explain the concurrent decrease in the magnitude of CGR variability (Luo & Keenan, 2022) as the water control on CGR variability is suggested to increase (Liu et al., 2023). It should be noted that $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ was calculated by the multiple linear regression to remove the effects of other climate variables (see Section 2.5). Although the tropical temperature sensitivity of CGR variability estimated by simple linear regression (*d* CGR/*d* MAT) also increases before the 1990s and decreases in the last two decades, the magnitude of recent decline in dCGR/dMAT is smaller than $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ (Figure 1a). This may result from the covariations between tropical temperature and precipitation (Gloor et al., 2018) and the increase in tropical vegetation sensitivity to water availability (Li et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022), which compensates the decrease in $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$.

We then investigated the mechanisms of $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ changes, which are mainly driven by the shifts in the apparent sensitivity of tropical NEE to anomalies of tropical mean temperature ($Γ_{NEE}^{T}$) (Piao et al., 2020; Rödenbeck et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2014). We found that 10 state-of-the-art models could reproduce both the increase in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ before the 2000s and the recent decrease in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ (Figure 1c). This gives us confidence to quantify the contributions of the potential factors in the long-term changes of $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ using outputs of these models. Specifically, we mainly considered three factors affecting $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ change: change in local temperature sensitivity $γ_{NEE}^{T}$, change in local temperature variations, and change in spatial covariations of tropical land temperature (see Section 2.6). Four scenarios (T0–T3) were designed (Table 1) and the effects of changes in $γ_{NEE}^{T}$, local temperature variability, and spatial covariations of tropical land temperature could be quantified by the differences between $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ in T0 and $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ in each of other three scenarios, respectively (see Section 2.6).

### 3.2 Effects of local temperature sensitivity on $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ changes

We found that the enhancement of $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ from 1959–1978 to 1979–1998 mainly resulted from changes in $γ_{NEE}^{T}$ (Figure 2a), which were widespread and mostly concentrated in eastern South America, semi-arid regions in Africa, and northern Australia (Figure 3a). Moreover, we noted that in regions with increased $γ_{NEE}^{T}$, soil moisture has decreased during the second half of the 20th century (Dai, 2011; Wang et al., 2014). This finding supports the mechanism that soil moisture modulates the response of tropical land carbon cycle to temperature fluctuations (Wang et al., 2014), and also reflects the development of DGVMs in simulating carbon cycle responses to climate variations during the recent decade (Delire et al., 2020; Friedlingstein et al., 2022). In addition, the change of local temperature variability and that of spatial covariations in tropical land temperature contribute negatively to the increase in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ (Figure 2a).

However, during the recent decades, changes of $γ_{NEE}^{T}$ play minor roles in the decline of $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ and $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$. Instead, changes in local temperature fluctuations and more specifically, changes in spatial covariations of temperature drive the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ and $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ declines (Figure 2b; Figures S3–S5). The influence of spatial covariations in temperature is significantly larger than that of $γ_{NEE}^{T}$ in the decrease in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$. To test the robustness of the critical role of spatial covariations of temperature in the decrease in $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$, we also included two long-term atmospheric inversions in our analyses of $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ and $γ_{NEE}^{T}$ (see Section 2.4). The two atmospheric inversions could both reproduce the observed decline of $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ during the recent decades, but show divergent change in $γ_{NEE}^{T}$ (Figure S6). This supports that the recent declines of $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ and $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ are hardly related to changes of $γ_{NEE}^{T}$.

### 3.3 Effects of spatial covariations in tropical land temperature and land use change on $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ changes

We next illustrated the recent shift of spatial covariations in tropical land temperature using cluster analysis (see Section 2.7). For the period of 1979–1998, interannual temperature variations over most tropical land regions are strongly correlated (Figure 4a), thus facilitating the high spatial coherence in interannual NEE variability. However, tropical land temperature variations separate into two different clusters for the period of 1999–2019 (Figure 4b), implying a decrease in spatial coherence in temperature variability and thus NEE variability. Moreover, additional analysis indicates that compared to the period of 1979–1998, the correlation between local temperature variability in most tropical land regions and CGR IAV is much lower for the period of 1999–2019 (Figure S7). This finding verifies that annual temperature variations in tropical land regions have become less spatially consistent during the recent two decades. Furthermore, we explored the possible causes of the decrease in spatial coherence in tropical land temperature fluctuations. Previous studies have found that a high spatial coherence in tropical land temperature fluctuations is related to the warm phases of Pacific Decadal Oscillation (Mantua et al., 1997; Yang et al., 2019). For the period when the spatial coherence in tropical land temperature fluctuations decreases, we indeed found cold phases of Pacific Decadal Oscillation contemporarily (see Text S1; Figure S8).

The decrease in spatial coherence in tropical land temperature variability could cause more spatial compensation of both temperature fluctuations and NEE fluctuations. Theoretically, more spatial compensation of temperature fluctuations would increase the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ while more spatial compensation of NEE fluctuations would decrease the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ (Yang et al., 2019) (see Section 2.6). The net effect of the decrease in spatial coherence in tropical land temperature variability on the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ is thus uncertain. Our analyses demonstrate that the effect of decreasing spatial coherence in tropical land temperature fluctuations on spatial compensation of NEE fluctuations overrides that on spatial compensation of temperature fluctuations (Figure 2b), explaining the recent declines in the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ and $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$.

Furthermore, we found that the declines in the $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ and $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ could also be contributed by land use change, such as forest fragmentation (Taubert et al., 2018). For example, within intact forest, NEE variabilities are highly correlated due to similar climate fluctuations. However, after forest loss, the responses of NEE to climate fluctuations over lands with forest loss would largely change, causing more spatial compensation of NEE IAV. Consequently, climate sensitivity of NEE tends to decrease in ecosystems with forest loss. Indeed, when we analyzed the remote sensing-based forest change data (Hansen et al., 2013), the results show that forest loss has progressively increased when the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ decreases (Figure 5a). Furthermore, we calculated the correlation between NEE IAV over lands undergoing land use change (including with forest loss) and that over intact lands estimated by an improved biosphere model (Yue et al., 2020). We found a significant decline of the correlation since the 1990s (Figure 5b), implying that the spatial compensation of NEE IAV has been strengthened. Therefore, our results elucidate that land use change plays a critical role in the declines of $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ and $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$. The impacts of land use change could partly explain the differences between the magnitude of $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ change and the magnitude of $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ change (Figure 1b,c) because the DGVMs in this study are not forced by land use change. It should be noted that the state-of-the-art models have deficiencies in the representation of land use change (Arneth et al., 2017; Yue et al., 2020), which could cause some biases in simulating the magnitude of $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ change.

We also investigated the potential impacts of spatial shifts in temperature anomalies and in resulting NEE anomalies on the recent decline in the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ because local sensitivity of NEE to temperature anomalies varies among regions. First, we calculated the temporal changes in NEE variability and in local sensitivity of NEE to temperature anomalies across different regions (Figures S9 and S10). We found that the NEE variability has increased during the recent two decades in some parts of South America, the northern Australia, the India Peninsula, and the Indo-China Peninsula (Figure S9), indicating that these regions have been more important to the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ than during 1979–1998. Furthermore, the increased NEE variability in some parts of South America and the Indo-China Peninsula tends to increase the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$ because the temperature sensitivity of NEE is relatively high in these regions (Figure S10), although the increased NEE variability in the northern Australia and the India Peninsula tends to decrease the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$. Therefore, we suggest that the spatial shifts in temperature anomalies and in resulting NEE anomalies would have little impacts on the recent decline in the $Γ_{NEE}^{T}$.

## 4 SUMMARY

In summary, our results demonstrate that the recent $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ is hardly indicative of a weakened response of ecosystem carbon cycle to temperature fluctuations at local scale, and is instead caused by a decrease in spatial coherence of temperature variations and land use change. The dramatic impacts of spatial coherence in temperature variations illustrate the role of spatial compensation of NEE variations in driving the variability of global land carbon uptake (Jung et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019). In addition, our findings suggest that the recently reported loss of rainforest resilience (Boulton et al., 2022; Forzieri et al., 2022) could have minor influence on the temperature sensitivity of ecosystem carbon cycle, yet it is still unclear whether the temperature sensitivity would be stable if rainforest resilience continues to decrease and drought events get more frequent and intensive (IPCC, 2021; Piao et al., 2019). Critically, besides previously reported regulation of moisture conditions (Wang et al., 2014), our study emphasizes the impacts of land use change on $Γ_{CGR}^{T}$ changes. Therefore, given more variable climate and increasing extent and intensity of human activities in the future (IPCC, 2021), we highlight the importance of continuously monitoring the temperature sensitivity of CGR variability and improving representation of land use change in Earth system models to predict the ecosystem stability and carbon–climate feedback.
