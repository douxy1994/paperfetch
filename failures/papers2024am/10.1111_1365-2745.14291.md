# Spatiotemporal variation in the negative effect of neighbourhood crowding on stem growth

## Abstract

Neighbourhood interactions drive tree growth and forest ecosystem functioning. The strength of interactions depends on the climate. However, it remains unclear how plant–plant interactions are modified by environmental conditions operating both spatially and temporally, which is crucial for predicting forest dynamics under climate change and for effective forest management.
In this study, we used annual growth data for 4139 stems from 2010 to 2021 across 50 permanent forest plots located at six sites in eastern China. We quantified the effect of neighbourhood crowding on the annual basal area increment. We explored how interactions among neighbouring trees vary with water availability and temperature gradients in the spatial (across sites) and temporal (across time within sites) dimensions.
Our findings revealed a negative impact of neighbourhood crowding on stem basal area growth, which is size- and trait-dependent at some sites. The negative effects of light competition tended to be more intense at warmer sites, supporting the stress-gradient hypothesis (SGH) in a spatial dimension. However, the patterns of crowding effects along interannual climate anomalies are inconsistent across sites, making it difficult to predict crowding effects under the SGH framework in a temporal dimension.
*Synthesis*: Our study demonstrated that tree interactions depend on the climate context. The climate dependence of interactions may be inconsistent between the spatial and temporal dimensions. Light competition across sites supported the SGH in the spatial dimension but not in the temporal dimension. These results further highlight the complexity of biotic interactions and the need for caution when extrapolating findings from the spatial to the temporal dimension.

## 1 INTRODUCTION

Plant–plant interactions play pivotal roles in shaping community structure and dynamics. In forest communities, negative interactions in the form of competition with neighbouring trees are well known to limit tree growth (Kunstler et al., 2016; Rozendaal et al., 2020). In addition to competition, recent studies have focused on the positive effects of facilitation and resource partitioning, which have been demonstrated to enhance tree growth by mitigating biotic or abiotic stress under certain conditions (Fichtner et al., 2017; Kothari et al., 2021). Overall, combinations of positive and negative interactions can result in different net effects, which vary with the environmental background and ecophysiology of the species involved (Montgomery et al., 2010; Soliveres et al., 2015).

Competitive interactions for resources, such as light, water and nutrients, shape tree performance, particularly in forests with dense canopies (Forrester, 2019). These interactions are mediated by various factors, including tree size and functional traits (Canham et al., 2006; Lasky et al., 2015; Zhang, Gheyret, et al., 2024), such as wood density, which is expected to be related to wood construction and shade tolerance (Poorter et al., 2010; Wright et al., 2010). Smaller trees may be more vulnerable to crowding due to size-asymmetric competition. For instance, light competition tends to be size asymmetric, as large trees can intercept light out of proportion to their size (Schwinning & Weiner, 1998). In addition, shade-tolerant species may be less sensitive to or even benefit from neighbourhood crowding (Kothari et al., 2021; Kunstler et al., 2011) because they can make better use of light in low-light environments. Trees with high wood density tend to grow relatively slowly but can be more tolerant to light competition with neighbouring trees (Kunstler et al., 2016).

Neighbourhood interactions are also environment-dependent (Chamberlain et al., 2014). For example, the stress-gradient hypothesis (SGH) proposes that species interactions tend to be more competitive in favourable environments but more facilitative in harsh environments, resulting in a shift in net interactions from negative to positive along stress gradients (Bertness & Callaway, 1994). The SGH has been supported by many studies (Callaway et al., 2002; He et al., 2013) that are based largely on removal experiments of pairwise species interactions in herbs, shrubs and saplings (He & Bertness, 2014; Soliveres et al., 2015). However, these short-term experiments provide limited insight into long-lived multispecies forests. Some studies have shown more intense competition effects on tree growth with increased crowding when resources are more limiting (Gomez-Aparicio et al., 2011; Lu et al., 2019; Sanchez-Salguero et al., 2015), while others have reported opposite results (Carnwath & Nelson, 2016; Muscarella et al., 2018). Thus, there is a lack of clarity regarding how neighbourhood crowding affects tree growth across climate gradients.

The lack of clear understanding of the mechanisms underlying context-specific tree interactions (Coates et al., 2013) hinders our ability to predict how neighbourhood interactions affect the response of forests to ongoing global climate change. Considering two factors may help clarify these inconsistencies. First, plant interactions can respond differently to different stress types, such as resource (e.g. water) and physical (e.g. temperature) factors (He et al., 2013; Maestre et al., 2009). Second, previous studies have not strictly distinguished between environmental gradients along the spatial (i.e. different sites) (Gomez-Aparicio et al., 2011; Muscarella et al., 2018) and temporal (i.e. repeated measurements at the same site) (Carnwath & Nelson, 2016) dimensions. The spatial and temporal environmental dependencies of plant interactions may differ due to different mechanisms (Metz & Tielborger, 2016). Along the spatial dimension, species composition and interactions at different sites would adapt to local environments after long-term community assembly processes such as environmental filtering and competitive exclusion (Kraft et al., 2015), as well as co-evolution of interacting species (Thorpe et al., 2011). However, the responses of plant interactions to interannual climate fluctuations or extreme weather events reflect transient dynamics that deviate from average conditions. In this sense, short- and long-term responses to climate change may differ (Sandel et al., 2010), leading to differences in the climate dependence of plant interactions in temporal and spatial dimensions (Metz & Tielborger, 2016).

Detecting how the effects of neighbourhood crowding on tree growth vary along climate gradients is important for terrestrial carbon balance and forest management. In this study, we used 8–11 years of annual tree growth data from 50 permanent plots located in six mountainous regions in eastern China to explore the effects of neighbourhood crowding on the growth of individual trees. We hypothesized that neighbourhood crowding decreases tree basal area growth (H1) and that smaller trees and shade-intolerant species with lower wood density would be more sensitive to crowding (H2). Additionally, we examined the crowding effects on tree growth along climatic gradients on both the spatial and temporal dimensions. We have three different scenarios based on the predictions of the SGH (Figure 1, H3): the first scenario supports the SGH only on the spatial dimension, in which crowding has a stronger negative effect on tree growth at sites with less stressful climates (H3a); the second scenario supports the SGH only on the temporal dimension, in which crowding has a stronger negative effect on tree growth in years under less stressful climates (H3b); and the third scenario supports the SGH on both the spatial and temporal dimensions (H3c).

## 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

### 2.1 Forest monitoring data

The growth data were collected from 50 permanent forest plots located in six mountains (sites) in eastern China (Figure 2, Table S1). These forests are mature, unmanaged and less affected by human activities. These plots, mostly 900 or 1000 square metres in size, cover latitudes from 23.5° N to 42.4° N, altitudes from 251 to 3090 m, mean annual temperatures (MATs) from 3.4 to 19.6°C and annual precipitation (AP) from 688.4 to 1945.8 mm. The forest types vary from boreal in the north to evergreen broad-leaved forests in the south. Within each plot, all stems with a diameter ≥5 cm at 1.3 m above the ground (DBH) were identified to the species level and mapped. We installed field-made steel dendrometer bands at ~1.3 m in height, approximately 30–50 cm above the top of any significant damage (due to fluting, deformation, or disease) on these trees between 2010 and 2013 (Chi et al., 2015). The earliest plots were established in 2010 on the Gutian Mountain (GTS), and the latest plots were established in 2013 on the Taibai Mountain (TBS; Table S1). We did not need permission for fieldwork and the changes in the windows of the dendrometer bands, which correspond to changes in tree circumference, were measured with digital callipers (precision of ±0.01 mm) in April and October of each year from the year of plot establishment to 2021. A total of 9211 stems were monitored, and the time series of tree growth ranged from 8 to 11 years (Table S1). The annual basal area growth rate for each stem was calculated as the difference in basal area between two consecutive Octobers.

### 2.2 Neighbourhood crowding index

Neighbourhood crowding is a proxy for neighbourhood competition, and tree competition for light and other resources is likely to become more intense with increased crowding. In this study, the neighbourhood crowding index (NCI) was calculated as the total basal area of neighbouring trees around a focal tree (Kunstler et al., 2016; Rozendaal et al., 2020). Given the size constraints of most plots, we did not define the neighbourhood scale as a certain radius (e.g. 7.5 m) around the focal tree. Instead, we calculated the NCI per stem following Coomes and Allen (2007). We initially divided these plots into subplots (5 × 5 m) every five metres. The NCI was calculated for each focal stem by summing the basal area of all neighbouring stems with a DBH ≥5 cm within a 15 × 15 m area centred upon the 5 × 5 m subplot containing the focal tree. Trees within a five-metre distance from the boundary of plots were not included as focal trees to avoid having incomplete neighbourhoods. We also calculated the NCI<sub>large</sub> using only neighbouring trees with a DBH higher than the focal tree as a proxy for light competition (Coomes & Allen, 2007).

### 2.3 Climate variables and trait data

We selected MAT and AP as climate variables because tree growth is limited by water and temperature (Cabon et al., 2020). We extracted monthly precipitation and monthly maximum and minimum temperatures from 1990 to 2021 based on the geographic coordinates of the plots from the WordClim dataset (Fick & Hijmans, 2017; Harris et al., 2020). The spatial resolution is 2.5 min. The mean monthly temperature was calculated as the average of the monthly maximum and minimum temperatures. The annual temperature and precipitation were calculated by averaging and summing, respectively, the monthly data from November to October corresponding to the period of the tree growth census. The 30-year historical mean and standard deviation were calculated over the 1991–2020 period as a long-term baseline of climate conditions in the spatial dimension. Interannual climate anomalies for each plot were calculated as the degree of deviation from baseline climate conditions (Bauman et al., 2022) and were treated as climate gradients in the temporal dimension.

**Equation 1.**

$$
X_{anomaly_{p, y}} = (X_{p, y} - μ_{p}) / σ_{p}
$$

$X_{p, y}$ is the MAT or AP of plot p in year y, and μp and σp are the 30-year mean and standard deviation of the corresponding plot, respectively.

Wood density at the species level was measured in situ in the summer of 2014. We collected stem cores from at least three mature and healthy individuals of each species. The volume of each core was measured by the drainage method, and the dry weight was obtained by drying the samples at 65°C. Wood density was calculated as the ratio of dry weight to volume and ranged from 0.32 to 0.97 g/cm<sup>3</sup> for the investigated species.

### 2.4 Statistical analysis

The distribution of basal area growth rate data is highly left-skewed and includes negative values. On the one hand, there are reversible changes in stem radius due to water balance (Hilty et al., 2021). On the other hand, there may be random errors during the measurement. Negative growth is allowed in the analysis because excluding it would bias growth rates upwards. We applied power transformation and maintained negatives as follows (Davis & Condit, 2022; Kenfack et al., 2014):

**Equation 2.**

$$
T = {\begin{matrix} g^{k}g \geq 0 \\ - [{(- g)}^{k}]g < 0 \end{matrix}
$$

where g is the annual basal area growth rate in cm2 and T is the data after transformation. We tested the skewness of T across a range of values of k between 0.3 and 0.5 following Kenfack et al. (2014) and found that the exponent 0.4 was most effective at reducing skewness (Figure S1).

A portion of the monitored trees died during the investigation, and the overall mortality rate was 16.4% (1511/9211). Trees may exhibit slower growth rates before succumbing to mortality (Coomes & Allen, 2007). We found that small trees had higher mortality than large trees (Figure S2b) and that the growth rate of trees before they died was lower than that of surviving trees (Figure S2a). We considered only the growth rate of the trees that survived throughout the survey period in the analysis. Finally, the basal area growth of 4139 stems belonging to 228 species was included as focal trees in the analysis.

To address these hypotheses, we used the linear mixed modelling framework fitted in the Bayesian framework separately for each site. The basal area and neighbourhood crowding indices (NCI and NCIlarge) of the focal tree were log-transformed in all analyses to achieve an approximately Gaussian distribution. To account for the dependency structure of the data, we included normally distributed random effects (ε) of the individuals (i), species (s) and plot–year combinations (p, y) in all the models. To address Hypothesis H1, we included the main effects of the variables basal area of the focal tree (BA), standardized climate anomalies of MAT (Tanomaly) and AP (Panomaly), wood density (WD) and (NCI or NCIlarge) in Model M1 as fixed effects.

**Equation 3.**

$$
\begin{matrix} T_{i, s, p, y} = β_{0} + β_{BA}ln(BA_{i, s, p, y}) + β_{T}T_{anomaly_{p, y}} + β_{P}P_{anomaly_{p, y}} \\ + β_{WD}WD_{s} + β_{NCI}ln(NCI_{i, s, p, y}) + ε_{i} + ε_{s} + ε_{p, y} \end{matrix}
$$

To address Hypothesis H2, we extended $β_{NCI}$ in M1 to include additional fixed effects and the interactions NCI × BA and NCI × WD in M2,

**Equation 4.**

$$
β_{NCI} = β_{1} + β_{2}ln(BA_{i, s, p, y}) + β_{3}WD_{s}
$$

To address Hypothesis H3a, we regressed the site-specific estimates of the NCI term (βNCI) of Model M1 against the 30-year mean climate conditions of each site, using the mean values of plots as the site measure. To explore the effect of crowding along temporal gradients (H3b), we extended $β_{NCI}$ in M1 to include additional fixed effects and the interactions NCI × Tanomaly and NCI × Panomaly in M3. We examined, separately for each site, the level of posterior support by which the estimates of these terms were negative, meaning that more benign years would show a more negative effect of crowding,

**Equation 5.**

$$
β_{NCI} = β_{1} + β_{2}T_{anomaly_{p, y}} + β_{3}P_{anomaly_{p, y}}
$$

These multivariate models were fitted with the R package *Hmsc* (Tikhonov et al., 2020). We sampled the posterior distribution with four Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) chains assuming the default prior distribution (Ovaskainen & Abrego, 2020). The chains were thinned by 100 to yield 250 posterior samples per chain and thus 1000 samples in total. We assessed MCMC convergence by examining the potential scale reduction factors (Gelman & Rubin, 1992) of the model parameters. The variance partitioning of explanatory variables in *Hmsc* is conducted at the level of the linear predictor (Ovaskainen & Abrego, 2020).

## 3 RESULTS

We fitted the effects of NCI and NCI<sub>large</sub> separately (Table S2), and the results of NCI are presented in the Supporting Information. The MCMC convergence of the models was satisfactory, as the potential scale reduction factors (psrf) of the model parameters were <<1.1. Examination of model residuals did not indicate strong deviations from the assumptions of the linear model (Figure S3), supporting the robustness of the results. Model M1 explained 68.7% to 82.7% of the variance in basal area growth across sites. Tree size was the most important factor, as the basal area of the focal tree explained 27% to 61% while NCI<sub>large</sub> explained 1.3% to 3.6% of the variance in basal area growth (Figure S4). The effect of NCI<sub>large</sub> was estimated to be significantly negative at five out of the six sites but non-significant at site CBS (Figure 3).

**Figure 3.** Posterior means and 95% credible intervals of parameter NCI <sub>large</sub> in model M1. Linear regression is performed between mean value and (a) mean annual temperature (°C) and (b) mean annual precipitation (mm) of sites. Line is solid when regression is significant (*p* < 0.05). Points are solid when 95% CIs do not overlap zero. Colours indicate different sites.

The size and trait dependence of the neighbourhood crowding effect varied between sites. The BA × NCI<sub>large</sub> interaction was significant at two sites (GGS and SNJ). At these sites, the effect of larger neighbouring trees had a more intense negative influence on large trees than on small trees. The WD × NCI<sub>large</sub> interaction was significant at two sites (negative for TBS and positive for HSD). At site HSD, trees with higher wood density were less sensitive to competition from larger neighbouring trees (Figure 4).

![Figure 4](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/cms/asset/59c6ee12-8607-41cb-b712-4aff2232bd46/jec14291-fig-0004-m.jpg)

**Figure 4.** Posterior means with 95% credible intervals of parameter (a) BA × NCI <sub>large</sub> and (b) WD × NCI <sub>large</sub> in model M2. Points are solid when 95% CIs do not overlap zero. Colours indicate different sites.

Combining the results from Models M1 and M3, we examined three scenarios of the SGH (H3). The effect of neighbourhood crowding of all neighbouring trees (NCI) had no clear trend along climate gradients in the spatial dimension (Figure S5). However, when considering only trees larger than the focal tree, the effect of neighbourhood crowding (NCI<sub>large</sub>) at different sites exhibited a significantly negative trend along the temperature gradient, indicating that competition from large trees was more intense at warmer sites. A negative trend was also observed along the precipitation gradient, but it was not statistically significant (Figure 3). Concerning the temporal SGH (H3b), the coefficient of T<sub>anomaly</sub> × NCI<sub>large</sub> of Model M3 provided statistical support for negative effects at four sites (CBS, GGS, SNJ and HSD) and for a positive effect at one site (GTS). The interaction terms T<sub>anomaly</sub> × NCI<sub>large</sub> at one site and P<sub>anomaly</sub> × NCI<sub>large</sub> at most sites, except for HSD, did not have statistical support (Figure 5). Hence, our results support the spatial (H3a) but not temporal (H3b) or spatial–temporal (H3c) versions of the SGH.

## 4 DISCUSSION

### 4.1 Neighbourhood crowding decreases stem growth

Consistent with our first hypothesis (H1), we found that neighbourhood crowding negatively impacts stem basal area growth. These findings align with previous studies demonstrating the detrimental effects of competition on tree growth (Coomes & Allen, 2007; Fien et al., 2019; Kunstler et al., 2016; Rozendaal et al., 2020). Competition for density-dependent resources such as water, light and nutrients significantly impacts tree performance (Craine & Dybzinski, 2013). In forest management, thinning treatments such as prescribed fires or mechanical thinning can effectively reduce competition and promote growth (Anning & McCarthy, 2013).

Tree size is the primary factor explaining the variation in tree growth, with basal area increments generally increasing with tree size (Figure S8). Large trees benefit from size-asymmetric competition because they occupy resources out of proportion to their size and/or have more leaf area available to support carbon assimilation (DeMalach et al., 2016; Guo et al., 2017; Stephenson et al., 2014). We also found that the effect of neighbourhood crowding on growth depends on tree size. However, inconsistent with our second hypothesis (H2), competition intensity for small trees is not greater than that for large trees. At some sites, larger trees are more sensitive to crowding, which is consistent with the findings of previous studies conducted in temperate forests (Anning & McCarthy, 2013; Ren et al., 2022). On the one hand, there may be a trade-off between light interception efficiency and light use efficiency (Onoda et al., 2014). Short trees tend to have high light use efficiency, compensating for low light interception. On the other hand, large trees are more limited by water and nutrient availability (Niinemets, 2010). For instance, canopy trees generally experience higher levels of radiation and vapour pressure deficit, resulting in greater evaporative demands than those of short trees (Bennett et al., 2015). The growth of large trees may be more limited by water competition as crowding increases.

Species with high wood density are expected to be more tolerant to shade (Wright et al., 2010) and neighbourhood competition (Kunstler et al., 2016). However, trees with high wood density were less sensitive to light competition than were those with high wood density only at site HSD. Previous studies have also shown that the varied effects of interactions between wood density and competition in different places (Rozendaal et al., 2020) also depend on the environmental and crowding conditions (Kunstler et al., 2011). The HSD site in the subtropical region contains the warmest and wettest forests, as well as the strongest light competition (indicated as the effect of NCI<sub>large</sub>) in our dataset. One possible explanation is that in forests with intense light competition, trees tend to invest in shade tolerance as an important co-existence strategy (Poorter et al., 2010). In contrast, in forests dominated by competition for other unmeasured resources, trees may adapt to competition via other ecological strategies.

### 4.2 The SGH holds true for climate gradients on the spatial dimension

We found evidence to support the SGH for climate gradients in the spatial dimension, but this depended on the competition index used. The effect of crowding of all neighbouring trees on growth showed no obvious trend along climate gradients. A global meta-analysis of pairwise plant interactions also revealed a weak dependence on climate (Yang et al., 2022). However, the crowding of larger neighbouring trees had a stronger negative effect on basal area increment in warmer sites. As the MAT decreased, the negative effects of the same level of crowding on tree growth diminished. Biotic interactions are believed to be important at low latitudes, and abiotic factors are believed to be major limiting factors at high latitudes (Schemske et al., 2009). The effects of NCI<sub>large</sub> indicate that trees are more devoted to light competition in warm forests. Forest structure across sites may lead to a shift in light competition. Tropical forests have greater species diversity and more complex vertical structures than temperate forests (Spicer et al., 2020). The vertical stratification and crown plasticity allow mixtures to optimize canopy space filling, leading to more dense crowns (Jucker et al., 2015). In addition, conifers dominate cold forests in which tree crowns at high latitudes are narrowly conical in profile but tend to be planar or shallowly dome-shaped at tropical latitudes (Terborgh, 1985). Consequently, light availability within tropical forests may decline more rapidly than within temperate forests, leading to greater light competition from large neighbouring trees at warm sites.

The climate dependence of neighbourhood competition on the temporal dimension was not consistent at the different sites. At most sites, light competition from large neighbouring trees was more intense in warm years. One possible explanation is that warming causes earlier spring leaf unfolding (Fu et al., 2015), which may lead to earlier competition for light. However, interannual temperate anomalies also had non-significant or positive effects on neighbourhood competition at other sites. This may be due to the relatively small range of interannual fluctuations, as interannual climate anomalies were not found to be a ‘stress’ for basal area growth in our data (Figure S9), which makes it difficult to determine the significant interactions between interannual climate anomalies and competition in the framework of the SGH. Forest structure is an important factor affecting tree interactions in the spatial dimension (Jucker et al., 2016), and its interannual variation is low, which may result in inconsistent patterns between the spatial and temporal dimensions. Temporal variation in plant interactions is also influenced by other factors, such as soil and site productivity, making it difficult for any of the dominating theories to explain these interactions fully (Calama et al., 2019).

Although previous studies have indicated that the applicability of the SGH depends on how the examined stress gradient is created (He et al., 2013), investigations into the differences between the spatial and temporal dimensions have been limited. The differing outcomes observed between spatial and temporal dimensions were also found in shrub–annual interactions under the Mediterranean-type climate (Metz & Tielborger, 2016). The relationship between tree diversity and productivity in European forests also shows different dependencies on climate in temporal and spatial dimensions, supporting the SGH on the spatial dimension (Jucker et al., 2016). The mechanism and results of climate modulation on plant interactions may be inconsistent in the space and time dimensions. Predicting changes in plant interactions under future climate scenarios is crucial for assessing the response of ecosystems to climate change (Blois et al., 2013). Space-for-time approaches are used to predict plant interactions based on predictions of the SGH (Brooker, 2006). However, the contradictory conclusions between spatial patterns and temporal dynamics of plant interactions suggest that spatial patterns may be poor proxies for temporal dynamics.

## 5 CONCLUSIONS

In this study, we investigated the impact of neighbourhood interactions, represented by crowding, on basal area growth and further explored the spatial and temporal variation in tree interactions along climate gradients. We found that neighbourhood crowding decreased basal area growth, with the strength of the effect depending on the size and wood density of the focal tree at some sites. Specifically, the neighbourhood competition intensity for large trees was greater in two sites. Trees with high wood density tend to be more tolerant to light competition at sites with the strongest light competition. Furthermore, the spatial patterns of light competition along climate gradients supported the SGH. However, site-specific outcomes of interannual climate variances were inconsistent across sites in the temporal dimension. These contradictory conclusions indicate that different mechanisms underlie the climatic dependence of plant interactions between the spatial and temporal dimensions. The inconsistent patterns of interactions between spatial patterns and temporal dynamics pose a significant challenge in predicting how plant communities will respond to climate change. This highlights the complexity of biotic interactions and the need for caution when extrapolating findings from spatial to temporal dimensions in ecological studies.
