# Holocene paleoenvironment of the Nihewan Basin, China, inferred from high-resolution luminescence dating and a multiproxy analysis of gully sediments

## Abstract

As a pivotal region for studying early human occupation in East Asia, the Nihewan Basin has witnessed numerous studies on Paleolithic sites and associated paleoenvironmental records. However, little attention has been given to the Holocene period, despite the basin being a crossroads for the exchange of NE China Neolithic cultures. In the central part of the Nihewan Basin, we found a gully sediment section with a thickness of ∼9 m, which exhibits clear parallel bedding throughout. The section is composed of ten sediment layers (Layers 10–1 from bottom to top), from which three distinct dark-colored soil layers (Layers 8, 4 and 2) and two erosion surfaces (between Layers 10 and 9, and Layers 2 and 1) were identified. Forty-four sediment samples from the section were optically dated, and the obtained ages were refined using Bayesian statistical modeling. The age-depth relationship and sedimentary characteristics suggest that the sedimentation process of Layers 9–2 was continuous. To reconstruct the paleoenvironment, the grain-size distribution, magnetic susceptibility, and pollen content of the sediments were analyzed. The three soil layers exhibit high magnetic susceptibility values. Based on these climatic proxies, five climate stages were recognized. From ∼12.7 to ∼6.8 ka, the climate in the basin region was cold and dry. The period between ∼6.8 and ∼5.4 ka was marked by optimal climate conditions that led to soil formation. Subsequently, the climate was colder and drier during the period of ∼5.4 – ∼4.0 ka, and transitioned to warm and wet conditions again from ∼4.0 to ∼2.7 ka. Another stage of soil formation occurred between ∼2.7 and ∼1.7 ka, during which the climate was predominantly warm and humid, albeit punctuated by a brief interval of colder and drier conditions. These climate variations coincided with cultural evolution stages within the basin, highlighting a close relationship between environmental change and human adaptation.

## Introduction

Since the early discovery of the Paleolithic site of Shiyu (Jia et al., 1972) in 1963 and some other sites around the Hutouliang Village in 1965 (Xie et al., 2006), an increasing number of Paleolithic sites have been discovered and excavated in the Nihewan Basin, spanning from the early Pleistocene (e.g., the Majuangou, Xiaochangliang and Dongtutuo sites, Deng et al., 2006; Xie et al., 2006; Zhu et al., 2004), the middle Pleistocene (e.g., the Motianling and Queergou sites, Guo et al., 2016; Zuo et al., 2011) to the late Pleistocene (e.g., the Xujiayao-Houjiayao, Banjingzi, Xiamabei and Youfang sites, Ao et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2016; Nian et al., 2014; Wang et al., 2022). A series of such Paleolithic sites make the basin a crucial area for studying the origin and evolution of early humans in East Asia (Deng et al., 2006; Gao et al., 2005; Zhu et al., 2001), as well as the development of Paleolithic industries including the blade and microblade technology in NE China (Feng, 2020; Guan et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2023).

The Nihewan paleolake formed during a period of intense basin subsidence, primarily from the late Pliocene to the early Pleistocene (Chen et al., 2015a; Deng et al., 2008; Liu et al., 2018a; Yuan et al., 2009). Vegetation in the basin during the late Pliocene was predominately forest, and climate was warm and wet (Li et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2022). In the early Pleistocene, the vegetation was still dominated by forest, with rare steppe, and the climate was slightly drier and colder than in the late Pliocene (Ding et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2022). The middle Pleistocene experienced moderate climate fluctuations between cold and warm, wet and semi-arid conditions (Yang et al., 2020). The vegetation during this period was mainly composed of steppe and forest-steppe, with occasional conifer forest (Da et al., 2023; Mu et al., 2015; Zhu et al., 2022, 2021). In the late Pleistocene, the climate fluctuated more frequently towards colder and drier conditions, leading to a transition in vegetation from forest-steppe to desert steppe (Wang et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2022). As the Holocene began, climatic proxies from the archaeological sites of Yujiagou (Rui et al., 2019; Xia et al., 2001) and Jijitan (Lu et al., 2018) suggest a shift in regional vegetation from temperate grassland to wooded grassland from 13 to 6 ka, aligning with the intensification of the East Asian Summer Monsoon (EASM, Chen et al., 2015b; Kang et al., 2020). After ∼6 ka, it is still in debate whether the climate deteriorated (Lu et al., 2018), or remained warm and wet (Rui et al., 2019; Xia et al., 2001). In essence, the absence of suitable sediment sections and climatic proxies with high-resolution chronology for climate reconstructions limits our understanding of the paleoenvironmental changes and their influence on human activities in the Nihewan Basin during the Holocene, a critical period that witnessed the evolution from the Paleolithic to the Neolithic (Mei, 2007; Rui et al., 2019).

Here, we found remnants of gully deposits with parallel bedding in the central part of the Nihewan Basin. These deposits consist of yellowish-brown sandy silt interbedded with dark brown sandy silt (soil layers). Several detailed investigations including the analyses of magnetic susceptibility, grain-size and pollen content, and high-resolution optically stimulated luminescence (OSL) dating were conducted to reveal paleoenvironmental changes in the basin. By integrating current archaeological findings, we seek to uncover a potential link between cultural evolution and climatic conditions.

## Study area

### Geomorphological settings

The Nihewan Basin, situated at the transition zone between the North China Plain and the Inner Mongolian Plateau (Fig. 1a), has a dual definition, a broader comprising three sub-basins — Datong, Yangyuan and Yuxian, and a narrower encompassing only the entire Yangyuan basin and the northern part of Yuxian basin. In this study, ‘Nihewan Basin’ is used in its broader definition.

The Nihewan Basin is a fault basin within the Shanxi Graben System, and has formed since the late Pliocene uplift of the Himalayas (Chen et al., 2015a; Liu et al., 2018a; Yuan et al., 2009). The Sanggan River flows through the basin from west to east. During the Pliocene, the drought climate in Central Asia intensified and red clay was widely accumulated in the Nihewan Basin (Ao et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2018b). The Nihewan paleolake developed over the Pleistocene, deposited hundreds to thousands of meters of grayish-green fluvial-lacustrine sediments known as the Nihewan Formation (Deng et al., 2008; Yuan et al., 2009). After the demise of the paleolake in the late Pleistocene, the Nihewan Formation was exposed and was incised by the Sanggan River and its tributaries, as well as pulses of surface runoff (Pang et al., 2015; Yuan et al., 2009). The basin margins were blanketed by the alluvial fan deposits from surrounding mountains, and these deposits were covered by a thin layer of loess (Yuan et al., 2009). The erosion caused by fluvial process led to the formation of dendritic gullies along the banks of the Sanggan River in the basin, and has exposed abundant Paleolithic sites. Among them, most of the early Pleistocene Paleolithic sites such as the Xiaochangliang and Majuangou (Xie et al., 2006; Zhu et al., 2004, 2001) were discovered around the Datianwa platform in the east part of the basin, where was once a lakeside area (Pang et al., 2015). In the late Pleistocene, people turned to settle alongside the river, such as the Xiamabei (Wang et al., 2022) and Xibaimaying sites (Guo et al., 2017; Lei et al., 2022). This demonstrates a strong correlation between human settlement patterns and the geomorphic characteristics of the basin.

### The study section

Numerous gullies along the banks of the Sanggan River have formed due to headward erosion. The distribution of these gullies and their deposits reflect multiple stages of incision and accumulation. Many Paleolithic sites such as the Xibaimaying (Lei et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2017) and Xiamabei sites (Wang et al., 2022) were found within the gully sediments. The Laodonggou gully, situated on the northern bank of Sanggan River in the central part of the Nihewan Basin and 7 km east of Yangyuan City (Fig. 1a), is one of such gullies. The gully, extending approximately 5 km in length and 100 m in width with a depth of around 15 m, stretches roughly from north to south and joins the Sanggan River (Fig. S1a). It formed within the Nihewan Formation characterized by grayish-green silty clay through headcut retreat. Within the gully, remnants of earlier gully deposits, varying in color from dark brown to light yellowish-brown, are clearly distinguishable from the surrounding Nihewan Formation (Fig. 1b). One of these remnants was sampled for luminescence dating and paleoenvironmental investigation. The sampling section (40°5'34.11" N, 114°13'42.96" E, 876 m above sea level), called Laodonggou (LDG), is located in the central part of the gully (Fig. 1b). The section is divided into ten sediment layers according to their sedimentary characteristics (Fig. 2):

Layer 10 (874–812 cm in depth) is composed of grayish-green sandy silt with a massive structure, and its color is similar to the lacustrine silty clay of the Nihewan Formation. A sharp contact with the overlying Layer 9 is irregular, indicating an erosional surface.

Layer 9 (812–550 cm) is light yellowish-brown sandy silt with a massive structure. Some grayish-green clay pellets were found in the lowermost 50 cm part of the layer, and these pellets were deduced from the underlying Layer 10.

Layer 8 (550–375 cm) consists of buried soil, and is characterized by a dark brown color and a massive structure. The contact between Layers 8 and 9 is diffuse and gradational.

Layer 7 (375–259 cm) mainly comprises light grayish-yellow sandy silt with parallel bedding and a 1-cm thick sand layer at the bottom. The contact between Layer 7 and Layer 8 is sharp and represents a parallel unconformity, and marks an erosional surface.

Layer 6 (259–200 cm) mainly consists of grayish-yellow sandy silt with parallel bedding and a 0.5-cm thick sand layer at the bottom. The contact between Layer 6 and Layer 7 is continuous.

Layer 5 (200–152 cm) is dark yellowish-brown sandy silt with parallel bedding.

Layer 4 (152–126 cm) is composed of buried soil, and the sandy silts are characterized by a dark brown color and a massive structure. The contacts between Layers 4 and 5 or 3 are diffuse and gradational.

Layer 3 (126–86 cm) consists of yellowish-brown sandy silt with parallel bedding.

Layer 2 (86–55 cm) comprises buried soil, and the sandy silt is characterized by a dark brown color and a massive structure. The contact with the underlying layer is gradual, and that with overlying layer is erosional.

Layer 1 (55–0 cm) is composed of a 20-cm thick sand layer with parallel bedding at the lower part, the upper 35-cm thick part is yellowish-brown topsoil.

## Material and methods

### Field sampling

Due to the steepness of the LDG section, it was divided into three sampling segments: LDG-A, LDG-B, and LDG-C (Fig. S1b–f). Forty-four samples for luminescence dating were collected, numbered LDG-OSL-A-1–19 from Segment LDG-A, LDG-OSL-B-1–13 from Segment LDG-B and LDG-OSL-C-1–12 from Segment LDG-C. Note that Layers 7 and 9 were sampled from two segments in order to assure high-resolution sampling. The sampling strategy aimed to collect samples from all the layers, and to construct a high-resolution chronology for the section and to provide information about stratigraphic integrity. For luminescence dating, stainless steel tubes (25 cm in length and 3 cm in diameter) were hammered horizontally into the sections, from which at least 10 cm of surface sediments was removed. The sampling interval of ∼15 cm was used for the uppermost 2 m of LDG-A, where the lithology changes rapidly, and the interval of 25 cm for other parts. Samples for magnetic susceptibility and grain-size analyses were taken continuously, with intervals varying according to lithology. Specifically, samples were taken at 5 cm intervals for Layers 1 and 10, and at 2 or 3 cm intervals for Layers 2–9. For pollen analysis, samples were selected separately to ensure a consistent interval of 20 cm within each layer.

### Luminescence dating

#### Sample preparation and luminescence measurement

Sample preparation was carried out under subdued yellow light (∼590 nm, Sohbati et al., 2017), which provides the same visibility at 1/3 intensity compared to red LEDs at ∼630 nm. The outer sediments (at least 3 cm thick) at both ends of the sample tubes were scrapped off for dose rate estimation. The remaining sediments were treated with 10 % HCl and 30 % H2O2 to remove carbonates and organic matter, respectively. Fine-grained quartz (4–11 μm) was extracted following Stokes' law of settling and etched with 35 % H2SiF6 for seven days to dissolve feldspar contaminants, and then rinsed in 10 % HCl to remove precipitated fluorides. The purity of the quartz extracts was checked by OSL-IR depletion ratios (Duller, 2003). Finally, the extracted grains were settled on stainless steel discs (9.7 mm in diameter) for luminescence measurements.

OSL measurements were performed on a Risø TL/OSL-DA-20 reader equipped with a calibrated<sup>90</sup> Sr/<sup>90</sup> Y beta source, and OSL signal was detected by a 9235QB15 photo-multiplier through a Hoya U-340 filter for quartz OSL measurements. The single-aliquot regenerative-dose (SAR) protocol (Table S1, Murray and Wintle, 2000; Wintle and Murray, 2006) was employed to measure the equivalent dose (*D*<sub>e</sub>) of the samples. Optical stimulation was carried out using blue LEDs (470 nm, ∼80 mW cm<sup>2</sup>) at 125 °C for 40 s. The intensity of OSL signals was calculated by subtracting the integral of the last 3.2 s of photocounts from the initial 0.64 s.

Before *D*<sub>e</sub> measurements, a suitable preheat temperature was determined based on preheat plateau and dose recovery tests. The preheat plateau tests were carried out on three samples from each of the three sampling segments of the section, and three aliquots for each temperature were measured using the full SAR protocol (Table S1), with preheat temperatures ranging from 180 to 280 °C in increments of 20 °C. The dose recovery tests were performed on eight samples at different depths using the same preheat temperatures as the preheat plateau tests, and three aliquots for each sample were tested for each temperature. In the dose recovery tests, the aliquots were first bleached with the blue LEDs within the reader at room temperature for 100 s. The residual OSL signals were examined by a 40 s OSL measurement at least 10,000 s after bleaching. The aliquots were then irradiated with a known dose close to their natural doses, and were measured using the same procedure as the preheat plateau tests. Finally, we chose a preheat temperature of 240 °C for our *D*<sub>e</sub> measurements according to the results of preheat plateau and dose recovery tests (see Section 4.1.2).

To save machine time, a standardized growth curve (SGC) was established for all samples, following the procedure of Li et al. (2016) and employing a single saturating exponential function below:

(1)

$$
I = I_{0}∗(1 - exp(- \frac{x}{D_{0}})) + y_{0}
$$

where *I* is a measured *L*<sub>x</sub> */T*
*x*<sub>ratio</sub>, *I*<sub>0</sub> is the saturated value of a dose-response curve, *D*<sub>0</sub> denotes a characteristic dose that describes the shape of the curve, and *y*<sub>0</sub> is a constant value.

First, nineteen samples at different depths were selected for full SAR procedure measurements (Table S1), and five to seven aliquots of each sample were measured. Then the resulting sensitivity-corrected signals (*L*<sub>x</sub> */T*
*x*) are ‘Ls-normalized’ and fitted using the function lsNORM() in ***R*** package ‘numOSL’ (Peng et al., 2013; Peng and Li, 2017), yielding a refined SGC. To estimate single-aliquot *D*<sub>e</sub> values using the SGC, ten aliquots per sample (440 aliquots in total) were measured for the natural (*L*<sub>n</sub> */T*
*n*) and one regeneration dose signal (*L*<sub>r</sub> */T*
*r*). Afterwards, the *L*<sub>n</sub> */T*
*n*<sub>ratio</sub> is multiplied by a scaling factor *η*, which is related to *L*<sub>r</sub> */T*
*r*<sub>and</sub> calculated using the following equation:

(2)

$$
η\frac{L_{r}}{T_{r}} = f(D_{r})
$$

where $f(D_{r})$ is the function for SGC, and $D_{r}$ is the regeneration dose. Finally, the scaled natural signal ($η\frac{L_{n}}{T_{n}}$) is projected onto the SGC to calculate the *D*<sub>e</sub> value for a measured aliquot. Note that the above calculations could also be implemented automatically in ***R*** package ‘numOSL’, using the function calSGCED() (Peng and Li, 2017). The *D*<sub>e</sub> values calculated from the SGC and the SAR protocol for each sample were combined using the central age model (CAM, Galbraith et al., 1999).

#### Dose rate and age estimation

The concentrations of U and Th of the sediment samples were measured using Nexion 300D inductively coupled plasma spectrometer (ICP-MS), and K contents were analyzed using HITACHI Z-2000 atomic absorption spectrophotometer (AAS). The dose rate contributed by cosmic ray was calculated as a function of longitude, latitude, altitude, as well as burial depth for each sample (Prescott and Hutton, 1994). Given that the LDG section had been exposed to air for long time, in-situ water content was not measured. Instead, we adopted the in-situ water content of 12 ± 6 % for the sediment samples from a borehole at the Xibaimaying site (Lei et al., 2022) near the LDG gully. The alpha efficiency factor (*a*-value) of 0.038 ± 0.003 was used (Rees-Jones, 1995). Finally, the total dose rates and OSL ages were calculated and summarized using DRAC v1.2 program (Durcan et al., 2015), in which the cosmic ray contribution, the dose-rate conversion factors (Guérin et al., 2011), and the alpha (Brennan et al., 1991) and beta (Guérin et al., 2012) attenuation factors were all considered.

#### Bayesian age modeling

To refine the chronological framework and deal with the age outliers of the samples, all OSL ages were combined to construct a Bayesian age-depth model using OxCal V 4.4.4 (Ramsey, 2009a), taking stratigraphic information as priors and dating results as likelihoods. One of the key concepts of Bayesian age modeling is the Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm, which is achieved by iteratively sampling from prior probability distributions to converge on posterior distributions that represent both the refined ages and associated uncertainties (Jones and Qin, 2022).

In our model, the age for each sample was input as *N*(mean, sigma) to generate a normal distribution. The general outlier model was applied to assign outlier probabilities to these ages (Ramsey, 2009b). For some obviously overestimated and underestimated ages, a prior outlier probability of 95 % was assigned. Two depositional hiatuses were set at the erosional contacts occurring at depths of 812 cm and 55 cm. The *P*_Sequence model was employed as the deposition framework (Ramsey, 2008), which accounts for the intricate and unpredictable nature of the sedimentation process by treating it as a random (or Poisson) process, thereby yielding a more realistic age-depth model for our sediment section.

### Grain-size and magnetic susceptibility analyses

A total of 327 samples were analyzed to determine their grain-size distributions and magnetic susceptibility (MS). The samples for grain-size analysis were first treated with 30 % H2O2 to remove organic matter, and then 10 % HCl to remove carbonates. The sample solutions were then ultrasonicated, with 10 % (Na3PO3)6 solution as dispersant. The grain-size distributions of the sample solutions were measured using a Malvern Mastersizer 3000 grain-size analyzer (range 0.01–3500 μm), and the parametric end-member modeling was implemented using a MATLAB based software AnalySize v 1.2.2 (Paterson and Heslop, 2015). For MS determination, the sub-samples of about 7 g were first weighed and air-dried. The low-frequency MS (χlf) and high-frequency MS (χhf) were measured using Bartington MS2, and the frequency-dependent MS (χfd) was calculated.

### Pollen analysis

Pollen analysis was performed on forty-eight samples. The sub-samples of 30 g were treated with a modified HCl-NaOH-HF procedure (Faegri et al., 1989). Pollen grains and spores were extracted by wet-sieving and density separation. Identification and counting were conducted under an Olympus BX51 microscope, with species identification based on the reference atlas by Tang et al. (2016), and the results were analyzed using software Tilia v 3.0.1.

## Results

### OSL dating

#### Dose rates

The alpha, beta and gamma dose rates were determined by measuring the concentrations of U, Th and K (Table 1 and Fig. 3a). The U contents of the samples from Layers 9–1 are similar between 1.71 and 3.2 ppm, with an average of 2.18 ± 0.06 ppm, but the two samples from Layer 10 exhibited high contents of 5.9 ± 0.04 and 11.9 ± 0.13 ppm, respectively. The Th content ranges from 7.63 to 12.64 ppm, with an average of 9.54 ± 0.15 ppm, and the K content ranges from 1.44 to 1.89 % with an average of 1.68 ± 0.01 %. The calculated dose rates from Layers 9–1 are within the range of 2.76–3.72 Gy/ka, with an average of 3.65 ± 0.04 Gy/ka, and the two samples from Layers 10 have the dose rates of 4.25 ± 0.18 and 5.83 ± 0.25 Gy/ka, respectively (Table 1 and Fig. 3b).

#### OSL ages and Bayesian modeling

The two samples from Layer 10 yielded OSL ages of 31.4 ± 1.5 and 40.0 ± 1.8 ka, respectively, and an age gap was observed between Layers 10 and 9 (Table 1 and Fig. 3c). Most of the OSL ages of the samples from Layers 9–1 conform to the stratigraphic order, ranging approximately from 15 to 2 ka. However, some ages, especially those from Layers 7 and 6, appear overestimated compared to the adjacent layers above and below (Table 1 and Fig. 3c).

Our Bayesian modeled results show that Layer 10 formed before ∼30 ka, and Layers 9–1 were deposited between 12.7 ± 0.8 and 1.5 ± 0.1 ka (68.3 % confidence intervals, Fig. 5 and Table 1). The erosion at the top of Layer 10 occurred during 31.3 ± 6.6–12.7 ± 0.8 ka, and the time interval for the erosion at the top of Layer 2 is negligible (Table 1). Layer 10 will not be discussed further in this paper due to its much older age than the overlying sediments.

## Discussion

### Reliability of the ages

In this study, fine grains (4–11 μm) were used for dating because fine-grained quartz from the Nihewan Basin exhibits better luminescence properties for the SAR protocol than coarse grains (Lei et al., 2022). Our samples are primarily composed of silts (Fig. 6b). We did not employ radiocarbon dating due to the absence of suitable dating material, the possibility of contamination by plant roots, and the inclusion of old carbon in the gully depositional environment.

A comparison of the typical decay curves of our samples with Risø calibration quartz shows that the natural signals from our samples are dominated by the fast component (Fig. 4a). The dose recovery tests demonstrate that at 240 °C, both the dose recovery ratios and the recycling ratios are most closely clustered around 1. The recuperations are less than 5 % (Fig. S2d–f). Besides, the *D*<sub>e</sub> values calculated from the SGC and those from the full SAR protocol are consistent with each other (Fig. 4b). These favorable characteristics suggest that both the *D*<sub>e</sub> measurement conditions and the SGC method for *D*<sub>e</sub> estimation are suitable for our samples, thus validating the *D*<sub>e</sub> values obtained.

However, these water-laid samples might be overestimated due to poor bleaching of luminescence signals prior to deposition. Usually, the distribution and overdispersion of *D*<sub>e</sub> values are used to assess the bleaching quality of a sample. The scatters in *D*<sub>e</sub> values between aliquots for our samples are very small (Table 1, with an average relative error of 1.7 ± 0.1 %). The small variation may be attributed to the averaging effect of the large number of grains in each aliquot due to the use of fine grains and/or the homogeneity of our samples. Such dose distributions cannot contain much useful information about bleaching history.

According to the stratigraphical Law of Superposition (sediments are expected to get older with depth), the age-depth relationship for our samples can be, to some extent, used to assess the reliability of the OSL ages. The OSL ages for most of the samples are stratigraphically consistent within errors and distributed around a line with a slope of 82.4 ± 3.9 cm/ka (Fig. 3c), determined by linear regression, which represents an average depositional rate during gully deposition. Except for one sample, the other five from Layers 4–2 yielded OSL ages ranging from 2.65 ± 0.12 ka at the depth of 151 cm to 1.79 ± 0.08 ka at the depth of 70 cm. Additionally, in Layer 8, the samples from the depths of 540 to 415 cm yielded OSL ages ranging from 6.93 ± 0.30 ka to 5.50 ± 0.25 ka. These results suggest that the above-mentioned samples were relatively well bleached prior to deposition and the ages distributed around the line are relatively reliable. In contrast, some of the deviated ages are inferred to result from poor bleaching, leading to overestimation (Fig. 3c). In our Bayesian age model, these aberrant ages were confirmed as outliers, indicated by posterior outlier probabilities of >95 % (Table S2). Therefore, the modeled ages for each layer, ranging from 13.0 to 11.6 ka for the bottom sample of Layer 9 to 1.6–1.4 ka for the top sample of Layer 1, are considered highly reliable.

### Stratigraphic integrity

The Holocene paleoenvironment of the Nihewan Basin region remains incompletely reconstructed due to rare continuous regional paleoclimatic records. The LDG section, characterized by gully deposits interbedded with dark-colored soil layers, provides valuable data for paleoenvironmental analysis. For such deposits, stratigraphic integrity and continuity are key to correctly reconstructing environmental changes.

The target layers of the LDG section (Layers 9 to 2) can be clearly divided into two parts based on sediment structure: the lower part (Layers 9 and 8) with a massive structure, and the upper part (Layers 7 to 2) generally with parallel bedding (Fig. 2). The infilling of the LDG gully started after a strong gully incision event that cut ∼9 m into the Nihewan Formation. The highest mean grain-sizes in Layer 9 implies relatively strong water power at the onset of the gully sedimentation process (Fig. 6b). A decrease in the medium-sized fraction, along with an increase in the fine-sized fraction in Layer 8, reflects a less energetic depositional environment (Fig. 6b). A dramatic fluctuation of grain-size fractions and the parallel bedding of Layers 7 and 6 reflect fast-changing water power (Fig. 6b). Additionally, we observed that the silty sand laminae at the depths of 375 and 259 cm (Fig. 6b) are each associated with a trend towards older OSL ages (Fig. 6a), although further exploration of this correlation falls beyond the scope of this study. The depositional environment of Layers 5 to 2 is considered more subdued than before, as indicated by the formation of dark-colored soils within (Fig. 2). Given the absence of a discernable erosion surface between Layer 9 and Layer 2, combined with the stratigraphic order of the OSL ages without any gap, it is reasonable to treat them as continuous sedimentation, making the LDG section suitable for paleoenvironment reconstruction.

### Implication for paleoenvironmental change

Based on Bayesian modeled ages (Fig. 5) and multi-proxy analyses (Figs. 6 and 7), we can infer the Holocene climate change in the Nihewan Basin region. Five climatic stages have been recognized and are described below:

**Stage 1: 12.7 ± 0.8–6.8 ± 0.3 ka.** During this period, Layer 9 was deposited, with its bottom age of 12.7 ± 0.8 ka marking the onset of the accumulation of gully sediments at the beginning of the Younger Dryas (YD) cooling event (Cheng et al., 2020). The climate of this period was relatively cold and dry, as indicated by generally low χlf values (Fig. 6c), with the lowest observed in the middle part, corresponding to an age of ∼10.5 ka. The cold-aridity climate is also supported by a very low pollen concentration of 6 grains/g (Fig. 7). However, a slight warming trend occurred from ∼10.5 to ∼6.8 ka, as indicated by increasing χlf and χfd values (Fig. 6c) and the appearance of hydrophytes and broad-leaved plants (Fig. 7), representing a gradual environmental change.

**Stage 2: 6.8 ± 0.3–5.4 ± 0.3 ka.** During this period, Layer 8 was accumulated. This period was characterized by dark brown color (Fig. 2), elevated χlf and χfd values (Fig. 6c) and relatively high pollen content compared to the underlying layer (Fig. 7). These proxies all suggest a warm and wet climate that favors soil formation during this period. An increase in fine-sized fraction compared with Layer 9 (Fig. 6b) may imply an intensification of chemical weathering under these climate conditions. Additionally, the age-depth relationship (Fig. 3c) shows no overestimation in the OSL ages for the seven samples from this layer, suggesting the sediments received sufficient sunlight exposure and were well bleached before burial, indicative of a stable depositional environment.

**Stage 3: 5.4 ± 0.3–4.0 ± 0.7 ka.** This period covers Layers 7 and 6. These two layers are characterized by lighter color (Fig. 2), lowest χlf values (Fig. 6c), and highest contents of *Picea* pollen (Fig. 7)*.* These characteristics signify a dramatic climate change from the warm and humid phase between ∼6.8 and ∼ 5.4 ka (Stage 2), to the coldest and driest phase of the Holocene in the basin. Moreover, the sedimentary features such as interbedded sand laminae and drastic fluctuations in grain size (Fig. 6b), together with the large scatter in OSL ages between samples and age overestimation (Fig. 3c), reflect an unstable environment during this time.

**Stage 4: 4.0 ± 0.7–2.7 ± 0.3 ka.** This period consists of Layer 5. From bottom to top, the χlf values increase from 55 to 90 × 10<sup>−8</sup> m<sup>3</sup> kg<sup>−1</sup> (Fig. 6c), the herb pollen begins to rise (Fig. 7), and the sediment color changes from light to dark (Fig. 2). Although the OSL ages of the two bottom samples are overestimated, the age of the top sample aligns closely with the age-depth curve (Fig. 3c). Collectively, these indicators suggest the period of climate transition from the cold and dry conditions observed in Layer 6 to the warm and wet conditions in Stage 5.

**Stage 5: 2.7 ± 0.3–1.7 ± 0.3 ka.** This period contains three layers (Layers 4–2). It is challenging to define the precise duration of each layer due to age uncertainties. Despite this, these layers are readily distinguishable in the field by their colors (Fig. 2). Layers 4 and 2, with a dark color (Fig. 2), high χlf and χfd values (Fig. 6c), and high pollen concentrations (Fig. 7), indicate a warm and wet climate conducive to soil formation. In contrast, Layer 3 exhibits a lighter color (Fig. 2) and slightly lower χlf and χfd values (Fig. 6c), implying a brief cooling interval amidst this warm and humid stage. Besides, small variations in grain size and OSL ages at different depths might point to a stable depositional environment for Layers 4 and 2, whereas Layer 3 shows signs of instability, as revealed by more significant changes in grain-size distributions (Fig. 6b).

### Comparison of climate records from nearby areas

The five climate stages of the basin region inferred from the LDG section correspond well with regional paleoclimatic records (Fig. 8). Stage 1 commenced with a cold and dry climate, potentially related to the continental YD event (Cheng et al., 2020), and experienced a slight temperature rise. The rise was primarily a response to the intensification of the EASM since the early Holocene (Fig. 8a, Chen et al., 2015b; Kang et al., 2020), and is reflected in the increase in tree pollen content observed in sediments from the Jijitan archaeological site in the basin (Figs. 1a and 8e, Lu et al., 2018) and Gonghai Lake in the southwest of the basin (Figs. 1a and 8c, Xu et al., 2017), and an increase in precipitation as reconstructed from the Daihai lake sediments in the north of the Nihewan Basin (Figs. 1a and 8b, Xu et al., 2010).

The warm and wet climate during Stage 2 is also evidenced by the highest tree pollen content from Jijitan between 7.5 and 5.5 ka (Figs. 1a and 8e, Lu et al., 2018), and the most abundant precipitation inferred from the Daihai Lake sediments between 7.9 and 4.5 ka (Figs. 1a and 8b, Xu et al., 2010). Furthermore, high δ<sup>13</sup> Corg values in the sediments from Gouchi Lake within the Chinese Loess Plateau hinterland (Figs. 1a and 8d, Yang et al., 2023), high concentrations of tree pollen from the Gonghai Lake sediments (Figs. 1a and 8c, Xu et al., 2017), and the strong paleosol S0S3 at the Jinjie-3 section in the northeast of the Chinese Loess Plateau (Fig. 1a, Anwar et al., 2018) further validate optimal environmental conditions. This warm and wet mid-Holocene was aligned with the northward movement of the monsoon rain belt (Xu et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2015), synchronous with the peak intensity of the EASM (Fig. 8a, Chen et al., 2015b; Kang et al., 2020) and a relative weakening of the East Asian Winter Monsoon (Kang et al., 2020).

The cold and dry climate during Stage 3 is supported by a reduction in tree pollen levels in sediments from Jijitan (Figs. 1a and 8e, Lu et al., 2018) and Gonghai Lake (Figs. 1a and 8c, Xu et al., 2017). Likewise, lower δ<sup>13</sup> Corg values in the Gouchi Lake sediments (Figs. 1a and 8d, Yang et al., 2023), and reduced precipitation as reconstructed from the Daihai Lake sediments (Figs. 1a and 8b, Xu et al., 2010) also indicate a regional decline in humidity, likely linked to a weakening EASM (Fig. 8a, Chen et al., 2015b).

The climate patterns during the late Holocene (Stages 4 and 5) varied across different regions. Generally, humid conditions prevailed in northeast and northwest China (Xu et al., 2019; Zheng et al., 2018), while much of NE China experienced dry conditions (Wang et al., 2008; Zheng et al., 2018), which were associated with the sustained weakening of the EASM since the late Holocene (Chen et al., 2015b; Kang et al., 2020). However, within NE China, evidence of humid phases includes the increase in δ<sup>13</sup> Corg values at 2.5–1.7 ka in the Gouchi Lake sediments (Figs. 1a and 8d, Yang et al., 2023), a high precipitation interval at 1.7–1.5 ka in the Daihai Lake sediments (Figs. 1a and 8b, Xu et al., 2010), and the paleosol S0S2 of 2.9–1.7 ka at the Jinjie-3 section (Fig. 1a, Anwar et al., 2018). On the other hand, the dryness is recorded by reduced precipitation in the Daihai Lake sediments (Figs. 1a and 8b, Xu et al., 2010) and a decline in tree pollen in the Gonghai Lake sediments (Figs. 1a and 8c, Xu et al., 2017). Based on these observations, we hypothesize that soil formation and high χlf and χfd values in Layers 4 and 2 may be due to favorable microclimates, or possibly the influence of intensified human activities (Zhang et al., 2019), or possibly a combination of both, since the late Holocene.

### Relationship between Holocene climate change and cultural evolution

In the Nihewan Basin, the occurrence of the wedge-shaped core technique was widely considered a consequence of human migration southward since the Last Glacial Maximum (Wang, 2018, 2022; Zhu, 2023). In the transition from the late Pleistocene to the early Holocene, the wedge-shaped core technique survived during the YD cold event (Cheng et al., 2020), and prevailed in the basin, including at the sites such as Hutouliang (about 14–10 ka, Fig. 1a; Zhu and Gao, 2006; Shitaoka and Nagatomo, 2013), Jijitan (about 16.0–11.2 ka, Fig. 1a, Guan et al., 2021) and Yujiagou (about 15.5–9.4 ka, Fig. 1a, Mei, 2007; Lin et al., 2018; Rui et al., 2019). Especially for the Yujiagou site, from which the earliest pottery with the age of ∼13 ka in North China was found, the transition from the Paleolithic to the Neolithic occurred within Layer 2 with the age of ∼9.4 ka (Fig. 1a, Lin et al., 2018; Rui et al., 2019). In short, from the last deglacial to ∼10 ka, an intensification in the exploitation of animal resources indicates that the social economy in the basin was primarily hunter-gatherer based (Wang et al., 2019), though evidence of sedentism or semi-sedentism, along with budding early agriculture, had been observed (Mei, 2007).

During the period of ∼10 to ∼6.8 ka, as the EASM strengthened, millet cultivation became a widespread practice in the pre-Yangshao culture system across North China (Dong et al., 2016). During this period, the transition from the Paleolithic to the Neolithic in the Nihewan Basin was marked by the advent of agriculture (Xie et al., 2006), a transformation further evidenced by an increase in polished stone tools and pottery. This transition also saw the substitution of the wedge-shaped core technique with the cone-shaped core technique, as found in Layer 2 of the Yujiagou site (Fig. 1a, Mei, 2007) and the Heitupo site (8.3 ± 0.1 cal. ka BP, Fig. 1a, Xie et al., 2006). Apart from that, signs of cultural exchange became more apparent. The southward migration of the Xinglongwa culture (Zhang, 2021) is evidenced by the abrupt decrease in many archaeological sites in southern Inner Mongolia (Sun and Chang, 2018). This movement exerted a profound influence on the regions along its path, indicated by the Sitai site on the Bashang plateau (Zhao et al., 2023), and the earlier Area I at the Jiangjialiang site (7.7 ± 0.1 cal ka BP, Fig. 1a, Li et al., 2001) in the Nihewan Basin.

Optimal climate conditions occurred during the period of ∼6.8 – ∼4.8 ka in the basin. The abundant rainfall in North China during this period facilitated the establishment and expansion of dryland agriculture (Dong et al., 2016). This period also saw the most frequent cultural exchanges within the Nihewan Basin. For instance, Phase I of the Hougang culture expanded northward to the Sishilipo site (Fig. 1a, Kong et al., 1986). Similarly, influences from the Longshan culture of the northeast and the Miaodigou culture of the west were detected at the earlier Sanguan site (about 6.0–5.8 ka, Fig. 1a, Kong et al., 1986, 1981; Zhang, 2021) and the Area II at the Jiangjialiang site (about 7.5–6.1 cal ka BP, Fig. 1a, Li et al., 2022) at the same time. The charcoal contents at the Jijitan profile also peaked during 6.6–6.0 ka (Lu et al., 2018). Summing up this period, the high density of houses, tombs, and abundant polished stone tools (Li et al., 2022) signified increased human settlement and thriving agriculture, fostered by the ideal climate conditions.

During the period from ∼5 to ∼4 ka, the climate deteriorated suddenly. The resurgence of microliths, particularly arrowheads, at the Shaizilingluo site (4.8 ± 0.2 cal ka BP, Fig. 1a, Kong et al., 1981) suggested that agriculture alone could no longer meet basic survival needs, leading people to turn back to hunting and fishing for sustenance. Moreover, the scarcity of the Longshan period sites in northern Hebei (Zhang, 2021) likely reflects the adverse impacts of worsening climate conditions. Likewise, Dong et al. (2016) found that there are limited sites of millet remains unearthed in North China during this period.

In the late Holocene, after ∼4 ka, the climate in the basin became increasingly warmer, however, archaeological sites from this period are rarely reported. Shifting our focus, the concentrations of black carbon in the Guanzhong Basin increased between 4.3 and 3.5 ka (Tan et al., 2015), corresponding to the late period of Longshan culture and the early Xia dynasty period. Furthermore, agricultural activities expanded into the Hetao Basin during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods (Zhang et al., 2015). Given the advancements in humans' capacity to utilize and transform natural resources during this time (Zhang et al., 2019), it is plausible to believe that the Nihewan Basin, especially areas near the Sanggan River with their abundant soil and water resources, was particularly well-suited for human settlement.

## Conclusions

The LDG section of gully deposits in the Nihewan Basin can be divided into ten layers, from which three soil layers have been identified. The age of the section ranges from ∼38.7 to ∼1.5 ka, as inferred from the Bayesian statistical modeling of 48 OSL ages. The sedimentation process between Layers 9 and 2, ranging from ∼12.7 to ∼1.7 ka, was continuous. Five distinct climate stages have been reconstructed for the Nihewan Basin region, spanning from ∼12.7 to ∼1.7 ka. From ∼12.7 to ∼6.8 ka, the climate was characterized by cold and dry conditions. A shift occurred around 6.8 to 5.4 ka, where the climate became warm and humid, leading to soil formation. The climate was followed by another cold and dry period from ∼5.4 – ∼4.0 ka. A transitional climate was observed from ∼4.0 to ∼2.7 ka, during which the climate changed from cold and dry conditions to warm and humid conditions. Finally, from ∼2.7 to ∼1.7 ka, the climate was predominantly warm and humid, conducive to soil formation, but was briefly interrupted by a short period of colder and drier conditions. During the soil formation, the region experienced a relatively stable depositional environment. Cultural evolution and human activities in the Nihewan Basin during the Holocene were closely tied to paleoenvironmental changes. The wedge-shaped core technique prevailed in the cold early Holocene, but was substituted with the cone-shaped core technique as the living environment became progressively more suitable. It was during this technological transition that the shift from the Paleolithic to the Neolithic occurred, marked by an intensification of agricultural activities, around ∼9.4 ka. Climatically favorable conditions during the period of ∼6.8 – ∼5.4 ka led to the prosperity of the Neolithic cultures. Microliths experienced a brief resurgence as the climate turned slightly colder and drier between ∼5.4 and ∼4.0 ka. The climate gradually returned to warm and humid between ∼4.0 and ∼1.7 ka, however, due to the scarcity of archaeological evidence for this period, drawing direct correlations between environmental changes and specific human activities remains challenging.
