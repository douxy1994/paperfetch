# Characterizing land use transition in China by accounting for the conflicts underlying land use structure and function

## Abstract

Human activities and global changes have undergone significant transformations in land systems. It is imperative to comprehend the intricacies of land use transitions (LUT), as they serve as indicators of the evolving dynamics within the complex human-environmental relationship. In this study, we adopt the conflict-coordination theoretical framework to delve into the role of land-use conflicts (LUC) in driving LUT. Our investigation involves the quantification of LUT and the assessment of LUC within China, spanning from 1980 to 2020. Subsequently, we scrutinized the interplay between LUT and LUC using Spearman correlation analysis and the Wilcoxon test. Our findings reveal that greater diversity in land-use structure can be attributed to ecological land fragmentation and the restoration of unused land, while the expansion of urban and cropland areas contributes to a less diverse land-use pattern. Furthermore, there is a discernible upward trend in land use function. Although the overall degree of LUC in China is relatively mild, there is a noticeable upward trajectory over the past four decades. More pronounced conflicts within land-use patterns promote a diversified land-use pattern, while conflicts arising from competing land-use functions elevate the level of land-use functionality. Moreover, a dynamic interplay between land-use transitions and conflicts becomes evident, with intense conflicts within land-use patterns weakening land-use functionality, and imbalances in functionality driving greater diversity in land-use patterns. Our research introduces an innovative analytical framework that elucidates LUT through the lens of LUC, with the potential for broader applicability across different regions. This framework not only enhances our understanding of LUT but also provides valuable scientific guidance for the effective management of conflicts to promote the sustainable development of land resources.

## Introduction

Intense human activities and global environmental changes have exerted a profound impact on global land systems (Foley et al., 2005; Winkler et al., 2021; Gao and Bryan, 2017; Brown et al., 2013). These alterations manifest visibly in land cover/use changes, encompassing phenomena such as urban expansion, cropland loss, cultivated land intensification, deforestation, afforestation, and grassland degradation, among others. From the implementation of IGBP (International Geosphere-Biosphere Programme) to IHDP (Integrated Human Development Programme), the research topics of land monitoring, change mechanism analysis, and future prediction have received much more attention (Long, 2022). In particular, the development of remote sensing technology and artificial intelligence has enabled precise acquisition of land cover/use information across various spatio-temporal scales, improving our understanding of the processes of land use change. However, changes in land system invariably coincide with shifts in social, economic and ecological conditions, as well as changes in recessive land attributes, such as land quality and land use function (Meng et al., 2020; Hersperger et al., 2010). Land use transition (LUT), comprehensively reflects the shift of land as a coupled human-environmental system from one state to another, has garnered increasing attention (Lambin and Meyfroidt,2010). For instance, LUT occurs when a parcel of cropland, initially meeting household grain consumption needs, is converted into forest for timber production or ecological preservation purposes. With rapid industrialization and urbanization, and the implementation of ecological conservation projects, China's land system has undergone profound transformations (Kong et al., 2022). On the one hand, China has exhibited one of the world's most substantial greening trends, driven by agricultural intensification and afforestation. On the other hand, the relentless expansion of urban areas has resulted in significant cropland loss. Revealing the trajectory of LUT in China not only advances our comprehension of the evolution of land use as an integrated human-environmental system, but also holds crucial and practical implications for scientific land use planning and the pursuit of land use sustainability (Liang et al., 2020).

Characterizing the visible form of LUT (i.e., land-use/cover change) has benefited from the availability of multiple remote sensing images with varying spatial, temporal and spectral resolutions. With the spatially explicit land use/cover information, various indicators, such as transition matrices (Xie et al., 2022) and information entropy (Fu et al., 2021), have been developed to capture the changes in land use composition and configuration. In contrast, characterizing and quantifying the recessive forms of LUT poses significant challenges, as they often cannot be directly measured (Ma et al., 2019). These recessive aspects encompass element such as land quality (Song et al., 2022), land use efficiency (Wang et al., 2023) and land use function (Meng et al., 2022). LUT should well account for both the visible and recessive transformations with land systems, yet the latter are usually neglected and insufficiently investigated.

LUT represents an evolutionary process involving the interplay of various factors, including land use functions, land fragmentation or homogeneity, and the level of competition among these functions (Long, 2022; Paracchini et al., 2011; Hersperger et al., 2010). Due to the scarcity of land resources, as well as the growing and diversifying human needs, land use at a given period and region cannot provide sufficient products or services to meet demands. Consequently, this mismatch leads to imbalances or what we term as land use conflicts (LUC) (Qu et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2019; Karimi et al., 2017). The land use stakeholders tend to adapt land use activities in response to these conflicts, thereby potentially reshaping the structure of the land system. This, in turn, can impact the availability of land use function to better meet human needs and alleviate LUC. This dynamic process constitutes a complete cycle of LUT through the conflict-coordination mechanism (Long, 2022). Therefore, evaluating LUC is key to elucidating LUT trajectories. LUC manifests in two aspects. First, land use structure conflict occurs when the pattern or configuration of land use displays imbalances, such as increased landscape fragmentation or heightened vulnerability (Junior et al., 2014; Karimi et al., 2017). These conflicts are often evaluated using metrics that assess landscape pattern and complexity (Jiang et al., 2021). Second, land use function conflict arises when the provision of multiple land use functions lacks coordination or equilibrium, compromising the ability to fulfill diverse human needs through land. These conflicts can be quantified using a multifunctionality index (Paracchini et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2019) and by analyzing tradeoffs among these dimensions (Han et al., 2022).

There are gaps in research on LUT. Firstly, comprehensive characterizations of LUT that encompass both structural and functional aspects remain limited. Second, no study has quantified the mechanisms of LUT using the solid conflict-coordination theory for regional LUT. To address these deficiencies, our research aims to quantify the trajectory of LUT, and examine the relationship between LUT and LUC based on the conflict-coordination theory. We assessed land use structure, land use function, and the underlying conflicts related to land use structure and function in China from 1980 to 2020. To quantify the relationship between LUT and LUC, we adopt the conflict-coordination theoretical framework, which states that land use transition is driven by complex interactions among land use structure, functions, and the conflicts occurring within the coupled human-environment system (Long, 2022). When land use functions cannot meet demands or imbalances lead to conflicts, transformation of structure may occur, influencing the trajectory of transition over time. Our research seeks to provide new empirical insights into the complex interplay between LUT and LUC within the coupled human-environment system in China over the past four decades. The analysis framework also holds transferable value for characterizing land use transitions in other nations where land resources are undergoing increased pressures due to rapid socioeconomic transformation.

## Materials and methods

### Study area

We took Chinese mainland as the study area (Fig. 1). The topography of China shows a three-level stepped pattern, with higher elevations in the west and gradually descending to lower elevations in the east. It spans a wide range of climate zones, progressing from tropical, subtropical, and temperate climates from south to north, and humid and arid climate from east to west mainly due to the influences of Asian monsoon and the distance to ocean. Land use activities within China are diverse and exhibit highly spatial differentiation. Cropland is mainly distributed in the plains in Eastern China and the Sichuan Basin, while forest dominate the northeastern, southwestern, and southern regions. Grassland and unused land are prevalent in the arid and semi-arid regions of Northern and Western China. Urban areas are prominently clustered in city centers. Since the implementation of the reform and opening up policy in 1980s, China has undergo a remarkable economic transformation, leading to substantial changesin land use patterns. These changes encompass urbanization accompanied by the associated loss of cropland, agricultural intensification, and pervasive afforestation (Song et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2022). Our analyses encompassed the entirety of China, with a particular focus on three major economic zones: Eastern, Central, and Western China, which have different environmental and resource conditions, and socio-economic development paths (Li et al., 2010). We also made analysis for regions divided by Hu Huanyong Line, which was determined by the China's population distribution and density (Hu, 1935). The line divides China into a densely populated southeast and a sparsely populated northwest, signifying its significance as a key geographical division within China.

### Data sources

We obtained land use data at 1-km resolution in 1980, 1990, 2000, 2010 and 2020 from the China Multi-Temporal Land Use Remote Sensing Monitoring Dataset (CNLUCC). This dataset was sourced from the Resource and Environmental Science and Data Center of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (www.resdc.cn) (Xu et al., 2018). Socio-economic statistical data at both city and county levels were collected from the China Economic and Social Big Data Research Platform (data.cnki.net). The gridded population density data at 1-km resolution for the years 2000, 2010, and 2020 were obtained from the official WorldPop website (www.worldpop.org). Population density data for the year 1990 were downloaded from the Resource and Environmental Science and Data Center of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (www.resdc.cn). To obtain actual evapotranspiration ratio data covering the period from 1981 to 2015, we referred to the National Ecosystem Science Data Center (nesdc.org.cn). This data was available at a spatial resolution of 0.05° (Niu et al., 2020). PM2.5 concentration data were obtained from the China Meteorological Science Research Institute's Laboratory for Atmospheric Composition Analysis (Zhong et al., 2022) from 1960 to 2020 with a resolution of 0.25°. PM2.5 concentration data for the years 2000, 2010, and 2020 were obtained from the Atmospheric Composition Analysis Group at Washington University in St. Louis (sites.wustl.edu), with a resolution of 0.01°. Net Primary Productivity (NPP) data were derived from the GLASS series of global surface vegetation parameter products based on AVHRR data from 1982 to 2018 with a spatial resolution of 5 km.

### Quantifying land use structure and its conflict

#### Characterizing land use structure

We calculated structure index for single and multiple land use types to characterize land use structure. The structure index of single land use (SSI) represents the proportion of a specific land use type to the total land area in a given region, whose formula is written as:

(1)

$$
SSI = \frac{LU_{i}}{LU}
$$

where LU *i*<sub>repres</sub> ents the area of land use type *i*, and LU represents the total land area of a region or grid. We identified the land use type with the highest SSI within a 15 km by 15 km grid as the dominant land use type, whose change could well indicate land use transition.

The comprehensive structure index of multiple land use types (CSI) reflects the diversity of land use structure in a region (Song et al., 2021), whose formula is expressed as:

(2)

$$
CSI = 1 - ∑{(SSI)}^{2}
$$

The higher CSI value indicates a greater richness of land use types within the grid. In our study, land use was classified into six categories: cropland, forest, grassland, waterbody, urban, and unused land.

#### Assessing land use structure conflict

We adopted the method developed by Jiang et al. (2021) to evaluate land use structure conflict. This method draws on the conceptual framework of ecological risk assessment and the theories in landscape ecology to construct a model to derive the spatio-temporal patterns of land use conflicts. It integrates the indicators of complexity(C), vulnerability(V), and fragmentation(F) to quantify ecological risks associated with land use. Subsequently, these indicators are linked to the different dimensions of the land use structure conflict (Jiang et al., 2021). The conflict of land use structure can be represented by the sum of Complexity, Vulnerability, and Fragmentation:

(3)

$$
LUSC = C + V + F
$$

in this study, we calculated the landscape indices with a grid size of 15-km. Since the land use structure conflict varied with scale, we tested how the conflict results responded to the selected grid size, and found that. Referring to existing research and conducting multiple comparative experiments, a 15 km grid size could well balance the stability of results and computational efficiency (Cheng et al., 2022). The detailed description of this method can be found in Jiang et al. (2021). We divided the land use structure conflict intensity into 4 levels using the natural breaks method, including weak (0–0.35), relatively weak (0.35–0.53), relatively strong (0.53–0.66), and strong (0.66–1).

### Quantifying land use functions and their conflict

#### Evaluating land use functions

Drawing upon the principles of sustainable development and the predominant land use functions (Wei et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2021), we assessed the multifunctionality of land use through three dimensions: production, living, and ecological functions. We constructed an assessment index system by drawing upon prior research. The production functions pertain to the land's capacity to promote economic development and productivity, indicating its role in contributing to economic growth. The living functions are essential for maintaining residents' quality of life. The ecological function refers to the ability of land to maintain the stability of ecosystem and protect them from external disturbances. In our assessment of land use functions, we considered eight indicators (Table 1). Correlation analysis revealed a modest association among these indicators. We standardized the indicators into the range of 0∼1 to make them comparable. For determining the weight of each indicator, we used the Principal Component Analysis (PCA) (Table 1), a method known for providing objective weights and widely utilized in assessment (Li et al., 2017). The comprehensive scores for each of the three first-level functions, as well as the overall score, were calculated using the weighted sum method.

The output values derived from primary, secondary, and tertiary industries serve as effective indicators of a region's production function, representing its capacity to supply agricultural and industrial products as well as services, of (Fan et al., 2021).

We chose indicators that well reflect the capacity of an area to accommodate residents including population density, access to healthcare resources, resident deposits (Cheng et al., 2021; Niu et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023). Access to healthcare services is pivotal for ensuring residents' well-being, as it necessitates land for constructing and operating hospital infrastructure. Additionally, healthcare resources tend to concentrate in more developed urban areas with higher land values. Resident deposits provide insights into economic vitality and the capacity of residents to invest in local businesses and infrastructure. This in turn can influence land use decisions, with areas boasting greater resident deposits attracting more commercial and recreational facilities, ultimately enhancing residents’ quality of life.

Given the ecological challenges of China, we chose indicators of ecological function, encompassing carbon sequestration capacity, climate regulation, and air purification (Meng et al., 2022). Carbon sequestration capacity reflects land's effectiveness in absorbing and storing CO2, a key factor in mitigating climate change. Climate regulation, which indicates ecosystems' ability to moderate local climate conditions, plays a vital role in reducing the impact of extreme weather events and ensuring stable water resources. Air purification is equally pertinent as China grapples with air pollution issues in its urban areas. Natural ecosystems, such as forests and vegetation, contribute to air purification by removing pollutants including PM2.5, which has a substantial impact on public health.

#### Evaluating land use function conflict

We quantified the degree of conflict among multiple land use functions using the RMSE method (Liu et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2022). In comparison to the commonly used correlation method which only reflects pairwise tradeoffs/synergies between land use functions, RMSE can assess the tradeoff relationships among multiple functions. The calculation of RMSE is as follows:

(4)

$$
RMSE = \frac{1}{n - 1}\sqrt{\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}{(LUF_{i} - \overset{‾}{LUF})}^{2}}
$$

where $LUF_{i}$ represents the standardized value of the land use function *i*, $\overset{‾}{LUF}$ represents the mean of n land use functions (*n* = 3). A higher RMSE indicates a greater tradeoffs intensity among different land use functions, suggesting stronger conflict. We also classified the conflict of land use function into 4 levels using the natural break method, including weak (0–0.15), relatively weak (0.15–0.25), relatively strong (0.25–0.35), and strong (0.35–1).

### Examining the relationships between LUT and LUC

We adopted the conflict-coordination theoretical framework for the analysis of LUT. Theoretically, LUT is driven by the complicated interactions among land use structure, land use functions, and the underlying conflicts within a coupled human-environmental system. At a specific stage, land use structure and function can reach an equilibrium state, where the provision of land functions effectively meets human demands for products, services and living conditions (Long, 2022). When a certain land use function fails to fulfill human needs, intentionally or unintentionally transformation in land use structure may occur, resulting in land use structure conflicts (e.g., more vulnerable and fragmented land use pattern). Simultaneously, the fulfilment of one human demand might come at the expense of other land use functions, potentially leading to new states of land use function conflicts. In line with the conflict-coordination for LUT, we hypothesized that conflicts in both land use function and structure would exhibit positive correlations with their levels. Land use function conflicts would interact with land use structure, while land use structure conflicts would be related with land use functions. To untangle these relationships, we conducted Spearman correlation analysis to assess the correlation between the time series of LUC and LUT for the whole China and within different regions.

To assess the potential impacts of LUC within different LUT directions, we first identified the top 3 major transformations with the largest areas in each region. Subsequently, we analyzed the changes in land use structure and functions conflicts within these transformations. To determine the direction of change in land use structure and functions conflict, we employed the Wilcoxon rank-sum test. The Wilcoxon-sum test is a non-parametric test that does not assume normal distribution of data and is not sensitive to outliers (Liu et al., 2022), making it well-suited for our study. This analysis serves as a supplement to the correlation between the time series of LUC and LUT mentioned earlier, enabling us to evaluate the spatial patterns of the relationships between LUT and LUC.

## Results

### Spatio-temporal pattern of LUT

#### Transition of land use structure

The overall pattern of the predominant land use type remained relatively stable on a national scale (Fig. 2 a–b). Regions dominated by cropland were primarily distributed in the Northeast China Plain, the North China Plain, and Sichuan Basin. Forest-dominated areas were predominantly found in Northeastern China, and Southern China. In the vast western areas of China, grassland and unused land prevailed. Grids dominated by urban land dominance were primarily situated within urban clusters including Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei, Yangtze River Delta, and Pearl River Delta, as well as in the provincial capitals located in the eastern and central economic belts.

Spatio-temporal pattern of shifts in land use structure from 1980 to 2020. (a), (b) Spatial patterns of dominant land use with 15-km grid in 1980 and 2020. (c) Shifts in dominant land use from 1980 to 2020. (d), (e) Spatial patterns of CSI in 1980 and 2020. (f) Pattern of CSI change from 1980 to 2020.

Significant shifts in predominated land use were observed across China (Fig. 2c). Areas dominated by urban area expanded by about 635.8%, primarily within urban clusters and capital cities. The shifts in ecological land-dominated area from 1980 to 2020 was most notable in western China due to various driving factors, including grassland degradation, ecological restoration, and cropland reclamation. For example, cropland reclamation and urban expansion led to a reduction in grassland and unused land in the oases along the northern foothills of the Tianshan Mountains and the peripheries of the Tarim Basin. Tibet experienced grassland degradation, soil erosion, and desertification, resulting in a remarkable loss of ecological lands, largely attributed to climate warming (Kong et al., 2022). The expansion of forest-dominant areas was primarily observed in semi-arid areas of China, where afforestation policies and projects were implemented to promote ecological protection.

There was no significant change in integrated land use structure from 1980 to 2020 (Fig. 2d–e). High CSI values were found mainly in the agro-pastoral ecotone, followed by the southeast and southwest regions (Fig. 2d–e), because different land use types were evenly distributed within grid areas. In contrast, in the eastern and northwestern regions of China, CSIs were lower because the 15-km grid mostly consisted of a single land use type like cropland, grassland, or unused land. Tibet saw a significant increase in CSI due to more fragmented grassland landscapes caused by climate warming (Fig. 2f). Urban expansion in regions like Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei, the Yangtze River Delta, and the Pearl River Delta, resulted in a more uniform land use pattern and a decrease in the CSI. In Xinjiang, the conversion of unused land to cropland led to large, contiguous cropland areas and a decline in CSI. In the southern part of Qinghai Province, extensive grassland restoration efforts in the Three-River Source region decreased CSI.

#### Transition of land use function

Land use functions exhibited a general decrease from the southeast to northwest of China (Fig. 3). Production function was more pronounced in the Northeast Plain, Huang-Huai-Hai Plain, the Middle-Lower Yangtze Plain, the Pearl River Delta Plain, and Sichuan Basin (Fig. 3a, d).

![Fig. 3](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S0301479723020996-gr3_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

Spatio-temporal patterns of changes in land use functions from 1980 to 2020 (a) production function (b) living function (c) ecological function in 1980; (d) production function(e) living function(f) ecological function in 2020.

Regions with major urban agglomerations or agricultural regions had witnessed high values of living function (Fig. 3b, e). High level of ecological function was distributed in the Northeast, Southeast, and Southwest (Fig. 3c, f). The Northwest displayed lower values for all three land use functions.

The level of land use function showed a pervasive increase across China, especially in the southeastern part from 1980 to 2020. Production, living and ecological functions increased by 63.6%, 36.5%, and 15.46%, respectively. The expansion of regions with high-level production and living functions was notably bounded by Hu Huanyong Line. The prevalent and significant increase in production and living functions was observed in the southeastern section of China. In contrast, the general pattern of ecological function level remained relatively stable.

#### Transition of combined land use structure and function

In 2020, the H–H area (high level of land use structure and function) had the highest proportion of about 31.96%, followed by L-L (low level of land use structure and function) and L-H (low level of land use function and high level of land use structure) with a proportion of about 28.17% and 24.06%, respectively. The H-L (high level of land use function and low level of land use structure) area was the smallest, accounting for 15.81%. The H–H and H-L areas were mainly distributed in the southeast, while areas of L-L and L-H were mainly distributed in the northwest (Fig. 4).

![Fig. 4](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S0301479723020996-gr4_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

Spatio-temporal patterns of land use transition shown with combined shifts in land use structure and functions from 1980 to 2020. (a) 1980, (b) 1990, (c) 2000, (d) 2010 and (e) 2020.

Over the past four decades, we've seen a significant increase in the H–H and a consistent decrease in the L-L category. H-L area has been rising, while L-H area has been falling, indicating a shift towards a more diverse structure with higher functionality. The substantial expansion of H-L was mainly in the Huang-Huai-Hai Plain and the Sichuan Basin, where cropland expansion and intensification have refined land use structure and improved functionality. The L-L and L-H types dominated the northwest because continuously-distributed grassland and unused land often had more dynamic land use structure and low function level. These pure land patches diversify due to factors like fragmentation or ecological protection, increasing the CSI.

### Spatio-temporal patterns of LUC

The LUC assessment in China was shown in Fig. 5, highlights that weak to relatively weak conflicts in both land use structure and function dominate, accounting for 31.12%. Strong conflicts in both are rare, at just 1.74% of the total. Higher land use structure conflicts are widespread, especially in agro-pastoral ecotone, while land use function conflicts tend to be lower. In the northwestern desert region, the higher land use structure conflict is predominantly due to the vulnerability of land use system, while in the eastern region and agro-pastoral ecotone, fragmented landscapes contribute to this conflict. Land use function conflict generally exhibits higher values in the southwest, southeast, and northeast, and lower values in the northwest.

![Fig. 5](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S0301479723020996-gr5_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

Spatial patterns of land use conflict shown with combined conflicts of land use structure and function from 1980 to 2020. (a) 1980, (b) 1990, (c) 2000, (d) 2010 and (e) 2020.

Strong and relatively strong conflict zones expanded by 9.69%, while weak and relatively weak conflict zones reduced by 70.5%, indicating a continuous overall increase in LUC. Hotspots of "strong-strong" conflict shifted from northward Chongqing and Fujian to the southern part of Shaanxi, with "weak-weak" conflict clusters primarily found in Tibet. Regions with significant changes in LUC levels are mainly in central China, such as the southwestern part of Gansu, the central-southern part of Shaanxi, Chongqing, and Shanxi. These regions consistently exhibit high levels of land use structure conflict, undergo continuous adjustments influenced by fluctuations in land use function conflicts.

### Relationships between LUT and LUC

We analyzed the temporal correlation between LUT and LUC (Fig. 6). Nationally, there's a significant positive correlation between land use structure and its conflict, as well as between land use function and its conflict, with Spearman correlation coefficients exceeding 0.40 (Fig. 6a). Similar correlations exist in three subregions (Fig. 6b, c, d), indicating that higher conflict tends to drive more diverse land use structure and improved function level. Land use function is significantly and negatively correlated with land use structure conflict in China, as well as in eastern and central economic belts (Fig. 6a, b, c), although no significant relationship is observed in the western economic belt (Fig. 6d). Intense conflict in land use structure hinders function improvement. There is a significant positive correlation between land use structure and land use function conflict in China. This relationship is particularly strong in the western economic belt, suggesting that itensified conflict can drive the land use adjustment of and enhance land use function (Zhang and Li, 2022).

We also analyzed the spatial relationships between LUT and LUC (Fig. 7)and found that different LUT directions are closely related to LUC (Fig. 7). The transition from L-H (low level of function and high-level structure) to H–H (high level of function and structure), which accounts for over 30% of the total transition area, is generally accompanied by the alleviated land use structure conflict and the enhanced land use function conflict. This suggests that the optimized land use structure could lead to higher level function, but may also result in unbalanced functions. However, in the central economic belt, this kind of transition is associated with increases in both land use structure and function conflict. The second most common transition, from L-L (low level of function and structure) to H-L (high level of function and low-level structure), often leads to increased land use structure and function conflicts in the eastern and central economic belts (Fig. 7b–c). This is because these belts, characterized by high economic growth, experience intense land use structure conflicts, leading to shifts in land use patterns such as cropland or urban expansion. These shifts tend to homogenize land use patterns while achieving higher but unbalanced functions. Nationally, this transition results from reduced land use structure conflict and increased land use function conflict (Fig. 7a).

Different dominant LUT types are observed in each economic belt. In the eastern economic belt, the transition from L-L to H–H is mainly affected by land use function conflict, with little impact from structure conflict (Fig. 7b). In the central economic belt, the transition from H-L to H–H is accompanied by the intensification of land use structure conflict and the relaxing of function conflict (Fig. 7c), indicating that the multifunctional land in high-function areas has entered a stage of coordinated development. At this time, the changes in land spatial patterns mainly affected by conflict of land use structure. In the western economic belt, the land structure and function are still in the early stages of development, mainly from L-L to L-H and from L-L to H-L. The intensification of LUC has brought fragmentation and degradation of ecological land, especially the increase of land use function conflict has led to the transformation of land use into a multifunctional direction (Fig. 7d).

## Discussion

### Land use conflict and transition patterns

We comprehensively quantify LUT in China from the perspectives of land use structure and functions, while also considering LUC to provide a more comprehensive explanation of LUT. LUT in China shows a trend towards more diverse structure and enhanced land use multifunctionality. This transformation in land use function is rapid and continuously adapts to economic and social development. However, the explicit form of land use is less likely to undergo significant short-term changes (Fig. 8). This is consistent with the agreement that there is an inconsistency between the pace of change in visible and recessive forms of LUT (Li, 2021).

![Fig. 8](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S0301479723020996-gr8_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

Changes in land use transition and conflict in (a) China, (b) eastern economic belt, (c) central economic belt and (d) western economic belt.

Our results show that the diversity of land use structure often increases land use structure conflict, while the enhancement of land use function leads to a more coordinated land use pattern, thereby reducing the degree of land use structure conflict. This is consistent with previous findings (Zhang and Li, 2022) that the coordinated multifunctionality of land use enable to optimize land use pattern. However, the multifunctionality of land use plot has its limits and can only provide a specific set of functions and services at a given stage of development (Cord et al., 2017; Turkelboom et al., 2018). As human demands become more diverse or new technologies emerge, the increased land use function conflicts might lead to the shifts in land use structure. In areas with higher levels of urbanization, the extensive expansion of construction land may simplify land use structure without generating significant conflict. Moreover, the positive correlation between land use function and its conflict tends to diminish as land use function become more advanced. In the early stages of social development, land use function may be relatively weak (Van Vliet et al., 2015; Verburg et al., 2009). With the rapid enhancement of production and living functions, short-term tradeoffs among different functions can emerge, leading to increased land use function conflict. However, a coordinated development among different functions would finally be achieved with continuous LUT (Fig. 8). The socio-economic development brings about a new cycle of conflict-transition interaction (Long, 2022).

### Conflict-coordination mechanism

We have conducted an analysis of the driving mechanisms behind LUT in various regions of China. Since these regions are at different development stages, and have wide-range states of land use structure, multifunctionality, and conflict. This in-depth examination is aimed at compensating for the short study period in our research, which may not fully capture the complexity of LUT (Lambin and Meyfroidt, 2010; Pazúr et al., 2017). In the eastern region, marked by rapid urbanization and industrialization, LUT is driven predominantly by conflicts in land use functions (Niu et al., 2023). Alleviating these functional conflicts requires integrated land use planning and zoning strategies (Liu et al., 2018; Güneralp et al., 2020). In the central region with large fluctuations in cropland and forest cover, leading to increased conflicts in both structure and function. To effectively manage this complexity, comprehensive land use coordination is required. Priority should be given to ecological restoration in northern areas, where structure conflicts are more pronounced, while simultaneously boosting urban development in the southern regions to reduce regional disparities (Jian et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023). The western region remains dominated by ecological functions. Maintaining this function is crucial for healthy land use transition as human activities expand into fragile environments (Zhang et al., 2019; Levers et al., 2018). Overall, our analysis has unveiled distinct mechanisms of regional LUT, largely influenced by the varying stages of development. These findings underscore the importance of targeted policy and planning responses to mitigate conflicts and promote sustainable LUT.

LUT in China provides important insights into land management globally. The transition toward more diverse land use structure along with enhanced multifunctionality found in our study indicates that competition for land eventually decreases through conflict alleviation process. However, the multifunctionality cannot be sustained on a land parcel when conflicts emerge due to increased demands or new technology, and trigger new round of land-use transitions (Howe et al., 2014; Verburg et al., 2015). The regional analysis of LUT drivers conducted in this study also has implications for urban planners. In regions characterized by pronounced land use structure conflicts, the top priority should be the implementation of ecological restoration and protection strategies (Sirami et al., 2019; Kong et al., 2022). In regions with imbalanced development of land use functions, measures for coordination, such as functional zoning, become essential. Our research demonstrates the importance of examining changes in both land use structure and functions for understanding LUT dynamics and identifying sustainable pathways.

### Uncertainty and limitations

Due to the complexity of implicit LUT and data availability (Ma et al., 2019), there is some uncertainty in our assessment of land use functions. The selection of indicators is severely limited by data collection conditions (Cheng et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2021). Moreover, our study aims to examine how LUT is driven by the conflicts among different land use types and functions, but more intricate mechanisms might also influence LUT. Further research is also needed to investigate the driving mechanisms of structure-function conflicts in the LUT process, in order to provide better guidance for land management.

## Conclusion

We conducted a comprehensive analysis of LUT spanning from 1980 to 2020, encompassing both structural and functional aspects while considering LUC. Our findings are not unique to China, but have boarder implications for other counties and regions. Moreover, our research introduces a novel analytical framework for comprehending LUT, which holds promise for application in diverse geographical contexts. LUT should be characterized by both land use structure and function. Decreases in ecological land led to more diverse land use structure, while urbanization and cropland expansion tends to simplify land use structure. There is an overall increase in land use function, particularly in production and living functions. Examining the relationships between LUT and LUC makes more sense to understand the mechanisms of LUT. Second, limits exist for multifunctionality before renewed competition arises. While coordination initially mitigates structural conflicts, there exists a finite capacity to sustain these multifaceted land uses. Further research should be dedicated to identifying these thresholds within various contextual settings. Third, there is a strong positive correlation between land use structure and its conflict, as well as between land use function and its conflict. However, there is a negative correlation between land use function and land use structure conflict, suggesting that intense conflict of land use structure tends to prevent improving land use functions. In contrast, the positive correlation between land use structure conflict and land use function implies that high-level functionality is favorable for the reduction in structure conflict. But such relation might reverse when land use function could not meet the increased demands or new technologies emerge. Overall, our research serves as a valuable resource for understanding the transformations within land system, particularly those characterized as coupled human-environmental system. Furthermore, our findings offer critical insights to inform land use management and planning processes, thereby contributing to more effective and sustainable practices.
