# A size‐constrained feeding‐niche model distinguishes predation patterns between aquatic and terrestrial food webs

## Abstract

Understanding the formation of feeding links provides insights into processes underlying food webs. Generally, predators feed on prey within a certain body-size range, but a systematic quantification of such feeding niches is lacking. We developed a size-constrained feeding-niche (SCFN) model and parameterized it with information on both realized and non-realized feeding links in 72 aquatic and 65 terrestrial food webs. Our analyses revealed profound differences in feeding niches between aquatic and terrestrial predators and variation along a temperature gradient. Specifically, the predator–prey body-size ratio and the range in prey sizes increase with the size of aquatic predators, whereas they are nearly constant across gradients in terrestrial predator size. Overall, our SCFN model well reproduces the feeding relationships and predation architecture across 137 natural food webs (including 3878 species and 136,839 realized links). Our results illuminate the organisation of natural food webs and enables novel trait-based and environment-explicit modelling approaches.

## INTRODUCTION

Understanding the emergence of food web architecture is essential for disentangling the mechanisms underlying ecosystem organisation, functioning and stability (Bascompte, 2010; Jacquet et al., 2016; Thompson et al., 2012). Despite their structural complexity, all food webs have in common that they, as well as their building blocks (e.g. food chain and omnivory modules), are composed of feeding relationships, for example, predator–prey interactions. Thus, resolving the feeding niches of species can be the key to understanding the architecture of complex food webs (Portalier et al., 2019; Wootton et al., 2021).

Several food web models have been proposed to characterize feeding relationships and the architecture of empirical food webs. The key conceptualisation underpinning these models is the concept of the ‘ecological niche’, which determines the feeding relationships between co-occurring species (Williams & Martinez, 2000). Specifically, species are assumed to occur along an abstract niche axis, which is determined by a uni- or multi-dimensional trait space (Cohen, 1977; Eklöf et al., 2013; Gravel et al., 2013; Williams & Martinez, 2000). Each predator has a feeding niche, represented as an interval along the niche axis. The classic niche model requires only information of the total number of trophic links (*L*) and species (*S*) as inputs, but it successfully reproduces a number of realistic structural properties (e.g. average trophic levels, omnivory, etc.) in empirical food webs (Williams & Martinez, 2000). The elegance and effectiveness of the niche model have made it a central tool for simulating food web architecture, and a number of variants have been developed (Allesina et al., 2008; Cattin et al., 2004; Williams et al., 2010; Williams & Martinez, 2008; Williams & Purves, 2011).

However, while the niche model is powerful in reproducing general community-level properties, the simplicity of the parameters required prevents the consideration of how food web architecture varies with local environmental conditions, for instance ecosystem type (Brose et al., 2019; Shurin et al., 2006) and temperature (O'Gorman et al., 2019; Petchey et al., 1999). Such lack of consideration may lead to misrepresentations of how communities respond to environmental changes (Gauzens et al., 2020). Another limitation of the niche model is that it cannot predict the presence or absence of feeding relationships between a given species pair, because the niche position of species assumed in the niche model is abstract and hard to specify in natural communities. To overcome these limitations, recent studies have increasingly advocated the trait-explicit approach, where species' ecological niches and feeding interactions are characterized by measurable species traits (e.g. body size) and dependent on environmental context (Brose et al., 2019; Hirt et al., 2020; Schneider et al., 2016; Sentis et al., 2021).

Body size has long been recognized as a key trait shaping the behaviours and biological rates of species that are relevant to feeding interactions (Brown et al., 2004; Peters, 1983; Woodward et al., 2005). Feeding interactions are realized by a set of foraging-related events, namely detection, attack, handling etc., and these events can be influenced by species' body size (Wootton et al., 2021). Early conceptualisations of foraging processes highlight that predators prefer prey with body sizes neither too large, to increase attack success, nor too small, to reduce energy and time cost to find and handle prey individuals (Persson et al., 1998). Such a hump-shaped feeding-niche pattern has been supported by food web data across various ecosystems (Brose et al., 2006, 2019). Several size-based food web models have thus been proposed, such as the allometric diet breadth model (Petchey et al., 2008), allometric niche model (Williams et al., 2010), latent-trait model (Rohr et al., 2010), size quantile-niche model (Gravel et al., 2013) and size-constrained food web model (Schneider et al., 2016; summarized in Table S1). Although these models all use species body size as a super trait to determine feeding interactions, the rules for determining the feeding niche of predators differ among different models. For instance, the feeding range of predators relative to their own body size was considered to change with predator body size (i.e. varying size-ratio pattern) in some models (Gravel et al., 2013; Williams et al., 2010), but to be constant (i.e. constant size-ratio pattern) in others (Rohr et al., 2010; Schneider et al., 2016) (Figure 1). To unify these models, we need to systematically evaluate the different assumptions involved in size-constrained feeding-niche patterns, taking advantage of the accumulated empirical food web data (e.g. Brose et al., 2019).

Several recent studies have examined the empirical relationship between predator body size and its feeding niche, characterized by the centre (i.e. optimal prey size) and range (i.e. prey size range) of the feeding interval along the axis of log<sub>10</sub> body size (Brose et al., 2019; Gravel et al., 2013) (Figure 1). However, these studies mostly used information only on realized feeding links (Gravel et al., 2013; Brose et al., 2019; but see exceptions in Table S1), while ignoring that on ‘non-realized’ feeding links, which usually represent the majority of all possible pairwise species combinations within food webs (e.g. the food web connectance or the proportion of realized links is generally smaller than 0.3 in empirical food webs; see Dunne et al., 2002; Digel et al., 2014) (Figure 2). To understand intuitively how the ignorance of information on non-realized links may mislead our inference, consider a predator that has a probability of 0.25 to consume a potential prey species with size 1 kg and a probability of 0.5 to consume a potential prey species with size 10 kg. If the food web contains 100 species of size 1 kg and 10 species of size 10 kg, we expect that the realized diet of the predator includes 25 species of size 1 kg and 5 species of size 10 kg. Based on the realized links, one may conclude that the predator prefers prey of size 1 kg more than that of size 10 kg, in contrast to the actual preference. Therefore, both information of realized and non-realized links is important for understanding the likelihood of feeding links and reconstructing food web architecture. By incorporating the information of non-realized links, Rohr et al. (2010, 2016) showed that their models can better explain empirical food web architectures than models including only realized links. However, their model was food web-specific, limiting its generalisation across ecosystems, especially along environmental gradients.

In this study, we developed a size-constrained feeding-niche (SCFN) model, in which the characteristics of the feeding niche of predators (e.g. the centre and range of feeding intervals and the maximum feeding probability along this interval) depend on predator size. Different from classical niche models, our SCFN model can predict the probability of feeding links between particular species pairs, by inferring the predators' feeding niche quantitatively rather than randomly assign niche positions to each species. Using a large dataset (Brose, 2018; Brose et al., 2019, GATEWAy) that comprises hundreds of food webs spanning different ecosystem types over a broad environmental gradient, we parameterised our model using the Bayesian method and examined how ecosystem type (aquatic vs. terrestrial) and temperature influenced the size-constrained feeding niche. Importantly, our model used information on both realised and non-realised links of predatory interactions. We evaluated the performance of our model in reproducing link-level feeding interactions and community-level predation architecture, with comparison to the size quantile-niche model (Gravel et al., 2013), which had similar complexity as our model (see Table S1 and Appendix S1). Our SCFN model provides a flexible framework for illustrating species' feeding niche and predicting their possible responses to environmental changes.

## MATERIAL AND METHODS

### The GATEWAy dataset

The GATEWAy dataset contains 290 food webs that cover both aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems across the globe, including in total 5736 species (all with body size information) and 222,151 realised feeding links (Brose, 2018; Brose et al., 2019). In our study, we focused only on those large food webs that include at least 15 predators and examined the predatory interactions through individual-based intake and attack, thus excluding herbivory, cannibalism and predation by filter feeding. This resulted in 137 food webs (aquatic: 72; terrestrial: 65) with 3878 prey/predator species (aquatic: 2196; terrestrial: 1682) and 136,839 realised feeding links (aquatic: 39,413; terrestrial: 97,426). For each food web, we extracted all prey and predator species and constructed an adjacency matrix to characterise the predation architecture of the food web. More specifically, if a food web includes $n$ animal species in total and *k* predators ($k \leq n$), its predation architecture is represented by an $n \times k$ matrix *M*, with its columns and rows representing potential predators and potential preys, respectively. The element of the matrix, $M_{ji}$, equals 1 if species $i$ is a predator of species $j$, and it equals 0 otherwise (Figure 2). The average connectance (i.e. the proportion of realised links) is 0.11 and 0.21 for aquatic and terrestrial food webs, respectively.

To understand how temperature may influence the predator feeding niche, we extracted the water temperature data from NOAA optimum interpolation (OI) sea surface temperature (SST) V2 dataset (https://psl.noaa.gov/). Specifically, using the latitude and longitude of each aquatic food web, we extracted the monthly average SST from December 1981 to May 2022 from NOAA_OI_SST_V2 dataset and then calculated the annual average temperature. We did not analyse the temperature dependence of terrestrial predators because the environmental temperature does not vary much across the 65 terrestrial food webs.

### Size-constrained feeding-niche model

Given a predator, we assume that its feeding probability along the axis of body mass is characterised by a hump-shaped curve (Gravel et al., 2013; Rohr et al., 2010; Schneider et al., 2016; Williams et al., 2010). In other words, the predator has the highest feeding probability on prey with an intermediate predator–prey body size ratio (referred to as optimal size ratio). Here we used a Gaussian function to describe the probability that a predator species $i$ feeds on a potential prey species $j$ ($P_{ij}$) (Williams et al., 2010; see Appendix S2 for discussion on alternative functional forms):

**Equation 1.**

$$
P_{ij} = θ_{i}exp(- \frac{{(m_{i} - m_{j} - μ_{i})}^{2}}{2σ_{i}^{2}})
$$

where $m_{i}$ and $m_{j}$ are the log10 body sizes of predator $i$ and prey j, $μ_{i}$ is the log10 optimal size ratio of predator $i$, and $σ_{i}^{2} > 0$ captures the feeding range of predator $i$. $θ_{i}$ is the maximum feeding probability of predator $i$ ($0 \leq θ_{i} \leq 1$), that is, the feeding probability on a prey j matching the optimal size ratio (i.e. $m_{j} = m_{i} - μ_{i}$). The presence of the feeding interaction between species $i$ and $j$ follows a Bernoulli distribution with a success probability $P_{ij}$.

Following the idea of predator dominance, that is, the predator plays a leading role in the foraging process (Brose et al., 2019; Cohen, 1990; Petchey et al., 2008), we assume that each predator has its specific diet preference, including optimal size ratio $μ_{i}$, feeding range $σ_{i}^{2}$ and maximum feeding probability $θ_{i}$ (Figure 1). Thus, we assume that $μ_{i}$, $σ_{i}^{2}$, $θ_{i}$ all depend on the predator body size:

**Equation 2.**

$$
μ_{i}(m_{i}) = β_{μ, 0} + β_{μ, 1}m_{i}
$$

**Equation 3.**

$$
σ_{i}^{2}(m_{i}) = exp(β_{σ^{2}, 0} + β_{σ^{2}, 1}m_{i})
$$

**Equation 4.**

$$
θ_{i}(m_{i}) = \frac{exp(β_{θ, 0} + β_{θ, 1}m_{i})}{1 + exp(β_{θ, 0} + β_{θ, 1}m_{i})}
$$

By definition, $β_{μ, 1}$ $β_{σ^{2}, 1}$ and $β_{θ, 1}$ characterise, respectively, how the optimal size ratio, feeding range, and maximum feeding probability change with predator size. We use an exponential transformation to ensure the foraging range $σ^{2}$ to be positive (Equation 3) and a logit transformation to constrain the maximum predation probability $θ$ within the interval $[0, 1]$ (Equation 4). For comparison, we also fit a model with constant parameters ($μ_{i}$, $σ_{i}^{2}$ and $θ_{i}$) by fixing $β_{μ, 1}$ $β_{σ^{2}, 1}$ and $β_{θ, 1}$ to zero, as assumed in some previous models (Rohr et al., 2010; Schneider et al., 2016). For brevity, we refer to the models with varying feeding-niche parameters (i.e. Equations 2-4) and constant ones (i.e. $β_{μ, 1}$ $β_{σ^{2}, 1}$ and $β_{θ, 1}$ set to 0 in Equations 2-4) as the varying size-ratio model and constant size-ratio model, respectively.

To incorporate the temperature dependence of the feeding niches of aquatic predators, we further consider a model where the optimal size ratio, feeding range and maximum feeding probability change with temperature:

**Equation 5.**

$$
μ_{i}(m_{i}, ∆T_{s}) = β_{μ, 0} + β_{μ, 1}m_{i} + β_{μ, temp}∆T_{s}
$$

**Equation 6.**

$$
σ_{i}^{2}(m_{i}, ∆T_{s}) = exp(β_{σ^{2}, 0} + β_{σ^{2}, 1}m_{i} + β_{σ^{2}, temp}∆T_{s})
$$

**Equation 7.**

$$
θ_{i}(m_{i}, ∆T_{s}) = \frac{exp(β_{θ, 0} + β_{θ, 1}m_{i} + β_{θ, temp}∆T_{s})}{1 + exp(β_{θ, 0} + β_{θ, 1}m_{i} + β_{θ, temp}∆T_{s})}
$$

where $∆T_{s} = T_{s} - T_{avg}$, with $T_{s}$ denoting the average annual temperature (°C) of the food web s, and $T_{avg}$ the average temperature across all aquatic food webs ($T_{avg} =$ 10.94°C).

#### Model fitting

We fit our models for aquatic and terrestrial food webs separately using the Bayesian method (van de Schoot et al., 2021). The likelihood function is written as:

**Equation 8.**

$$
L = \underset{s}{∏}\underset{\begin{matrix} i = 1…k(s) \\ j = 1…n(s) \end{matrix}}{∏}{P_{ij}}^{M_{ji}(s)}{(1 - P_{ij})}^{1 - M_{ji}(s)}
$$

where s denotes different food webs, and k(s) and n(s) are the numbers of predators and animal species, respectively, encountered in the food web s. $M_{ji}(s)$ indicates the presence (1) or absence (0) of feeding relationships. Based on the above likelihood function (Equation 8) and the input data of predation architecture and species' body size, we fitted the varying size-ratio model by allowing all parameters ($β_{μ, 0}$, $β_{σ^{2}, 0}$, $β_{θ, 0}$, $β_{μ, 1}$, $β_{σ^{2}, 1}$,$β_{θ, 1}$) to change freely (i.e. by substituting Equations 2-4 into Equation 8) and the constant size-ratio model by fixing $β_{μ, 1}$ $β_{σ^{2}, 1}$, and $β_{θ, 1}$ at zero. For the varying size-ratio model, we further fitted a model with temperature dependence (i.e. by substituting Equations 5-7 into Equation 8) for aquatic predators. Note that in fitting our SCFN models using Equation 8, we used all aquatic or terrestrial food webs to derive the overall feeding-niche patterns across ecosystems. For comparison, we also fitted the feeding-niche parameters for each individual food web using the varying size-ratio model and obtained the distributions of fitted parameters across different food webs.

To speed up the Bayesian computation, we used noninformative priors for all parameters and implemented the stochastic gradient Nosé-Hoover thermostat (SGNHT) sampling method (Ding et al., 2014). We ran the Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling for 20,000 iterations with 10,000 warm-ups. We obtained the posterior distributions of feeding-niche parameters for the constant size-ratio model ($β_{μ, 0}$, $β_{σ^{2}, 0}$, $β_{θ, 0}$), the varying size-ratio models without ($β_{μ, 0}$, $β_{σ^{2}, 0}$, $β_{θ, 0}$, $β_{μ, 1}$, $β_{σ^{2}, 1}$, $β_{θ, 1}$) and with ($β_{μ, 0}$, $β_{σ^{2}, 0}$, $β_{θ, 0}$, $β_{μ, 1}$, $β_{σ^{2}, 1}$,$β_{θ, 1}$, $β_{μ, temp}$, $β_{σ^{2}, temp}$,$β_{θ, temp}$) temperature effects.

#### Model evaluation by reconstructing feeding links and predation architecture

Based on our SCFN model and parameters derived from the Bayesian method, we performed simulations and reconstructed the feeding links and predation architecture of the 137 empirical food webs. To this end, we used species body size and the estimated feeding-niche parameters (i.e. their posterior means) as inputs. We compared our models to the size quantile-niche model (Table S1; Gravel et al., 2013), which used only information of realised links (see Figure 2) to estimate two key parameters. The first parameter is the predator's feeding centroid (i.e. the mean prey body size), which is derived by linear regressions between (log<sub>10</sub>-) predator and (log<sub>10</sub>-) prey body size; the second parameter is the predator's feeding range (e.g. 5% and 95% boundaries), which is derived by quantile regressions between (log<sub>10</sub>-) predator and (log<sub>10</sub>-) prey body size.

We first evaluated and compared the accuracy of different models in predicting link-level feeding interactions. Given any pair of species $i$ and $j$, a link probability $P_{ij}$ was predicted by the varying or constant size-ratio models. To convert the probability $P_{ij}$ to the presence/absence of a feeding link, we considered that species *i* is a predator of species *j* if $P_{ij}$ is larger than a pre-assigned threshold *p**. Similarly, in the size quantile-niche model, we considered species *i* as a predator of species *j* if the body size of species *j* fell within the predicted feeding ranges for species *i* corresponding to the quantiles $\frac{p^{\times}}{2}$ and $1 - \frac{p^{\times}}{2}$ (note that $p^{\times}$ was fixed at 0.1 in Gravel et al., 2013, but alterable in our study). Obviously, the proportion of predicted ‘presence’ decreased from 1 to 0 as *p* * increased from 0 to 1. We thus used the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve to compare the overall accuracies of different models across varying threshold *p** (Davis & Goadrich, 2006). The ROC curve characterises the information of the confusion matrix under different *p**, namely true positive (TP), true negative (TN), false positive (FP) and false negative (FN). Specifically, the ROC curve exhibits the bivariate relationship between false positive rate (i.e. FPR = FP/[FP + TN]) and true positive rate (i.e. TPR = TP/[TP + FN]) (Fawcett, 2006). A larger area below the ROC curve indicates a better performance of the model. In addition, we also calculated the fraction of the correctly predicted links following Rohr et al. (2016), defined as the TPR corresponding to the value of p* satisfying that FPR equals the false negative rate (FNR = FN/[FN + TP]).

We then evaluated the performance of different models in capturing the community-level predation architecture. For each food web, we randomly generated feeding links according to the predicted *P*<sub>*ij*</sub> and repeated this process for $100$ replicates using both varying and constant size-ratio models. We also reconstructed each food web using the fitted size quantile-niche models by fixing *p** = 0.1 (Gravel et al., 2013). We calculated five structural properties of the reconstructed food web: *Connectance*, the ratio of the number of realised feeding links to that of all possible links; *Clustering Coefficient*, the fraction of connected triangles among all possible feeding triangles (i.e. three-species modules with at least two feeding links); *Generality s.d*. and *Vulnerability s.d*., the standard deviation of predators' generality (the number of prey species per predator species) and prey's vulnerability (the number of predator species per prey species), respectively; *Trophic Similarity*, the average trophic similarity of all predator species pairs. Details of the explanations and calculations of these five structural properties were summarised in Table S2. We calculated the deviation and mean squared error (MSE) of the five structural properties between the observed and simulated food webs. For illustration, let $C$ be any of the five structural properties. For food web $k$, ${\overset{̂}{C}}_{k, l}$ denotes the index of the simulated food web (replicate *l*) and $C_{k}$ denotes the observed value. Then the deviation and MSE of $C$ was calculated as: deviation = $\frac{∑_{l = 1}^{100}({\overset{̂}{C}}_{k, l} - C_{k})}{100}$ and $MSE(C) = \frac{∑_{l = 1}^{100}{({\overset{̂}{C}}_{k, l} - C_{k})}^{2}}{100}$. We performed a paired *t*-test of MSE across the 137 food webs to compare the accuracies of different models.

## RESULTS

Our analyses revealed ecosystem-specific predation architectures. In aquatic ecosystems, the optimal size ratio and feeding range of predators increased with predator size, whereas the maximum feeding probability decreased as the predator size increased (Figure 3; Table 1). In other words, larger predators in aquatic ecosystems tended to have a broader feeding range centering on relatively smaller prey with less intense feeding on optimally sized prey (e.g. blue curve in Figure 1). In terrestrial ecosystems, the optimal size ratio and the maximum feeding probability both decreased with predator size, whereas the feeding range increased with predator size (Figure 3; Table 1). That said, a comparison between the varying and constant size-ratio models revealed only moderate differences for terrestrial ecosystems (Figure 3), indicating that prey–predator interactions in terrestrial ecosystems may be approximated by constant size-ratio models (e.g. orange curve in Figure 1). In addition, these feeding-niche parameters by fitting all food webs together generally fell within the range of parameters fitted for individual food webs, except for $β_{σ^{2}, 1}$ and $β_{θ, 1}$ in terrestrial ecosystems (Table S3). The later inconsistency is possibly due to differences in feeding-niche patterns across food webs (Table S4).

We further found that in aquatic ecosystems, the feeding-niche parameters of predators changed with environmental temperature (Table 1, Figure S1). The optimal size ratio and feeding range of aquatic predators both decreased, whereas the maximum feeding probability increased. It is noted that the dependency of feeding-niche patterns on predator size (i.e. the signs of $β_{μ, 1}$, $β_{σ^{2}, 1}$ and $β_{θ, 1}$) are highly consistent in models with and without temperature (Table 1). Additionally, we explored how the feeding-niche parameters fitted for individual food webs changed with temperature across aquatic ecosystems. Our results showed qualitatively similar temperature dependence of feeding niches, that is, the feeding range increased and maximum feeding probability decreased with increasing temperature, though the optimal size ratio did not change significantly (Figure S2).

Our SCFN models could well recover the link-level feeding relationships between species pairs and the community-level predation architectures observed in empirical food webs (Figures 4 and 5). At the link level, model comparisons showed that whereas all models surpassed the random classifier, the varying size-ratio model performed better than the constant size-ratio model and the size quantile-niche model, particularly in aquatic food webs (Figure 4). Specifically, in aquatic ecosystems, the percentage of correctly predicted links is 66.4% and 68.2% for varying size-ratio models without and with temperature dependence, respectively, 62.4% for the constant size-ratio model, and 59.0% for the size quantile-niche model. In terrestrial ecosystems, the percentage of correctly predicted links is 79.6% for the varying size-ratio model, 79.3% for the constant size-ratio model, and 76.0% for the size quantile-niche model. At the community level, the varying size-ratio model had overall smaller prediction errors compared to the constant size-ratio model (Figure 5), mainly due to the better performance of the varying size-ratio model in aquatic food webs (Figure S3). In terrestrial food webs, the constant size-ratio model performed as well as the varying size-ratio model (Figure S4), consistent with the intuitive similarity between the two models (see Figure 3b). In comparison, the size quantile-niche model led to significantly larger deviations (Figure 5).

## DISCUSSION

Body size constrains have increasingly been incorporated into food web models (summarised in Table S1). Our study, by combining a size-constrained feeding-niche (SCFN) model and a large food web dataset, distinguishes itself from previous studies in three aspects. First, while confirming a systematic relationship between the body sizes of predators and their prey (Brose et al., 2019), our analyses provided novel, systematic quantitative estimates of how the center, range, and probability of the predator feeding niches vary along the axis of prey body size (i.e. log<sub>10</sub> body-mass). Optimised with 137 food webs, these parameter values provide important references for food web modelling in future studies. Second, our analyses revealed striking differences in feeding niches between aquatic and terrestrial predators: the feeding niches of aquatic predators vary significantly with predator size (i.e. a varying size-ratio model), whereas those of terrestrial predators could be approximated by a constant size-ratio model. These contrasting patterns provide justifications for previous models assuming both varying (Gravel et al., 2013; Williams et al., 2010) and constant (Rohr et al., 2010; Schneider et al., 2016) size-ratio patterns, and clarify the types of ecosystems for their applicability. Third, while previous studies have rarely formulated the effect of environment on feeding relationships (Gauzens et al., 2021), our model illustrated the temperature dependence of the feeding niches of aquatic predators. The SCFN model represents a flexible tool for predicting feeding links across environmental gradients, thereby enabling novel trait-based, ecosystem-specific modelling approaches.

Our model characterises the feeding niche of predators by three parameters, namely the centre and range of prey body sizes fed upon, and the maximum feeding probability. These parameters were found to depend on predator size and this dependency differs between aquatic and terrestrial predators (Table 1). In general, larger predators need more energy to sustain their living, reproduction and metabolism (Reiss, 1989; Sibly & Calow, 1986). To sustain long-term energy demand, predators may adapt different foraging strategies (assuming that the amount of resources is constant across different prey size classes, Perkins et al., 2022), for example, eating either large-sized prey that can provide higher energy to increase energy intake or small-sized but abundant ones to increase the predation success probability (Persson et al., 1998; Schoener, 1971). Our results showed that in aquatic ecosystems, large predators generally prefer relatively smaller prey, but in terrestrial ecosystems, the optimal size ratio of terrestrial predators changed slightly with predator size. This contrast is in line with the hypothesis that aquatic predators are more limited by their gape size than terrestrial predators (Brose et al., 2006; Hairston & Hairston, 1993). Although group-hunting by predators can overcome size constraints in both terrestrial (e.g. *Canis lupus*) and aquatic (e.g. *Orcinus orcas*) habitats (Hirt et al., 2020), our results suggested that such strategies might have larger overall effects on the predation mode of terrestrial predators. Indeed, we found that very large terrestrial predators may feed on prey of similar or even larger size (Figure 3), contradicting with the traditional recognition that predators tend to feed on smaller-sized prey (Brose, 2010; Brose et al., 2006; Cohen et al., 1993). One possible explanation for this feeding-niche pattern is that even though the gape size can set the upper limit on ingestible prey size, this restriction could be lifted if terrestrial predators can tear up their food for consumption, whereas aquatic predators need to swallow it to avoid the sink of killed prey (Brose et al., 2006). Besides, our results showed that for both aquatic and terrestrial predators, their feeding ranges increased, and the maximum feeding probability decreased, with predator size. This indicates that larger predators broaden their feeding ranges to balance their higher demands; such broader feeding ranges could intensify the competition between larger predators due to increased niche overlap, and result in a reduced feeding probability on species within their feeding range.

Recent studies suggested that food web structure could be influenced by environmental conditions, such as temperature (Gauzens et al., 2021; O'Gorman et al., 2019). One possible explanation is that predators may adapt their feeding niches in response to environmental changes. Our model framework and large-scale dataset allows researchers to test such a hypothesis across aquatic ecosystems. We found that in warmer places aquatic predators tend to narrow their feeding ranges and target more on energetically-rewarding, larger-sized prey (Portalier et al., 2019). This may be explained by the higher metabolic demand of predators in warmer conditions (Fussmann et al., 2014), which drives them to select prey that can provide higher energy (i.e. larger-sized prey) (Gordon et al., 2018). It should be noted, however, that the feeding-niche patterns vary only moderately along the temperature gradient, and the incorporation of temperature improves predictions on feeding links by only 1.8% (Figure 4).

Parameterised by the body size of predators and their prey, our SCFN model could recover both link- and community-level properties of the predation architecture across 137 food webs. In aquatic ecosystems, the varying size-ratio models (both with and without temperature effects) correctly predict 66–68% of all feeding links, outperforming both the constant size-ratio model (62.4%) and the size quantile-niche model (60%). In terrestrial ecosystems, the varying and constant size-ratio models perform equally well (79.6% vs. 79.3%), both outperforming the size quantile-niche model (76%; Figures 5, S3 and S4). In particular, the size quantile-niche model predicts higher connectance than observations because it can only produce perfect intervality, that is, predators feed on all species falling within their feeding range. In comparison, in our models, predators feed on any potential prey species within their feeding range following a Bernoulli distribution (with a probability $P_{ij}$), therefore generating non-interval networks which are the norm in nature (Eklöf et al., 2013; Stouffer et al., 2006). It is worth noting that our varying size-ratio models use a dozen of parameters (6 for aquatic and 6 for terrestrial ecosystems) and their accuracies are assessed for >130,000 realised links across 137 food webs. In this regard, our models achieve similar accuracies on substantially more food webs, compared with previous models fitted to individual food webs (e.g. Williams et al., 2010).

Lastly, we add two caveats on our results. First, in this study we have focused on individual-based predatory links, which is a central ingredient, but not the whole, of ecological food webs. Food webs include several other types of interactions (e.g. herbivorous, parasitic, filter feeding, etc.), and these interactions may be regulated differently by size constraints and by other species traits (e.g. plant nutrient content for herbivory) (Gravel et al., 2016). This implies that our results may not apply to other types of interactions. However, our model provides a flexible framework that could be extended to incorporate constraints by different traits, for instance by modelling grazing probability of herbivores as a function of plant nutrient content in light of ecological stoichiometry (Sterner & Elser, 2002). Second, while we have fitted together the 72 aquatic or 65 terrestrial food webs to obtain the overall feeding-niche patterns, considerable variation exists across different food webs (Table S3). Such variation may be accounted for by fitting food webs individually. This approach can indeed improve the overall prediction accuracy further by 1–3%, but it involves a vast number of parameters (408 and 384 for aquatic and terrestrial systems, respectively). Instead, variation across food webs may be explained by abiotic and biotic factors, for example, temperature (Table 1) or species richness (Table S4). Using aquatic food webs, we have illustrated how our model framework can incorporate the effect of temperature, but other environmental factors can be similarly incorporated.

## CONCLUSION

Size-constrained models have proven powerful in characterising feeding relationships in empirical food webs (Brose et al., 2006; Rall et al., 2012). Using a comprehensive food web database, we tested the dependence of arrangement of feeding niche on predator size across ecosystem types and temperature gradient. Our analyses revealed contrasting size-dependent feeding niches between aquatic and terrestrial predators. In aquatic ecosystems, larger predators tend to feed on relatively smaller prey with a larger feeding range and lower predation probability, whereas in terrestrial ecosystems, larger predators feed on ranges in prey size that are nearly constant relative to their own sizes. Thus, the varying and constant size-ratio models may be adopted for modelling aquatic and terrestrial food webs, respectively. The feeding niches of aquatic predators also change along the gradient of temperature, although incorporation of temperature only improves model performance moderately. Combined, our results advocate the incorporation of measurable species traits and environmental factors in food web modelling.

While a number of other models have been developed to model feeding links, most of them require estimation of a larger number of parameters representing species-specific latent traits (Table S1). Although such approaches provide good explanatory power for empirical data, they often lack predictive power on new species or new communities. Parameterised with a global dataset of high-quality food webs, our size-constrained feeding-niche model provides a promising tool to predict feeding relationships under different biotic (e.g. species trait distribution) or abiotic (e.g. temperature) conditions. In the future, the general formulation of our model and the large amount of food web data available will allow to integrate more ecological factors into the definition of species' trophic niches. Ultimately, this flexible approach enables a better understanding of the different forces driving the structure of food webs, as well as refining predictions on the responses of ecological communities to environmental changes.
