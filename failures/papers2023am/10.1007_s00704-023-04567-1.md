## **Abstract**

Based on the ERA5 reanalysis dataset, we analyzed the changes in extreme climate indices on the MP from 1981 to 2020, and the relationship between the extreme climate indices and atmospheric circulation indices. The results showed that (1) for temperature, (a) in terms of hot events, the inter-annual variation trends of summer days, warm days, and warm nights have significantly increased across the entire MP, with the central region experiencing a higher probability of extreme hot events. The inter-decadal variation of extreme hot indices showed a considerable increase from the period of 1991 to 2000 to the period of 2001 to 2010. (b) In terms of cold events, a substantial decrease in extreme cold events was revealed, with a great decrease from the period of 1981 to 1990 to the period of 1991 to 2000. (2) For precipitation, there was a decrease in extreme wet indices from 1981 to 2020. The simple precipitation intensity index (SDII) and total precipitation in wet days (PRCPTOT) exhibited a significant descending trend in the northern and northeastern MP. Decadal variations showed a decrease in extreme precipitation events, with a significant decline observed between the period of 1991 to 2000 and the period of 2001 to 2010. (3) For the relationship between the extreme climate indices and atmospheric circulation indices, extreme temperature indices are likely influenced by polar vortices, with the Asian polar vortex area index (APVAI) negatively correlated with extreme hot indices and the Asian polar vortex intensity index (APVII) positively/negatively correlated with most extreme hot and cold indices. Asian zonal circulation index (AZCI) showed significant correlations with most extreme hot and cold indices. Extreme precipitation indices only showed significant negative correlations with the Atlantic Meridional mode (AMM).

According to the Sixth Assessment Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC AR6), the intensity and frequency of climate extremes have increased since pre-industrial times (Seneviratne et al. 2021). High-temperature extremes and heavy precipitation were predicted to be more frequent in the future. Climate model results indicated that high-temperature extremes and heavy precipitation will occur more frequently in the future (Easterling et al. 2000), which means that the frequency of hazards induced by climate extremes will increase as well. Affected by global climate change and the location of the Mongolian Plateau, the extreme climate of Mongolian Plateau has changed significantly in the past decades. Some research has been conducted on the Inner Mongolian Plateau (IMP). It is pointed out that the indices characterizing extreme low-temperature events decreased, and those describing extreme high-temperature events increased in Inner Mongolia from the 1960s to 2010s, and the slopes have become steeper since the 1990s (Yan et al. 2014; Chen and Zhao 2017). The regional average of extreme precipitation indices shows a downtrend, and the trends of indices present significant spatial differences and inter-annual variation (You et al. 2010; Li et al. 2017; Chun et al. 2019). There will be more extreme precipitation in the dry area on the IMP (Li et al. 2018).

Some research focused on Mongolia or the entire Mongolian Plateau (MP). Almost all the extreme temperature indices show warming trends on the MP. The number of cool days and nights increases in all seasons, while the number of hot days and nights increases only in summer in Mongolia (Dashkhuu et al. 2015; Liu et al. 2019). The trends of the extreme temperature indices are consistent with the trends on other plateaus, such as the Loess Plateau and the QinghaiTibet Plateau (Du et al. 2013; Yan et al. 2015). The trends and the spatial distribution of precipitation extremes seem to be more complex than those of the temperature, and the decadal precipitation shows a downtrend from east to west (Wang et al. 2016). In most part of Mongolian Plateau, the extreme precipitation indices, including simple precipitation intensity index (SDII) and heavy precipitation index (R95p), decreased from 1982 to 2012 (Ren et al. 2022). Projections based on (Coupled Model Intercomparison Project 5) CMIP5 model output show that drought will happen more often under the RCP4.5 and RCP8.5 scenarios, and the

drought in Mongolia will be more severe than that in Inner Mongolia (Li et al. 2020).

The change of regional patterns of circulation can affect the extreme temperature trends (Horton et al. 2015). The interaction between arctic teleconnections and other process could affect mid-latitude summer circulation and cause more climate extremes (Coumou et al. 2018). The expansion of polar vortex area leads to the increase of cold days/nights in most parts of China except for Northeast China (Zhang et al. 2014). Mongolian Plateau is located in East Asia and highly susceptible to polar vortices. Northern hemisphere zonal circulation (NHZC) showed a significant correlation with the precipitation in northeastern Mongolia (Wang 2006). Pacific decadal oscillation (PDO), Atlantic multidecadal oscillation (AMO), and sea surface temperature (SST) could influence the drought variation on the eastern MP (Bao et al. 2015). According to Tong et al. (2019), El Niño-southern oscillation (ENSO) (Arctic oscillation) AO and Indian Ocean dipole (IOD) showed significant influences to the extreme cold and warm indices in Inner Mongolia.

Over the past two decades, hazards caused by climate extremes have occurred many times, including the severe winter of 2000–2002 and 2010 (Fernández-Giménez et al. 2012; Iijima and Hori 2016). It is of great importance to analyze the change of climate extremes over the whole MP. However, the spatial distribution of the meteorological stations on the MP is uneven. Previous studies have mostly studied the extreme climate of Mongolian Plateau based on station data. The research on extreme climate change in areas limited by high altitude and other factors without meteorological stations is not comprehensive enough on the MP. Therefore, the ERA5 reanalysis dataset is applied in this study to make up for the sparse meteorological stations. This study aims to resolve the following two questions: (1) Use the ERA5 dataset to calculate extreme indices and analyze changes of extreme indices. (2) Investigate the relationship between extreme climate indices and circulation indices and explore possible mechanisms.

## **2.2.1 Observation data**

Meteorological station data sets used in this study were the China National Terrestrial Meteorological Station Basic Meteorological Element Day Value Data Set (V3.0) (http:// data. cma. cn/) and the Global Historical Climatology Network-Daily (GHCN Daily) Version 3 (Menne et al. 2012a, b) (https:// www. ncei. noaa. gov/ access/ metad ata/ landi ng- page/ bin/ iso? id= gov. noaa. ncdc% 3AC00 861). Both data sets were under quality control. In all, 134 stations from 1981 to 2020 were used, with 94 stations in the Inner Mongolia Autonomous Region and 40 stations in Mongolia (Fig. 1). Basic meteorological elements include temperature (maximum and minimum) and precipitation (with a precision of 0.1 mm).

## **2.2.2 ERA5 data**

The reanalysis data used in this study were obtained from the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather

Forecasts (ECMWF) ERA5 hourly data on single levels. This dataset has a spatial resolution of 0.25° and provides hourly estimates of dozens of variables on a global scale. Hourly data from 1981 to 2020 was selected in this study. Two variables, 2m temperature, and total precipitation were selected to calculate and analyze the extreme temperature and precipitation indices on the MP. The ERA5 hourly data was converted into daily data using MATLAB software programming. For temperature data every 24 h, we calculate the average, maximum, and minimum values to obtain the daily average, maximum, and minimum temperatures. For precipitation data every 24 h, we calculate the sum to obtain the daily precipitation.

## **2.3.1 Extreme climate indices**

Extreme climate indices defined by the Expert Team on Climate Change Detection and Indicators (ETCCDI) (http:// etccdi. pacif iccli mate. org/ indic es_ def. shtml) (Alexander et al. 2006) were used in this study. The 27 core extreme indices are based on daily temperature and precipitation. In this article, sixteen extreme climate indices were selected for analysis, as listed in Table 1. The extreme climate indices were calculated in MATLAB using the ERA5 grid data on annual and decadal scales for further trend analysis.

The raster-averaged annual, decadal,and trends of extreme indices on the MP were calculated. We applied Sen’s slope method to calculate trends and MK test method to calculate the significant probability value of the trends. To investigate decadal changes of indices, we divided the research period into four equal periods calling period 1 (1981–1990), period 2 (1991–2000), period 3 (2001–2010), and period 4 (2011–2020), and calculated the difference between two adjacent periods.

## **2.3.3 Stepwise regression**

Stepwise regression is a statistical technique used to select the most relevant variables for predicting an outcome variable. It is an automated iterative procedure that enters or removes variables based on their contribution to the prediction model. This technique has gained popularity in recent years as it provides a more parsimonious and efficient way of modeling a large number of predictors. We conducted a stepwise linear regression using the circulation index as the independent variable and different extreme indices as the input variables to explore the relationship between atmospheric circulation and climate extremes. Stepwise regression was conducted in MATLAB.

## **3.1 The overview of temperature and precipitation on the Mongolian Plateau**

For temperature, a significant increase was found in the past 40 years (Fig. 3a and c). All temperature measurements exhibited increasing trends, with rates of 0.40 °C/decade, 0.37 °C/decade, and 0.38 °C/decade, respectively (Fig. 3a). Average temperature significantly increased during 1981–2020 on the almost entire MP at the 0.05 level and insignificantly decreased on small part of the southwestern MP (Fig. 3c). The increasing trend was more obvious in the central MP, along the Khangai mountains.

For precipitation, the raster-averaged annual total precipitation in MP is presented, ranging from 240 to 400 mm during the 1981–2020 period. The change in annual precipitation was complex, increasing from 1980 to 1990, decreasing from 1990 to 2010, and then ascending again after 2010. Notably, precipitations in 2000–2010 were the lowest observed throughout the entire study period. In general, precipitation in the first two decades is more than that in the last two decades (Fig. 3b). Precipitation significantly decreased at the 0.05 level on the northern, northwestern, and eastern MP and increased insignificantly on the southwestern MP. Northern and northeastern parts of the MP are facing more severe threat of water shortage (Fig. 3d).

## **3.3 Relationship between extreme indices and circulation indices**

In order to attribute the change of extreme indices, we conducted stepwise regression between ocean–atmosphere oscillations indices and extreme indices. All the indices had been standardized to compare the impact of different circulation indices. The coefficients and adjust _R_[2] of stepwise regression are shown in Table 6. Atlantic meridional mode (AMM) mainly showed negative correlation with extreme precipitation indices and was excluded in the stepwise regression with temperature indices. AO only showed negative correlation with Tn10p and Tx10p and PDO only showed negative correlation with SU25 and TR20. APVAI showed negative correlation mainly with extreme

hot indices. APVII and AZCI mainly showed significant correlation with percentage-based indices and the absolute value of the coefficients of AZCI is generally greater than APVII. The results suggest that the changes of temperature indices and precipitation indices are affected by different ocean–atmosphere oscillation patterns. Adjusted _R_[2] represents the degree to which the independent variable explains the dependent variable in stepwise regression. In general, the adjusted _R_[2] is higher when the independent variable is a temperature index than when the independent variable is a precipitation index. However, some adjusted _R_[2] are no more than 0.3, especially in the regression equation of extreme precipitation indices. It indicates that the change of extreme indices is probably partially attributable to large-scale circulation.

## **4 Conclusions and discussions**

The extreme climate of MP has changed significantly in 1981–2020. Based on the ERA5 reanalysis dataset to calculate the extreme climate indices, this study mainly draws the following conclusions:

**Changes of temperature and precipitation on the MP** Average, maximum, and minimum temperatures showed increasing trends during 1981–2020, with rates of 0.40 °C/decade, 0.37 °C/decade, and 0.38 °C/decade, respectively. The variation trends are similar to previous study results (Ling et al. 2008; Liu et al. 2016). From the perspective of spatial pattern, average temperature significantly increased during 1981–2020 on the almost entire MP at the 0.05 level and insignificantly decreased on small part of the southwestern MP. The rising trend of temperature on the central MP, where the Khangai Mountains are located, is higher than that in other regions. The lower the temperature, the faster and more significant the increase. Total precipitation ranged from 240 to 400 mm on the MP during 1981–2020 and presented a changing pattern of inter-decadal fluctuations. Precipitation significantly decreased at the 0.05 level on the northern, northwestern, and eastern MP and increased insignificantly on the southwestern MP. Northern and northeastern parts of the MP are facing more severe threat of water shortage. Liu et al. (2016) obtained similar spatial pattern based on the CMIP5 model.

**Changes of extreme hot indices on the MP** The degree of extreme hot events facing the MP has gradually increased over the past decades (Dashkhuu et al. 2015; Wang et al.

2016; Yan et al. 2014; Chen and Zhao 2017). The five indices used to characterize extreme hot events, including summer days (SU25), tropical nights (TR20), warm days (Tx90p), warm nights (Tn90p), and warm spell duration index (WSDI), have all increased significantly with trends of 5.19 days/decade, 1.58 days/decade, 2.45 %/decade, 2.7 %/ decade, and 1.41days/decade during 1981–2020. The trends 2015) and are different from the results of Dashkhuu et al. (Wang et al. (2016), and the reason may be that these two studies were based on site data, while this study was based on ERA data. The spatial pattern of the inter-annual variation trends of SU25, Tx90p, and Tn90p is similar, increasing significantly on almost the entire MP. The magnitude of upward trends of these three indices in the central part of the MP is higher than those around the MP. Therefore, the probability of the central MP facing extreme hot events in the future is higher than that of other regions. Unlike the other three indices, tropical nights only increased significantly on the southwestern MP, where deserts are located, and showed no obvious trend in other parts of the MP. However, studies based on Princeton University Global Daily Temperature and Precipitation Dataset (http:// hydro logy. princ eton. edu/ data/) seemed to get a totally different result that extreme hot indices mainly decreased on the central MP (Ren et al. 2022). This conclusion is different from that of this study and previous study (Dashkhuu et al. 2015; Wang et al. 2016).

As for inter-decadal variation, nearly all the extreme hot indices rose the fastest from period 2 to period 3. The interdecadal variation of the hot day indices in the northern part of the MP is greater, while the inter-decadal variation of the hot night indices in the southern part is greater at the stage when the extreme heat index changes most violently,

**Changes of extreme cold indices on the MP** The frequency and duration of extreme cold events have been decreasing since 1981. This is supported by the significant downward trends exhibited by all five extreme cold indices (frost days (FD), icing days (ID0), cold days (Tx10p), cold nights (Tn10p), and cold spell duration index (CSDi)) with trends of − 2.94 days/decade, − 3.25 days/decade, − 0.63 %/decade, − 0.86 %/decade, and − 0.19 days/decade over the period of 1981–2020. The trends of FD and Tn10p are close to while the trends of ID0 and Tx10p is smaller than the results of Liu et al. (2019). The fastest decrease in extreme cold indices occurred from period 1 to period 2, except for FD which decreased the fastest from period 2 to period 3.

**Changes of extreme precipitation indices on the MP** The results showed that all the extreme precipitation indices decreased except CDD during the period of 1981–2020, indicating that extreme precipitation on the MP decreased both in duration and intensity. SDII, PRCPTOT, R10mm, CDD, CWD, and R95p showed trends of − 3.3 mm/day/ decade, − 17.45 mm/decade, − 0.44 days/decade, 2.12 days/ decade, − 0.24 days/decade, and − 3.57 mm/decade. The spatial pattern of trends of extreme precipitation indices from 1981 to 2020 was also examined. CWD mainly presented a downward trend in the northwest mountainous areas, while CDD mainly presented an upward trend on the central MP. R10mm and R95p mainly decreased in the northern and eastern MP and showed no obvious change trend in the southwest of the MP. due to its desert region with little annual precipitation. Chun et al. (2019) obtained similar spatial pattern of extreme precipitation indices on the IMP, while the results of Ren et al. (2022) was totally different; extreme precipitation indices mainly decreasing on the western MP.

In terms of decadal variation of different extreme precipitation indices, almost all the indices increased slightly in period 2 compared with period 1 and decreased intensely in period 3 compared with period 2. The inter-decadal fluctuations of extreme precipitation indices were greater in areas with high precipitation levels. Based on the analysis, it can be concluded that extreme precipitation in the MP has been decreasing, and the magnitude of decline between the second and third periods is the largest.

**Relationships between extreme climate indices and atmos‑ pheric circulation indices** We conducted stepwise regression between extreme climate indices and atmospheric circulation indices. The results revealed that extreme precipitation indices were negatively correlated with Atlantic meridional mode (AMM). AMM may have an impact on precipitation on the MP by affecting the East Asian jet stream. AO showed significant correlation with Tn10p and Tx10p and PDO with SU25 and TR20. Some studies have also reached similar conclusions. Extreme positive AO pattern will cause extreme cold events in East Asia (Kim et al. 2022). It has also been found that there is a good correlation between PDO and the extreme high temperature in North China (Zhang et al. 2020). APVAI mainly showed significant negative correlations with extreme hot indices. APVII (AZCI) mainly showed negative (positive) correlations with extreme hot indices and positive (negative) correlations with extreme cold indices. Although there are few studies on the impact of polar vortex index on extreme climate on the MP, some studies have reached similar conclusions in nearby area (Zhong et al. 2017). The results suggest that changes in temperature and precipitation indices are likely influenced by different ocean-atmosphere oscillation patterns. The adjusted _R_[2] values indicate that the independent variables explain the dependent variables to a certain extent. The adjusted _R_[2] of extreme temperature indices are around 0.45, which means the selected indices could well explain the change of extreme climate on the MP. Nevertheless, the adjusted _R_[2] values were relatively low, suggesting that the changes in extreme indices are only partially attributable to large-scale circulation. Overall, the results of this study enhance our understanding of the complex relationships between extreme precipitation indices and ocean-atmosphere oscillations.
