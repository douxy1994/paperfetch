# Grassland degradation affects the response of soil bacterial and plant but not fungal diversity to nitrogen addition

## Abstract

1. Atmospheric nitrogen (N) deposition poses serious threats to plant and soil microbial communities in natural grasslands, which can have consequences on ecosystem functioning. However, the effects of atmospheric N deposition can be modulated by grassland degradation, defined here as soil erosion, soil nutrient decline and loss of plant diversity, which has become a global threat to the functioning of these ecosystems. It remains poorly understood whether the responses of plant and soil microbial communities to N deposition vary with grassland degradation.
2. We conducted a 9-year multilevel (0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 g N m<sup>−2</sup> year<sup>−1</sup>) N enrichment experiment at four sites in Inner Mongolia, China. We evaluated grassland degradation using a compound index (grassland degradation index) and classified grassland sites into four categories: non-degraded, moderately, severely and extremely degraded grasslands. One site was selected for each category, and then three replicates were set up within each site. N was added in the form of urea.
3. We first observed that the response of soil bacterial diversity to N addition was stronger in degraded grasslands than in non-degraded grasslands, whereas the response of soil fungal diversity was not affected by degradation. Meanwhile, N enrichment had a weaker impact on plant diversity in degraded grasslands compared to non-degraded grasslands. The controlling factors that affected the responses of soil microbial diversity and community composition to N addition were inconsistent among grasslands with different degradation status. Soil pH was the main driver in non-degraded grasslands, and plant community composition became an important driver in degraded grasslands.
4. *Synthesis*. Our findings highlighted that grassland degradation affected the response of bacterial and plant communities to N deposition and provided new information for the management and conservation of biodiversity in degraded grasslands.

## 1 INTRODUCTION

Grasslands, which cover approximately 40% of the Earth's surface, play a critical role in biodiversity conservation and global biogeochemical cycles (Bardgett et al., 2021). However, natural grasslands are facing severe degradation world-wide (Bardgett et al., 2021), which means soil erosion, soil nutrient decline and loss of plant diversity (UNCCD, 2012), that ultimately changes biodiversity (Cubillos et al., 2016; Rey et al., 2011). Furthermore, the increased atmospheric nitrogen (N) deposition over the past several decades (Galloway et al., 2004) also poses a serious threat to plant (Clark & Tilman, 2008; Isbell et al., 2013) and microbial diversity (Leff et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2020) in natural grasslands. However, it remains largely unknown how biodiversity responds to N enrichment in grasslands with varying degradation status. This may represent a knowledge gap for comprehensively evaluating the impact of N enrichment on grassland biodiversity.

Much research effort has been devoted to evaluating the mechanisms that mediate the responses of soil microbial communities to N enrichment in natural grasslands, providing valuable information on the underlying processes (Wang, Liu, et al., 2018; Zeng et al., 2016). First, increasing N availability could directly lead to the extinction of microbial species that are adapted to nutrient-deficient soils and alter competition patterns among taxa with different nutrient acquisition abilities (Cline et al., 2018; Wang, Liu, et al., 2018), with important consequences for ecosystem function (Cline et al., 2018) and stability (Copley, 2000; Zhang et al., 2022). Second, soil acidification after N enrichment has shaped the diversity and structure of soil microorganisms (Delgado-Baquerizo et al., 2017; Fierer, 2017) because soil pH may impose direct stress on microbial cells, exerting selection pressure for certain taxa over others (Lauber et al., 2008). Third, under increasing N inputs, loss of plant diversity and alteration of community composition could decrease the diversity of substrate resources, plant hosts for symbiotic and pathogenic microbes and microclimate variability, and thus could alter microbial community structure (Klironomos et al., 2011; Lange et al., 2015; van der Heijden et al., 2008).

Compared to non-degraded grasslands, responses of soil microbial communities to N enrichment could be different in degraded grasslands due to alterations in soil physiochemical properties (i.e. soil pH and N availability) and vegetation characteristics (i.e. plant biomass, plant diversity and community composition) (Wu et al., 2021). Compared to non-degraded grasslands, soil bacterial diversity can decline significantly at lower levels of N input due to the greater reduction in soil pH in degraded grasslands (Zeng et al., 2018), indicating the higher susceptibility of soil bacteria in response to N addition. Regarding soil fungi, Song et al. (2022) demonstrated that high application of N exerted negative effects on soil fungal diversity due to substrates with a lower C:N ratio, while low N addition rates had no effects in a temperate grassland in Inner Mongolia. However, in degraded grasslands, N enrichment would exacerbate phosphorus (P) limitation (Dong et al., 2019), possibly resulting in higher susceptibility of soil fungal communities to N addition in degraded grasslands compared to non-degraded grasslands. In plant communities, degradation can result in lower vegetation coverage, and light becomes less limited for plant communities compared to non-degraded grasslands (Bai et al., 2010); this may result in lower susceptibility of plant diversity to N enhancement in degraded grasslands compared to non-degraded grasslands. However, it remains unclear whether the responses of soil bacterial, fungal and plant diversity to N addition will change in degraded grasslands, limiting our ability to fully understand the effect of N addition on grassland biodiversity.

Multilevel (0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 g N m<sup>−2</sup> year<sup>−1</sup>) N addition experiments have been conducted on grasslands with different degradation status (non-degraded, moderately, severely and extremely degraded grassland) in Inner Mongolia, China. We aimed to determine: (i) whether grassland degradation would alter the response of soil bacterial, fungal and plant communities to N addition, and (ii) the mechanisms underlying responses of soil bacterial and fungal communities to N addition in grasslands with different degrees of degradation. We hypothesized that (i) soil bacterial and fungal communities exhibit stronger responses to N addition in the degraded grasslands compared to the non-degraded grasslands due to the greater magnitude of reduction in soil pH and increased P limitation, while plant communities show a contrasting pattern because of lower vegetation coverage and less light limitation in the degraded grasslands. (ii) Soil pH and N availability would predominantly affect responses of soil microbial communities to N addition in the non-degraded grasslands. Due to the low reliance of plants on microbes in nutrient-sufficient environments (Wei et al., 2013), plant variables would have few effects on soil microbial communities in the non-degraded grassland. However, both soil properties (soil pH and N availability) and plant communities would jointly regulate soil microbial community responses in degraded grasslands.

## 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

### 2.1 Study site and experimental design

Our study site is located in a semi-arid steppe, Inner Mongolia, China (42°23′–42°29′ N, 116°43′–117°16′ E), 1640 m above sea level. In this region, the mean annual temperature is about −1.4°C and the mean annual precipitation is approximately 400 mm, with 76% of the precipitation falling from May to August. The type of soil is determined as Chernozem according to the classification of the Food and Agriculture Organization, whose surface layer consists mainly of sand and silt. However, grasslands in this area have suffered from degradation due to cropland expansion and overgrazing. Consequently, there is a reduction in plant productivity and species richness, soil erosion and soil nutrient loss (Liu et al., 2018). We chose four sites where each area has three replicates and represents a degradation status for investigation on flat land with slopes <5°C and elevation differences between them of less than 23 m. The experimental sites were positioned <5 km apart, ensuring similar climate and native vegetation before degradation. A fieldwork permit was not required.

The grassland degradation index (GDI) quantitatively assesses the degree of grassland degradation (Dong et al., 2019), which was calculated as:

**Equation 1.**

$$
\begin{matrix} GDI = (P_{1} \times 1 / 3 + P_{2} \times 2 / 3 + P_{3}) \times 1 / 3 + (STC \times 1 / 2 + STN \times 1 / 2) \\ \times 1 / 3 + (1 / SAND) \times 1 / 3, \end{matrix}
$$

where P1, P2 and P3 represent the relative cover of annual pioneer species, degradation indicator species and climax species respectively (Table S1); STC and STN represent the soil total carbon and nitrogen concentrations respectively; and SAND represents the soil sand content. The parameters in the above equation were calculated as averages of three replicates at each grassland site. According to the GDI evaluation, these grasslands were divided into the following four degradation levels: non-degraded (with a GDI of 0.91), moderately degraded (with a GDI of 0.64), severely degraded (with a GDI of 0.54) and extremely degraded (with a GDI of 0.38) grassland. We also performed a principal component analysis with plant and soil characteristics. Both methods showed that these sites were clearly distinguished from each other (Figure S1).

Eighteen 6 × 6 m plots that received N addition since 2011 were organized into three blocks, which were randomly established at each site. There were 2 m buffer zones between adjacent plots. Each of the six plots randomly received one of the six levels of N fertilization (0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 g N m<sup>−2</sup> year<sup>−1</sup>). Previous studies have reported that substantial changes in soil microbial diversity occurred when N input was above 30 g N m<sup>−2</sup> year<sup>−1</sup> (Dong et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2020). This wide range of N application rates was scheduled to explore plant and microbial performance under N enrichment. N was added in the form of urea mixed with 10 L of water and sprayed four times a year, from May to August, and the same volume of water was applied to the control plot.

### 2.2 Plant communities and soil chemical properties

During the peak growing season in 2019, we surveyed plant communities within a 1 × 1 m quadrant established in each plot, and the plot level richness was estimated as the number of plant species present in the quadrant. Subsequently, all above-ground plant biomass was clipped on an arbitrarily chosen 0.5 × 0.5 m quadrat within each plot and then oven-dried to a constant mass at 65°C for 48 h.

Five arbitrarily located soil cores (10 cm deep) were collected from each plot and combined into one sample in August 2019. We removed roots and stones by sieving through 2 mm mesh and measured root biomass by drying the separated roots. Soil texture was determined with a laser particle size analyser (Mastersizer 2000; Malvern). The soil water content was estimated using the gravimetric method, weighing soil samples before and after oven drying at 105°C for 48 h. The soil pH values were determined using a pH metre in the soil suspension (ratio: 1:2.5 w/v) of each sample. The inorganic N concentration was estimated using a continuous flow analyser (AA3; Seal), following extraction with 2 M KCL (Hood-Nowotny et al., 2010). STC and STN were determined with an elemental analyser (Vario EL III; Elementar), while soil total phosphorus (STP) was measured using the H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>-HCLO<sub>4</sub> fusion method (Sparks et al., 2020).

### 2.3 Microbial biomass measurements

We used the chloroform fumigation extraction method with the extraction of 0.5 M K<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>(ratio: 1:2.5 w/v) to estimate microbial biomass carbon (MBC) and microbial biomass nitrogen (MBN), which were calculated by subtracting the C and N concentrations of the unfumigated samples from the fumigated samples. 0.45 and 0.54 were considered as extraction efficiency factors for MBC and MBN, respectively (Joergensen & Mueller, 1996).

### 2.4 Measurements of the microbial community

DNA was extracted from 0.5 g of fresh soil of each sample using the FastDNA Spin Kit (MP Biomedical), following the manufacturer's instructions. The quality of DNA was detected by 1% agarose gel electrophoresis, and the concentration and purity of DNA were determined by NanoDrop2000. To profile soil bacterial communities, we amplified the hypervariable region V4–V5 of the 16S rRNA gene with the set of primers 515F (5′-GTGCCAGCMGCCGCGGTAA-3′) and 907R (5′-CCGTCAATTCCTTTGAGTTT-3′). For fungal communities, we amplified the hypervariable region V3–V4 of the ITS gene with the primer set ITS3-F (5′-GCATCGATGAAGAACGCAGC-3′) and ITS4-R (5′-TCCTCCGCTTATTGATATGC-3′). The PCR amplicons were recovered using 2% agarose gel and purified using the AxyPrep DNA Gel Extraction Kit (Axygen Biosciences) and then quantified with QuantiFluorTM-ST (Promega). Subsequently, the purified PCR products were sequenced on the Illumina MiSeq platform (Illumina).

Raw sequences were processed using QIIME 1.7.0 (Caporaso et al., 2012). The sequences containing ambiguous bases with lengths shorter than 200 bp, as well as singletons, were deleted. The remaining 16S rRNA and ITS gene amplicons sequences were clustered into operational taxonomic units (OTUs) at a 97% similarity level using UPARSE 7.1 (http://drive5.com/uparse/), and the chimaeras were eliminated. The Ribosomal Database Project Classifier tool (Wang et al., 2007) and UNITE 7.2 database (Nilsson et al., 2019) were used to taxonomically categorize bacterial and fungal OTU sequences, respectively, and OTUs that were not classified as bacteria or fungi were not included in subsequent analyses. We arbitrarily selected a subset of 23,669 sequences and 37,521 sequences per sample from bacterial 16S rRNA and fungal ITS datasets, respectively, to standardize the sampling effort. All sequence data were deposited in NCBI under project accession numbers PRJNA857751 and PRJNA858165.

### 2.5 Statistical analyses

Two-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was performed to estimate N addition, degradation and their interaction effects on soil properties (soil texture, soil water content, soil pH, soil inorganic N, STC, STN, STP, soil C:N ratio, soil N:P ratio and soil C:P ratio), plant variables and microbial biomass, followed by Duncan's test for multiple comparisons. Additionally, one-way ANOVA was performed to determine the effects of N addition on soil bacterial, fungal and plant diversity, and relative abundance of certain microbial taxa, followed by Duncan's test for multiple comparisons. ANOVA based on the general linear models (GLMs) was used to identify independent and interactive effects of N addition rate and grassland degradation on soil abiotic and biotic variables. Nonmetric multidimensional scaling (NMDS) analysis based on Bray–Curtis dissimilarity matrices was performed to visualize the bacterial, fungal and plant community structure among different sites using the R package vegan (Oksanen et al., 2022). Permutational multivariate analysis of variance was selected to examine the effects of N addition, degradation and their interactions on microbial community structure using the function adonis in R package vegan (Oksanen et al., 2022). All results were considered statistically significant when *p* < 0.05.

Random forest analysis was performed to analyse and coordinate the contributions of soil properties (soil water content, soil pH, STC, STN, STP and soil inorganic N) and plant factors (above-ground biomass, root biomass, diversity and community composition) to bacterial and fungal diversity and community composition alteration using the rfPermute (Archer, 2022) package in R 4.1.1 (R Core Team, 2021). Based on the results of the random forest analysis, statistically significant variables containing soil pH, soil inorganic N, plant root biomass, plant diversity and plant community composition were used to establish structural equation model (SEM). Initially, we developed a priori model (Figure S2) that was allowed to hypothesize a causal interpretation of the links between soil properties, plant variables, microbial diversity and community composition to estimate their direct and indirect effects on soil microbial diversity and community composition in different degraded grasslands. The explanation of the links included in the SEM is summarized in Table S2. Then, non-significant paths were eliminated until an optimal model was constructed, which was determined by a non-significant *χ*<sup>2</sup> test (*p* > 0.05) and low root mean square error of approximation (RMSEA). The SEMs were tested using AMOS software (IBM SPSS AMOS 20.0.0).

## 3 RESULTS

### 3.1 Responses of soil bacterial, fungal and plant communities to N addition in grasslands with varying degradation status

Soil bacterial, fungal and plant diversity were significantly affected by N addition, while the interaction of N enrichment and degradation only had significant effects on bacterial and plant diversity (Table 1). Across different levels of degraded grasslands, the responses of soil bacterial, fungal and plant diversity to N addition differed substantially (Figure 1; Figure S3). Soil bacterial and fungal diversity did not significantly decline until 40 g N m<sup>−2</sup> year<sup>−1</sup> in non-degraded grassland. Unlike soil microorganisms, plant diversity significantly decreased at 20 g N m<sup>−2</sup> year<sup>−1</sup> in non-degraded grassland. However, soil bacterial diversity markedly declined at 20, 30 and 10 g N m<sup>−2</sup> year<sup>−1</sup> in moderately, severely and extremely degraded grassland respectively. N application rate at which soil bacterial diversity significantly decreased was lower in three degraded grasslands compared to the non-degraded grassland. Soil fungal diversity did not decrease until the N input level reached 40 g N m<sup>−2</sup> year<sup>−1</sup> in the moderately and extremely degraded grassland, but was not affected by N addition in the severely degraded grassland. Furthermore, plant diversity markedly declined at 40 g N m<sup>−2</sup> year<sup>−1</sup> in the moderately and severely degraded grassland, but was not affected by N addition in the extremely degraded grassland.

### 3.2 Linking soil bacterial and fungal diversity and community composition with soil properties and plant community

In non-degraded grassland, random forest analysis showed that soil pH was the major factor that influenced soil microbial diversity and community composition (Figure S5). Accordingly, our SEM suggested that decreased soil pH after N enrichment directly affected bacterial diversity, bacterial community composition, fungal diversity and fungal community composition (the standard total effect was 0.481, −0.591, 0.490 and −0.905 respectively; Figure 4).

Interestingly, plant variables, especially plant community composition, became important in regulating both soil bacterial and fungal communities in all degraded grassland sites (Figure 4; Figure S5). In moderately degraded grassland, bacterial diversity and community composition were directly determined by soil pH (the standard total effect was 0.494 and −0.458, respectively) and plant community composition (the standard total effect was −0.334 and 0.324, respectively). Fungal diversity was directly determined by inorganic N (the standard total effect was −0.553) and plant community composition (the standard total effect was −0.426). Fungal community composition was directly affected by plant community (the standard total effect was 0.401). In severely degraded grassland, plant community composition was the major factor directly regulating bacterial diversity, bacterial community composition, fungal diversity and fungal community composition (the standard total effect was −0.455, 0.406, −0.869 and 0.404, respectively). In extremely degraded grassland, bacterial diversity and community composition were directly determined by inorganic N (the standard total effect was −0.520 and 0.501, respectively) and soil pH (the standard total effect was 0.450 and −0.519, respectively). Fungal diversity was directly determined by inorganic N (the standard total effect was −0.522), while its community composition was affected not only by soil pH (the standard total effect was −0.589) and inorganic N (the standard total effect was 0.299) but also by plant community composition (the standard total effect was −0.356).

## 4 DISCUSSION

### 4.1 Grassland degradation affected the response of soil bacterial and plant but not fungal communities to N addition

To our knowledge, this is the first comparison of responses of soil microbial and plant communities to N addition in grasslands with different degradation status. Consistently with our hypothesis, the responses of soil bacterial diversity and community composition to N addition were stronger in degraded grasslands than in non-degraded grasslands. By contrast, the response of soil fungal community to N enrichment was unaffected by grassland degradation, which is inconsistent with our hypothesis. Unlike soil microorganisms, the response of plant diversity to N application was weaker in degraded grasslands than in non-degraded grasslands (Figure 1; Figure S3), which was consistent with our hypothesis.

Three reasons could explain the higher susceptibility of bacteria to N addition in comparison to fungi and plants. First, compared to fungi, the narrow pH range for optimal bacterial growth (Rousk et al., 2010) is probably the main factor underlying the higher susceptibility of bacteria to N addition with an increasing degree of degradation. Accordingly, SEMs demonstrated that decreases in bacterial diversity and alterations in community composition were strongly associated with the N-induced decline in soil pH (Figure 4). N addition decreased soil pH by 1.1, 1.8, 1.5 and 1.5 units in non-degraded, moderately, severely and extremely degraded grassland, respectively. A previous study reported that deviations of 1.5 pH units from in situ pH of bacterial communities consistently decreased their activity by 50% (Fernandez-Calvino & Baath, 2010), which would result in a population being rapidly outcompeted by other bacteria that were not impeded (Rousk et al., 2010) and lead to bacterial diversity loss. Second, soil bacteria that live in a near-neutral environment were more susceptible to soil acidification (Wang, Liu, et al., 2018). Given that soil pH was higher and closer to neutrality in degraded grasslands than in non-degraded grasslands, bacterial diversity and community composition were more susceptible to N-induced reduction in soil pH. Third, P limitations may also account for the increased susceptibility of bacteria to N addition with the increasing degree of degradation. A previous study found that temperate grasslands shifted from N to P limitation, which was induced by both degradation and N addition (Dong et al., 2019). Soil bacteria have been suggested to exhibit higher growth rates (Strickland & Rousk, 2010) and thus have higher P requirements than fungi and plants. Consequently, they were more sensitive to N addition as a result of P deficiency in degraded grasslands compared to non-degraded grasslands. However, the response of soil fungal diversity to N enrichment was consistent across different degrees of grassland degradation. One possible reason is that fungi are more tolerant to the variation in soil pH compared to bacteria, thus soil fungal community may be less impacted by pH (Lauber et al., 2008; Strickland & Rousk, 2010). This may lead to less variability in soil fungal diversity and community composition in degraded grasslands. On the other hand, fungal diversity was lower in extremely degraded grassland compared to non-degraded grassland (Figure 1), and this is why less response is observed. Sensitive species have been filtered, and only very resistant species remain (Van Beelen & Doelman, 1997). Compared to non-degraded grasslands, the relative abundance of Chytridiomycota and Ascomycota was higher in degraded grasslands, while the relative abundance of other taxa was lower. It has been reported that Chytridiomycota could recover quickly from environmental disturbance/stress (Fay et al., 2016), and Ascomycota members are generally adapted to harsh habitats (Wu et al., 2021). Thus, these fungal phyla could remain in these highly degraded sites and could also be resistant to N addition. Consequently, only high N inputs could decrease soil fungal diversity in degraded grasslands.

According to the oligotrophic–copiotrophic concept, the oligotroph class of microbes is capable of maintaining growth under nutrient-poor conditions, while copiotrophic microbes are adapted to nutrient-rich environments (Koch, 2001). Consequently, N enhancement should facilitate copiotrophic microbial growth while suppressing that of oligotrophic taxa (Leff et al., 2015). However, the relative abundance of copiotrophic Gemmatimonadetes decreased with increasing N inputs, which could be related to soil acidification induced by N addition. Previous studies documented a higher relative abundance of Gemmatimonadetes in soils with near-neutral pH than in acidic soils (Lauber et al., 2009; Mendez et al., 2008). This indicates that soil pH is an important factor that shifts specific bacterial taxonomic groups. Few soil fungi phyla were significantly affected by N addition in grasslands with different degrees of degradation, which verified that the responses of fungal taxa to N enrichment were also weaker than those of soil bacterial taxa.

The mechanisms underlying plant diversity loss after N enrichment have already been extensively explored, including the effects of soil acidification on plant communities (Zhang et al., 2014), root competition (Rajaniemi et al., 2003) and light competition (Hautier et al., 2009) among different plant species. However, we found a greater magnitude of soil pH reduction in degraded grasslands than in non-degraded grasslands (Table S4). Root biomass also increased more in degraded grasslands compared to non-degraded grasslands (Table S5), indicating a stronger competition among plant communities for nutrients. These two factors would have led to an increased susceptibility of the response of plant diversity to N enrichment, whereas our results contradicted that. Therefore, we suspect that increased competition for light may be a major mechanism of plant diversity loss after N enrichment. In non-degraded grasslands, N addition could increase productivity and canopy height, and lead to reduced light in the understory, resulting in low-statured species loss (Hautier et al., 2009). However, in degraded grasslands, light competition would be alleviated because there was no high vegetation cover and a thick litter layer (Bai et al., 2010). Therefore, the N input level which significantly decreased plant diversity was higher in the moderately and severely degraded grassland than in the non-degraded grassland. Many species that prefer nutrient-sufficient conditions could not survive in the moderately degraded grassland, especially some tall plants; therefore, plant diversity was lower in the extremely degraded grassland than in the other three grasslands. The dominant vegetation species in this highly degraded grassland was *Leymus chinensis*, which has strong adaptability and tolerance to drought and salt-alkaline stress (Chen & Wang, 2009), and may also be resistant to N addition. Thus, plant diversity was even unaffected by N addition in the extremely degraded grassland.

Our results revealed that soil bacteria would be most threatened by anthropogenic disturbance in widespread degraded grasslands. N deposition under future climate change could pose a greater threat to soil microbial diversity than previously thought. Some prior ideas on soil bacterial responses to N addition in natural grasslands may not be appropriate for degraded grasslands due to the increasing susceptibility of soil bacteria to N inputs. Furthermore, the uncoupling responses of soil microbial and plant diversity suggest that plant diversity may not be a suitable predictor of below-ground microbial diversity in grasslands suffering from degradation, especially for bacteria.

### 4.2 The links between plant and microbial communities under N deposition were regulated by grassland degradation

Consistent with our hypothesis, we found that plant communities regulated responses of soil microbial diversity and community composition to N enrichment at degraded grassland sites. Soil microbial communities would be less affected by plant variables in the low dependence of plants on microbes in nutrient-sufficient environments (Wei et al., 2013). Unlike the non-degraded grassland, plant community composition mainly shaped microbial diversity and community composition in the moderately, severely and extremely degraded grassland. One possible explanation is that the nutrient conditions changed from sufficient to poor with increasing degree of degradation (Table S4) and the plant–microbe interactions became stronger, which helped to obtain the necessary resources for plant and microbial growth (i.e. P). With increasing N enrichment, the above-ground biomass increased and then reached saturation due to nutrient limitations (i.e. P and calcium limitations; Wang, Zhang, et al., 2018) and ion toxicity (i.e. aluminium and ammonium toxicity; Tian et al., 2016; Wei et al., 2013). Then more C would be allocated below-ground to capture limited resources for plant growth (Shipley & Meziane, 2002). In the present study, root biomass was mostly stimulated by N enrichment in the moderately degraded grassland (Table S5). On the one hand, increased root biomass would lead to more niches and C-based substrates for microbes (Korthals et al., 2001). On the other hand, increased root biomass may also intensify competition between plants and microorganisms for limited soil nutrients. These two contrasting effects may cancel each other out. However, P became limited to soil microbes in degraded grassland under N enrichment (Dong et al., 2019). Consequently, P limitation may result in soil microbial diversity decline and there would be a transition in soil microbial community composition. In the severely degraded grassland, decreased soil pH affected plant community composition (Figure 4), which then influenced soil bacterial and fungal communities. Previous studies have demonstrated that N-induced soil acidification can result in the leaching of magnesium and calcium, and activate mineral-associated aluminium, which could be adverse to plant growth because of magnesium limitations (Lucas et al., 2011) or aluminium toxicity (Wei et al., 2013). Thus, plant community composition would be altered, which could affect soil microbial diversity and community composition because soil microorganisms are highly dependent on plants for habitat and substrate provision (van der Putten et al., 2013). In the extremely degraded grassland, P became more limited for microbial growth, while increasing N availability could exacerbate P deficiency (Dong et al., 2019). Thus, the linkages between plant and microbial communities became stronger due to the need to gain the necessary nutrients for survival.

## 5 CONCLUSIONS

We first reported that grassland degradation affected the way soil bacterial and plant communities responded to N enrichment, but not fungal communities. Furthermore, our findings revealed that plant–microbe linkages under N addition were altered by grassland degradation. In non-degraded grasslands, soil pH was the dominant factor controlling soil microbial diversity and community composition. However, plant community composition became more important in affecting soil microbial communities in degraded grasslands than in non-degraded grasslands. Collectively, our findings move a step forward in understanding whether N addition would interact with degradation to accelerate changes in soil microbes, and we emphasize the importance of considering the degree of grassland degradation when exploring the responses of soil microorganisms to N inputs.
