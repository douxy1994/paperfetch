# Determinants of growth and carbon accumulation of common plantation tree species in the three northern regions, China: Responses to climate and management strategies

## Abstract

The Three-North (Northwest, North and Northeast) Shelter Forests Program (TNSFP) in China has effectively promoted vegetation growth and carbon sink in the temperate semi-humid and semi-arid regions. To compare the afforestation benefits of commonly used tree species in the area and explore the effect of environment on growth and carbon accumulation in plantations, backpack LiDAR was used to acquire 3 dimensional lidar point clouds of forests from a total of 480 pure plantation patches consisting of *Pinus sylvestris* (*P.s.*), *Pinus tabuliformis* (*P.t.*), *Populus* spp. (*Pop.*), and *Robinia pseudoacacia* (*R.p.*). Then, diameter at breast height (DBH), forest height, canopy coverage, and aboveground carbon accumulation were calculated for each plantation patches, which ranged from 7.0 to 37.3 cm, 1.5–14.5 m, 10–99 % and 4.2–205.9 Mg/ha, respectively. Generalized linear mixed-effect models and ANOVA were applied to account for the environmental constraints on the variations of forest parameters. Results showed that precipitation had a stronger effect on all the above parameters of plantations than temperature, and *P.t.* was more sensitive to climate than other three species. With regard to forest management in *Pop.* plantations, thinning could improve afforestation efficiency because carbon accumulation would reduce after the age exceeds 30 years. In contrast, *P.s.* populations maintained a continuous increase in carbon accumulation at least before 40 years old, while the radial growth of canopy became saturated after 12 years of age. The optimal planting density for *P.s.* and *Pop.* are about 1000 trees/ha, beyond which the increase in carbon accumulation will slow down or change rate of canopy coverage will be insignificant. Within the TNSFP area, *P.t.* and *R.p.* plantations would be more suitable in southern regions, while *P.s.* and *Pop.* plantations grow better in the northeastern regions. Meanwhile, mountains along the “Hu Line” showed high potential for growth and carbon accumulation for all tree species examined.

## Introduction

Afforestation, combined with the reduction of deforestation and proper management, can improve the productivity of forest ecosystems and effectively mitigate climate change (Dixon et al., 1994; Humpenöder et al., 2014; Yue et al., 2021). >7 % (290 million ha) of the global forest area was artificially planted, and the area of plantations has increased by 123 million ha since 1990 (FAO, 2020). In China, the extension of forests reaches 158.94 million ha, accounting for 16.5 % of the territory and accumulating 118.1 Mt. of carbon annually (Jin et al., 2020). As China has committed to achieving carbon neutrality by 2060, afforestation has become an important way to promote carbon sinks and mitigate climate change (Nunes et al., 2020). China's afforestation programs were shown to have a leading role in the global “greening” process (Chen et al., 2019). In particular, the Three-North Shelter Forests Program (TNSFP) has promoted the increase in vegetation coverage and soil nutrients (Hong et al., 2018, 2020) in the marginal regions of Chinese forests. Since the beginning of the TNSFP in 1978, afforestation has played an important role in vegetation recover (Wang et al., 2014; Yan et al., 2015) and contributes to the forest carbon sinks of eastern Asia (Fang et al., 2014; Guo et al., 2013). However, remote sensing data have shown that the increase in the leaf area index observed over the last 20 years was not significant in most of the Three North region (Chen et al., 2019). The massive “greening” effort of the TNSFP has been less effective than expected (Cao et al., 2011; Zhu et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2019a). In the past 30 years, it has been difficult to afforest areas west of the “Hu Huanyong Line (Hu Line)”, a famous biogeographical and ecological line in China, which roughly coincides with the contour line of annual precipitation reaches 400 mm (Liu, 2019; Kong et al., 2022). The survival of plantations in arid and semi-arid areas was still severely limited due to the water shortage, severe climatic conditions, and infertile soils (Qi et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2018).

In addition to natural factors, parameters associated with plantation management, such as forest age and afforestation density, also play a role in plantation growth and carbon accumulation (Fang et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2019b). Forest carbon accumulation in tree layers usually increase with age (Justine et al., 2017). A study of *Pinus sylvestris* plantations in semi-arid areas found that carbon sequestration increased in the early stage (from 12 to 48 years of age), but decreased slightly after the trees reached 58 years of age (Zhang et al., 2019b). Similarly, the carbon accumulation of *Mytilaria laosensis* plantation forests were shown to increase at tree ages ranging from 7 to 29 years and remained stable after the tree growth rates declined (Ming et al., 2014). Tree density affects the intensity of competition for resources and regulates timber production (Bai et al., 2020; Harrington et al., 2009; Jandl et al., 2007). It has been reported that trees are shorter and have a larger DBH, crown area, and volume in wider spacing conditions, while the maximum total biomass is obtained in narrow spacing (Nagar et al., 2015). Other studies have shown that forests with different densities do not have different biomass values, as forests with high tree density reach maximum growth rates and decline earlier than those low density forests (Marziliano et al., 2015). In forestry management, thinning would reduce competition and buffer tree growth response to climate change and also mitigate the drought stress (Navarro-Cerrillo et al., 2016; Sohn et al., 2016). However, to determine whether different tree species are suitable for thinning and choose the appropriate time for this operation, it is necessary to further investigate the ecological benefits and economic values of plantations based on optimal density management.

Numerous studies have explored the forest–environment relationship and the variation of forest parameters in the TNSFP region based on remote sensing data (Jia et al., 2015; Lu et al., 2015; Yu et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2013). However, it is difficult to distinguish plantations from natural forests based on these data, and it is even more difficult to classify tree species. A number of studies have also explored the interannual variation in plantation growth based on tree rings or stable carbon isotopes regarding environment change (Sun et al., 2018), but the analyses tended to be limited by research scales and often focused only on a single afforestation tree species, lacking inter-species comparisons. Therefore, only few studies have compared ecological suitability among afforestation species and explored interspecific differences in environmental responses at large scales, which calls for a combination of remote sensing and ground investigation to simultaneously improve the precision and scale of plantation research.

The Backpack LiDAR system provides an efficient approach to obtain 3D point clouds and determine forest structure (Xie et al., 2020, 2022). The output point clouds data were used to calculate the growth parameters (i.e., mean DBH, forest height, and canopy coverage) and estimate aboveground carbon accumulation of 480 sample plots in pure even-aged plantation patches dominated by different tree species in the TNSFP region. The afforestation species included two conifer species and two broadleaf tree species that commonly used in the TNSFP of China: *Pinus sylvestris* var. *Mongolia (P.s.), Pinus tabuliformis (P.t.), Populus* spp.*(Pop.) and Robinia pseudoacacia (R.p.)*, which not only provided a large amount of timber, but also greatly promoted ecosystem services such as forest carbon sinks and soil conservation. The aims of this study was to elucidate: 1) how do plantation growth and carbon accumulation of different species respond differly to environmental variations in the TNSFP region? 2) how do plantation growth and aboveground carbon accumulation respond to trees age and plantation density, and how can this assist in forest management; and 3) where are the suitable planting areas for different tree species in the TNSFP region? We assumed that the responses of plantation to the environment vary with tree species and forest structure, and we hypothesized that the growth and carbon accumulation of plantations may have species specific optimal thresholds for planting age, density, or climatic factors.

## Materials and methods

### Sampling design in the study area

A total of 480 pure even-aged plantations patches, with a size of 40 m × 40 m, were selected and investigaged during summer in 2019 and 2020. These plots were distributed in the semi-arid and semi-humid areas of the TNSFP region of China, which is characterized by a mean annual precipitation of 200–800 mm and a mean annual air temperature ranging from −1 to 10 °C. The pure plantation patches included 254 *P.s.* plots, 74 *P.t.* plots, 127 *Pop.* plots, and 25*R.p.* plots (Fig. 1a), which contained only one tree species of the same age, was far from settlements and roads, and rarely disturbed by human activities. The spacing of plants within the same plantation patches was uniform, and the trees grow under natural conditions after planting without artificial nurturing.

### Field works

The LiBackpack DG-50 backpack LiDAR system (Digital Green Valley Technology Co., Ltd., Beijing, China) was used to obtain the basic forest parameters of plantations, and specifically, the three-dimensional (3D) point clouds. Compared with traditional field work techniques, LiDAR can collect more extensive and accurate 3D forest structure data more rapidly (Xie et al., 2020). The LiDAR system used in this study was equipped with two Velodyne Puck VLP-16 LiDAR sensors that rotated both horizontally and vertically. It had a vertical field angle of 30° and measurement range of 100 m, and emitted 600,000 laser pulses per second (Xie et al., 2022). Compared with single-sensor equipment, our dual-sensor instrument was more suitable to collect canopy information in closed forests. During the data acquisition process, an operator walked along a zigzag route in the forest carrying the LiDAR equipment in a backpack, and in this way sample plots of approximately about 40 m × 40 m were accurately scanned. We set up three to five replicate sample plots in the interior of each plantation patch away from the boundary part to take into account the topographic variation. The point cloud datasets obtained are shown in Fig. 1b. At the same time, soil samples within the LiDAR scanning area were collected from a depth of 0–10 cm using the five-point sampling method for subsequent analyses. Finally, 10 core samples were taken at breast height (1.3 m) from five trees randomly selected in each plot. All the collected cores reached the pith of the tree to allow the estimation of forest age based on tree rings.

### Forest growth parameters and carbon accumulation measurement

Forest parameters were calculated using the LiDAR 360 v5.0 (https://www.greenvalleyintl.com/LiDAR360/) and ArcGIS 10.8 software. DBH was calculated by adapting the cylindrical fitting method to cloud point slices at a height of 1.3m (Xie et al., 2020). Forest height was defined as the mean value of the canopy height model (CHM) for canopies higher than 2 m, while canopy coverage was defined as the proportion of grids larger than 2 m in the CHM, which was calculated as the difference between the digital surface model (DSM) and the digital elevation model (DEM) (Sadeghi et al., 2014). Aboveground forest carbon accumulation was obtained by converting aboveground biomass (AGB) using a carbon fraction of 0.5, a method that has been widely used in forest carbon research worldwide (Brown, 2002; Fahey et al., 2010; Goodale et al., 2002; Harmon et al., 1990; Jones and O'Hara, 2018; Van Deusen and Roesch, 2011). Aboveground biomass was calculated as a function of DBH and tree height (TH) using eq. (1):

(1)

$$
AGB = a \times DBH^{b_{1}} \times TH^{b_{2}} + c
$$

where the units of AGB, DBH, and TH are Mg (=10<sup>6</sup> g), cm, and m, respectively. TH was calculated by identifying the crown top of each tree after the segmentation of canopy point clouds based on a comparative shortest path algorithm (Tao et al., 2015). The a, b1, b2, and c values are for each species, and obtained from previous studies based on field measurements (Fang et al., 1998; Wang, 2011; Xu et al., 2017) and Chinese national and local standards (such as LY/T 2260–2014, DB15/T 1459.6–2018 and DB11/T 953–2031), as shown in Table 1. All the researches mentioned above are located in the northern China.

### Influence factor calculation and collection

The environmental factors affecting the canopy structure of plantations were classified into climate, soil, topography, and management. Climatic variables included mean annual temperature (MAT) and mean annual precipitation (MAP), with data obtained from the CHELSA v2.1 dataset (Karger et al., 2017), which contains monthly data on climatic indicators from 1980 to 2019 with a resolution of 1 km. Soil variables included organic carbon (SOC) and clay content (Clay) in topsoil, which were measured from the soil samples collected in the field, using the spectrophotometry method based on potassium dichromate oxidation and the hydrometer method, respectively (Ashworth et al., 2001; Ni et al., 2001; Tang et al., 2012). Topographic variables included mean terrain slope and aspect of the plots and were calculated based on the DEM generated from field scanned point cloud data. Management included tree density and forest age. Tree density was obtained from the results of point cloud segmentation and plot area. Forest age was determined based on our tree ring samples collected in each plot. Moreover, we checked the age of seedlings used in the local afforestation projects and examined whether it corresponded to the tree ring measurements to prove the reliability of the age records based on the assumption that all plantations are even-aged.

### Data analysis

#### Effects of environmental factors and the interspecific differences

To compare the effects of different factors on forest parameters and carbon accumulation, we selected DBH, forest height, canopy coverage, and carbon accumulation as response variables for the construction of linear mixed models (LMMs) using the function “lmer” in the “lme4” package in R (Bates et al., 2015). The random effect was tree species and fixed effect included above eight influencing variables. The effect size and direction of each variable was indicated by the slope estimate in the LMMs after all variables were normalized. The “r.squared GLMM” function in the “MuMIn” package in R was used to compare model explanations of the relative importance of influencing variables by calculating the conditional (R<sup>2</sup> c) and marginal (R<sup>2</sup> m) coefficients of determination (Nakagawa and Schielzeth, 2013), and the “glmm.hp” package was used to assign the LMMs' marginal R<sup>2</sup> to each fixed effect variable (Lai et al., 2022).

To compare the relative importance of environmental factors affecting different afforestation tree species, a general linear model (LM) was constructed for different forest parameters for each tree species, with the slope estimate indicating effect size and direction after all variables were normalized. To measure the relative importance, the proportionate contribution of each predictor to R<sup>2</sup> was calculated by considering the incremental contribution of each predictor combined with the other predictors (Johnson, 2004; Johnson and LeBreton, 2004).

#### Independent contribution of climate and management

We explored the independent contribution of important factors to forest growth and carbon accumulation. DBH, forest height, canopy coverage, and carbon accumulation of each species (*P.s.*, *P.t.*, *Pop.*, and *R.p.*) were selected as response variables, while four factors including MAT, MAP, forest age, and tree density as explanatory variables. Considering that these variables may have nonlinear effects,we built generalized additive models (GAM) based on the “mgcv” R package (Wood et al., 2016). When exploring the independent contribution of a single factor, the effects of other factors should be excluded due to correlation between different factors, for each response variables, we constructed the first model with any three of the four factors as explanatory variables, and then constructed the second model with explanatory variables including all the four factors. Subsequently, the different of residuals for the two model could be regarded as the independent contributions of the factor that had not been included in the explanatory variables of the first model. And then the values of explanatory factors, i.e. MAT, MAP, forest age, and tree density were categorized into three levels based on commonly used methods. For example, in forestry research, the age classes of artificial forests are usually divided in 10-year intervals; therefore, in this study they were classified into three age groups: < 20 years, 20–30 years, and > 30 years (90 % of the artificial forest sample plots involved in the study were between 10 and 40 years old). Finally, ANOVA and the Bonferroni method were used to compare the independent contributions of variables varying with species within the same variable level, as well as varying among levels for the same species. To verify and quantify the transformation of variable contributions between different gradients, we identified the abrupt points of nonlinear relationships by looking for break-points based on piecewise regression fitting using the “segmented” R package (Muggeo, 2003). To determine whether a segment was statistically significant, the piecewise regressions were compared with liner regressions using ANOVA.

#### Potential planting suitability for each species across the region

Based on the relationship between response variables and influencing factors detailed above, spatial extrapolation based on the plantation-climate relationship was carried out to evaluate the potential forest growth and carbon accumulation of different afforestation species across the TNSFP region for assessment of planting suitability. The LM and sample plots were considered as the prediction model for extrapolation and training set, respectively. As for the datasets for extrapolation prediction, MAT and MAP were obtained from CHELSA v2.1, SOC and clay content were derived from the SoilGrids250m v2.0 datasets (Hengl et al., 2017; Poggio et al., 2021) while the topographic slope and aspect were calculated based on SRTM 90 m DEM data (Farr et al., 2007). Tree density and forest age were uniformly set as 1000 plants per ha (which is the mean value of all sample plots) and 30 years, respectively. The above predictors were resampled at a resolution of 1000 m, and the extrapolation results were normalized to [0,1]. The normalized DBH, forest height, and canopy coverage values belonging to each species were summed up as “growth scores”, which were then used to characterize the average size of trees in plantations. To improve the reliability of space predictions, only the climate zones involved in the sample plots were considered, that is, the semi-arid and sub-humid areas located within the TNSFP region (MAP >200 mm).

## Results

### Interspecific comparison of forest parameters and carbon accumulation

The value of different forest parameters showed inconsistent spatial patterns for different species(Fig. 2). The mean DBH, forest height, canopy cover and mean aboveground carbon accumulation of all plantion patches ranged from 7.0 to 37.3 cm, 1.5–14.5 m, 1–99 % and 4.2–205.9 Mg/ha, respectively. Among our sample plots, *Pop.* plantations were the tallest and had the largest mean DBH. The canopy coverage of *P.t.* and *R.p.* was significantly higher than that of *P.s.* and *Pop.*, while there was no significant difference in average carbon accumulation among the different plantation species (Supplementary materials Fig. S1).

### Effects and relative importance of environmental factors

According to the LMM results, 45 %, 20 %, 31 %, and 47 % of spatial variation in DBH, canopy height, canopy coverage, and carbon accumulation were explained by fixed effects, respectively (Fig. 3a). Tree density negatively affected DBH (−0.53) while it positively affected canopy coverage (0.46) and carbon accumulation (0.43) with the largest effect size among variables. Forest age had the largest positive effect on forest height (0.33). Among climate factors, MAP showed a significant effect on forest parameters and carbon accumulation (except for DBH), while MAT had no significant effect. Among soil factors, SOC positively affected DBH and carbon accumulation, while clay content positively affected forest height. As for topography factors, terrain slope positively affected carbon accumulation. As shown in Fig. 3b, climate factors had a more significant effect on *P.t.* plantations than on the other plantations. With the highest explanatory value among independent variables, tree density had the most important effect on the DBH of all tree species, forest height of *R.p.*, canopy coverage of all species except *P.t.*, and carbon accumulation of all species except *P.s.* Forest age had the largest influence on forest height and carbon accumulation in *P.s.* plantations. The distribution of environmental factors in plantation patches was shown in Supplementary materials Fig. S2.

### Response of plantations to climate

As shown by the black letters in Fig. 4 a-d, within the same MAT level there were no significant differences among species, except for MAT>10 °C, at which forest height and canopy coverage in *P.t.* plantations were significantly greater than those in *R.p.* plantations. Carbon accumulation in *P.s.* plantations at MAT>5 °C was significantly higher than that at MAT<5 °C, however, the opposite was revealed for forest height and canopy coverage in these plantations. In semi-arid areas (MAP<400 mm), forest height, canopy coverage, and carbon accumulation were significantly lower in *P.t.* than in the other plantation species (black letters in Fig. 4 e-h). As MAP increased, forest height in *P.s.* plantations decreased, while carbon accumulation, forest height, and canopy coverage increased in *P.t.* plantations (colored letters in Fig. 4 e-h).

### Response of plantations to age

When a forest was younger than 20 years, all parameters in *R.p.* plantations were higher than those in the other plantations. At the age of 20–30 years, DBH and forest height in *P.s.* plantations were higher than those in *P.s.* plantations, while no difference were detected in canopy coverage and carbon accumulation among species. When forest was older than 30 years, DBH and canopy coverage in *P.s.* and *Pop.* plantations were higher than those in *P.s.* plantations, while forest height and carbon accumulation in *P.s.* plantations reached the highest values among species (black letters in Fig. 4i-l). As shown by the colored letters in Fig. 4 i-l, all the four parameters in *P.s.* and *Pop.* as well as canopy coverage in *P.s* increased significantly with aging. However, there were no significant changes in canopy coverage in *P.s.* or *P.t.* and carbon accumulation in *Pop.* between the medium-aged (20–30 years) and mature (>30 years) classes, and only carbon accumulation in *P.s.* increased continuously from young to mature ages. The results of piecewise regressions showed that canopy coverage in *P.s.* increased significantly before trees reached approximately 12 years of age, and then did not change significantly with further aging (Supplementary materials Fig.S3a). Carbon accumulation in *Pop.* increased significantly before trees reached 28 years of age and then even decreased with further aging (Supplementary materials Fig.S3b).

### Response of plantations to tree density

As shown by the black letters in Fig. 4m-p, when tree density was low (<1000 plants/ha), carbon accumulation in the coniferous plantations (*P.s.* and *P.t.*) was significantly lower than that in *Pop.* plantations. At a medium tree density (1000–2000 plants/ha), there was no significant difference in carbon accumulation among species, however, the other parameters varied: DBH reached the highest mean value in *P.t.* plantations, while forest height and canopy coverage were the highest in *P.s.* plantations. When tree density was high (>2000 plants/ha), there was no significant difference among species. As shown by the colored letters in Fig. 4m-p, DBH, canopy coverage, and carbon accumulation in all species varied significantly with tree density; however, when this parameter changed from medium to high, canopy coverage would not increase significantly in any of the tree species. The results of piecewise regressions indicated that, after tree density reached around 1000 plants/ha, the rates of variation of DBH, canopy coverage, and carbon accumulation in *P.s.* as well as DBH and canopy coverage in *Pop.* decreased and the accurate breakpoints were 1280, 870, 773, 725, and 1114 plants/ha, respectively (Supplementary materials Fig. S3c-f).

### Potential plantation growth and carbon accumulation at the regional scale

The four tree species examined in this study can be planted in the region extending from the Greater Khingan Mountains to Yinshan Mountains (along the Hu Line with 400 mm annual precipitation) to enhance growth and carbon accumulation (Figs. 5 and S4). The potential distribution of these two parameters in *P.s.* and *Pop.* forests exhibited similar patterns: the regions with high scores concentrated in the northeast and showed a decreasing trend from north to south. The same potential distribution of forest growth and carbon accumulation was observed in *P.t.* and *R.p.* plantations, which was spatially complementary to that in *P.s.* and *Pop.* plantations. The high-scoring regions for *P.t.* and *R.p.* forests were concentrated in southern regions with an annual precipitation >400 mm.

## Discussion

### LiDAR scanning strategy and sample plot setting

This study applied a novel approach, distinct from traditional tree-by-tree measuring method, to extract multiple canopy structure parameters from 3D lidar point clouds, presented a much more efficient and accurate methodology in forestry survey. As a portable instrument for collecting 3D point clouds, backpack LiDAR has been proved capable of extracting tree trunk parameters such as DBH in forests at a millimeter-level accuracy (Xie et al., 2020, 2022). We found that the LiDAR scanning based survey method was sufficient to fully capture the canopy structure of artificial plantation with a homogeneous structure, planting density, species and age. The sample size of 40 m × 40 m was determined for the following reasons: The scanning error increases with measuring distance; and shading between trees also increase with distance, which leads to incomplete LiDAR point clouds and large measurement errors of structural parameters such as DBH (Xie et al., 2022). Therefore, the LiDAR scanning route should be set dense enough to ensure a high-quality point cloud data within a battery life of about 30–40 min. Based on our field experience, we generally set a square survey area of 50–80 m side length, which can result in a 40 m × 40 m data with boundary part cut. However, due to the limited penetration ability of laser and scanning angle of sensors, backpack-LiDAR has difficulties in accurately extracting canopy, branch, or leaf structures when canopy density and overlap are high (Liu et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2018; Paris et al., 2017; Ramirez et al., 2013). The LiDAR used in the present study was equipped with two scanners, one horizontal and the other vertical, which provided a better field of view compared to a single scanner (Su et al., 2021) and improved the accuracy in extracting forest height and canopy coverage. However, by examining the collected point cloud data, it was found that dense leaves still lead to the absence of branch point clouds inside the canopy. As the quantitative structure model (QSM)(Brede et al., 2019; Fekry et al., 2022; Krooks et al., 2014; Takoudjou et al., 2018) cannot be used to completely for imcompletely extract branches and trunks extraction from point clouds, it would be difficult to calculate the aboveground biomass and carbon accumulation directly. Therefore, only the transformational models from tree structure parameters to carbon storage could be used. These models were obtained explored from previous studies based on a large number of field measurements and local standards for indirect calculations.

### The influence of climatic factors on plantations

Our results showed that precipitation affected the canopy growth (forest height, canopy coverage) of plantations more than temperature (Fig. 3a), supporting previous studies suggesting that the efficiency of afforestation and greening in northern China is limited by water (Gazol et al., 2018; Babst et al., 2019; Liu, 2019; Wang et al., 2020). However, the radial growth (DBH) of plantation trees did not respond significantly to precipitation (Figs. 3a and 4). It was inferred that in areas subjected to drought, plantation trees prioritize the reduction of water transport pathways and transpiration by limiting their height rather than radial growth (Dai et al., 2020). Previous studies of tree rings have suggested that the variation in radial growth often lags behind climatic variations in time (Peng et al., 2006), which may also be reflected in space. The effects of soil factors observed in this study indicated that forest height was more sensitive to moisture, because it was significantly related to soil clay content which indicates soil moisture (Jarvis, 2007), while DBH as well as carbon accumulation responded significantly only to soil organic carbon. The response of plantations to climate varied with tree species. Specifically, *P.t.* showed more sensitivity to climate than other species (Fig. 3b), which indicates it is suitable for planting in wetter and warmer areas to obtain more greening and carbon sink benefits. In semi-arid regions, *P.t.* trees grew smaller and shorter canopies and accumulated less carbon compared with the other tree species. *P.s.* and *Pop.* responded to climate variation more robustly, and should therefore be planted in drier areas. As for *R.p.*, results were limited by number and the distribution of the samples.

Although many studies emphasized the importance of seasonal precipitation to forests (Wu et al., 2013), this study used MAP as an indicator of precipitation for reasons. In the temperate semi-arid and semi-humid regions of China, precipitation is mostly concentrated in summer. Rainfall from May to September in our study areas can account for >80 % of MAP (Li et al., 2023). Therefore, MAP is a very good indicator for precipitation in the growing season that plays an important role in tree growth (Wu et al., 2013), although inconsistent in its starting/ending dates across our broad study area.

### The influence of forest age on plantations

Interspecific differences in the trends of plantation growth and carbon accumulation were observed with aging. Canopy coverage and DBH in *Pop.* kept increasing from young (<20 years) to mature (>30 years) ages, while carbon accumulation and forest height increased significantly from young to middle (20–30 years) ages but kept stable or even decrease with increasing ages (Fig. 4). This may indicate crown dieback symptoms (Ji et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2018) in older *Pop.* (Supplementary materials Fig.S5), which are related to the decline of physiological functions as a natural result of aging (Sun et al., 2018). Alternatively, this variation with age may mean that older *Pop*. trees have a lower resistance to drought and insect outbreaks (McDowell et al., 2011). Therefore, from the perspective of plantation management, *Pop.* forests should be harvested when they are >30 years old as to improve afforestation efficiency, because the carbon sink capacity of *Pop.* trees would decline after 28 years of age (Supplementary materials Fig. S3b). In *P.s.,* DBH, forest height, and carbon accumulation kept increasing from young (<20 years) to mature (>30 years) ages, while canopy coverage significantly increased from young to middle (20–30 years) ages and then remained stable (Fig. 4). This showed that *P.s.* plantations did not experience crown dieback symptoms in the study area; older *P.s.* trees maintained the growth of tree trunks to accumulate biomass, and canopy coverage tended to be saturated after 12 years of age (Supplementary materials Fig. S3a). Among the four species, *P.s.* showed the fastest growing carbon accumulation, which in young trees was significantly lower than that in the other tree species at young age. In contrast, carbon accumulation in mature *P.s.* trees was the highest (Fig. 4), which means that this parameter continues to increase with age before trees are 40 years old, in line with the results of previous studies (Zhang et al., 2019b). Therefore, it is necessary to protect the older *P.s.* plantations. As plantations are not able to maintain a high level of carbon accumulation for a long period, it is essential to make full use of the stages of rapid growth and carbon accumulation and conduct reasonable thinning management to avoid plantations turning from carbon sinks to carbon sources.

### The influence of planting density on plantations

Afforestation density is related to the ecological and economic benefits of plantations. We found that tree density was the primary factor affecting DBH, canopy coverage, and carbon accumulation (Fig. 3a); therefore, determining the appropriate tree density could not only help to save afforestation costs, but also limit intraspecific competition to benefit tree growth (Arnaud et al., 2019). Based on the carbon sink function of forests, Qiu et al. (2019) and Liu et al. (2019) proposed that the optimal afforestation density of P.s. should be 1111 plants/ha. By analyzing the hydrological functions of forest litter and soil, Deng et al. (2019) found that the best afforestation density of *P.s.* in Mu Us Sandy Land, China, was 1200–1250 plants/ha. Zhang et al. (2022) maintained that afforestation density in northern China should not exceed 1500 plants/ha based on the ecological functions of the forest ecosystem. Our study further proved that around 1000 plants/ha would be the best afforestation density for *P.s.* and *Pop.* plantations because, above this level, the increase in carbon accumulation and canopy coverage starts to slow down or is no longer significant, and DBH no longer decreases significantly although the intensification of intraspecies competition increases (Supplementary materials Fig.S3 cf). We suggest that thinning should be timely conducted to prevent the recession of forests and improve carbon sink efficiency.

### Potential planting suitability of plantations with different species

Predicting the planting suitability of each tree species in the North Three region revealed that, it was highly suitable to plant *P.s.* and *Pop.* in the northeastern areas while *P.t.* and *R.p.* in the southern areas. The eastern part of the Hu Line (i.e., from the Greater Khingan Mountains to Yinshan Mountains) revealed a high potential for growth and carbon accumulation for all four species. This may be attributed to the advantageous moisture and soil conditions in mountain areas (Fan et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2021; Tromp-van Meerveld and McDonnell, 2006). In addition, the topographic slope showed a positive effect on carbon accumulation in plantations (Fig. 3a), and a number of previous studies confirmed that tree cover is positively associated with slope (Nuchel et al., 2019; Nuchel and Svenning, 2017) because steeper slopes are less attractive or accessible for human activities and large herbivores (Odgaard et al., 2014; Sandel and Svenning, 2013; Sankaran et al., 2013). However, few natural explanations have been found for this association (Nuchel et al., 2019). In any case, although it is controversial whether large-scale afforestation can cross the Hu line (Liu, 2019), our study shows that mountainous areas near the Hu line can be afforested efficiently.

Unfortunately, because the sampling size of *P.t.* and *R.p.* plantations in our study was insufficient (as they were mostly >20 years old, and especially the distribution *R.p.* plantations was concentrated in smaller areas), the understanding of the responses of these plantations to forest age and climate was limited. In the future, attempts should be made to obtain the actual planting areas of each tree species in the Three North region, and expand the sampling range to increase the representativeness of samples.

## Conclusions

Although precipitation had a stronger impact on plantations than temperature in the temperate semi-humid and semi-arid regions of China, the impact of climate factors generally showed a weaker effect than that of afforestation density and forest age on the growth, structure, and carbon accumulation of of four most commonly planted tree species. In particular, *P.t.* and *R.p.* are more suitable for wetter and warmer areas, while *P.s.* and *Pop.* should be planted in more arid areas. Mountain areas around the 400-mm annual precipitation line showed high potential benefits of afforestation with all four species and should be developed and managed effectively. In addition, it was revealed that the plantations changed with aging in different ways depending on tree species. Mature *Pop.* exhibited crown dieback symptoms and, to address this issue, reasonable thinning can be carried out to improve afforestation efficiency in mature plantations. In *P.s.*, growth and carbon accumulation showed the fastest increase with age. No significant declining trend was observed before 40 years of age and the canopy's radial growth was saturated after 12 years of age; therefore, it is necessary to protect older *P.s.* plantations, and branch pruning could be carried out to this end. Considering that afforestation density is associated with the ecological and economic benefits of plantations, our study suggests that a tree density of approximately 1000 plants per hectare is suitable for afforestation in the Three North region of China.
