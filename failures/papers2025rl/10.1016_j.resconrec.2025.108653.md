# Multi-scale spatiotemporal interactions between land use transformation and carbon emissions in China from 1980 to 2020

## Abstract

Land use transformation (LUT) is a critical driver of greenhouse gas emissions, interacting dynamically with carbon fluxes across spatial and temporal scales. In response to China’s rapid urbanization and ecological civilization initiatives, we construct a multi-perspective framework to assess both explicit (land cover) and implicit (functional) LUT across 370 cities. We integrate sectoral energy consumption with land-use carbon accounting and incorporate both production and living functions to generate emission estimates for 329 cities. Using Spearman correlation analysis and panel quantile regression, we identify significant scale-dependent interactions between LUT and carbon emissions. (1) China’s LUT was dominated by implicit changes, with significant spatial differentiation across cities; (2) land-use carbon emissions increased sharply post-2000, particularly in eastern and industrial regions; and (3) carbon emissions and LUT showed significant scale-dependent interactions—LUF and LUE were positively correlated with emissions at city and parcel scales, while LUE only contributed to emission reductions after reaching advanced stages. Our study provides an integrated perspective for understanding the carbon impacts of land use and offers spatially adaptive guidance for sustainable land management and carbon neutrality strategies.

## Introduction

Land use serves as one of the major human activities contributing to greenhouse gas emissions (Hong et al., 2021), profoundly altering carbon cycle of terrestrial ecosystems (Friedlingstein et al., 2022). From the second industrial revolution to late 20th century, land use changes accounted for >30 % of the total global anthropogenic carbon emissions (Houghton et al., 1999), decreasing to 12.5 % during 2000–2009 (Friedlingstein et al., 2010). Land-based climate mitigation is critical for achieving the climate targets outlined in the Paris Agreement, garnering heightened scientific and political attention (Grassi et al., 2023; Schwingshackl et al., 2022). China, in particular, has emerged as a major contributor to the global greening through agricultural intensification and the policy of returning farmland to forests. However, urbanization and industrialization have increased carbon emissions due to construction and transportation (Wu et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023). Therefore, a comprehensive assessment of China’s land-use carbon emissions is crucial to reflect the specific management and conservation efforts for each region.

Land use transition (LUT) manifesting through land-use/land-cover change, such as urban expansion, cropland loss, cropland intensification, deforestation, afforestation, and grassland degradation, has been studied for its impacts on carbon emissions (Winkler et al., 2021). Early studies focused on quantitative structure changes (Lambin and Meyfroidt, 2011), however, the recent frameworks integrate land use structure and landscape multifunctionality to capture key characteristics of LUT (Liang et al., 2020). Reducing carbon emissions through LUT has become a key focus (Liu et al., 2023). Land-use carbon emissions can be categorized into direct effects, such as change of vegetation carbon stock or soil carbon stock (Houghton, 2012), and indirect effects due to human socio-economic activities on the land (Zhang et al., 2022). Previous studies have assessed land-use carbon emissions across a variety of spatial scales. At the regional scale, research has highlighted the role of provincial urbanization and industrial structure in driving emissions (Huang and Matsumoto, 2021). At the municipal scale, comparative analyses across Chinese cities have linked urban form, density, and infrastructure expansion to energy use and carbon outcomes (He et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022). At finer levels, studies at the county or block scale have demonstrated how localized land-use change, such as urban fringe expansion or block-level land-use conflicts, contributes to emission intensity (Chuai et al., 2015; Zhang and Li, 2022). While these works provide valuable insights, they are generally limited to a single scale of observation, which constrains the ability to detect cross-scale interactions or thresholds. Understanding the interactive relationships between LUT and carbon emissions across different scales is vital for achieving carbon neutrality and advancing sustainable land governance (Huang et al., 2024).

Existing accounting methods, including the inventory of sample plots (Zhao et al., 2016), the bookkeeping estimates (Houghton, 2003), and the biogeochemical model (Schimel et al., 2000), also have limitations in spatio-temporal scalability. Top-down methods focusing on socio-economic systems, such as the material balance method (Hossain, 2020), the emission coefficient method (Wang et al., 2014), and the actual measurement method (Ding et al., 2025), demonstrate better applicability at the macro scale but lack parameter localization. These limitations hinder the effective integration of composite system characteristics and lead to inaccuracies in multi-scale conversions. In addition, quantifying the multi-scale impact of LUT on carbon emissions still remains highly uncertain (Friedlingstein et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2021). Existing energy consumption accounting systems are limited to administrative divisions such as national, regional, and provincial levels, as well as developed urban clusters like the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region and the Greater Bay Area (Zhou et al., 2021; Z Wang et al., 2023). These limitations, combined with spatial resolution mismatches between energy statistics and land use data (Chuai et al., 2015), further hinder accurate quantification of process-specific carbon effects (Liang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2016).

To elucidate the spatiotemporal interplay between LUT and carbon emissions across multiple scales, we introduce a multi-perspective evaluation framework that couples explicit land-cover changes with implicit functional shifts. Methodologically, this framework is innovative in two respects. First, we introduce a novel urban typology that integrates four LUT indicators to classify cities into five categories, validated through clustering metrics. By linking land-use characteristics to sector-specific energy consumption, we construct a 1980–2020 panel of city-level carbon inventories for 329 Chinese cities. Second, we employ panel quantile regression, which captures heterogeneous effects of LUT across the carbon emission distribution, extending beyond mean-based approaches commonly used in this field. Together, these innovations provide a more nuanced and scale-sensitive analysis of land-carbon dynamics. The framework advances both methodological practice and policy relevance: it clarifies how urbanization-driven land restructuring shapes emission trajectories, informs dual-carbon targets, and underpins regionally tailored, low-carbon land strategies, which offers a scalable solution adaptable to other national contexts.

## Methodology

### Methods

In this study, we propose a multi-perspective LUT evaluation framework, reconstruct carbon emission inventories of 329 cities in China from 1980 to 2020 and evaluate the relationships between LUT and carbon emissions. The overall workflow includes: (1) analyzing dynamic patterns in LUT, (2) quantifying land-use carbon emissions at municipal scale, (3) evaluating dynamic interactions between LUT and carbon emissions across multiple scales (Fig. 1). The “multiple scales” here explicitly refers to the combined analysis of (i) parcel-scale dynamics within 1 km grid units. (ii) municipal scale carbon inventories across 329 prefectural-level cities and (iii) regional scale, including the central, eastern, and western economic belts. This three-tier approach enables simultaneous assessment of macroeconomic drivers and micro-landscape processes, thereby capturing scale-dependent heterogeneity in land use-carbon interactions that conventional single-scale analyses overlook (Gately and Hutyra, 2017).

#### Analysis of dynamic patterns in land use transformation

To analyze land use transformation, we construct a composite indicator system encompassing land use quantity (LUQ), pattern (LUP), function (LUF), and efficiency (LUE), integrating both explicit and implicit land characteristics (Lambin and Meyfroidt, 2011; Liang et al., 2020). LUQ reflects the spatial distribution of land use types within grids, while LUP captures land layout complexity using a landscape fragility index (Jiang et al., 2021). LUF evaluates production, living, and ecological functions (Meng et al., 2022), and LUE measures the balance of land input and output (Hu et al., 2018). Detailed formulas are available in Supplementary Table 1.

The four indicators—land use quantity (LUQ), pattern (LUP), function (LUF), and efficiency (LUE)—were chosen to represent both the explicit and implicit dimensions of land use transition. LUQ and LUP capture structural and spatial characteristics of land systems, consistent with transition frameworks emphasizing changes in land quantity and configuration (Liang et al., 2020). LUF and LUE reflect functional and efficiency attributes, aligning with multifunctional land system research that stresses production, ecological, and living functions (Meng et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2023). Similar composite approaches have been applied in China to reveal trade-offs between land functions and carbon dynamics, confirming the explanatory power of these dimensions (Ge et al., 2022). Other factors such as socio-economic pressures or meteorological influences were excluded because they act mainly as external drivers of land transitions rather than intrinsic descriptors of land system change. Including them as core indicators would blur the distinction between transformation characteristics and driving forces. Instead, LUQ-LUE provide a parsimonious but comprehensive typology of land transition, while external factors are considered as contextual variables in regression analyses (Huang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024).

Following Yunxiao et al. (2024) and Zhao et al. (2023), we further categorize cities into five LUT transformation types (Stable, Increasing Grade, Rapid Development, Fluctuation, Lagging) using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and natural breakpoint method. First, the four LUT indicators (LUQ, LUP, LUF, LUE) were normalized and subjected to hierarchical clustering to detect consistent groupings across cities. Second, the clusters were interpreted based on dominant indicator profiles, producing five types: expansionary, intensive, transitional, stable, and fluctuating. This procedure follows established typology methods in urban studies, where indicator-based clustering is widely used to identify functional or morphological city types (Liu et al., 2018). We further validated the classification using silhouette coefficients (>0.6 in all cases), indicating strong internal consistency of clusters. By integrating both explicit (LUQ, LUP) and implicit (LUF, LUE) land-use dimensions, the typology enables a more nuanced understanding of how cities evolve across developmental gradients. Details on formula derivation and index components are also available in Supplementary Table 1.

#### Quantification of land-use carbon emissions at municipal scale

In this study, carbon emissions from provincial sectoral energy consumption are allocated through social statistical indicators to obtain municipal energy consumption carbon emissions. We calculate direct land-use carbon emissions and indirect land-use carbon emissions based on different land use types. Total land-use carbon emissions are obtained by calculating the correction coefficients of carbon emissions from each sectoral energy consumption (Shan et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2021, 2022). Comparisons with Zhou et al. (2021) and Liu et al. (2022) confirm the reliability of our data through their high consistency (Supplementary Figure 11).

##### Calculation of carbon emissions from energy consumption

Based on the energy balance table at the provincial level, there are a total of seven end-consumer sectors. The allocation ratio of each sector is calculated through the energy allocation targets. With these ratios, we allocate provincial carbon emissions from energy consumption to municipalities (Supplementary Table 2). Carbon emissions from energy consumption in the municipal sector are calculated based on IPCC (Eggleston et al., 2006) as follows:

(1)

$$
\begin{matrix} E_{i}^{city} = \underset{n = 1}{∑}E_{i, n}^{city} \times NCV_{n} \times CEC_{n} \times COF_{n} \times \frac{44}{12} \end{matrix}
$$

where $E_{i}^{city}$ is the municipal energy carbon emissions of sector *i. n* is the type of fossil fuel consumed. $NCV_{n}$ is the net calorific value of fossil fuel type *n*. $CEC_{n}$ is the carbon emission factor. $COF_{n}$ is the carbon oxidation rate. Based on the energy balance sheet statistics, eleven kinds of fuels are selected to calculate the carbon emissions of energy consumption. Based on existing studies (Eggleston et al., 2006; Zhou et al., 2021), we set the carbon emission factors (Supplementary Table 3).

##### Calculation of land use carbon emissions

We calculate the land-use carbon emissions based on a widely used emission factor method (Zhou et al., 2021; Yang and Liu, 2022; Yi et al., 2022).

(2)

$$
\begin{matrix} E_{L} = A_{i}δ_{i} \end{matrix}
$$

(3)

$$
\begin{matrix} E_{L} = ∑E_{n}θ_{n}f_{n} \end{matrix}
$$

where $E_{L}$ is the land use carbon emissions. $A_{i}$ is the area of land use type *i*. $δ_{i}$ is the carbon emission or uptake coefficient. *n* is the type of fossil fuel. $E_{n}$ is the energy consumption. $θ_{n}$ and $f_{n}$ denotes the standard coal conversion factor and carbon emission factor, respectively. Referring to China Energy Statistics Yearbook and the 2006 IPCC Guidelines for National Greenhouse Gas Inventories (Wang et al., 2014 and Yang and Liu, 2022), we set the standard coal conversion factor, carbon emission factor for major energy sources and carbon emission factors for five land use types (Supplementary Table 4–5) (Zhou et al., 2021; Rong et al., 2023).

##### Correction coefficients for land-use carbon emissions

By calculating the average ratio between sectoral energy consumption carbon emissions and the corresponding land-use carbon emissions, we obtain carbon emission correction coefficients for different land use types (Zhou et al., 2021). Among them, carbon emission correction coefficients for cultivated land, forest land, grassland, and water are calculated using Eq. (4), and carbon emission correction coefficients for construction land are calculated using Eq. (5):

(4)

$$
\begin{matrix} M_{l} = \frac{∑_{c}\frac{E_{1}^{city}}{∑E_{Cropland, Forestland, Grassland, Water}}}{c} \end{matrix}
$$

(5)

$$
\begin{matrix} M_{l} = \frac{∑_{c}\frac{E_{2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7}^{city}}{E_{Urban}}}{c} \end{matrix}
$$

where $M_{l}$ is the correction coefficient for land-use carbon emissions calculation and *c* is the number of cities. $E_{i}^{city}$ is the carbon emissions from energy in sector *i* of the city and *Ej* is the carbon emissions from land use type *j*.

Finally, land-use carbon emissions at municipal scale is calculated by Eq. (6):

(6)

$$
\begin{matrix} LCE = \underset{l = 1}{∑}E_{L}M_{l} \end{matrix}
$$

For the energy-based carbon emissions, we adopt standard IPCC factors (Eggleston et al., 2006), with energy-specific emission coefficients (e.g., coal: 2.637 × 10<sup>–11</sup> tCO₂/KJ) and net calorific values (e.g., diesel: 4.2652 × 10<sup>–11</sup> KJ/t). We calculate correction coefficients to align sectoral energy consumption with actual land use patterns. For land-based emissions, we use empirically validated land-type-specific coefficients: 42.20 tCO₂/km² for cultivated land, −57.80 for forest, −14.00 for grassland, −2.52 for water, and −5.00 for barren land (Zhou et al., 2021 and Rong et al., 2023).

#### Dynamic interactions of LUT and carbon emissions

##### Spearman correlation

Spearman correlation is a non-parametric test method with a broad scope of application (Ji et al., 2023). In this study, the Spearman correlation coefficient is utilized to analyze the interactions between carbon emissions and LUT as follows:

(7)

$$
\begin{matrix} ρ(X_{i}, Y_{i}) = 1 - \frac{6∑_{i = 1}^{n}{(X_{i} - Y_{i})}^{2}}{n({n}^{2} - 1)} \end{matrix}
$$

where $ρ(X_{i}, Y_{i})$ represents the Spearman correlation coefficient. $X_{i}$and $Y_{i}$ represents the evaluation value of LUT indicators or carbon emissions in *X* and *Y* units. *n* is the number of evaluation units. The value of the correlation coefficient ranges from −1 to 1. The value of the correlation coefficient indicates the direction and strength of the correlation between the two elements.

##### Panel quantile regression model

In previous land-use carbon emission research, diverse methodological approaches have been applied to capture the heterogeneity in carbon emissions. Geographically Weighted Regression (GWR) and its temporal extension (GTWR) have been widely used to account for local spatial heterogeneity (Wang et al., 2022). Cellular automata-based models such as PLUS and FLUS-CA enable simulation of land-use change dynamics and scenario predictions (Liang et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2024). Recently, machine learning methods, including Random Forests, Gradient Boosted Regression Trees, and deep learning frameworks, have been applied for city-level carbon estimation (Lin et al., 2022). Against this backdrop, we adopt a panel quantile regression model, which reveals heterogeneous impacts across the emission distribution. Compared to machine learning and deep learning models, panel regression methods offer greater interpretability, aiding in understanding the driving mechanisms behind land-use carbon emissions (Koenker and Bassett, 1978). This is particularly advantageous when handling environmental datasets that are skewed, heteroscedastic, and non-normal, enabling robust inference of scale-dependent land-carbon interactions (Powell, 2022; Awan et al., 2022). By fitting the model at nine quantiles (0.1–0.9), we can discern how each LUT dimension exerts distinct influences on low-, medium-, and high-emission regions and trace the phased effects of LUT on carbon dynamics. (Niu et al., 2023):

(8)

$$
\begin{matrix} Q_{LCE_{kt}}(τ|x_{kt}) = β_{1}(τ)LUQ_{kt} + β_{2}(τ)LUS_{kt} + β_{3}(τ)LUF_{kt} + β_{4}(τ)LUE_{kt} + μ_{k}(τ) + e_{kt} \end{matrix}
$$

where $τ$ is the quantile, $τ∈$(0, 1). $Q_{LCE_{kt}}(τ|x_{kt})$is the conditional quantile of the explanatory variable LCE. $LUQ_{kt}, LUS_{kt}, LUF_{kt}, LUE_{kt}$ is the explanatory variable. $β_{n}(τ)$ is the regression coefficient of the explanatory variable at the quantile $τ$. $μ_{k}(τ)$ is the individual effect at the quantile $τ$. $e_{kt}$ is the model perturbation term.

#### Robustness tests

To ensure the reliability of our results, we conducted three robustness tests. First, we cross-validated our city-level emission estimates against two widely used benchmark datasets—ODIAC (Oda et al., 2018) and CEADs (Shan et al., 2017)—to assess consistency at different scales. Second, we performed a sensitivity analysis by perturbing the carbon emission correction coefficients (±5 %, ±10 %, ±20 %) to test the stability of total emissions. Third, we evaluated the stability of panel quantile regression results by examining coefficient trends across quantiles and testing subsample splits by economic zone. These steps follow established practices in carbon accounting and spatial econometrics for uncertainty assessment (Oda et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2022).

### Data sources

We obtain 1980–2020 land use data from the Resource and Environment Science Data Platform of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (http://www.resdc.cn). The socioeconomic statistics are obtained from the statistical yearbooks. We obtain the energy consumption data from the regional energy balance sheet (EBT) in China Energy Statistics Yearbook. The low-precision PM2.5 concentration data used in the study are the reconstructed PM2.5 dataset of China from 1960 to 2020 with a resolution of 0.25° (Zhong et al., 2022). We obtain the high-precision PM2.5 concentration data from the Atmospheric Composition Analysis Group (http://www.sites.wustl.edu) at Washington University in St. Louis. In addition, transpiration and evapotranspiration data are obtained from the National Ecological Science Data Center (http://www.nesdc.org.cn). NPP data are obtained from the Global Land Surface Satellite (GLASS) Dataset (http://www.glass.umd.edu). In this study, we validate regression results based on ODIAC data (The Open-Data Inventory for Anthropogenic Carbon dioxide, https://db.cger.nies.go.jp/dataset/ODIAC/) and CEADs China Urban Emission Inventory data (https://www.ceads.net/data/city/) for estimation accuracy validation (Supplementary Table 6). Similar to existing literature (Guo et al., 2025), Tibet and Hainan are not included in our study due to the lack of socio-economic statistics and energy consumption data. Hong Kong, Macao and Taiwan are not included with the limitations in obtaining statistics.

## Results

### Characteristics of land use transformation in China

During the period of 1980–2020, LUT in China was primarily characterized by hidden transformation and significant spatial patterns (Supplementary Figure 1–2). In terms of transformation dimensions, LUQ and LUP exhibited relative stability, with Level III and Level IV cities accounting for over 70 % of the total. After 1990, the proportion of cities with an increase in quantitative grades stabilized at 5–13 %. In contrast, LUF and LUE underwent substantial changes, as evidenced by a 40-fold increase in Level V cities. Furthermore, the implicit transformation (LUF and LUE) was significantly higher than that of explicit transformation (LUQ and LUP), indicating function first in land system evolution (Lambin, E. F., et al., 2006).

By synthesizing the annual characteristics of LUQ, LUP, LUF and LUE, we classified the comprehensive LUT patterns of Chinese cities into five types (Fig. 2):

![Figure 2](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S0921344925005300-gr2_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

Spatial patterns of land use transformation (LUT) across 329 Chinese cities from 1980 to 2020. Cities are categorized into five types based on changes in LUQ, LUP, LUF, and LUE: Type I (Stable), Type II (Increasing Grade), Type III (Rapid Development), Type IV (Fluctuation), and Type V (Lagging).

Stable (Type I) (38.10 %): LUQ and LUP of cities tended to be stable as a whole, and the degree of land use fragmentation was low (Class III and below). Taking Beijing and Hangzhou as representatives, these cities were characterized by stabilization of explicit indicators and gradual improvement of implicit indicators.

Increasing Grade (Type II) (15.68 %): LUQ in these cities increased gradually and the indexes of land-use patterns were lower. At the same time, LUF and LUE in these cities tended to be stable or slightly improved, showing synergy between land use intensification and function optimization.

Rapid Development (Type III) (31.08 %): These cities, predominantly located east of the Hu Huanyong Line, exhibited high LUF and LUE (Level III and above) or achieved substantial improvements in LUF and LUE, ultimately reaching Level IV–V. They formed an efficient transformation belt that aligned with the “three horizontal and two vertical” urbanization pattern.

Fluctuation (Type Ⅳ) (10.00 %): The LUQ, LUP, LUF or LUE of such cities showed different degrees of fluctuation and lacked an obvious direction of transformation. Possibly influenced by a variety of factors such as policy and economy, these cities were mostly located in policy-sensitive areas in the northwest and northeast.

Lagging (Type V) (5.14 %): In these cities, LUP was high, land-use fragmentation was pronounced, and overall urban continuity was weak. LUQ, LUF and LUE improved only slowly—sometimes even declined—leaving service levels behind and land-use efficiency low. The scattered pattern underscored the governance dilemma of simultaneous fragmentation and functional decline.

### Spatial and temporal patterns of land-use carbon emissions in China

Land-use carbon emissions in China displayed a pronounced east–west gradient, with the eastern provinces consistently outpacing the west. North China emerged as the dominant high-emission cluster (Fig. 3). During the period of 1980–2020, carbon emissions increased by 734.51 %, with most of the growth occurring after 2000. Specifically, growth accelerated post-2000 by over 400 %, indicating a shift aligned with industrialization trends. Several high-emission zones gradually formed around major urban centers, particularly Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Chongqing. Notably, although Chongqing reports relatively high absolute carbon emissions due to its large industrial base and population, its LUT indicators exhibit low variability and balanced performance across LUQ-LUE dimensions. This stability in land transition dynamics explains its classification into the “stable” type despite high emission totals. The typology reflects land transition characteristics rather than absolute emission magnitudes. These high-emission zones expanded spatially: eastern cities remained key emission hotspots, while western cities increasingly contributed to high-emission regions.

![Figure 3](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S0921344925005300-gr3_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

Spatial and temporal changes in total land-use carbon emissions in China during 1980–2020.

Carbon emissions of cultivated land demonstrated a consistent upward trend, registering a growth rate of approximately 75.38 %. The primary high-emission areas were concentrated in major cultivated areas, particularly the Northeast and North China plains. Notably, emissions from cultivated areas in dryland substantially exceeded those from grassland (Supplementary Figure 3). In contrast, carbon uptake of forest and grassland showed a significant increasing trend, with the total amount increasing from 102.56 × 10<sup>6</sup> tons of CO2e to 384.58 × 10<sup>6</sup> tons of CO2e. The growth rate of forest and grassland carbon uptake fluctuated and changed with the implementation of the Grain for Green program, exerting particularly notable impacts on carbon uptake enhancement (Supplementary Figure 4). Carbon uptake in water and barren land increased slightly, with a growth rate of only 13.52 %, and the high value areas were concentrated in the northwestern region and cities along the Yangtze River. Since 1990, benefiting from rich watershed resources and active wetland protection and ecological environment construction, carbon uptake in southern cities had risen (Supplementary Figure 5). Carbon emissions from construction land accounted for >90 % of the total carbon emissions, and the growth rate of carbon emissions accelerated significantly after 2000. The high-value areas had gradually expanded in spatial distribution from point to surface, especially during 2010–2020. Carbon emissions in eastern and southern cities increased significantly, reflecting the trend of rising energy consumption and industrial emissions in these regions as economic centers and manufacturing bases (Supplementary Figure 6).

### Dynamic interactions of land use transformation and carbon emissions

#### Spearman correlation

There was a significant correlation between carbon emissions and LUT, with each economic zone showing differentiated characteristics. At the national scale (Fig. 4), LUF showed a positive correlation with total carbon emissions ($ρ$ > 0.131), indicating that an increase in fragmentation might lead to a rise in carbon emissions. Carbon uptake by forest and grassland was negatively correlated with LUP ($ρ$: −0.2), and the rise in fragmentation slightly increased carbon uptake; carbon uptake by water area and barren land was positively correlated with LUF ($ρ$ >0.52). Carbon emissions from cultivated land were negatively correlated with LUF and LUE ($ρ$: −0.47—−0.17), but might reverse to positive correlation at the later stage of function enhancement, suggesting that the rise in production function was accompanied by an increase in carbon emissions.

In the eastern economic belt, the influence of LUF and LUE on carbon emissions was relatively significant, with the correlation between construction land and total carbon emissions gradually turning from negative to positive (Supplementary Figure 7). The positive correlation of carbon uptake in water area and barren land ($ρ$: 0.5–0.6) gradually decreased, while carbon uptake of forests and grasslands was negatively correlated with LUF. Carbon emissions from cultivated land were negatively correlated with efficiency.

In the central economic belt, the overall correlation between carbon emission and LUT was weaker, which was significantly manifested in the positive correlation between carbon uptake and LUE in water area and barren land ($ρ$: 0.22–0.43), negative correlation between carbon uptake in forests and grasslands and the type of land use (correlation coefficient −0.4–0.3), as well as negative correlation between carbon emissions from cultivated land and LUE, albeit with obvious fluctuations in changes. Although land fragmentation was high, its impact on carbon emissions might be masked by other transformation factors (Supplementary Figure 8).

In the Western Economic Belt, carbon uptake in water and barren land was significantly and positively correlated with LUT characteristics (LUF: $ρ$>0.66, LUP: $ρ$: >0.56). Cultivated land carbon emissions were negatively correlated with efficiency before 2010 ($ρ$: −0.32 to −0.44). However, due to lagging regional development and increased fragmentation, ecological functions had been impaired and carbon emissions had risen, suggesting that the transition needed to focus on balancing ecological preservation and efficiency enhancement (Supplementary Figure 9).

#### Panel quantile regression model

At the land parcel scale, LUP and LUF had a positive effect on carbon emission from cultivated land, while LUQ and LUE showed a negative effect. Forest and grassland carbon uptake was positively affected by LUQ and LUF, while LUP and LUE showed a negative relationship. The positive effect increased with the rise of the quartile, and the negative effect gradually weakened. The correlation between carbon uptake in water and barren land was overall weak. Only in the lower interquartile range (0–0.2), carbon uptake was weakly negatively correlated with LUQ and weakly positively correlated with LUP, LUF and LUE. The regression coefficients converged to 0 when the interquartile range increased. Carbon emissions from construction land showed no significant relationship with the LUQ and LUP before the quartile of 0.6. However, beyond the 0.6 quartile, the relationships between carbon emissions of construction land and LUQ turned from negative to positive, and the impact of function became positive and enhanced. Overall, diversification of land use types significantly suppressed carbon emissions from cultivated land and enhanced carbon uptake from forest and grassland. Large monoculture cultivated land increased carbon emissions, and centrally distributed forest and grassland enhanced carbon uptake. Increased LUF contributed to carbon emissions from cultivated land, but high fragmentation (quartile >0.6) suppressed carbon emissions from construction land. Improving LUF significantly enhanced production activities, increased carbon emissions from cultivated and construction land, and suppressed carbon uptake from forest and grassland. Enhanced LUE reduced carbon emissions from cultivated land, forest and grassland, and promoted carbon emissions from construction land in the high quartile range (>0.6). This indicates that functional improvements generally increased carbon emissions, except for LUE, which contributed to carbon emissions reduction, except in the case of construction land (Fig. 5).

At the municipal scale, the quantile regression performance of land-use carbon emissions is shown in Supplementary Figure 10. Below the 0.7 quantile, the regression coefficients of LUQ and LUP were close to 0, indicating no significant impact on carbon emissions. Between the 0.7 and 0.9 quantiles, LUQ had a slight positive effect, while LUP had a slight negative effect on carbon emissions, but the regression coefficients remained between −0.2 and 0.2, suggesting weak relationships.

The regression coefficients of LUF were positive between 0.1 and 0.9 quartiles, and the LUE exhibited an abrupt change at the 0.7 quartile. Since the carbon emissions from construction land accounted for >90 % of the total, the panel quantile regression of the total carbon emissions in the municipal scale were also similar to those of the construction land, with only minor differences in coefficient values. In the municipal scale, the mutation point of the regression coefficient of the carbon emission and LUE was at the quartile of 0.7, which was a little bit later than that of the critical point of the carbon emission from the construction land (0.6), suggesting that the carbon emissions from forest land, grassland, and other land use type account >90 % of the total carbon emission in the municipality. Thus, the positive impact of LUE improvements on carbon emissions lagged behind the role of other carbon sinks like forests and grasslands, which to some extent mitigated the rapid growth of carbon emissions.

### Robustness and uncertainty analysis

To evaluate robustness, we first compared our city-level emission estimates against ODIAC and CEADs datasets (Fig. 6). Although differences exist (particularly in central and western regions due to sparse socio-economic data and allocation uncertainties), over 80 % of city-level estimates fall within ±50 % of CEADs, and the national mean deviation narrows to 3.8 % by 2020 (Supplementary Figure 11). Agreement with ODIAC also improves over time, consistent with known underestimation of industrial emissions in night-light-based inventories (Oda et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020). To further evaluate discrepancies between our estimates and ODIAC/CEADs, we conducted a comparative validation using Pearson and Spearman correlations (Supplementary Table 8). Comparative testing shows strong agreement in temporal trends between our dataset and ODIAC (Pearson *r* = 0.88, *p* < 0.01; Spearman *r* = 0.90, *p* < 0.01) and with CEADs (Pearson *r* = 0.72, *p* < 0.01; Spearman *r* = 0.75, *p* < 0.01). These outcomes demonstrate that our approach captures consistent patterns, though some discrepancies may exist between datasets in specific region.

Sensitivity analysis shows that perturbing correction coefficients by ±20 % alters national emission totals by <10 % (Supplementary Table 9), confirming the stability of our carbon emission calculations. Besides, panel quantile regression results remain consistent across quantiles: coefficient signs and magnitudes are stable, with no reversal under subsample tests by economic belt. This suggests that the scale-dependent effects identified are not artifacts of model specification but reflect underlying land-carbon dynamics.

Although the national and provincial inventories we employ are well-validated, uncertainty inevitably arises when disaggregating sectoral energy data onto land-use units, central and western China, where socio-economic statistics are sparse and land-use classifications less harmonized (Yue et al., 2016). Such bias is common when proxy variables—night-time lights, GDP density, or urban land fractions—are used to allocate coarse-scale energy statistics to finer land systems (Lin et al., 2022). Differences between our estimates and ODIAC/CEADs largely stem from methodological divergences. ODIAC distributes national totals using night-light intensity, which tends to underestimate emissions in industrial regions with weak nighttime luminosity (Oda et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020). CEADs, by contrast, is based on bottom-up energy statistics at the provincial level, which are then allocated to cities, sometimes smoothing out intra-provincial heterogeneity (Shan et al., 2017). Our approach, which directly integrates land-use transitions with corrected energy inventories, produces higher sensitivity to local land changes, explaining discrepancies. The detailed comparison indicates that while absolute levels differ, the temporal trends across datasets converge, supporting the credibility of our results. Together, these checks indicate that while localized discrepancies remain—especially in data-scarce western regions—the main temporal trends and spatial patterns identified in this study are robust.

## Discussion

### Relationships between land-use carbon emissions and economic growth

It had been shown that there was a close correlation between energy consumption and economic growth, and the correlation coefficients were usually in the range of 0.6∼0.9 (Liu et al., 2022). We calculate the correlation coefficients between the estimated carbon emissions and GDP in the eastern economic belt, the central economic belt, the western economic belt, as well as nationwide. The results were shown in Supplementary Table 7.

At the national scale, the correlation coefficients between land-use carbon emissions and GDP were above 0.6. After 2000, these coefficients exceeded 0.8. Among the economic belts, the eastern region showed the highest correlation, with an average coefficient of 0.8240. The central and western regions had average coefficients of 0.7004 and 0.6824, respectively. This finding indicates a strong positive correlation between land-use carbon emissions and economic development, which strengthened over time, particularly in the eastern region. This pattern may be attributed to the eastern region's higher energy demands during economic development, leading to greater land-use carbon emissions, compared to the central and western regions, where lower correlation coefficients may be influenced by regional economic structures and development levels.

The correlation coefficients between the estimated carbon emissions and economic indicators exhibited relatively high stability, with a coefficient of variation of 14.46 % from 1980 to 2020. The eastern economic belt showed slightly more stable coefficients with a coefficient of variation of 14.10 %, while the central and western belts had coefficients of variation of 19.46 % and 21.35 %, respectively. This suggests that the land-use carbon emission estimation method used in this study provides more stable economic significance.

### Land-use carbon emissions through a four-dimensional lens

Our study designs and implements a unified, four-dimensional typology that integrates land-use quantity (LUQ), pattern (LUP), function (LUF) and efficiency (LUE) to characterize urban land-system transformation. Unlike Wang et al. (2022), who consider only subsets of these dimensions, our framework captures both structural and functional evolution and links them explicitly to carbon emissions. Echoing Liu et al. (2018), we confirm that compact spatial forms enhance efficiency; however, we further demonstrate that even fragmented cities can achieve high efficiency when land-use functions are well-aligned with socio-economic outputs (i.e., high LUF). The explicit inclusion of LUQ allows us to quantify scale effects of land-resource intensity—an aspect often neglected in previous studies. Consequently, our typology reveals non-linear, scale-dependent relationships between land transformation and carbon dynamics, extending the analytical frontier beyond existing work.

In addition, our findings on functional asymmetry resonate with Lambin’s (2011) global land-system transition theory while highlighting distinctively Chinese trajectories. The observed forty-fold intensity of implicit transformation far exceeds the global average reported by Winkler et al. (2021), evidencing unprecedented land-use efficiency gains during China’s compressed urbanization. Particularly striking is the 275 % increase in forest carbon uptake, which far surpasses the IPCC (2021) Asian baseline and offers concrete proof of the success of China’s Grain-for-Green. These exceptional metrics not only validate China’s ecological-civilization policies but also challenge conventional assumptions about the speed and scale at which developing economies can achieve sustainable land-use transitions.

### Strategies for green and low-carbon land use

Our multi-scale analysis reveals three novel mechanisms in interactions between LUT and carbon emissions: (1) A quantile-threshold effect at the 0.7 level in urban emissions indicates that improvements in land-use efficiency (LUE) only translate into net carbon reductions once cities reach a critical development stage. (2) Functional asymmetry: intensifying production-oriented land functions invariably raises emissions, whereas ecological-function gains exhibit a pronounced but delayed mitigation effect. (3) Spatial compensation: carbon sequestration in western ecological zones offsets 18–22 % of eastern urban emissions, evidencing a hitherto under-recognised spill-over mechanism. These insights advance land-system science by embedding scale-dependent thresholds and spatial spill-overs within a unified theoretical framework, revealing the underlying mechanisms driving the spatiotemporal variation of carbon emissions from land use. Specifically, the spatial heterogeneity of LUT-carbon interactions is shaped by a combination of economic, demographic, and policy drivers. Eastern coastal cities show high emissions due to intensive industrialization and urban expansion, consistent with studies on industrial relocation and economic clustering (Zhou et al., 2021). Western regions exhibit lower emissions but greater sensitivity to land expansion, reflecting their reliance on resource-intensive growth models. National land use policies, such as the “Red Line” for cultivated land protection and ecological restoration programs, further modify regional trajectories (Zhao et al., 2024). These factors jointly explain the observed multi-scale spatiotemporal patterns.

Guided by these findings, we propose targeted policy instruments aligned with China’s “1 + *N*” dual-carbon architecture: (1) Eastern megacities (Type III): introduce a “Land-Use Carbon Budget” anchored to the Ministry of Ecology and Environment’s 2025 emission-peak roadmap, with the 0.7 quantile serving as the allocation benchmark. (2) Western ecological belts (Type V): expand the Payments for Ecosystem Services (PES) scheme under the Ecological Conservation Redline policy, using the carbon-uptake coefficients in Supplementary Table 5 as baseline values. (3) Central transition regions (Type II): leverage the detected spatial compensation to launch inter-provincial carbon-offset markets in line with NDRC’s 2023 trading guidelines. These measures are spatially differentiated and element-specific, offering governments and stakeholders a robust scientific basis for accelerating low-carbon land transition while reconciling economic growth and environmental protection—thereby advancing the dual-carbon agenda and sustainable development goals.

### Limitations and future directions

Missing data is a major challenge in carbon emission accounting, especially when analyzing long time - series data (Cheng et al., 2013; Zhao et al., 2016). To tackle this, our study resampled provincial, scale energy consumption data to the prefecture level municipal scale, using each sector’s total output value, traffic volume, and total population data in those cities. However, due to the study’s long time span and slight differences in data statistical caliber across regions, we had to substitute and supplement various indicators when selecting downscaling indicators, which might have increased the study's uncertainty. Liu et al. (2022), in a similar study, addressed data discrepancies and missing data by calculating distribution coefficients or correcting them using GDP statistics. Future studies could refine correction methods to enhance the accuracy and credibility of data processing.

A limitation of this study is that the correlation coefficients between LUT indicators and carbon emissions are relatively modest. This outcome is not unexpected, as land-carbon dynamics are inherently shaped by multiple interacting factors, including industrial structure, energy mix, technological change, and data inventory methods. Prior research has shown that uncertainties in emission inventories, particularly those relying on downscaling proxies such as night lights, can substantially weaken pointwise correlations at the municipal scale (Gately and Hutyra, 2017; Oda, 2019; Chen et al., 2020). Similarly, empirical analyses of intensive land use in China demonstrate that land transitions alone explain only part of the variation in carbon emissions, with socio-economic and policy factors exerting equally important influences (Zhang, 2022; Liu et al., 2023). While our regression results indicate consistent signs and statistical significance across quantiles, the relatively low coefficients suggest that LUT indicators should be interpreted as partial associations under the current model specification. Future research should therefore integrate additional covariates such as technological efficiency, renewable energy adoption, and industrial upgrading, and further apply robustness checks within the panel quantile framework to enhance explanatory power (Powell, 2022; Wang et al., 2019).

Although this study proposed a methodological framework for refined land-use carbon emissions calculation at the land use scale, the main idea was still to account for and allocate carbon emissions of different land use types. However, this method might overlook the differences in carbon emissions between different regions, climatic conditions, crop production methods and land use intensity regions (Zhao et al., 2016; Yi et al., 2022), and the calculation process was not well correlated with actual production and life. Therefore, in future research, it is necessary to comprehensively consider the land use flux observation results, the carbon emission intensity of production units, and the carbon emission pattern of parcel units (Bao et al., 2022) to improve the accuracy of land-use carbon emissions accounting.

## Conclusion

This study examines multi-scale (1 km gird parcel scale, municipal scale and regional scale) spatiotemporal interactions between land use transition (LUT) and carbon emissions in China from 1980 to 2020, integrating city-level and parcel-level data. Four LUT indicators (LUQ, LUP, LUF, LUE) are used to construct a typology of five urban development patterns, while panel quantile regression reveals heterogeneous effects of LUT across emission distributions. The main findings are as follows: (1) Carbon emissions grew rapidly from 1980 to 2020, with significant variation across cities. (2) LUT indicators show distinct temporal trajectories and spatial heterogeneity, with LUQ and LUP dominating in eastern industrialized regions, while LUF and LUE are more prominent in central and western regions. (3) The regression analysis confirms scale-dependent relationships, with stronger effects observed at higher quantiles of emissions. (4) The five-category urban typology explains differences in LUT–carbon interactions and highlights policy-relevant distinctions between expansionary, intensive, stable, transitional, and fluctuating cities. The findings underscore the importance of analyzing land–carbon dynamics at multiple scales and demonstrate the value of typology and quantile approaches in revealing heterogeneous interactions.
