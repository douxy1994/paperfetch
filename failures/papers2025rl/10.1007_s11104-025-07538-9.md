# Effects of nitrogen and phosphorus additions on soil particulate organic carbon through altering microbial decomposition in two tropical montane rainforests

## Abstract

Nutrient availability affects soil microbial activity, thereby regulating soil carbon (C) formation, turnover and storage. Anthropogenic nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) inputs are altering soil nutrients, but their consequences for soil organic carbon (SOC) and its components in P-limited tropical forests are poorly understood.
We conducted a 13-year-long nutrient addition experiment in two tropical montane forests to investigate the effects of N and P additions on soil particulate organic carbon (POC) and mineral-associated organic carbon (MAOC).
Long-term high-N addition increased soil POC concentration and its contribution to SOC through altering edaphic conditions (i.e., soil acidification and nutrient imbalance) and suppressing microbial biomass C together with activity of lignin-modifying enzymes. However, P addition had minor effects on POC pool, which was mainly attributed to alleviate P limitation and stimulate microbial decomposition. In addition, N and P additions did not significantly change soil MAOC in both forests.
Our results indicated that soil microorganisms play key roles in regulating soil C cycling through extracellular enzymes under N and P additions, suggesting a necessity of incorporating it into the prediction of ecosystem C dynamics under global change.

## Introduction

Human-induced nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) input is a ubiquitous disturbance to natural ecosystems, and the extent of the impact is particularly pronounced in tropical regions (Smil 2000; Galloway et al. 2004). Increasing evidence has shown that added N and P is likely to alter carbon (C) cycling processes though affecting plant-soil-microbe interactions (Averill & Waring 2018; Chen et al. 2018; Feng & Zhu 2019). In general, tropical forests harbor a large net primary productivity (NPP) and store a substantial portion of soil C (Phillips et al. 1998; Lehmann & Kleber 2015; Jackson et al. 2017; Pan et al. 2024). Thus, the effects of N and P inputs on bulk soil organic carbon (SOC) pool in tropical forests receive more attention (Cusack et al. 2011a; Feng et al. 2023). Nonetheless, the precise impacts of N and P additions on the SOC within various soil fractions in tropical forests, as well as the underlying mechanisms, remain poorly understood.

The SOC pools have complex compositions with diverse functional fractions, reflecting distinct stabilization characteristics (von Lützow et al. 2007). Utilizing particle-size fractionation classification approach, SOC is conventionally stratified into two distinct fractions: particulate organic carbon (POC, > 53 µm) and mineral-associated organic carbon (MAOC, < 53 µm). POC mainly originates from plant residues and is formed by incomplete decomposition detritus of plant litter and dead roots (Lavallee et al. 2020). MAOC mainly originates from mineral adsorption of dissolved organic carbon and microbial necromass formed through modification or transformation by soil biota (Lavallee et al. 2020). Compared with POC, MAOC is stable and microbial-inaccessible owing to its mineral protection and physical isolation (Cotrufo et al. 2019; Lugato et al. 2021). Given the divergent stabilization characteristics between POC and MAOC, the effects of N and P additions on these two C fractions through plant–microbe regulation pathways may be substantially distinct.

Typically, plant-derived residues directly contribute to soil POC pool and preservation in the form of recalcitrant C (e.g., lignin polymers) (Lavallee et al. 2020; Yang et al. 2025). The breakdown of plant-derived recalcitrant C is microbially mediated through oxidative enzymes (e.g., lignin-modifying enzymes, LMEs) (Carreiro et al. 2000; Chen et al. 2018), where the magnitude of enzymatic activity is usually proportional to the microbial biomass (Burns et al. 2013). Thus, alterations in plant detritus inputs and microbial metabolic activity indicated by microbial biomass and extracellular enzyme activities could affect soil POC pool (Frey et al. 2014; Chen et al. 2018; Lavallee et al. 2020). Recent fertilization experiments showed that N addition enhanced the input of plant-derived lignin into soils (Yang et al. 2025), reduced microbial biomass (Tang et al. 2023), and inhibited production of LMEs (Chen et al. 2018). These findings suggested that N addition may accrue POC by promoting plant-derived C input and/or impeding microbial decomposition (Ye et al. 2018; Chen et al. 2024). Meanwhile, P addition exhibited divergent effects on POC accrual; for instance, it reduced the labile SOC fractions by reducing C investment to belowground or enhancing LMEs activity (Huang et al. 2019; Li et al. 2023); but did not alter microbial biomass and POC in alpine and coastal forests (Yuan et al. 2020b; Luo et al. 2022). As for MAOC, according to microbial efficiency and matrix stabilization hypothesis (MEMs) (Cotrufo et al. 2013), microbial substrate use efficiency and soil mineral adsorption jointly control the formation of MAOC (Bradford et al. 2013; Lavallee et al. 2020). N-induced soil acidification may decrease soil C accumulation in the organo-mineral fraction by limiting microbial growth and diminishing mineral protection (Averill & Waring 2018; Chen et al. 2020). If microbes are not limited by N, then N addition may not alter the accumulation of microbial necromass (as precursor substance for MAOC formation), and thus have minor effects on MAOC pool (Liang et al. 2017; Fan et al. 2020; Tang et al. 2023). A global meta-analysis showed that P addition weakly promoted SOC by stimulating plant-derived C inputs; however, the accelerated decomposition of soil organic matter induced by P addition may be a critical factor for regulating changes in POC and MAOC (Feng et al. 2023). Moreover, P addition may directly influence SOC and its fractions independently, as well as interact with N addition. Both of the additive and neutral effects of combined N and P addition on SOC and its fractions have also been observed (Bradford et al. 2008; Yuan et al. 2020b). In summary, studies on the impact of nutrient additions on SOC and its fractions exhibit ecosystem independence, and there remains a controversy in the research findings. Especially, there is still a lack of long-term experimental evidence in tropical forests.

Acidic soils of tropical forests, as a huge terrestrial organic C reservoir, contain large POC and MAOC stocks due to microbial activities being suppressed by low pH (Averill & Waring 2018; Zhou et al. 2024). In many tropical forests, soil N is relatively abundant and rock-derived P is deficient (Hedin et al. 2003), P generally limits forest productivity on highly weathered soils (Cunha et al. 2022). Thus, N addition may lead to rapid soil acidification in tropical forests via enhanced depletion of cations, which is not conducive to forming MAOC (Chen et al. 2020). P addition may have a neutral effect on MAOC through accelerating microbial growth and simultaneously intensifying its residue decomposition in tropical forests (Yuan et al. 2020b; Feng et al. 2023). In addition, the response of POC to N addition may be predominantly caused by the alteration of microbial decomposition (reflected by microbial biomass and oxidase enzyme activities) because plant-derived C input is changeless in N-rich tropical forests. By contrast, P addition may increase C input by alleviating P limitation in plants and microbes; meanwhile, in turn, promote SOM decomposition. In this case, the effect of N and/or P addition on POC depends on the relative intensity of C input and microbial decomposition, and the phenomenon warrants more examination in P-limited tropical forest.

In this study, we conducted a 13-yr nutrient addition experiment in two tropical montane rainforests (primary and secondary forest) to examine how N and P additions, alone and in combination, affect SOC fractions as well as their underlying mechanisms. Specifically, we hypothesized that: (1) N induced reduction in microbial biomass and oxidase activity (LMEs) would inhibit microbial decomposition, consequently enhancing POC; (2) N-induced soil acidification, depletion of base cations and reduction in microbial biomass is expected to diminish MAOC accumulation; and (3) P addition would have a minor effect on POC and MAOC.

## Materials and methods

### Study sites

Our study was installed within the Jingfengling (JFL) National Park (18°23′−18°50′N, 108°36'−109°05′E), Hainan province, China. It was part of Nutrient Enrichment Experiments in China’s Forests Project (NEECF) (Du et al. 2013). The experimental site is covered by tropical montane rainforests (875 m asl.), including two forest types: primary forest and secondary forest (Zhou et al. 2013). The primary forest exhibits a high species diversity with ~ 171 plant species per hectare, including the common species within the families of Lauraceae and Fagaceae (Fang et al. 2004). The secondary forest underwent selective logging (mainly large trees) in the 1960s and recovered naturally to the present, and mainly consists of Schefflera octophylla, Castanopsis fissa, Sapium discolor and Syzygium tephrodes (Xu et al. 2009).

The mean annual air temperature in primary and secondary forests are c. 19.7 °C and 20 °C, respectively (Ma et al. 2022a). The mean annual precipitation is 2199 mm in both forests, and more than 80% of rainfalls occur during the wet season (May—October) and the remaining portion is in the dry season (November—April) (Ma et al. 2021). Soils in both forests belong to typical lateritic yellow soil (Inceptisols). The subsoils of primary and secondary forests are characterized by low bulk density (1.1 and 1.2 g cm<sup>−3</sup>), typically acidic (pH approximately 4.5 and 4.4), with low concentrations of nutrients such as total N (1.7 and 2.1 g kg<sup>−1</sup>) and total P (0.13 and 0.16 g kg<sup>−1</sup>) (Ma et al. 2021).

### Experimental design

Our experimental design employing randomized blocks comprehensively incorporated the representative types of tropical montane rainforest within the study area, and the results can effectively indicate the characteristics of these forest ecosystems. Briefly, each of the two forest types comprises 18 plots, 20 m × 20 m each, distributed across 3 blocks separated by at least 500 m (total of 36 plots in this study). Each of the blocks consists of 6 plots, which are separated by at least 20 m, indicating that 6 treatments applied in a fully factorial randomized block design: control (with no addition of nutrients), low-N (N25, 25 kg N ha<sup>−1</sup> yr<sup>−1</sup>), medium-N (N50, 50 kg N ha<sup>−1</sup> yr<sup>−1</sup>), high-N (N100, 100 kg N ha<sup>−1</sup> yr<sup>−1</sup>), combined N and P (N + P, 50 kg N ha<sup>−1</sup> yr<sup>−1</sup>+ 50 kg P ha<sup>−1</sup> yr<sup>−1</sup>) and P (P50, 50 kg P ha<sup>−1</sup> yr<sup>−1</sup>). The annual application rates of N and P are standardized to match those used in established tropical forests fertilization studies (e.g., Panama, Dinghushan, Amazon and Puerto Rico experiments) to enable cross-site comparisons (Lu et al. 2015; Wright et al. 2011; Cunha et al. 2022; Cusack et al. 2011a). The N addition gradients are designed to reflect both current atmosphere deposition levels and projected future scenarios, thereby simulating potential impacts of accelerating anthropogenic emissions from intensifying agricultural and industrial development (Du et al. 2013; Gruber & Galloway 2008). Ammonium nitrate (NH<sub>4</sub>NO<sub>3</sub>) and triple superphosphate (Ca-(H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>) serve as N and P fertilizers, respectively. Fertilization commenced in September 2010, with monthly applications throughout the entire year. The experimental fertilizers were dissolved in 30 L water and evenly sprinkled on the surface of each plot, while the control plots received an equivalent volume of water with no fertilizer.

### Sample collection

Soils (0—10 cm) were sampled (after removing surface litter) by 8-cm diameter soil auger in December 2023 at each forest type with 5 cores per plot composited together. In the field, soils were passed through a 2 mm sieve and all the visible roots were carefully selected and washed. Then the diameter < 2 mm fine roots were dried and weighted as the fine root biomass. The sieved soils were divided into two subsamples to measure: (1) soil pH, moisture, SOC, POC, MAOC, total N (TN), total P (TP), and soil exchangeable cations; and (2) soil inorganic N (NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>-N and NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>-N), microbial biomass C (MBC), and enzyme activity.

### Edaphic conditions

In the laboratory, air-dried soil was used for soil pH measurement (1:2.5 soil:water slurry) with a pH meter (Metter-S210 SevenCompactTM, Switzerland). Concentrations of SOC and TN were measured using a TOC/TN analyzer (multi N/C 3100, Analytik Jena, Germany). Soil TP was determined with molybdate/ascorbic acid method after H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>-HClO<sub>4</sub> digestion. Soil (5 g fresh soil) NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup>-N and NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>-N were extracted with 20 ml 2 M KCl and measured using a flow injection analyser (AA3, Bran + Luebbe, Germany). Soil exchangeable cations (K<sup>+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, Na<sup>+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup>, H<sup>+</sup>, Al<sup>3+</sup>, Fe<sup>3+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup>) were measured using the method reported by Chen et al. (2020). Briefly, air-dried soils were extracted with 0.1 mol L<sup>−1</sup> BaCl<sub>2</sub>. All extracts were filtered through a 0.45 µm cellulose acetate filter and the filtrates were measured for cations with an inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometer (ICP-OES, PerkinElmer, USA).

### Soil enzyme activities and microbial biomass C

Seven extracellular enzyme activities (EEAs) in soils involved in C (BG, β−1,4-glucosidase; CB, β-D-cellobiosidase; POX, phenol oxidase; PER, peroxidase), N (NAG, β−1,4-N-acetylglucosaminidase) and P (AP, acid phosphatase) were measured. The detailed protocol described by Saiya-Cork et al. (2002) and German et al. (2011). Briefly, 96-well micro-plates were used to perform enzyme assays. 1.5 g fresh soil was added in 125 ml of 50 mM sodium acetate buffer (pH = 4.5) in each soil sample. For the hydrolytic enzymes (BG, NAG, LAP and AP), 200 µl of soil slurries were aliquoted to 96-well plates along with 50 µl of 200 µM enzyme substrate. For the oxidative enzymes (POX and PER), 50 µl of 200 µM DOPA solution was combined with 200 µl of soil slurries in each well. Each microplate was incubated for 2.5 h for hydrolytic enzymes and 24 h for oxidative enzymes. After incubation, a micro-plate fluorometer reader (SynergyH1M, Biotek, USA) was used to measure enzyme activities, while assayed at 360 nm excitation for hydrolytic enzymes and 460 nm emission for oxidative enzymes (Jing et al. 2020).

Soil microbial biomass C (MBC) was measured using the chloroform fumigation extraction method (Vance et al. 1987). In brief, 8 g sieved soil was fumigated with chloroform (CHCl<sub>3</sub>). Both fumigated and unfumigated samples were extracted with 50 ml of 0.5 M K<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> and filtered through a 2.5 µm membrane filter. Filtered extracts were analyzed with a TOC/TN analyzer (multi N/C 3100, Analytik Jena, Germany). Correction coefficient (K<sub>C</sub> = 0.45) was applied to correct the carbon content of microorganisms (Vance et al. 1987).

### SOC fractions

Utilizing particle-size fractionation classification approach, SOC is conventionally stratified into two distinct fractions: POC (> 53 µm) and MAOC (< 53 µm) (Marriott and Wander 2006; Bradford et al. 2008). In brief, 20 g air-dried soil was thoroughly mixed with 100 ml 5% (w/v) sodium hexametaphosphate solution ((NaPO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>6</sub>) and subjected to shake on an oscillator for 20 h to ensure complete dispersion. Following this, the soil slurries were separated into two fractions by passing them through a 53 µm sieve with the flowing deionized water within the sieving apparatus. Both fractions were dried at 65 °C for 48 h and weighed, then analyzed the organic C of each fraction on a TOC/TN analyzer (multi N/C 3100, Analytik Jena, Germany). The mass and carbon recovery rates during fractionation are presented in Table S1.

### Statistical analyses

First, we fitted linear mixed-effects model using the “ lme4” package to assess the responses of edaphic conditions, SOC fractions, plant fine root biomass, soil enzyme activities and MBC to nutrient additions (Bates et al. 2015). In these models, nutrient additions were treated as fixed factors and a plot nested within block was treated as a random factor. Second, we performed linear regression to analyze the relationships of SOC, POC, and MAOC to MBC and LMEs for nutrient addition experiments, respectively. We also fitted linear regression to determine the relationships of MBC and LMEs to soil pH, respectively. In addition, ANOVA was used to extract the prediction results of these regressions (Lüdecke 2018). Coefficients of determination (R<sup>2</sup>) were calculated by using the “MuMIn” package (Bartoń 2023). We also ensure that all model's residuals follow a normal distribution. If the variable fails the normality test, we then employ non-parametric methods for statistical analysis. All statistical analyses were performed in R 4.2.1 (R Core Team 2022).

According to the potential relationships among soil variables (Chen et al. 2018, 2020; Yuan et al. 2020a), we fitted a structural equation model (SEM) based on priori causal pathways to evaluate the direct and indirect effects of soil abiotic variables (AN and pH) and biotic variables (fine root biomass, MBC, and LMEs) on soil POC (Fig. S1). In the SEM model, we used the data from both forests where the SOC fractions, enzyme activities, and related edaphic conditions showed similar responses to N and P additions, to further investigate the general responses of soil POC to nutrient additions. The SEM analysis was constructed using the “ piecewiseSEM” package (Lefcheck 2016). Fisher’s C was used to test the overall goodness of model fit, as well as when the P > 0.05.

## Results

### Effects of nutrient additions on plant properties and edaphic conditions

N and P additions did not significantly affect fine root biomass in both forests (Fig. S2). N100 declined soil pH by depleting the base cations (primarily Ca<sup>2+</sup>) and generating exchangeable H<sup>+</sup> in both forests (Table 1 and Table S2). N100 significantly promoted NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>-N accumulation in the two forests. Moreover, soil TP increased in P50 and N + P plots in both forests, but remained unchanged in N addition plots (Table 1). P50 and N + P significantly decreased soil N:P in the primary forest, while soil N:P declined under P50 plots in the secondary forest (Table 1).

### Effects of nutrient additions on soil microbial biomass and extracellular enzyme activities

Microbial biomass C (MBC) was markedly suppressed by N50 and N100 in both forests, while promoted by P50 in the secondary forest (Table 1). For the C-related hydrolytic enzyme activity, N100 significantly increased soil BG activity in the primary forest, contributing to cellulase activity, but no nutrient treatment had a significant effect on CB activity in both forests (Fig1 a-c). For the primary forest, soil POX activity significantly decreased in N100 plots, and PER and LMEs activities (PER + POX) showed declined trends under N100 treatment (Fig1 d-f). For the secondary forest, soil POX, PER and LMEs activities declined under N50 and N100 treatments, while increased under P50 treatment (Fig1 d-f). Moreover, N100 decreased N acquisition enzyme activity (NAG + LAP) in both forests (Fig. S3 a-c). P acquisition enzyme activity increased under N25 and N100 plots in the primary forest, and showed a similar enhancement under N100 treatment in the secondary forest (Fig. S3 d). While N50P50 and P50 declined P acquisition enzyme activity in both forests (Fig. S3 d).

## Discussion

In this study, we found that N addition increased POC, while P addition had a minor effect on SOC and its fractions in both tropical montane forests. Consistent with our conceptual framework, the accumulation of soil POC under N addition is attributed to the suppression of microbial decomposition by soil acidification while plant inputs remained unchanged. However, P addition accelerated microbial catabolism by alleviating phosphorus limitation, which was not conducive to SOC accumulation.

### Effects of nutrient additions on soil abiotic and biotic properties

Through the 13-yr nutrient additions experiment in tropical montane forests, our results showed that N100 led to soil acidification in both forests which was likely owing to the depletion of base cations (primarily Ca<sup>2+</sup>) and release of exchangeable H<sup>+</sup> (Table 1 and S2). Similar findings were frequently observed in other tropical forests with highly weathered soils and humid climates (Lu et al. 2015; Tian & Niu 2015). Moreover, N100 significantly increased nitrate but did not change ammonium in both forests (Table 1), which was consistent with the results of previous studies in (sub-)tropical forests (Mao et al. 2017; Chen et al. 2020). This could be attributed to the preference of plants and microorganisms for absorbing or assimilating NH<sub>4</sub><sup>+</sup> ions, leading to the accumulation of NO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup> ions in soils under N addition treatments (Brenner et al. 2005).

For soil biotic properties, N addition decreased MBC, in contrast, P addition tended to promote it in both forests (Table 1). Soil acidification and Al toxicity induced by N addition may inhibit microbial growth and metabolism (Janssens et al. 2010; Jiang et al. 2021), while alleviation of P limitation due to exogenous P input may stimulate the reproduction of microorganisms in P-limited tropical forests (Feng & Zhu 2019; Spohn & Schleuss 2019). Microorganisms produce extracellular enzymes that decompose residues of plants and microbes, obtaining energy and providing nutrients for living plants (Burns et al. 2013; Shah et al. 2015).

For C-related enzymes, N100 showed contrasting effects on cellulase and LMEs in the primary forest, while P addition tended to stimulate LMEs in both forests (Fig1). These results are consistent with Li et al. (2023) and Chen et al. (2018). This pattern may be related to several reasons. First, microorganisms tend to produce LMEs under N limitation because the stable substrates often block N-including molecules through physical and chemical ways (Manzoni et al. 2008; Kuzyakov & Xu 2013). Therefore, N addition may promote cellulase activity and inhibit LME activity by alleviating microbial N limitation and intensifying microbial C limitation (Burns et al. 2013; Kuzyakov & Xu 2013). Second, cellulases are produced by a multitude of microorganisms, yet only a few fungi secrete LMEs (Carreiro et al. 2000). N addition may reduce the abundance and richness of microbes that secrete LMEs (Fog 2008). Third, N-induced soil acidification may influence microbial physiology and formation of the enzyme protein (Sinsabaugh 2010), as evidenced by the significantly negative relationships between MBC or LMEs and pH in both forests (Fig4 and S4). While the tolerance of cellulase activity to low pH induced by N addition was stronger than that of LME activity (Lu et al. 2011; Tian & Niu 2015; Chen et al. 2016). Finally, P addition may stimulate LME activity through relieving P limitation of microorganisms and increasing F/B (ratio of fungi to bacteria) in P-limited tropical forests (Ma et al. 2021; Li et al. 2023). Therefore, the effects of N and P addition on C-related enzymes could be opposite, indicating that microorganisms within tropical montane forests exhibit variable decomposition capabilities in response to N and P enrichment.

### Effects of N addition on SOC fraction

In accordance with other N addition experiments (Riggs et al. 2015; Ye et al. 2018; Chen et al. 2020, 2024), we found that the contents of POC increased with the accumulation of exogenous N inputs in both forests, further contributing to the SOC pools (Fig2). The observed increase in POC due to N enrichment in other studies was frequently attributed to N-induced enhancement of C inputs from above- and/or belowground plant detritus, as well as a potential reduction in the decomposition rate of complex parts of POC fraction (e.g., lignin) (Riggs et al. 2015; Ye et al. 2018; Chen et al. 2024). However, the substrates of plant-derived C from fine root biomass input to POC pool did not alter by N additions, indicating that the accumulation of POC was mainly owing to inhibition of POC decomposition induced by exogenous N inputs in our N-rich and P-limited tropical forests. The suppression of POC decomposition was related to the decline in microbial biomass and LMEs which was induced by soil acidification under N addition, as evidenced by the significantly negative relationships between POC and MBC/LMEs in both forests (Fig3 and 4). LMEs are predominantly linked to the breakdown of chemically resistant substrates, like lignin (Davidson & Janssens 2006). It has been evidenced that reduced LMEs activity contributed to N-induced soil C accumulation (Liu et al. 2016; Chen et al. 2018). In addition, the imbalance of N and P caused by N addition may prompt microorganisms to tend to acquire P, which accelerates the secretion of P acquisition enzyme (Fig. S3 d) and reduces the energy investment to other enzymes in tropical forests (Fig1 and S3 a-c). Therefore, in line with our hypothesis (1), N addition can promote the accumulation of POC by suppressing microbial decomposition processes (specifically via reductions in microbial biomass and oxidase activity) in these tropical forests.

Inconsistent with our hypothesis (2), N additions did not change the MAOC fraction (Fig2), showing different results with some previous studies (Ye et al. 2018; Chen et al. 2020). There are several reasons for the weak response of MAOC to N inputs in our study. Some studies suggested that microbial residues are the main precursor substance of MAOC pools (Cusack et al. 2011a; Averill & Waring 2018). However, compared to fungi, bacteria residues are more inaccessible to microorganisms, dominating the heavy fraction of bulk soil (Griepentrog et al. 2013; Ma et al. 2020). Interestingly, there were no significant effects of N additions on soil microbial residues, while N addition declined the ratio of fungal to bacteria residues in our previous study (Ma et al. 2020). Thus, the proportion of bacterial residues increased and was more likely to contribute to MAOC due to a decline in decomposition induced by N addition. Alternatively, N-induced changes in base cations (decrease in Ca<sup>2+</sup>) and acidic cations were minor, while metal ions associated with MAOC, such as Mg<sup>2+</sup>, Fe<sup>3+</sup>, Al<sup>3+</sup>, did not alter significantly (Table S2), indicating that the mineral adsorption may not be affected during the formation of MAOC. Moreover, plant residues are primarily incorporated into MAOM via the POM pool (Cotrufo & Lavallee 2022; Chang et al. 2023), thus, MAOC pool can be supplemented by the N-induced enhancement of POC.

### Effects of P addition and NP co-addition on SOC fractions

In accordance with our hypothesis (3), we found that P addition had a minor effect on POC, while promoted the process of microbial decomposition (i.e., increase in MBC and LMEs) in both forests (Table 1 and Fig1, 2, 4, and 5). Our results in line with several findings that P addition promoted microbial oxidative activities in P-limited tropical forests but did not significantly change soil C pool (Mo et al. 2021; Li et al. 2023). The neutral effect of P addition on POC may be caused by the offset of C input and output. P addition alleviated ecosystem P limitation and diminished the competitive pressure for P between microorganisms and plants, and subsequently stimulated microbial proliferation and accelerated decomposition processes (Camenzind et al. 2017), which was corroborated by the accumulation of soil P and MBC induced by P addition in our study (Table 1). Furthermore, our previous research (Ma et al. 2022b) and a global meta-analysis (Feng & Zhu 2019; Sun et al. 2022), demonstrated that P addition stimulated soil respiration, indicating that exogenous P inputs promoted soil C output and was not favorable for the sequestration of SOC. In addition, P addition can lead to changes in microbial community structure in P-limited tropical forests. Fungal communities and gram-positive bacteria are considered to degrade more recalcitrant substrates (Kramer & Gleixner 2008; Berg & McClaugherty 2020). Our previous study found that P addition enhanced the F/B (ratio of fungi to bacteria) and G<sup>+</sup>/G<sup>−</sup> (ratio of gram-positive bacteria to gram-negative bacteria) in both forests (Ma et al. 2021), indicating that microbes tended to develop towards communities with enhanced decomposing capabilities. Moreover, P addition may stimulate the secretion of C-related enzymes. P is an important component of rRNA which regulates synthesis of protein (Sterner & Elser 2002). P addition may facilitate this process and produce extracellular enzymes for catabolism (Li et al. 2023). Confirming the above view, some studies found that P addition stimulated the decomposition of plant residues in tropical forests (Chen et al. 2015; Camenzind et al. 2017; Gora et al. 2018), but it also may promote the replenishment of plant-derived C (such as litterfall or root residues/exudates) into soil (Wright et al. 2018; Cunha et al. 2022). A meta-analysis revealed that P addition simultaneously enhanced plant-derived inputs and accelerated microbial decomposition (i.e., increase in MBC and SOM decomposition rates), yet did not alter POC (Feng et al. 2023). However, given the limited sample size of POC, the observed overall response of POC to P addition might not fully represent general responses of SOC fraction. Furthermore, the impacts of P addition on SOC—both in terms of fractions and pathways—remain poorly investigated across contrasting ecosystems, resulting in a limited understanding of the related mechanisms. Therefore, sustained investigations with systematic measurements on existing long-term P addition experiments are critical for advancing a holistic realization of soil C cycling processes.

In line with Li et al. (2023), we found that NP co-addition had no significant effects on SOC fractions, MBC, pH, and C-related enzyme activities in both forests (Table 1 and Figs1 and 2). The neutral effects of NP co-addition may be attributed to the fact that P addition can weaken the inhibition of soil microorganism physiology induced by N inputs (Ma et al. 2021). Especially in P-limited tropical forests, the reduction of microbial decomposition caused by N addition may offset by P supplement. However, single P addition or NP co-addition did not change MAOC pool, indicating that the formation and decomposition of MAOC were weakly affected by P inputs. For example, microbial residues and soil exchangeable metal cations, as the main contributors of MAOC pool, were not altered by P and NP additions in our study sites (Ma et al. 2020). Although the microbial activity was slightly enhanced after P addition, the strong mineral protection effect of MAOC still produced a minor response.

### Limitations

Our study faced some limitations. Firstly, the analysis was restricted to 0–10 cm soil and did not analyze the responses in the deep soil, which is usually different with that of topsoil (Cusack et al. 2011b; Fan et al. 2024; Hu et al. 2024; Kuang et al. 2023). Therefore, future investigations should incorporate vertical soil stratifications as a critical experimental variable to resolve depth-dependent mechanisms of SOC-nutrient interactions. Secondly, the seasonal dynamics of biotic and abiotic drivers on SOC and its fractions warrant systematic investigation. Thirdly, despite distinct characteristics of tropical forest ecosystems in our study sites, soil POC exhibited consistent sensitivity to nutrient additions, demonstrating that anthropogenic N and P inputs can alter soil C cycling processes. Moreover, given the P scarcity in tropical forests, expanded investigations into P addition effects on SOC and its fractions are imperative. Nevertheless, considering the intrinsic heterogeneity of tropical edaphic systems and their associated communities, future experimental frameworks should examine the responses of tropical forests at different successional stages and soil types to fertilization treatments, and coupled with increased replication to ensure experimental robustness.

## Conclusions

Our results showed that, rather than P addition, long-term N addition increased POC, contributing to SOC pools in the primary and secondary tropical montane forests. These results suggest that N-induced POC accumulation is independent of succession stage and tropical forests would sequestrate C into soils, with improving soil N availability. Furthermore, the N-induced POC accumulation was related to suppressing microbial biomass C and LMEs activity, suggesting that microbial communities play a pivotal role in mediating soil C sequestration in tropical forest ecosystems. However, nutrient additions did not alter MAOC content but decreased MAOC:SOC, indicating that stable C pool is not sensitive to decadal nutrient treatments while non-stable C pool would contribute to C sink in tropical forests. Therefore, increasing nitrogen deposition may compromise the chemical stability of SOC, implying a higher C release into the atmosphere in the context of global warming. Taken together, our study reveals that nutrient addition exerts broad-reaching impacts on SOC and its fractions in tropical forests. It calls that the roles of microbially mediated processes of SOC accumulation and stability should be incorporated into the future management and conservation of the tropical forests under nitrogen deposition scenarios.
