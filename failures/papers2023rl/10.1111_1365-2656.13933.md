# Maintenance of biodiversity in multitrophic metacommunities: Dispersal mode matters

## Abstract

Although metacommunity models generally formulate dispersal as a random, passive process, mounting evidence suggests that dispersal can be an active process depending on species fitness over the landscape, particularly in multitrophic communities. How different dispersal modes (i.e. from random to increasingly fitness-dependent dispersal) modulate the effect of dispersal on biodiversity remains unclear. Here, we used a metacommunity model of food webs to investigate the effects of dispersal and habitat heterogeneity on biodiversity and how these effects may be dependent on dispersal mode.
Our results showed that compared to isolated systems, random dispersal increased local food web diversity ($α$ diversity) but decreased across-community dissimilarity ($β$ diversity) and regional food web diversity ($γ$ diversity), consistent with findings from competitive metacommunity models. However, fitness-dependency could alter the effects of dispersal on biodiversity. Both $β$ and $γ$ diversity increased with the strength of fitness-dependency of dispersal, while $α$ diversity peaked at intermediate fitness-dependency. Notably, strong fitness-dependent dispersal maintained levels of $β$ and $γ$ diversity similar to those observed in isolated systems. Thus, random dispersal and isolation (i.e. no dispersal) can be considered as two extremes along the continuum of fitness-dependent dispersal, in terms of their effects on biodiversity.
Moreover, both biodiversity-habitat heterogeneity and biodiversity-habitat connectivity relationships depended on the dispersal mode. Strikingly, under random dispersal, $γ$ diversity decreased with habitat heterogeneity and connectivity, but under strong fitness-dependent dispersal, it increased with habitat heterogeneity and remained unchanged as habitat connectivity increased.
Our study highlights the context dependence of dispersal effects on biodiversity in heterogeneous landscapes. Our findings have useful implications for biodiversity conservation and landscape management, where management strategies should account for different modes of dispersal across taxa, thus different responses of biodiversity to habitat heterogeneity and connectivity.

尽管集合群落模型通常将扩散描述为一个随机的、被动的过程，但越来越多的证据表明扩散可能是一个主动过程，其速率依赖于物种在生境斑块上的适合度，特别是在多营养级群落中。扩散模式(即从随机扩散到越来越依赖适合度的扩散)如何调节扩散对生物多样性的影响尚不清楚。在这里，我们使用一个食物网上的集合群落模型来研究扩散和生境异质性对生物多样性的影响以及这些影响如何依赖于扩散模式。
我们的结果显示，与孤立系统相比，随机扩散增加了局域食物网多样性(α多样性)，但降低了群落间的差异性(β多样性)和区域食物网多样性(γ多样性)，这与竞争性集合群落模型中的发现一致。然而，扩散速率对适合度的依赖性可能改变扩散对生物多样性的影响。β和γ多样性随着扩散的适合度依赖性增强而增加，而α多样性在中等适合度依赖性下达到峰值。值得注意的是，极端的强适合度依赖扩散使β和γ多样性维持在与孤立系统相似的水平。因此，在讨论对生物多样性的影响时，随机扩散和无扩散(即孤立群落)可以被视为扩散对适合度的依赖性从弱至强的两个极端。
此外，生物多样性与生境异质性以及生境连通度的关系都取决于扩散模式。令人惊讶的是，在随机扩散下，γ多样性随着生境异质性和连通度的增加而降低，但在强适合度依赖扩散下， γ多样性随着生境异质性的增加而增加，并在生境连通度增加时保持不变。
我们的研究强调了在异质景观中，扩散对生物多样性的影响随不同扩散模式而异。我们的发现对生物多样性保护和景观管理具有重要的实用意义——设计保护和管理策略时应考虑到不同物种采用不同的扩散模式，因而对生境异质性和连通度有不同响应。

## 1 INTRODUCTION

Human activities are driving massive biodiversity loss across the globe (Ceballos et al., 2015; Kolbert, 2014). Understanding the mechanism of biodiversity maintenance is key to conservation practices and sustainable land management (Dale et al., 2000). While early studies on biodiversity maintenance focus on a single spatial scale, particularly local communities, ecological patterns are inevitably shaped by processes operating at multiple scales (Levin, 1992; Rosenzweig, 1995). To this end, a research paradigm, namely the metacommunity framework, has arisen to address the interactive effects of local and spatial dynamics on biodiversity across scales (Leibold et al., 2004).

An important insight from metacommunity theory is that dispersal can affect species diversity differently at different spatial scales. At the local scale, dispersal allows recolonization of habitats where species go extinct by chance. Such rescue effects contribute to increasing local species diversity (Brown & Kodric-Brown, 1977). At the regional scale, dispersal can homogenize population distributions and promote species co-occurrence (Fodelianakis et al., 2019), which intensifies species competition and impairs regional community diversity (Matthiessen et al., 2010). In addition, high dispersal can also increase spatial synchrony of population dynamics, thus elevating the risk that populations from different locations become extinct simultaneously (Abbott, 2011; Liebhold et al., 2004). Using a metacommunity model of competitors, Mouquet and Loreau (2002, 2003) demonstrated scale-dependent responses of biodiversity to dispersal. Specifically, as the dispersal rate increased, local species diversity ($α$ diversity) exhibited a unimodal pattern, across-community dissimilarity ($β$ diversity) consistently decreased, and regional species diversity ($γ$ diversity) remained unchanged initially but then decreased when dispersal rate was high. These theoretical predictions on diversity-dispersal relationships across scales were supported by a meta-analysis of relevant experiments (Cadotte, 2006).

Compared to the vast literature on competitive metacommunities, studies on trophic metacommunities (i.e. metacommunities of food webs) remain limited (Leibold & Chase, 2018). Early studies on spatial dynamics of food webs focused largely on small modules, e.g. food webs consisting of 2–4 species. Amarasekare (2008) provided a synthesis on these studies and concluded that dispersal could either enhance or impair species diversity, depending on food web structure and the spatial scale of dispersal. More recent theoretical studies attempted to address metacommunities of complex food webs, which revealed an important role of dispersal in shaping species richness and various network properties, such as food chain length and connectance (Calcagno et al., 2011; Gravel, Canard, et al., 2011; Gravel, Massol, et al., 2011; Häussler et al., 2020; Holt, 1996; Pillai et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2021). However, these studies were mostly built upon extinction-recolonization processes of patch dynamics, which ignores local population dynamics and thus constrains our ability to predict food web dynamics (but see Ryser et al., 2021). Given the fact that species at higher trophic levels are faced with higher extinction risks (Binzer et al., 2011; Estes et al., 2011; Purvis et al., 2000), there has been an increasing call for developing metacommunity models of dynamical food webs to guide ecosystem management for biodiversity conservation (Guzmán et al., 2019; Leibold & Chase, 2018).

One difficulty faced by metacommunity models of food webs is how to formulate the process of dispersal across species at different tropical levels (Guzmán et al., 2019; Holt et al., 2005). Previous metacommunity models have mostly considered dispersal as a random process that is independent of local species interactions. Such a simplification facilitates theoretical analysis and may capture the reality for some organisms, for instance plants or planktons, that rely on wind or water for passive dispersal (Hanly & Mittelbach, 2017; Jenkins et al., 2007). However, most animal species disperse actively, with dispersal decision made on the basis of local species fitness (e.g. per capita growth rate) that is jointly determined by abiotic factors and biotic interactions, hereafter referred to as *fitness-dependent dispersal*. A recent synthesis of experiments spanning a broad range of taxa from protozoa to vertebrates demonstrated that species dispersal behaviours depended on the densities of both their preys and predators, that is species tended to disperse when prey availability was low and/or when predation risk was high (Fronhofer et al., 2018). Such a fitness-dependent dispersal as modulated by trophic interactions presents a major challenge when extending findings from competitive metacommunity models (often assuming random dispersal) to multitrophic systems.

Fitness-dependent dispersal has received some attention in theoretical literature. Previous studies have investigated the evolutionary origin of fitness-dependency of dispersal (Metz & Gyllenberg, 2001; Poethke & Hovestadt, 2002; Travis et al., 1999), or its impacts on species diversity in simple food web modules (Amarasekare, 2010; Fronhofer et al., 2018; Hauzy et al., 2007; Hein & Martin, 2020). For instance, Amarasekare (2007) found that fitness-dependent dispersal impaired species coexistence in a three-species module with intraguild predation. Yet, it remains unclear whether these results extend to complex food webs. A recent attempt was made by Mougi (2019), who showed that fitness-dependent dispersal increased food web stability, defined as the asymptotic resilience of food webs in face of perturbations, but its implications for biodiversity maintenance in complex food webs remain unexplored. Ryser et al. (2019) conducted another study that modelled dispersal as a fitness-dependent process in complex food webs, but this study mainly focused on the effect of mortality during dispersal, rather than that of the fitness-dependency of dispersal per se.

The effects of dispersal on biodiversity also depend on the configuration of landscapes, particularly habitat heterogeneity. Dispersal has been shown to have limited effects on species diversity in homogeneous landscapes, whereas its effects became more pronounced as habitat heterogeneity increased (Matthiessen et al., 2010; Plitzko & Drossel, 2015). On the other hand, habitat heterogeneity itself has been widely recognized as an important driver of biodiversity at large scales (Hortal et al., 2009; Stein et al., 2014), and its effect can be modulated by dispersal (Leibold & Chase, 2018). In particular, random dispersal tends to homogenize the dynamics and distributions of populations over space, which can weaken the effects of habitat heterogeneity. That said, it remains unclear how fitness-dependent dispersal may interact with habitat heterogeneity. Resolving the interaction between *dispersal mode* (i.e. from random to increasingly fitness-dependent dispersal) and habitat heterogeneity may provide a clue to reconciling contradictory results on dispersal effects in empirical studies, where habitat connectivity appeared to be ‘good’ for biodiversity in some studies but ‘bad’ in others (Fahrig, 2017; Fahrig et al., 2019; Fletcher et al., 2018).

In this study, we used metacommunity models of complex food webs to investigate how dispersal mode (from random to increasing fitness-dependent dispersal), in interaction with habitat heterogeneity and connectivity, influences biodiversity across spatial scales. We address three questions: (1) how do different modes of dispersal influence the maintenance of biodiversity across scales? (2) How does dispersal mode affect the relationship between habitat heterogeneity and biodiversity across scales? (3) How does dispersal mode affect the relationship between habitat connectivity and biodiversity across scales? Our results reveal novel predictions on the effects of dispersal mode: increasing fitness-dependency of dispersal maintains biodiversity across scales and modifies the relationship between biodiversity and habitat heterogeneity or connectivity. We end with a discussion about the mechanisms underlying our findings and their implications for biodiversity conservation.

## 2 MATERIALS AND METHODS

### 2.1 The model

We developed a spatially implicit metacommunity model, which consisted of three habitat patches and hosted a food web of initially 50 species. We first generated food web topologies using the niche model (Williams & Martinez, 2000). Briefly, a niche value was randomly assigned to each species; then for each species $i$, a feeding range was defined and any species $j$ with a niche value falling within this range was considered as prey of species $i$ (see Supplementary Text 1 for details). We defined species without any prey as plants, and species with at least one prey as animals. We modified the resulting food webs by removing any cannibalism link and adapted the food web to avoid disconnected species that have neither predator nor prey by recursively resigning new niche values to such species. For food webs without plant species, we reassigned a new niche value to the species with the smallest niche value so that at least one plant species is present. Examples of generated food webs are shown in Figure S1.

Based on these food webs, species dynamics were characterized by local population growth G, trophic interactions T, and dispersal M (Figure 1a). Specifically, the temporal dynamics of the population density of species $j$ on patch $k$ ($N_{jk}$) followed:

**Equation 1.**

$$
\frac{dN_{jk}}{dt} = G_{jk} + T_{jk} + M_{jk}.
$$

The first term in Equation (1) ($G_{jk}$) represents the rate of population growth under competition for plants or death for animals:

**Equation 2.**

$$
\begin{matrix} G_{jk} = {\begin{matrix} r_{jk} \cdot (1 - ∑_{i \in plant}\frac{α_{ij}N_{ik}}{K_{k}})N_{jk}, j \in plant \\ r_{jk} \cdot N_{jk}, j \in animal \end{matrix}, \end{matrix}
$$

where $r_{jk}$ is the intrinsic growth/death rate of species $j$ on patch $k$ ($r_{jk} > 0$ for plants, $r_{jk} < 0$ for animals), $α_{ij}$ is the competition coefficient of species $i$ on species $j$, and $K_{k}$ is the universal carrying capacity for all plant species on patch $k$.

![Figure 1](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/cms/asset/1806f047-e216-4d02-a063-9ddd815192db/jane13933-fig-0001-m.jpg)

**Figure 1.** Illustration of the metacommunity model (a) and fitness-dependent dispersal (b). (a) The dynamics of population density of species *j* on patch *k* ($N_{jk}$) is regulated by three processes: local population growth, trophic interactions, and spatial dynamics. (b) Dispersal is modelled with fitness-dependent emigration and random immigration. Consider an example species *j* with 30 individuals (represented by pack-men). Given the habitat connectivity (represented by potential emigration rate) *m*<sub>0</sub> = 0.1 (i.e. three pack-men as potential emigrants on each patch), the realized emigration rate $m_{jk}$ on patch k depends on fitness-dependency $λ$ of dispersal (left panel, blue curves with different darkness corresponding to different values of $λ$) and per capita net growth rate $r^{net}$ of species *j* on its original patch (left panel, orange, green, and blue dashed lines for three patches with corresponding colours). The emigrated population (dark grey pac-men) equally splits and immigrates into other patches in the landscape (right panel). Take for example the green patch with a small positive $r^{net}$, among the three potential emigrants: (i) two of them eventually leave the patch and each immigrates into the orange or green patch under random dispersal (upper); (ii) only one leaves the patch and immigrates into the other two patches under weak fitness-dependent dispersal (middle); and (iii) none leaves the patch under strong fitness-dependent dispersal (lower).

The second term in Equation (1) ($T_{jk}$) represents population growth or decline due to trophic interactions:

**Equation 3.**

$$
\begin{matrix} T_{jk} = - ∑_{h \in {predator ofj}}F_{hj, k}N_{jk} + ∑_{i \in {prey ofj}}e_{ji}F_{ji, k}N_{ik}. \end{matrix}
$$

where $e_{ji}$ is the assimilation efficiency when predator $j$ consumes prey $i$, and $F_{ji, k}$ is the functional response when predator $j$ consumes prey $i$ on patch $k$: $F_{ji, k} = \frac{w_{ji}a_{ji}N_{ik}^{q_{ji}}}{1 + ∑_{l}w_{jl}a_{jl}h_{jl}N_{ik}^{q_{jl}}}$. The parameter $q_{ji}$ is the Hill coefficient, which captures the dependency of consumption rate on prey population density (Real, 1977). $a_{ji}$, $h_{ji}$, and $w_{ji}$ represent the attack rate, handling time, and feeding preference, respectively, of predator $j$ on prey $i$.

The third term in Equation (1) ($M_{jk}$) captured dispersal of species across patches. First, we assumed that all populations in the focal patch have the same potential emigration rate (m0), which is the maximum emigration rate limited by physical constrains, for example habitat connectivity. Second, we calculated the realized emigration rate for each species based on its fitness (i.e. net per capita growth rate) on the focal patch (Figure 1b). By definition (Equation 1), the per capita net growth rate of species j on patch k is: $r_{jk}^{net} = \frac{G_{jk} + T_{jk}}{N_{jk}}$. We thus formulated the realized emigration rate of species $j$ from patch $k$ ($m_{jk}$) as:

**Equation 4.**

$$
m_{jk} = \frac{n_{P} - 1}{(n_{P} - 1) + exp(λ \times (r_{jk}^{net} + ϵ))} \times m_{0}.
$$

Here, $n_{P}$ is the number of habitat patches and $λ$ is the dependency strength of emigration decision on local fitness, hereafter referred to as fitness-dependency. $λ = 0$ represents random dispersal at a fixed emigration rate $m_{jk} = \frac{n_{P} - 1}{n_{P}}m_{0}$, and $λ > 0$ represents fitness-dependent dispersal that reacts to $∣r_{jk}^{net}∣$ with sensitivity at the order of $1 / λ$ (Figure 1b, Figure S2). When $r_{jk}^{net} = 0$, species j in patch k has zero net growth rate, so its realized emigration rate is close to that of the random dispersal scenario, that is $m_{jk}≈\frac{n_{P} - 1}{n_{P}} \cdot m_{0}$. The value $ϵ = 10^{- 5}$ captures a small tendency to stay in the original patch, which is introduced also to avoid numerical instability (Supplementary Text 2; note that our results are robust even for $ϵ = 0$, see Figure ST2.1). Third, we assumed that species have no information on potential destination patches, so emigrated individuals are equally split and immigrate into other patches in the metacommunity. Under these assumptions, the rate of species $j$ dispersing from patch $k$ to patch $k^{′}$ ($m_{j(k→k^{′})}, k^{′}≠k$) can be expressed as:

**Equation 5.**

$$
m_{j(k→k^{′})} = \frac{1}{n_{P} - 1}m_{jk}, k^{′}≠k.
$$

Combined, the dispersal term summarizes population change caused by emigration and immigration:

**Equation 6.**

$$
M_{jk} = - m_{jk}N_{jk} + ∑_{k^{′}≠k}m_{j(k^{′}→k)}N_{jk^{′}}.
$$

By definition, the potential emigration rate ($m_{0}$, reflecting habitat connectivity) together with the strength of fitness-dependency of dispersal ($λ$) defines a dispersal scenario. We note that, given that the number of patches ($n_{P}$) is kept constant throughout, our model assumes that individuals have information only about their original patch, and only the emigration process is fitness-dependent. However, the model can be extended to more general cases where individuals have information of all patches over the landscape (i.e. omniscience) and both emigration and immigration processes depend on fitness (see Supplementary Text 2).

### 2.2 Simulations

We first generated 500 food webs with randomly drawn parameters as described in Supplementary Table 1. For each food web, we simulated its dynamics on the three-patch metacommunity with varying levels of habitat heterogeneity (*H*) and scenarios of dispersal. Specifically, we considered six levels of habitat heterogeneity (*H* = 0, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8, 1). For each level of habitat heterogeneity $H$, we randomly sampled $x_{jk}, y_{k}~U(- H, H)$, then the intrinsic growth rate $r_{jk}$ of plant species *j* on patch *k* was multiplied by $1 + x_{jk}$, and the carrying capacity $K_{k}$ of patch *k* was multiplied by $1 + y_{k}$. Note that in our model, habitat heterogeneity only influenced the dynamics of plant species directly, but such influences could propagate to higher trophic levels. Given the habitat connectivity (e.g. *m*<sub>0</sub> = 0.1), we considered six scenarios with varying strengths of fitness-dependency of dispersal ($λ = 0, 10^{0}, 10^{1}, 10^{2}, 10^{3}, 10^{4}$) and compared them with the isolated scenario (i.e. *m*<sub>0</sub> = 0). Note that $λ = 10^{4}$ represents strong fitness-dependency of dispersal where a small value of $r_{net}$ at the magnitude 10<sup>−4</sup> would be sufficient to influence the dispersal decision; such an extreme value was designed to illustrate the effects of fitness-dependent dispersal in limiting scenarios.

To compare with early studies investigating the relationship between biodiversity and dispersal rates (e.g. Mouquet & Loreau, 2003), we simulated metacommunities with different levels of habitat connectivity, represented by a broad gradient of potential emigration rates ($m_{0} = 0$, and 13 emigration rates evenly distributed on a log scale from $10^{- 4}$ to $10^{0}$). We examined how dispersal mode (from random to increasingly fitness-dependent) affects the relationships between habitat connectivity ($m_{0}$) and biodiversity. For computational reasons, we simulated different habitat connectivity only under a median level of habitat heterogeneity, that is $H = 0.6$.

For each metacommunity, we started with an initial population density of 1 for all species and numerically solved our models (Equations 1-6) with ode15s (absTol = 10<sup>−11</sup>, relTol = 10<sup>−4</sup>) in MATLAB for 100,000 time steps, when most repetitions reach point equilibria. During the simulation, we immediately set any population density below $10^{- 10}$ to 0 to avoid obscuring numerical noise as real populations. We calculated equilibrium population density as the average population density across the last 1000 time steps, where populations with equilibrium densities smaller than a global cut-off were considered extinct. In the main text, we chose the global cut-off 10<sup>−6</sup> based on the population density distribution in the isolated scenario, but our results are robust to any cut-off value from 10<sup>−10</sup> to 10<sup>−2</sup> (Supplementary Text 3). It is worth noting that although at equilibrium $\frac{dN_{jk}}{dt} = 0$ by definition, net emigration rates are generally not zero except for the isolated scenario (Figure S3).

With these simulated metacommunities, we quantified biodiversity at different scales, namely α (local), $β$ (across community), and γ (regional) scales. $γ$ diversity was defined as the number of species with maximum population density (across patches) above the global cut-off (1e-6). $β$ diversity was defined as the mean value of Gower's distance between patch pairs:

**Equation 7.**

$$
β = \frac{∑_{k}∑_{k^{′}≠k}D_{kk^{′}}}{n_{P}(n_{P} - 1)}.
$$

Here, $D_{kk^{′}}$ represents the Gower's distance between patch k and k′ (Anderson et al., 2006; Gower, 1971):

**Equation 8.**

$$
D_{kk^{′}} = \frac{∑_{j}w_{j}|N_{jk} - N_{jk^{′}}| / R_{j}}{∑_{j}w_{j}},
$$

where $w_{j} = 0$ when species j is extinct in both patches ($N_{jk} = N_{jk^{′}} = 0$), and $w_{j} = 1$ otherwise. $R_{j}$ is the maximum population density for species j across all patches, used as a normalizer to account for the unevenness of population density across species. $α$ diversity was defined as the average number of local populations with densities no smaller than 10% of the maximum population density for respective species. The local cutoff fraction 10% is used to exclude local populations with low population densities (relative to populations of the same species in other patches) maintained by dispersal. Our definitions took into account the unevenness in population densities across patches, as well as the fact that population densities differed across species in orders of magnitude (see Supplementary Text 3 for detailed discussion on population density distribution and choice of cutoff values). We then compared biodiversity at these three scales among metacommunities with different dispersal modes (i.e. random [$λ = 0$] or fitness-dependent dispersal [$λ > 0$]), and different levels of habitat heterogeneity and connectivity (i.e. isolated [$m_{0} = 0$] or connected [$m_{0} > 0$]).

Moreover, to test the robustness of our results, we simulated all above-mentioned scenarios (different strengths of fitness-dependent dispersal in combination with different levels of habitat heterogeneity or connectivity) with two additional scenarios of plant dispersal: (1) plants did not disperse, and (2) plants always disperse randomly. For each of these additional scenarios, 50 food webs were simulated.

## 3 RESULTS

### 3.1 Fitness-dependent dispersal maintains biodiversity across scales

Compared to isolated systems (i.e. $m_{0} = 0$, broad light green lines in Figures 2 and 3), dispersal generally increased α diversity but decreased β diversity, leading to an overall declining γ diversity (Figure 2a–c). Such effects differed markedly among dispersal modes. As the strength of fitness-dependency of dispersal (*λ*) increased, β and γ diversity consistently increased (Figure 2b,c), whereas α diversity first increased following an increasing $γ$ diversity but then decreased due to selective patch occupancy (Figure 2a; also Figure ST3.4 in Supplementary Text 3 for change in population density distribution). Strikingly, when dispersal is strongly fitness-dependent (e.g. $λ = 10^{4}$), α, β and γ diversity all converged to their corresponding values in isolated systems (Figure 2a–c, also see Figure 5 and Section 4 for an intuitive explanation). Taken together, whereas random dispersal generally decreased β and γ diversity compared to isolated systems, fitness-dependency could alleviate the negative effects of dispersal on β and γ diversity, while still maintaining a higher α diversity unless the fitness-dependency becomes extreme.

The strength of the dispersal effects also depended on the level of habitat heterogeneity and connectivity (Figure 2, Figure S4). In completely homogeneous landscapes ($H = 0$, i.e. species have the same growth parameters across all patches), dispersal—either random or fitness-dependent—had no effect on biodiversity at any scale (Figure 2d–f). As habitat heterogeneity level increased, the effects of dispersal on biodiversity at all spatial scales (as described above) became more pronounced. The effects of fitness-dependent dispersal on biodiversity at different scales also became more pronounced as habitat connectivity $m_{0}$ increased, and remained qualitatively similar under varying levels of habitat connectivity, except for alpha diversity under very low connectivity (Figure 2g–i). These results remained qualitatively unchanged under different scenarios of plant dispersal (Figures S6 and S9).

### 3.2 Habitat heterogeneity–diversity relationships under different scenarios of dispersal

In isolated systems (*m*<sub>0</sub> = 0), higher habitat heterogeneity decreased α diversity, but increased β and γ diversity (Figure 3a–c, broad light green lines). The presence of dispersal could alter these patterns, and such effects depend on dispersal mode (Figure 3; Figure S4). Under the random dispersal scenario ($λ = 0$), while habitat heterogeneity still decreased $α$ diversity (Figure 3a), it had relatively weaker effect on $β$ diversity and thereby decreased $γ$ diversity (Figure 3b,c), contrasting to patterns under the isolated scenario. In other words, random dispersal homogenized the landscape and weakened the signature of habitat heterogeneity in spatial distributions of species (e.g. $β$ diversity). This conclusion is especially true when habitat heterogeneity level is low or when habitat connectivity is high. In extreme scenarios, the signature of habitat heterogeneity can be completely erased, such as in Figure 4b when *H* = 0.6 and $m_{0} = 1$. Under intermediate fitness-dependency of dispersal (e.g. $λ = 10^{2}$), α diversity first increased and then decreased as habitat heterogeneity increased, whereas β and γ diversity generally increased with habitat heterogeneity. Under strong fitness-dependency of dispersal (e.g. $λ = 10^{4}$), α diversity decreased, and β and γ diversity increased, with habitat heterogeneity, just as patterns under the isolated scenario. Therefore, fitness-dependency of dispersal retained the signature of habitat heterogeneity that would otherwise be weakened if dispersal were random. Again, these results were robust to different scenarios of plant dispersal (Figures S7 and S10).

### 3.3 Habitat connectivity–diversity relationships under different modes of dispersal

The relationship between species diversity and habitat connectivity ($m_{0}$) also depended on the mode of dispersal (Figure 4; Figure S8). When dispersal was random ($λ = 0$), $α$ diversity first increased and then decreased as *m*<sub>0</sub> increased, whereas $β$ and $γ$ diversity both decreased with $m_{0}$ (Figure 4), similar to results in competitive metacommunities (Mouquet & Loreau, 2002, 2003). Such patterns changed when dispersal was fitness-dependent. At the local scale, when fitness-dependency of dispersal is sufficiently strong (e.g. $λ \geq 10^{3}$), $α$ diversity increased monotonically with $m_{0}$, contrary to the hump-shape relationship observed under scenarios of random dispersal or weak fitness-dependent dispersal (Figure 4a). At the $β$ and $γ$ scales, diversity still decreased monotonically with $m_{0}$, but their decreases became negligible for sufficiently strong fitness-dependency of dispersal (Figure 4b,c). Again, these results are qualitatively robust to different scenarios of plant dispersal (Figures S8 and S11); however, when plants dispersed randomly at high rates, the decrease of $β$ and $γ$ with increasing $m_{0}$ could not be fully reversed by strong fitness-dependency of animal dispersal.

## 4 DISCUSSION

Our study demonstrates the importance of dispersal mode, by interacting with habitat heterogeneity and connectivity, in regulating food web diversity across spatial scales. In contrast to random dispersal as often assumed in metacommunity models, as the fitness-dependency increases, dispersal preserves spatial variation in species composition ($β$ diversity) and maintains regional $γ$ diversity in heterogeneous landscapes. Moreover, the relationships of biodiversity with habitat heterogeneity and connectivity change qualitatively as the fitness-dependency of dispersal increases. Our results provide new insights for understanding the context dependence of dispersal effects and have useful implications for biological conservation.

### 4.1 Dispersal mode matters for predicting the effects of dispersal

Based on competitive metacommunities with random dispersal, early theoretical and experimental studies demonstrated that local species diversity ($α$ diversity) first increases but then decreases with dispersal rate (dispersal rate is proportional to habitat connectivity when dispersal is random), whereas across-patch variation in species composition ($β$ diversity) and the overall regional species diversity ($γ$ diversity) generally decrease with dispersal rate (Cadotte, 2006; Mouquet & Loreau, 2003). By taking into account more realistic, fitness-dependent dispersal driven by trophic interactions, our analyses offered two new insights. First, we showed that random dispersal has similar effects on $α$, $β$, and $γ$ diversity in food webs as in competitive communities, thus extending early findings to multitrophic systems. Second, we found that fitness-dependent dispersal could substantially alleviate the negative effects of high dispersal on $β$ and $γ$ diversity and alter the relationship between $α$ diversity and habitat connectivity (Figure 4), suggesting that dispersal effects are context dependent of the mode of dispersal.

To intuitively understand the contrasting effects of random versus fitness-dependent dispersal, we exhibited the equilibrium species composition of a simulated metacommunity under different dispersal modes (Figure 5). As the fitness-dependency of dispersal increases, the equilibrium species composition across three patches changed from a homogenized distribution, under random dispersal, to a highly heterogeneous distribution, under strong fitness-dependent dispersal (Figure 5). Remarkably, spatial patterns of species populations under strong fitness-dependent dispersal match those in isolated systems, which explains the consistent biodiversity patterns under these two scenarios (Figures 2 and 3).

![Figure 5](https://besjournals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/cms/asset/a890720e-22d6-4778-aaf8-63f9e52162dc/jane13933-fig-0005-m.jpg)

**Figure 5.** Equilibrium species composition (a) and biodiversity (b) of a representative simulated metacommunity (*H* = 0.6, $m_{0} = 1$, repetition 2) under different dispersal scenarios: random dispersal (*λ* = 0; leftmost), fitness-dependent dispersal (*λ* > 0; middle), and isolated (rightmost). (a) Each panel represents the population density of 50 species (rows) on 3 patches (columns) on log scale (as shown in colour bar), a black colour indicates local absence of the corresponding species. (b) $α$, $β$, and $γ$ diversity for the above example metacommunity under different dispersal scenarios.

Here, we provide a heuristic explanation for understanding the different species diversity patterns under the different scenarios of dispersal. In isolated systems, the equilibrium species compositions in three local patches arise from species sorting processes (Leibold et al., 2004), which ‘selects’ species that are most suitable to local environmental conditions (characterized by plant growth parameters in our model) and drives local patches to reach their ‘optimal’ species composition. Now consider that the three patches are connected via random dispersal. By relocating species from ‘good’ habitats (i.e. with high population densities) to ‘bad’ ones (i.e. with low population densities), random dispersal eliminates the heterogeneity between patches (which intensifies competition) and enlarges the negative effect of ‘bad’ patches, which eventually reduces the regional-scale fitness of species (Mouquet & Loreau, 2003). Instead, if the three patches are connected through fitness-dependent dispersal, species are able to actively escape unsuitable patches and stay in suitable patches, which retained the optimal species composition within local patches. Therefore, the equilibrium species composition under strong fitness-dependent dispersal approaches that of isolated systems.

Taken together, the scenarios of random dispersal and isolation (i.e. no dispersal) may be regarded as two extremes along the continuum of fitness-dependent dispersal, in terms of their effects on biodiversity. That is, dispersal with weak fitness-dependency approaches the random dispersal scenario and dispersal with strong fitness-dependency approaches isolated systems.

### 4.2 Interactions between dispersal mode and habitat heterogeneity on biodiversity

Our results revealed significant interactions between dispersal mode and habitat heterogeneity. On the one hand, the effects of dispersal on biodiversity manifest themselves mainly in heterogeneous environments, and its strength increases as the level of habitat heterogeneity increases. On the other hand, the mode of dispersal determines how habitat heterogeneity translates into spatial variation in species composition, and hence the relationships between diversity and habitat heterogeneity across scales (Figure 3).

A noteworthy finding from our was that $γ$ diversity increases with habitat heterogeneity in both isolated systems and metacommunities with fitness-dependent dispersal, but decreased with habitat heterogeneity in metacommunities with random dispersal ($λ = 0$, Figure 3c). Such contrasting patterns can be understood from the double-sided effects of habitat heterogeneity on biodiversity. On the detrimental side, a higher habitat heterogeneity implies a higher probability of including habitats with harsh conditions, which causes a lower local α diversity (especially in isolated systems; Figure 3a). On the beneficial side, habitat heterogeneity introduces more diverse environmental conditions and thus lead to a higher $β$ diversity (Ben-Hur & Kadmon, 2020). In the absence of dispersal, the positive effect of habitat heterogeneity on $β$ diversity is stronger than its negative effect on α diversity, which results in a higher γ diversity. The presence of dispersal can modulate the balance between the beneficial and detrimental effects of spatial heterogeneity on biodiversity, but the outcome depends on the mode of dispersal. Specifically, whereas fitness-dependent dispersal can preserve $β$ diversity induced by habitat heterogeneity and hence γ diversity, random dispersal homogenizes the metacommunity and substantially weakens $β$ and γ diversity (Figure 3).

### 4.3 Conservation implications

Our study clarifies that the effect of dispersal is context dependent of dispersal mode, habitat heterogeneity, and the spatial scale of interest when measuring biodiversity. In this section, we focus on species diversity at larger scales (e.g. γ diversity in our model), because the main goal of biological conservation is to prevent the extinction of species at regional scales.

One important implication of our results is that habitat connectivity or fragmentation may have different consequences for taxa with different dispersal modes. For plants with passive dispersal, habitat connectivity may have largely negative effects on regional biodiversity (Figure 4; Mouquet & Loreau, 2003). But for animals with active, fitness-dependent dispersal can alleviate the homogenization-induced decline of regional biodiversity (Figure 4). These predictions are in line with a recent meta-analysis by Fahrig (2017). By synthesizing existing empirical evidence about the effects of fragmentation on species diversity, this meta-analysis showed that plant species diversity generally decreased with habitat connectivity (35 out of 36 studies). In comparison, animal diversity also decreased with habitat connectivity in most cases, but to a much lower extent and with many exceptions. For instance, 68 out of 187 studies on bird species (36%) reported increasing diversity with habitat connectivity.

Habitat heterogeneity has been regarded as a key factor for the maintenance of biodiversity at the regional scale (Ben-Hur & Kadmon, 2020; Rosenzweig, 1995). However, our results suggest that habitat heterogeneity may also have different consequences for biodiversity for taxa with different dispersal modes. For animal species with active, fitness-dependent dispersal, habitat heterogeneity provides a diverse source of niches and promotes species diversity. But for plant species with passive random dispersal, the benefit of habitat heterogeneity may be largely erased due to the homogenizing effect of dispersal, which could lead to a net flow of biomass towards poor habitats, reduce the regional-scale growth rates and decrease species diversity. Thus, to preserve biodiversity for taxa with random dispersal (e.g. plankton), a more homogenized, rather than diversified, landscape may be more appreciated.

### 4.4 Limitations and future extension

To address the effects of dispersal mode, our model characterized fitness-dependent decisions in emigration processes while ignoring other aspects of complexity. First, our metacommunity model contained three patches and assumed global dispersal. Preliminary analyses showed that our results hold for larger numbers of fully connected patches (i.e. with global dispersal; Figures S13 and S14), although the effect of heterogeneity can be alleviated as the number of patches with intermediate quality increases. Future research is needed to clarify whether our results remain robust to spatially explicit dynamics, for instance, embedded in particular patch networks and/or along environmental gradients. Second, our model generally assumed the same dispersal mode for all species, though supplemented with two scenarios where plants were allowed to adopt different dispersal scenarios (no dispersal or always random dispersal) from animal species. In natural metacommunities, different species can exhibit diverse patterns of dispersal, for example larger-sized species or higher trophic levels may have larger dispersal rates (Hirt et al., 2018, p. 20), and different species may perceive the physical landscape differently and show various dispersal networks (Zhang et al., 2021). Third, our model assumed constant dispersal modes over time. In the face of environmental changes, dispersal modes of species may evolve as responses to environmental heterogeneities across space and time (Hastings, 1983; Metz & Gyllenberg, 2001; Poethke & Hovestadt, 2002). Resolving how species with different dispersal strategies interact ecologically and evolutionarily will be an important avenue for future studies.

## 5 CONCLUSIONS

Two decades of research on metacommunities have reached a mature theory, which provides a unified framework to understand the interplay between local species interactions and dispersal on biodiversity across scales (Leibold & Chase, 2018). Previous models had generally considered local and spatial dynamics as independent input variables, for example dispersal is assumed to be independent of local interactions and community composition. But recent studies have clarified dispersal as a complex process that depends on local community properties including species traits, population density, the presence of predators or competitors and so forth. To make the metacommunity theory a practical toolbox for conservation, more realistic scenarios of dispersal need to be adopted. Our study provides one important step towards this goal, by modelling dispersal as an active process determined by local species fitness. Our results extend early predictions from competitive metacommunity models to a multitrophic context and clarify the context dependence of dispersal effects. Such insights are particularly relevant for conservation practices, where different strategies should be designed for the protection of taxa with different dispersal modes.
