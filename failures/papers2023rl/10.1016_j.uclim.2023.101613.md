# Analysis of the driving factors of precipitation change during the development of the Jing-Jin-Ji urban agglomeration

## Abstract

Urbanization has an important impact on local precipitation changes. Previous studies of urban precipitation have largely considered single precipitation events, with few long-term statistical studies based on multiple samples. Therefore, based on WRF model simulations, this study extracted the latent heat flux, sensible heat flux, net radiation, friction velocity, albedo, and precipitation of 156 urban units in Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei (Jing-Jin-Ji) urban agglomeration for the summers of 2001–2019, and identified the main factors driving long-term urban precipitation changes based on multiple samples. There were three key results. 1) The factors that had the greatest impact on urban precipitation changes in Jing-Jin-Ji were the sensible heat flux, latent heat flux, and friction velocity. The latent heat flux was the leading factor driving precipitation changes during the urbanization of Jing-Jin-Ji. 2) In the different stages of urbanization, there were different factors affecting urban precipitation changes. In highly urbanized cities, the sensible heat flux had the greatest impact on precipitation changes, followed by the friction velocity. For medium-sized cities, the friction velocity was the most important variable. For small cities, the latent heat flux was the most important factor. 3) The friction velocity was the leading factor in precipitation change at the 0.95 quantile in Jing-Jin-Ji, and was therefore the most important factor driving changes in urban extreme precipitation. The results provide a theoretical basis for the formulation of climate policies, and disaster prevention and mitigation in Jing-Jin-Ji. They will also assist in promoting the construction of an ecological civilization and building safe and livable cities.

## Introduction

Urbanization has become a significant social development trend worldwide (Chen et al., 2018; Qiu et al., 2020; Steensen et al., 2022; UN-Habitat, 2020a, 2020b; Wei et al., 2022a; Wei et al., 2022b). According to the United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs (UN-DESA), the proportion of the world's population living in urban areas is expected to reach 68% by 2050 (UN-DESA, 2019). Additionally, the urbanization process is accompanied by the rapid expansion of built-up land, and the expansion rate of urban built-up land far exceeds the population growth rate (Cai et al., 2020; Mahtta et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022a). In some cities in developing countries, the expansion rate of built-up land has reached >2.5 times the population growth rate (Cai et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022a). Due to the changes to the underlying urban surface during the rapid urbanization process, the urban surface albedo, surface roughness, and earth-air flux exchange will also vary, which will alter the atmospheric water and heat balance, and thus have an important impact on local precipitation and water resources management (Chen et al., 2018; Freitag et al., 2018; Golroudbary et al., 2018; Jiang et al., 2020; Seino et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2019). Because the majority of the world's population currently lives in urban areas, and cities are very complex systems, there have been changes in urban water status (Chapagain et al., 2022). For example, changes in precipitation intensity and frequency, especially floods, landslides, and debris flows caused by extreme precipitation events, may not only lead to casualties and huge direct economic losses, but will also affect the whole region through the industrial chain and social networks (Chen et al., 2022; Salman and Li, 2018; Su et al., 2021), resulting in increasing risk exposure for the urban population and economy (Asbridge et al., 2021; Chow et al., 2016; Qi et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2018). For example, over the last two decades, many cities such as Beijing, Jinan, and Chongqing have experienced heavy rainfall events, which resulted in several deaths (Zhai et al., 2019). Therefore, assessing the impact of urbanization on precipitation, and identifying the physical mechanisms behind the effects of urbanization on precipitation have become important aspects of urban climate research (Seino et al., 2018).

Recently, strong progress has been made in urban precipitation research in China and overseas. Many existing studies have shown that urbanization can change the amount, intensity, and frequency of precipitation, increase the incidence of extreme precipitation (Ren and Zhou, 2014; Wang et al., 2018a; Yang et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2019), and increase the probability of floods (Jiang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2018), which have made densely populated urban areas more vulnerable to extreme precipitation events (Debbage and Shepherd, 2019; Ely and Fortin, 2020). For example, Zhang et al. showed that urbanization can increase urban precipitation, especially precipitation caused by typhoons or hurricanes, making the risk of urban flooding several times higher than that of suburban areas (Zhang et al., 2018). In the context of climate warming, the rate of urban flooding will increase substantially (Doan et al., 2022). Previous research on the effects of urbanization on precipitation considered the impact of urbanization on precipitation and the impact of the spatial pattern of precipitation on the risk assessment of extreme precipitation disasters (Doan et al., 2022; Shahfahad et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2019). This focus has gradually shifted towards considerations of human well-being and future urban planning. Some studies have revealed the cascading effects of precipitation change on urban drainage and waterlogging prevention (Yao et al., 2022), municipal transportation (Yu and Shi, 2021), the natural environment (Shao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2018b), and even human health (Cao et al., 2018; Cheng et al., 2014; Wu et al., 2022a, 2022b), and a series of countermeasures for urban adaptation and regulation (Ding et al., 2022; Mei et al., 2020) have also been proposed. In terms of research methods, the development from single meteorological station observations to mesoscale network observations has been realized (Fahad et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022a, 2022b), and the resulting data has been combined with satellite monitoring using remote sensing (Lammers et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021). This has resulted in a transformation from point to area data availability.

It has proven to be difficult to identify the mechanisms by which urbanization impacts on precipitation using meteorological observation data and satellite remote sensing data (Zhao et al., 2019), and the future prediction and simulation of the impacts of urbanization on precipitation is also challenging. Numerical models can simulate a single rainfall event or the long-term climate characteristics of a region through sensitivity experiments (e.g., changing the underlying surface conditions in the input data, including land used type, albedo, roughness, soil thermal properties, evaporation rate, and other land surface sensitivity parameters) (Jandaghian et al., 2018; Lopez-Bravo et al., 2018; Morini et al., 2018) to obtain the change of the simulated meteorological element field element fields (e.g., relative humidity, precipitation, temperature, wind speed, air pressure, etc.). On this basis, changes in the latent heat flux, sensible heat flux, net radiation, and albedo were analyzed to determine the physical mechanisms of precipitation change (Jandaghian et al., 2018; Salamanca et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2018) and the role that urbanization plays in the process.

In recent years, the non-hydraulic model (NHM) (Seino et al., 2018), mesoscale model version5 (MM5) (Dandou et al., 2005; Trusilova et al., 2008), weather research and forecasting (WRF) model (Annor et al., 2018; Glisan and Gutowski, 2014; Huang et al., 2019; Mohapatra et al., 2017), and other numerical models have been widely used to study the impact of urbanization on precipitation. Because of its strong portability, the mesoscale WRF model can couple the urban parameterization scheme of the urban energy balance (TEB) model (Meyer et al., 2020), multi-layer/single-layer urban canopy model (MUCM/SUCM) (Paul et al., 2018; Rafael et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2018a), community multiscale air quality (CMAQ) model (de la Paz et al., 2016; Pleim et al., 2014) and other models. It can also take into account for the internal urban structure of the city, enabling detailed sensitivity experiments to be conducted, and making it possible to study more complicated actual precipitation events (Zhang et al., 2018). Therefore, studies of the impact of urbanization on precipitation based on the WRF model have gradually become the mainstream approach in recent years. Simulations based on the WRF model have also become an important way to study the mechanism behind precipitation. For example, Zhang et al. used the WRF model to compare urban and non-urban underlying surfaces, and found that urbanization caused changes in the location of the maximum cumulative precipitation in Houston. The study also revealed the impact of Earth's surface resistance and urban ground warming on precipitation enhancement in eastern Houston (Zhang et al., 2018). Li et al. revealed the mechanisms by which homogeneous and heterogeneous underlying surfaces impact on precipitation (Li et al., 2016). Huang et al. used the WRF model nested very-large eddy simulation (V-LES) to determine that urban forcing affects the time and location of the start of convection and helps to increase precipitation (Huang et al., 2019). Other studies have found similar results (Kusaka et al., 2014; Kusaka et al., 2019). Su et al. found a decrease in the total urban precipitation amount by establishing a relationship between the progress of urbanization (from no urban coverage to semi-urban coverage to full urban coverage) and precipitation in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei (Jing-Jin-Ji) urban agglomeration (Su et al., 2019). Many other studies have also found that urbanization can significantly change the onset time and intensity of precipitation (Han et al., 2012; Han et al., 2014; Sarangi et al., 2018; Zhong et al., 2017). Paul et al. coupled WRF with each of SUCM, MUCM, and WRF without an urban canopy model (NoUCM), and found that urbanization enhanced the spatial heterogeneity of extreme precipitation in Mumbai (Paul et al., 2018).

Currently the important advances have been made in the study of urbanization on precipitation, it has been found that changes in the surface albedo, friction velocity, latent heat flux, sensible heat flux, and net radiation are the main causes of changes in urban precipitation (Paul et al., 2018). However, most of the existing studies are case studies of a single city or urbanization simulation studies on a certain heavy precipitation event (Wang et al., 2018a; Yang et al., 2019). There is a lack of medium and large scale statistical case studies with different development dimensions, i.e., large, medium, and small scale development. There is also a lack of long-term representative statistical data. Additionally, the existing research has also failed to identify the leading factors affecting urban precipitation change at different stages of urbanization. To objectively and comprehensively evaluate the impact of urbanization on precipitation, a sufficient number of samples with spatial heterogeneity is needed.

Accordingly, this study, based on the WRF model (version 4.0), took 156 urban units in Jing-Jin-Ji as the research area, and the meteorological conditions in the summer of 2001 were taken as the input control conditions. Then, inputting the land use in 2001, 2005, 2010, 2015, and 2019, the impact of land use changes on precipitation changes was simulated and the effects of surface albedo, friction velocity, latent heat flux, sensible heat flux, net radiation, and other factors could be extracted in the different periods. On this basis, mathematical statistical methods, such as a hierarchical regression (HR) analysis and quantile regression were used to identify the factors influencing urban precipitation change. Through the combination of WRF simulation and traditional statistical methods, the impact of urbanization on precipitation change was analyzed from a large number of sample data of long-term series and different scales (large, medium, and small), so as to exclude the possible accidental conclusion of a single case, and thus get more reliable conclusions, which is also the innovation of this study.

The ultimate purpose of this study is to try to answer the following questions and so provide a scientific basis for urban disaster prevention and mitigation, and the implementation of strategies for climate change adaptation:1) What are the driving factors of precipitation change following urbanization? 2) What are the factors affecting precipitation change in the different stages of urban development? 3) What is the leading factor affecting urban extreme precipitation?

## Data sources and methods

### Study area

The Jing-Jin-Ji urban agglomeration (Fig. 1) is one of the largest city clusters in the world. This urban agglomeration has a dense population, with advanced regional economies, and close developmental ties between cities, which is typical of coordinated regional development. The coordinated development of Jing-Jin-Ji is one of China's most significant national strategies. The recently established plan aims to build a world-class urban agglomeration with the capital (Beijing) at the core. The type of climate is categorized as typical temperate monsoon climate which featured with four distinct seasons, cold and dry in winter, high temperature and rainy in summer. The precipitation in Jing-Jin-Ji shows a decreasing trend from southeast to northwest, with an average annual precipitation of 500-700 mm, of which summer precipitation can account for 75%–85%. Jing-Jin-Ji was selected as the research area, with a focus on disaster prevention and mitigation, as well as improving human well-being in the region. This urban agglomeration is also one of the regions of China that has experienced the most rapid urban development. The built-up land of Jing-Jin-Ji in 2018 was 2.5 fold greater than that of 2000. Taking this region as the research area would enable the impact of land use change on precipitation change to be determined during the process of rapid urbanization. Additionally, the region contains many cities of different grades and sizes. Through a statistical analysis of the long-term precipitation changes in many regional cities of different sizes, the spurious impact of irrelevant factors could be eliminated to a certain extent, enabling statistically significant research results to be obtained. Because the floods of Jing-Jin-Ji region mainly occurring from June to August. Therefore, in this study, the WRF simulation of the impact of urbanization on precipitation was conducted for summer (from June to August), enabling the research results to provide a scientific basis for urban flood control and disaster reduction in the city.

### Data sources

The remote sensing monitoring data of land use/cover status in this study were collected from the Resource and Environmental Science and Data Center of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (https://www.resdc.cn/DOI/DOI.aspx?DOIID=54) (Xu et al., 2018), with a spatial resolution of 30 m. The digital elevation model (DEM) data originated from the geospatial data cloud platform (https://www.gscloud.cn/sources/accessdata/305?pid=302) and the spatial resolution was 90 m. The meteorological station data was obtained from the historical monitoring data of the National Meteorological Center of China (http://data.cma.cn/data/detail/dataCode/A.0012.0001.html). Because ANUSPLIN, a spatial interpolation software for climate data, can smoothly process splines of more than two dimensions and simultaneously perform spatial interpolation on multiple surfaces simultaneously, it can be used to introduce covariant quantum models, such as the consideration of changes in air temperature and water vapor pressure with altitude. The results reflect the changes in the vertical lapse rate of air temperature and water vapor pressure, and the relationship between precipitation and the coastline (Liu et al., 2008). This makes it suitable for the spatial interpolation of meteorological data in time series, and it can generate very high-resolution climate element fields (Wei et al., 2022a). On this basis, the thin plate spline method was used in this study, the DEM was used as a covariable, and the ANUSPLIN software (Version 4.4) (https://fennerschool.anu.edu.au/research/products/anusplin) was used to interpolate data from temperature and precipitation stations to obtain spatial grid data. To ensure the accuracy of interpolation for data at the edge of the study area, data for the region surrounding the research area was used as a supplement (Fig. S1). The site data were evenly distributed around the research area as far as possible. The site data in the selected area were interpolated, and then the Jing-Jin-Ji boundary was used as the mask clip-interpolation data to obtain the meteorological raster data of the study area. Then, according to the boundaries of the urban units in each phase, the corresponding precipitation, temperature, and other meteorological elements within each urban unit in different periods were extracted using the ArcGIS zoning statistical tool. The simulated initial field and boundary conditions data of the WRF model simulation were derived from the global reanalysis data (final global analysis: FNL) provided by the National Centers for Environmental Prediction (NCEP), http://rda.ucar.edu/datasets). The horizontal spatial resolution of the FNL data was 1° × 1°, and the temporal resolution was 6 h. In the comparative data in the results section, this study selected high-precision data developed by Yang Kun's research group (YKRG) at Tsinghua University (https://data.tpdc.ac.cn/en/data/8028b944-daaa-4511-8769-965612652c49) (The dataset has a spatial resolution of 0.1° and a temporal resolution of 3 h, which has higher precision than the widely used GLDAS dataset in the world) (He et al., 2020).

### Methodology

#### The WRF model

##### The WRF parametric settings

In this study, WRF (version 4.0) coupled with SUCM was used to accurately simulate the land-air interaction process during urbanization in Jing-Jin-Ji to determine the impact of urbanization on precipitation. The model adopted a two-layer and two-way interactive setting, i.e., the inner grid provided the initial field and boundary field information and fed it back to the outer grid, and the outer grid also provided and fed back operational information to the inner grid at the same time. The simulation center was 40°N and 116.5°E. The horizontal resolutions of the double-layer nested grid were 10 and 3.33 km, respectively. The outermost region (d01) had a grid number of 130 × 121 and a coarse grid resolution. The second layer region (d02) was centered on Jing-Jin-Ji and included most parts of North China, with a grid number of 271 × 241 and a grid resolution of 3.33 km. The output time intervals of the simulation results were 12 and 6 h, respectively. See Fig. 2 for the schematic diagram of the simulation range of the double-layer nesting. The model simulation initial field and boundary condition data were derived from the global FNL data provided by the NCEP, http://rda.ucar.edu/datasets/), the level horizontal spatial resolution was 1° × 1°, and the temporal resolution was 6 h. Thirty-seven layers were set in the vertical direction, which adopted the ‘upper sparse and lower dense’ layering method. The pressure of the top layer was 50 hPa, and the time integral step was 30 s.

Regional settings of the WRF model.

Table S1 shows the data set of underlying surface inputs under the model. Land cover was obtained from the MCD12Q1 land type data product from the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectra Radiometer (MODIS), with a spatial resolution of 500 m. Both the leaf area index (LAI) and fraction of photosynthetically active radiation (FPAR) were derived from the MCD15A2H product with a spatial resolution of 500 m. The albedo was derived from the MCD43C3 product with a spatial resolution of 0.05°.

The settings of the model parameterization scheme are shown in Table 1. The surface parameterization scheme (Sf_surface_physic) adopted the Noah-Multi parameterization Land Surface Model (Noah-MP LSM), which was developed from the Noah-LSM. This model not only provides a variety of optional parameterization schemes (such as stomatal impedance, dynamic vegetation, surface drag coefficient, permafrost permeability, etc.), but also improves the two-stream transport scheme considering canopy gaps. It also introduces vegetation canopy and short-term dynamic vegetation types, enabling the temperature of the vegetation canopy and land surface to be calculated separately. Both the short-wave radiation parameterization scheme (Ra_sw_physics) and the long-wave radiation parameterization scheme (Ra_lw_physics) use the rapid radiative transfer model for general circulation models (GCMs) (RRTM-G) scheme. In this scheme, clouds can be divided into ice and water clouds, and the effective radius function of particles can be parameterized. Additionally, the scheme can provide two-way feedback information according to the actual conditions of the outer initial field and boundary field and the operating results of the inner model, and therefore the accuracy of the model prediction results can be improved. Planetary boundary layer (PBL) parameterization was achieved by adopting the Yonsei University (YSU) scheme. This scheme not only has the advantages of other boundary layer schemes, but also provides options for the correction of topographic effects, which enabled effective simulations in the plateau and mountainous areas with large terrain fluctuations in the north and west of Beijing, Tianjin and Hebei. Cumulus convection parameterization scheme was achieved by adopting the improved Kain-Fritsch (KF) scheme. Unlike the old KF, this scheme includes a trigger modulated by water advection (activated by a trigger function), allowing for the interaction of the sub-grid cloud portion with radiation, and also sets a minimum entrainment rate, which improved the simulation accuracy to a certain extent.

##### Model accuracy verification

To test the simulation ability of the WRF model on precipitation processes, this study compared the simulation results of the WRF model with the actual observation results.

Because meteorological field data for 2001 was used as the driving data to simulate the impact of urban expansion on precipitation during urbanization, the simulation results of 2001 could be compared with the observation results of the same year. The most important indicators used to measure climate change are temperature and precipitation. Hence, only the simulated temperature and precipitation results were combined with the YKRG (He et al., 2020). The daily average temperature and precipitation of the data set observed by meteorological stations (after the ANUSPLIN interpolation) were compared, and the accuracy of the WRF model simulation was determined by the trend change and spatial distribution characteristics.

#### Variable selection and calculation

In the process of urbanization, the increase of urban roughness (which can affect the friction velocity) will affect the increase of the height of the boundary layer at the bottom of the atmosphere (Paul et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018); changes in surface albedo, latent heat flux, sensible heat flux, and net radiation can lead to atmospheric instability changes and therefore has an effect on precipitation (Paul et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018a; Yang et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2018). Accordingly, on the basis of WRF simulation results, this study selected five variables, friction velocity, surface albedo, latent heat flux, sensible heat flux, and net radiation, to analyze the dominant factors in the urbanization process of Jing-Jin-Ji urban agglomeration. The calculation process of different variables is as follows:

WRF simulated friction velocity ($u_{∗}$) is formulated as:

(1)

$$
u_{∗} = \frac{kU}{ln(\frac{z}{z0}) - ψ_{m}(\frac{z}{L}, \frac{z0}{L})}
$$

where *k* is the von Kármán constant, *L* is the Monin-Obukhov length scale, *z*<sub>0</sub> represents the roughness height, *z* denotes the height above the ground, ψ
*m*<sub>is</sub> the function for stability correction (Jimenez et al., 2012) and *U* is the wind speed in the lower layer.

The sensible heat flux (H) and latent heat flux (LH) are calculated in the LSM as:

(2)

$$
H = - ρc_{p}C_{h}U(θ_{a} - θ_{g})
$$

(3)

$$
LH = λE
$$

where ρ is the air density, *c*<sub>p</sub> is the specific heat of air at constant pressure, and *U* is the wind speed. *θ* is the potential temperature and *q* represents the humidity. The subscripts *a* and *g* stand for the first model layer and ground surface, respectively. *C*<sub>h</sub> represents the transfer coefficient of heat. The exchange coefficient directly impacts the coupling strength of the land and atmosphere interactions in WRF (Chen and Zhang, 2009); *λ* is the latent heat of evaporation of water. *E* is evapotranspiration, which relies on parametrizations of soil moisture and stomatal resistance (Xiu and Pleim, 2001).

The calculation formulas of surface net radiation and albedo are as follows:

(4)

$$
R_{net} = SW_{net} + LW_{net} = SW↓ - SW↑ + LW↓ - LW↑
$$

(5)

$$
α = SW↑ / SW↓
$$

Where $R_{net}$is the net radiation, $SW_{net}$is the net solar radiation absorbed by the surface, $LW_{net}$ is the surface effective radiation, $W↓$, $SW↑$, $LW↓andLW↑$are the total solar radiation, reflected short-wave radiation, atmospheric and surface long-wave radiation, respectively, and $α$ is the surface albedo. The unit of radiant flux is W/m<sup>−2</sup>.

Based on the WRF simulation results, this study calculated the total precipitation in summer by calculating the cumulative precipitation in different periods. On this basis, this paper took precipitation as the dependent variable and the five factors mentioned above as independent variables to analyze the leading factors affecting precipitation changes in the process of urbanization.

#### Extraction of the urban unit boundary

The remote sensing monitoring data of land use/cover status in five phases in 2001, 2005, 2010, 2015, and 2019 (Here replace 2019 with 2020) were obtained from the Resource and Environmental Science Data Center of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (https://www.resdc.cn/DOI/DOI.aspx?DOIID=54http://www.resdc.cn/) (Xu et al., 2018) with a spatial resolution of 30 m. The urban area at the city scale was calculated according to the area occupied by urban patches in each phase of land use. At the same time, with the built-up area in 2019 as the target, the urban boundary and non-urban land patches were merged and screened according to the degree of patch continuity and the influence of the ocean or other provinces and cities. The continuous patches were taken as a research unit, and those affected by the sea water and inaccurate classification were deleted. Through screening and merging, 156 urban units were obtained (Fig. 1b). These urban units had the following characteristics: they were not close to the ocean and are less directly affected by the ocean, not affected by adjacent or adjacent patches, were independent of each other, and were time continuous (Wei et al., 2022a).

The calculation process of continuity is as follows:

(6)

$$
CONTIG = \frac{[\frac{\overset{n}{\underset{p = 1}{∑}}c_{ijp}}{a_{ij}^{∗}}] - 1}{s - 1}
$$

$c_{ijr}$= contiguity value of pixel p in zone *ij*, $a_{ij}^{∗}$= area of zone *ij* is represented by the number of grids, *s* = sum of the values in a 3-by-3 grid template. CONTIG assesses the spatial connectedness or contiguity of cells within a grid cell patch (equals 0 for a one-pixel patch and increases to a limit of 1 as patch contiguity or connectedness increases).

#### Division of the urban development stages

In previous studies, the degree of urban development has been classified in terms of the population density, the proportion of the total population residing in urban areas, the size of the urban area, or the nighttime light intensity index (Liang et al., 2020b). The impact of changes in different elements within the urban boundaries on precipitation were investigated in this study. It was necessary to consider the overall situation based on different indicators within the urban boundaries at different time periods. When the urban night light intensity reaches a certain value, it will not change further, and some cities have large built-up areas, but a low population density; therefore, this study distinguished the development level of different cities in Jing-Jin-Ji by combining the three indicators of night light intensity, urban population, and built-up area. Weighted values were primarily assigned to the three selected indicators of night light intensity, urban population, and built-up area, respectively, and the urban comprehensive development index was then constructed. The Arcgis10.2 natural breakpoint method was used to divide the urban development index into high to low levels of 1, 2, and 3, which corresponded to the three development stages of urban development, i.e., high, medium, and low level cities, respectively.

The weight value was calculated by the independent weight method, which is an objective weighting method based on the multiple regression method. The weight is determined by calculating the complex correlation coefficient *R*. The larger the *R* value is, the more the information is repeated and the smaller the weight is. The larger the value of the complex correlation coefficient 1/*R*, the greater the weight should be. The final weight is obtained by the normalization of the 1/*R* value. Unlike the information weight, entropy method, and other assignment methods, the independent weight considers the overall impact of different indicators, and was therefore considered to be more suitable for application in this study. To eliminate the impact of different development dimensions, the population, night light intensity, and urban area were first normalized and then the weight was calculated. The specific value was multiplied by the weight value and then summed to calculate the comprehensive development index. The year 2001 was taken as the base period for comparison. The classifications of the comprehensive development index were not divided separately into different periods, but were grouped together regardless of the period. The benefits of this classification are that it considers the possible dynamic changes (or possible changes) of some cities at different periods. For example, a target city may have been a small city in 2005 that grew to become a large city in 2019.

#### Panel data regression model

The aim of this study was to identify the leading factors that affect urban precipitation change. To solve the scientific problems of this study, based on the WRF simulation results, the precipitation, latent heat flux, sensible heat flux, friction velocity, albedo, and net radiation of 156 urban units in Jing-Jin-Ji from June to August 2001 to 2019 were calculated and extracted, respectively. Precipitation was taken as the dependent variable and the other factors were considered as independent variables. To eliminate the influence of endogenous variables and determine whether there was really a relationship between different variables and precipitation, a panel data model was established (Liang et al., 2020a). The general form of the panel data model was as follows:

(7)

$$
y_{it} = \overset{K}{\underset{k = 1}{∑}}β_{ki}x_{kit} + u_{it}
$$

where *i* = 1, 2, 3, …, *N*, represents the number of individuals; *t* = 1, 2, 3, …, T, represents the number of known time points; yit is the monitoring value of the dependent variable on individual i at time point t; xkit stands for the non-random explanatory variable of the k-th monitoring value of individual i at time t; βki is a parameter to be estimated; and uit is a random error term. Panel data models can take the form of a fixed effects model (FE), mixed effects model (Pool), and random effects model (RE). The selection of the optimal model is determined by an *F* test, BP test (the *R*<sup>*2*</sup> value of the auxiliary regression is used to test whether heteroscedasticity exists), and Hausman test. The *F* test is used to compare the selection of the FE model or Pool model, with a *p* value <0.05 meaning that the FE model is better, otherwise the Pool model is used. The BP test is used to compare the RE model and Pool model, with a *p* value <0.05 meaning that the RE model is better; otherwise the Pool model is used. The Hausman test is used to compare the selection of the FE model and RE model, with a *p* value <0.05 meaning that the FE model is better, otherwise the RE model is used. Compared with an ordinary least squares (OLS) regression analysis, the advantage of panel data models is that it not only takes into account the commonness of cross-sectional data, but also controls the deviations of OLS estimations that can be caused by unobservable variables, making the model setting more reasonable and the model parameter estimation more accurate. Compared with a time series, panel data expands the sample information, reduces the collinearity between variables, and improves the effectiveness of the estimators. Additionally, a panel data model can better identify and measure the undetectable effects that the cross-section and time series data cannot detect (Liang et al., 2020a), and can reflect the dynamic adjustment of variables related to precipitation; therefore, making the research results more valuable as reference data.

#### Hierarchical regression analysis

A HR analysis was used to determine the extent of the independent effects of each impact factor. The HR method is based on a semi-parametric regression analysis and calculates the relative importance of each factor by performing multiple random transformations on the original data matrix (Wei et al., 2022a). Compared with the traditional regression analysis method, the advantage of the HR method is that it overcomes the common collinear phenomena in environmental variables. Therefore, it is suitable for multidimensional environmental data analysis (Mac Nally, 2000) In this study, according to the HR analysis results, we obtained the independent importance through the changes of the *R*<sup>*2*</sup> value (*△R*<sup>*2*</sup>) for various factors. The greater the *△R*<sup>*2*</sup>, the greater the impact of the variable on precipitation change. Then, we ranked the *△R*<sup>*2*</sup> to determine the leading factors of the urban precipitation affecting the urbanization process.

#### Quantile regression

To reveal the trends of the influencing factors on precipitation changes of different degrees, the responses of precipitation changes to various factors at different loci were explored based on a quantile regression. Compared with the OLS regression, the advantage of a quantile regression is that it has no requirement for the normality of dependent variables, and it is robust to outliers (Chen, 2018). Therefore, it does not need to consider heteroscedasticity. Tests of the output regression coefficient and *R*<sup>*2*</sup> values can be used to analyze the impact trend and robustness analysis of different factors on precipitation change. By observing the regression coefficient values at different quantiles and the regression coefficients at different loci, the influences of different factors on precipitation changes can be comprehensively analyzed.

## Results and analysis

### Verification of model accuracy

Fig. 3 shows a comparison between the simulation results of temperature and precipitation in Jing-Jin-Ji in 2001 by the WRF model and the actual observation results. As it can be seen from Fig. 3, the spatial distribution characteristics of summer mean daily temperature (a) simulated by WRF and (b) from the observed data were basically consistent with each other, with high values distributed in plain areas and low values mainly distributed in northwestern areas. The simulated average maximum and minimum temperatures were slightly higher than the YKRG. The maximum average temperature was 2.1 and 3.6 °C higher, and the minimum average temperature was 0.4 and 3.5 °C higher, respectively. It can be clearly seen from Fig. 3 that the average temperature in Jing-Jin-Ji was higher than that in the surrounding area, but this was expected. Because the urban area has a large impervious area, a large population, and solar radiation is reflected multiple times by the urban canopy structure, the average temperature in the urban area is typically higher than that in the surrounding area (Souma et al., 2013). Additionally, the temperature of b and c were obtained after the interpolation of meteorological data, which resulted in the smoothing of some values, and therefore no obvious changes were apparent in small areas. In general, WRF can simulate the change of temperature well. It can be seen from Fig. 3 (d-f) that although there were some differences between the WRF simulated precipitation and the precipitation of YKRG, it was obvious from the three group figures that the spatial distribution of the high and low value areas of precipitation was basically the same. The high precipitation areas were mainly distributed northeast of Beijing, Tianjin, and Hebei (e.g., north and northeast of Beijing, east of Chengde, north of Tangshan and Qinhuangdao) and in the southeast (e.g., Cangzhou, Hengshui). The WRF simulation results were not as smooth as the observation results (e and f) in terms of their spatial distribution. This may be because the results in e and f were obtained after interpolation, while in d the influence of underlying surface properties was given greater consideration. Compared with temperature, the spatial heterogeneity of precipitation was stronger, and therefore the precipitation results of the WRF simulation were more trend oriented.

### Identification of the leading factors influencing urban precipitation change in Jing-Jin-Ji

To determine the leading factors influencing urban precipitation change from this mechanism, the zonal statistical method in ArcGIS was adopted to extract the mean values of albedo, latent heat, sensible heat, net radiation, friction velocity and other factors for each urban unit in Jing-Jin-Ji during different periods from 2001 to 2019, and the impact of each factor in the different periods was compared with that in 2001. The influences of the changes in various factors on precipitation changes in different cities were also analyzed (see Fig. S3). The stability of the albedo, latent heat, sensible heat, friction velocity, net radiation and other variables (four missing values were removed in each period) were first assessed, and then these factors were used as explanatory variables and precipitation changes were used as explained variables for analysis. Tables 3 and 4 present the test and summary results of the different models. It can be seen from Table 3 that the *F* test showed a significance of 5% *F* (151451) = 4.73, *p* < 0.05, and therefore the FE model was superior to the Pool model. The BP test showed a 5% level of significance *chi* (1) = 197.75, *p* = 0.000 < 0.05, indicating that the RE model was better. The Hausman test showed a 5% level of significance *chi* (5) = 11.20, *p* < 0.05, indicating that the FE model was better than the RE model. Therefore, the FE model results were used for the final analysis. Table 4 shows that from the overall situation of different urban units in Jing-Jin-Ji, the changes of albedo (*t* = −6.72, *p* < 0.01), surface friction velocity (*t* = −4.64, *p* < 0.01) and net radiation (*t* = −3.13, *p* < 0.01) were all significant at the 0.01 level, and their regression coefficient values were all negative, indicating that the three factors had a significant negative impact on precipitation change. The sensible heat flux (*t* = 3.38, *p* < 0.01) and latent heat flux were also significant at the 0.01 level, and their regression coefficients were 3.53 > 0 and 4.40 > 0, respectively, indicating that changes in these factors had a significant positive impact on the precipitation change. This study also analyzed the bi-directional fixed effect of individuals and time. As can be seen from Table 4, the coefficients of the different factors in the bi-directional fixed effect results were basically consistent with the symbols of FE model coefficients, indicating that the influence of individuals or time randomness was weak or had no impact on the model results in this study.

Fig. 8 shows that only the change in friction velocity was significant at the 0.95 quantile, indicating that the leading factor affecting the change of extreme precipitation in the inner urban area of Jing-Jin-Ji was the friction velocity. Previous studies have reported that surface roughness is one of the most important factors affecting the urban surface energy balance (Arnfield, 2003; Garuma, 2018; Zhao et al., 2014). An increase in the urban friction velocity results in the air flow bifurcating, which leads to a decrease in precipitation in urban areas, while precipitation on both sides of the divided air flow increases due to the convergence and uplift of the air. Therefore, the friction velocity is negatively correlated with precipitation changes.

## Discussion

In previous simulation studies of the impact of urbanization on precipitation, many researchers have replaced the underlying urban surface with grassland, forest, farmland, and other land use types (Li et al., 2016; Lishu et al., 2017; Lopez-Bravo et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018), to simulate the changes in falling water with and without an underlying surface, and determine the underlying mechanisms by which urbanization influences precipitation change. However, in real life, urban development tends to be a process of gradual expansion, with no absolute conversion of forest or farmland into cities. Additionally, in previous studies, most researchers simulated a single city or a change in a sensitivity parameter of an urban agglomeration over a short time to reveal the impact of urbanization on precipitation (Li et al., 2016; Seino et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018a; Yang et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2018). In this study, the impact of real underlying surface variation on precipitation change during the process of urbanization was simulated by controlling the initial meteorological field and inputting the land uses in different periods one by one. Although the overall concept is the same, i.e., the application of control or sensitivity experiments, this study used real land use types, and to some extent eliminated the impact of climate change. Additionally, the simulation was also combined with a statistical analysis to analyze the leading factors affecting urban precipitation change in different time periods, development stages, and precipitation change quantiles. The study area included various types of cities, i.e., large, medium, and small cities, with a large number of samples. Additionally, the time series simulated in the study covered a long period, from 2001 to 2019. Accordingly, the results obtained excluded chance effects, and the research results may have practical significance for urban development planning and climate change adaptation strategies.

The study found that although the simulated precipitation was higher than the observed values in some areas in the west of the study region (e.g., at the junction of Baoding and Zhangjiakou), this may be impacted by the complex terrain and the lack of meteorological observation stations in this region. As it can be seen in Fig. S1, due to the limited numbers of meteorological stations in this region and its mountainous terrain, the impact of the spatial heterogeneity of the underlying surface was likely to be ignored in the precipitation obtained through interpolation. However, this study focuses on the analysis of cities. Fig. 1 shows that there were few urban units in this area, and therefore the high precipitation in the simulation of this area, which had little impact on the analysis conducted in this study and could be ignored. Additionally, this high precipitation may be related to the selection of the parameterization scheme in this study. However, the same parameterization scheme, initial field, and boundary field data were adopted in each of the five phases of this study, and therefore the simulation errors that may have been caused by the parameterization scheme can be corrected by comparison with 2001. In conclusion, in this study, WRF simulated well the spatial distribution of both temperature and precipitation, with a relatively high accuracy.

In this study, the spatial resolution of the model output was 3.3 km. It was proven by comparison with the measured data that the WRF model simulated the spatio-temporal variation of precipitation well at this precision, indicating that the resolution was sufficient for the Jing-Jin-Ji urban agglomeration. This was consistent with the resolution of many previous studies at the urban agglomeration scale (Wang et al., 2015). The research showed that the precipitation increased and decreased in different cities, and precipitation in the same city at different stages of development also had spatial differences. When the urban area expands to a certain extent, urban precipitation may decrease. In different regional cities, the leading factors driving the changes in urban precipitation change are different. Therefore, in the process of planning and climate policy formulation, it is necessary to consider the spatial heterogeneity and scale of impact factors, and to then formulate effective urban climate planning policies according to local conditions.

In terms of the analysis of the influencing mechanism and dominant factors, it can be seen from the optimal model (FE model) determined by the panel model (Table 4) that changed in albedo and net radiation were negatively correlated with changes in precipitation, and this statistical result is also reasonable. As we all know, an important feature of the urban land surface is “high-rise buildings”. The geometric structure and physical and biochemical processes of the building canopy are completely different from the previous vegetation canopy: the sky window becomes smaller, the incoming short-wave radiation and the outgoing longwave radiation is trapped in street canyons and undergoes multiple reflection and absorption processes within the canopy ultimately resulting in lower overall reflectivity, less net upward radiation, and therefore more heat storage in the urban canopy. The saturated water vapor pressure in the urban air increases due to the increase of temperature in the urban canopy, which provides better water vapor conditions for heavy precipitation in the urban area. For example, Jiang and Liu (2007) simulated through the GRAPES weather forecast model and found that a 0.03 decrease in the albedo of the underlying surface of the city would increase the intensity of the urban heat island by about 0.8 °C, which would aggravate urban convergence and thus affect precipitation. Therefore, the statistical results of this study are consistent with those of Jiang et al.

Although some studies have shown that urbanization made the sensible heat flux in the urban area much larger than that in the suburbs, while the latent heat flux was just the opposite. The reason may be that the available water vapor source and evapotranspiration in the urban area decrease (Shem and Shepherd, 2009; Ma et al., 2014), leading to a decrease in precipitation. However, the statistical results of this study show that the sensible heat flux and latent heat flux of the urban areas in the Jing-Jin-Ji were positively correlated with the changed in precipitation, and became the dominant factor in the changes in urban precipitation in different stages of development. This may be due to the large increase in the proportion of impermeable surface area caused by urban expansion, which reduces local evaporation and increases sensible heat flux. The difference in the physical properties of the underlying surface between the urban areas and the suburbs results in a difference in surface thermal conditions, which is likely to form a mesoscale low-altitude wind field convergence line or low air pressure in the city, making the stratification above the city unstable and conducive to thermal convection (Lima et al., 2018). When water vapor is sufficient, it is easy to lead to the formation of convective clouds and convective precipitation (Zhong et al., 2017; Zhou and Chen, 2018; Li et al., 2019; Oliveros et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019). In addition, it may also cause the vertical shear of the wind field in the central urban area to strengthen in the boundary layer, and the configuration structure of low-level convergent cyclonic circulation and high-level divergent anticyclonic circulation is conducive to the maintenance and development of convective precipitation (Sarangi et al., 2018), and may directly induce and intensify some summer thunderstorms, and may even produce heavy rainstorms (Zhong et al., 2017). It should be noted here that the positive mechanism of urbanization to promote precipitation, such as urban near-surface warming, local atmospheric convergence, and vertical movement enhancement, plays a dominant role in the early stage of small-scale urbanization. However, when the city scale increases to a certain extent, the covering of a large area of impermeable layer leads to a great suppression of surface evaporation, which significantly reduces the energy of atmospheric instability (Wang et al., 2015).

Table 4 shows that although the change of friction velocity is inversely correlated with the change of precipitation in general, it is the most important factor of the change of precipitation at the 95th percentile point of the city. This seems to contradict each other, however, it is also in line with our understanding to a certain extent. Previous studies have shown that when this dynamic effect is strong, the precipitation southeast of Beijing increases (Jiang and Liu, 2007). Fig. S2 (f-j) also shows that with the expansion of urbanization, the summer precipitation on the southeast side of the Beijing urban area displayed a significant increasing trend, while precipitation in the center of the inner city presented a decreasing trend. However, an increase in urban roughness and surface warming led to an increase in the height of the atmospheric boundary layer, which was conducive to the occurrence of heavy precipitation (Zhang et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2017). Additionally, an increase in the friction velocity can block the movement of the precipitation system; thus, prolonging the precipitation time and increasing the intensity, which may increase the probability of extreme precipitation (Qiu, 2012; Shahid et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2018a; Zhang et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2019). Therefore, it was considered reasonable that the friction velocity was the most important factor driving precipitation change at the 0.95 quantile in Jing-Jin-Ji. Accordingly, in the urbanization process, attention should be given to the height of buildings or the construction of urban ventilation corridors to reduce the volatility of urban precipitation changes and the occurrence of extreme events.

The results of this study provide a theoretical basis for the formulation of urban climate policies, and disaster prevention and reduction in Jing-Jin-Ji, and will play an important role in promoting the construction of an ecological civilization and building a safe and livable city.

## Conclusions

Based on WRF simulation results, this study used mathematical methods, such as a panel data model and quantile regression, to identify the leading factors affecting precipitation change and their spatial distribution characteristics during urbanization from 2001 to 2019, based on large number of urban sample data. It was found that:
(1)
The sensible heat flux, latent heat flux, and friction velocity had an important impact on precipitation change in different cities in Jing-Jin-Ji. The sensible heat flux was identified as the leading factor affecting precipitation change in different cities in Jing-Jin-Ji.
(2)
In the different stages of urbanization, the leading factors affecting urban precipitation change also differed. For cities with a high development level (level 1), the sensible heat flux had the greatest impact on precipitation change, followed by the friction velocity. For medium-sized cities, the friction velocity was the most important variable. For small cities, the latent heat flux was the most important factor affecting precipitation changes.
(3)
From the perspective of different quantiles, the friction velocity was the leading factor driving precipitation change at the 0.95 quantile in Jing-Jin-Ji.

The novelty of this study was to combine model simulation and statistical methods to reveal the impact of urbanization on precipitation from a long-term series and a large number of urban sample data. The results overcome for the deficiencies of previous case studies, and to some extent the study overcame the problem of chance results occurring through the use of a large data set. In particular, it was found that the urban friction velocity was the leading factor (0.95 quantile) driving changes in urban extreme precipitation during urbanization. The main limitation of this study was that it did not identify the scale of urban precipitation change under the interaction of urbanization and climate change. In addition, there may be spillover effects between urban aeras, and this study did not explore the cross-regional impact, which is also the limitation of this study. In the future, precipitation change caused by urbanization and global climate change and the spillover effects between urban aeras will be superimposed over each other for a more detailed study.
