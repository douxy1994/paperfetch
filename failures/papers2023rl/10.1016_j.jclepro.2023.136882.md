# Understanding the spatial relationships and drivers of ecosystem service supply-demand mismatches towards spatially-targeted management of social-ecological system

## Abstract

Managing relationships among ecosystem service supplies and demands and manipulating certain drivers to maintain well-balanced ecosystem service suites are key challenges for ensuring sustainable social-ecological systems. However, comprehensive research on the spatial relationships and determinants of ecosystem service supply-demand mismatches remains rare. Therefore, we assessed trade-offs and synergies among bundled ecosystem service supply-demand balances and their underlying drivers in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region. Four distinct ecosystem service bundles revealed diverse ecosystem service supply-demand mismatches among different locations associated with various trade-offs and synergies. Building weighted networks specifically for different bundles promoted innovative understandings of the emergent properties of ecosystem services and unveiled the topological ecosystem service differences, synergistic interdependences among these services and essential umbrella ecosystem services for improving multiple ecosystem service supply-demand states. Social factors severely increased ecosystem service deficits in some bundles, while biophysical factors more critically improved ecosystem service surpluses in other bundles. Besides, the similar nonlinear responses of both the overall supply-demand states of multiple services and the supply-demand relationships of umbrella ecosystem services to their shared drivers in most bundles further supported the important roles of umbrella services in improving ecosystem service multifunctionality. Our study emphasized the significance of considering the spatial relationships and drivers of ecosystem service supply-demand mismatches when targeting effective spatial policies for realizing the sustainability of social-ecological systems and could provide useful guidance for addressing complex human-nature interdependencies.

## Introduction

Ecosystem services (ESs) are widely adopted as a conceptual framework for tackling human-nature relationships and play fundamental roles in achieving the sustainable development of social-ecological systems (Costanza, 2020; Torralba et al., 2018). Over recent decades, continuous efforts have been devoted to the conceptualization, classification, quantification and valuation of ESs, and the rapid expansion of ES research in the scientific community has promoted the application of ES assessments to support spatially based decision-making in different policy contexts (Cortinovis et al., 2021; Dang et al., 2022; Delibas et al., 2021). However, one of the key challenges in increasing the decision relevance of ES science and formulating effective spatial strategies to secure ESs is the lack of in-depth knowledge regarding the spatial relationships among and drivers of ES supply-demand mismatches.

ESs encompass a wide variety of benefits from nature to people and are often classified into provisioning, regulating and cultural services (Czúcz et al., 2018; Dang et al., 2022). Moreover, as underlined by the multiple conceptual frameworks used to relate nature’s contributions to people and to understand ES components, individual ES types with dual natural and societal properties can arise from the interplay between how services are supplied and how they are demanded in social-ecological systems (Chaplin-Kramer et al., 2019; Gourevitch et al., 2021). ES supplies reflect the capacity of an area to provide services, whereas ES demands represent the extent to which the services are used, needed, or preferred by society in an area (Villamagna et al., 2013; Wei et al., 2017; Wolff et al., 2015). The supplies and demands of different ESs usually interact with each other in complex ways, leading to the emergence of three main types of ES relationships that can be generally identified as bundles, trade-offs and synergies (Cord et al., 2017). ES bundles are coherent sets of ESs that are repeatable in space or time (Raudsepp-Hearne et al., 2010). Within a given ES bundle, trade-offs describe antagonistic situations in which an increase in one ES may cause a decrease in other services, while synergies apply when two or more ESs covary positively (Baró et al., 2017; Mouchet et al., 2014).

The spatial patterns of, relationships among and determinants of ES supplies and demands have been explored in interdisciplinary ES studies by integrating diversified approaches such as map comparisons, integrated indexes, statistical analyses and network assessments (Cortinovis et al., 2021; Guan et al., 2022; Hölting et al., 2019). In general, both the supplies of and demands for various ESs can vary spatially, and the different bundles that occur in either the supply or demands of ESs may exhibit different ES compositions and quantities (Dang et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022; Karimi et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022). From the supply or demand perspective, although trade-offs are prominent among provisioning and non-provisioning ESs and synergies are mostly found among regulating and cultural ESs, the directions and strength of these antagonistic and synergetic ES relationships can be spatially heterogeneous and regionally context-dependent (Aryal et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). Different combinations of driving forces play important roles in altering the supply and demand of ESs and shaping the relationships among ESs (Liu et al., 2022; Reale et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2017; Yan et al., 2022). The supply or demand of an individual ES are often underpinned by multiple common drivers, and one social-ecological factor may have varying effects with opposite or consistent directions on several ESs at once (Liu et al., 2021). The supply and demand of different ESs can respond positively or negatively to various social-ecological drivers depending on the context, and these responses may also have spatially varying magnitudes, be nonlinear and have threshold effects that accelerate the degradation of ESs (Sun et al., 2022; Neyret et al., 2021; Qiu et al., 2019; Runting et al., 2016).

Considering the existing findings, effectively managing social-ecological systems may require designing spatially targeted interventions to address the spatial variabilities of diverse ES supplies and demands based on context-dependent drivers. This need is highlighted by China’s endeavours to meet global sustainability challenges, achieve carbon neutrality and sustain vital ESs through spatial strategies such as ecological restoration, ecological redlines and ecological compensation (Bai et al., 2018; Zheng et al., 2019). In this sense, the ES bundle approach offers a promising way to target these spatial strategies by differentiating areas with homogenous ES types and magnitudes as a result of their shared socioecological profiles and common environmental issues (Raudsepp-Hearne et al., 2010; Saidi and Spray, 2018). Hence, it is crucial to achieve a systematic understanding of the complex relationships among and driving factors of multiple-ES supplies and demands in different bundles for guiding tailored actions and preventing ES imbalances in specific locations. Nevertheless, little attention has been given to comprehensively evaluating both the supply and demand sides of ES bundles, and there is a paucity of studies that investigate how the oversupply or overdemand of one ES can influence the state of another ES in different bundles (Baró et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022; Quintas-Soriano et al., 2019; Schröter et al., 2014). Besides, despite increased interest in revealing the impacts of social-ecological factors on the spatial dynamics of ES supplies or demands, only a few studies have assessed the driving mechanisms of supply-demand mismatches of ES sets. Such limited understanding may seriously inhibit our ability to develop efficient and effective strategies that promote the equilibrium between ES supply and demand.

Here, we advance the ES bundle approach and aim to provide a holistic view of how the supply-demand relationships of ESs are bundled antagonistically and synergistically and of how these relationships are affected by different social-ecological drivers. We focus on the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei urban agglomeration, one of the pioneering and representative regions implementing the abovementioned spatial strategies in China to improve human-nature relationships. We first evaluated the supply-demand relationships of six ESs in 2015 by combing quantification methods and spatially explicit indexes. Second, we delineated different bundles of supply-demand relationships of ESs (BSDRESs). Third, we disentangled the trade-offs and synergies between different BSDRESs. In particular, we built a synergy network for each bundle and calculated network metrics to reveal the topological differences among these networks and identified the network hubs as the supply-demand relationships of umbrella ESs that were essential for improving the supply-demand relationships of multiple services. Finally, we examined the impacts of different sets of social-ecological drivers on the supply-demand relationships of ESs in each bundle and explored the nonlinear responses of the supply-demand relationship of ESs to the major drivers.

## Material and methods

### Study area

The Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei urban agglomeration is located at the heart of the Bohai Rim in northern China between latitudes of 35°03′N and 42°40′N and longitudes of 113°27′E and 119°50′E, covering a total land area of approximately 216 thousand square kilometres. Its administrative regions consist of thirteen cities at the prefecture level and above, including two municipalities, Beijing city and Tianjin city, and another eleven prefecture-level cities in Hebei Province. The climate of the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region belongs to the temperate monsoon climate and exhibits distinct seasonal variations. The region has an annual mean precipitation of 304 mm–750 mm and an annual mean temperature of 3 °C–15 °C. The terrain in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region is tilted from northwest to southeast, with the north-western part of the region mainly encompassing the Yanshan and Taihang Mountains and the south-eastern part of the region being covered by vast plains. The ecosystems in the region are spatially heterogeneous, transitioning from forest and grassland ecosystems that play key roles in conserving the supply of multiple important ESs in the north-western mountains to the agricultural and urban ecosystems that are primarily distributed in the southeast (Fig. A.1). Apart from being the national capital region and cultural centre of China, the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei urban agglomeration is also considered the economic core of northern China. As rapid urbanization throughout the globe has caused multiple environmental challenges and resulted in growing disparities between the supply and demand of ESs (Chung et al., 2021; Keeler et al., 2019; McPhearson et al., 2022; Richards et al., 2022; Tao et al., 2022), the intensive urban expansion and rapid population growth during the accelerated economic development and continuous urbanization processes in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region also inevitably induced substantial ES supply losses and vast increases in the demand for ESs (Wang et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2020). Sustaining the supply of ESs and meeting ES demands are closely related to the livelihood of more than 100 million (8.11% of China’s population in 2015) people in the region.

### Evaluation of the supply-demand relationships of ecosystem services

#### Mapping the supply and demand of ecosystem services

The supply and demand of six ESs including provisioning, regulating and cultural services were quantified and mapped at a 1-km<sup>2</sup> scale in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei urban agglomeration in 2015 by adopting various methods. A brief overview of the selected indicators for the supply and demand of these services is provided in Table 1. The specific types and sources of utilized data and quantification approaches for different ES supplies can be found in Shen et al. (2020). Detailed information on the multisource and heterogeneous data for the ES demands and potential social-ecological drivers as well as full descriptions of the modelling methods utilized to obtain each ES demand are available in the supplementary material. The supply and demand of selected ESs with different units were standardized by using minimum-maximum normalization to enable comparison, before which 90% winsorization was first applied to the ES indicators to prevent erroneous transformations arising from the sensitivity of the normalization technique to outliers (Castro et al., 2014; Stürck and Verburg, 2017).

#### Quantifying the supply-demand ratios of different ecosystem services

The supply-demand ratios of ESs (ESDRs) represent the relationships between the supply of ESs and the human demand for these services and can facilitate visualizing the spatial congruencies and mismatches between the supply and demand of each ES (Jiang et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2021). The supply-demand ratios of different ESs were calculated as follows:

(1)

$$
{ESDR}_{ij} = \frac{{ES}_{ij}^{S} - {ES}_{ij}^{D}}{({ES}_{i}^{Smax} + {ES}_{i}^{Dmax}) / 2}
$$

where ${ESDR}_{ij}$ is the supply-demand ratio of ES type *i* in grid cell *j*, ${ES}_{ij}^{S}$ is the supply of ES type *i* in grid cell *j*, ${ES}_{ij}^{D}$ is the demand of ES type *i* in grid cell *j*, ${ES}_{i}^{Smax}$ is the maximum value of the supply of ES type *i*, and ${ES}_{i}^{Dmax}$ is the maximum value of the demand of ES type *i*.

The comprehensive supply-demand ratio of ESs (CESDR) can be used to demonstrate the overall status of the supply-demand relationships of multiple ESs and is estimated as the arithmetic mean of the supply-demand ratios of six selected ESs (Chen et al., 2019; Pan et al., 2021):

(2)

$$
{CESDR}_{j} = \frac{1}{n}\overset{n}{\underset{i = 1}{∑}}{ESDR}_{ij}
$$

where ${CESDR}_{j}$ is the CESDR in grid cell *j*, *n* is the number of types of ESs, and ${ESDR}_{ij}$ is the supply-demand ratio of ES type *i* in grid cell *j*.

Both the CESDR and the supply-demand ratios of individual ESs are relative indexes that show the locations in which relatively high ES demands correspond spatially with relatively low ES supplies and vice versa (Larondelle and Lauf, 2016; Martínez-López et al., 2019; Nedkov and Burkhard, 2012). In the resulting maps of the CESDR and the supply-demand ratio of each ES, a value of zero means that the relative magnitudes of the supply and demand of the ESs are in a balanced state, while relative differences between ES supplies and demands are indicated by positive (i.e., ES surpluses) or negative (i.e., ES deficits) values.

### Identification of bundles of supply-demand relationships of ecosystem services

As a robust and efficient algorithm that combines dimensionality reduction and cluster analysis capacities, the self-organizing maps method (R package kohonen, Wehrens and Kruisselbrink, 2018) was implemented to identify the BSDRESs by spatially clustering grid cells that shared common features among the ESDRs (Mouchet et al., 2014). To explore the sensitivity of the self-organizing map results, different numbers of clusters (from two to thirty) were first built and then the Davies-Bouldin index was calculated (R package cluster Sim, Davies and Bouldin, 1979) to determine the optimal number of clusters (Dittrich et al., 2017; Levers et al., 2015). The lowest Davies-Bouldin index obtained corresponded to four BSDRESs in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei urban agglomeration in 2015 (Fig. A.5).

### Investigation of trade-offs and synergies among the supply-demand relationships of bundled ecosystem services

The correlation analysis and network analysis were combined to untangle the intricate trade-offs and synergies among supply-demand relationships of bundled ESs. For each bundle type, Spearman correlations were performed on each pair of bundled services’ supply-demand relationships by using the R packages vegan and corrplot packages (Oksanen et al., 2019; Wei and Simko, 2017). The network science approach provides a common language and powerful tools for use in different disciplines and addresses the mathematical, statistical and structural properties of entities and the connections among them in complex multidisciplinary systems (Bohan et al., 2016). Among the limited prior studies that adopted network analysis tools in ES science, the focus has been mainly on multimode networks with a secondary interest in ESs (Felipe-Lucia et al., 2022; Keyes et al., 2021). Building one-mode networks specifically for ESs and using suitable network metrics that are potentially meaningful for ESs could promote innovative understandings of the emergent properties of ESs. Hence, unipartite, undirected and weighted networks were constructed for each BSDRES on the basis of the Spearman correlation matrix. All the significant positive correlations among the bundled services’ supply-demand relationships formed a correlation network representing the corresponding bundle type. To describe the topological characteristics of these synergy networks, a suite of network metrics from diverse fields were selected and calculated by using the igraph and weighted correlation network analysis (WGCNA) packages in R and the NetworkX package in python (Csardi and Nepusz, 2006; Hagberg et al., 2008; Langfelder and Horvath, 2008). In particular, as an endeavour to identify the priority ES supply-demand relationships that were essential for improving the supply-demand relationships of multiple services simultaneously and for avoiding the detrimental consequences of cascading effects of ESs (Keyes et al., 2021; Linders et al., 2020), the concept of “umbrella ESs”, proposed by Boesing et al. (2020) from the perspective of ES supply, was introduced and extended to the supply-demand relationships of ESs in this study. The umbrella ESs’ supply-demand relationships were defined as ES supply-demand relationship types having various or strong synergies with other services’ supply-demand relationships and were identified as the hubs of the synergy networks on the basis of centrality measures. The different synergy networks for the four BSDRESs were visualized using the R package igraph (Csardi and Nepusz, 2006). Descriptions of the network representation of the supply-demand relationships of bundled services and network metrics are given in Table A.3.

### Exploration of the social-ecological drivers of the supply-demand relationships of bundled ecosystem services

For each BSDRES, the redundancy analysis, hierarchical partitioning analysis and regression analysis were conducted to determine how different social-ecological drivers impact the supply-demand relationships of multiple services. Eleven potential social-ecological drivers were included in the analysis in consideration of both the relevance of these factors to the ES supply and demand and the availability of data (Table A.2). Different potential drivers were all transformed to a common scale ranging from zero to one through 90% winsorization and minimum-maximum normalization (Dou et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2021). The redundancy analysis was conducted with a forward stepwise procedure (R package vegan, Oksanen et al., 2019) to select the different combinations of important social-ecological drivers that significantly influence the ES supply-demand relationships in different bundle types. For each BSDRES, the variance inflation factors of the identified social-ecological drivers were all smaller than ten (Zuur et al., 2010), and the statistical significance of these drivers was tested by permutation tests (999 permutations). In addition, the hierarchical partitioning analysis (R package rdacca.hp, Lai et al., 2022) was performed to acquire an independent explanation of each social-ecological driver of multiple services’ supply-demand relationships in each bundle.

Generalized additive models (GAMs) (R package mgcv, Wood, 2017) were employed to investigate the potential nonlinear responses of ES supply-demand relationships to social-ecological drivers. Since the CESDR was calculated by the averaging approach to reflect the relative differences in ES supplies and demands at an integrated level under the assumption of ES substitutability (Cortinovis et al., 2021; Hölting et al., 2019), a synthetic index alone may not be able to adequately capture the underlying complexity of different ES supply-demand relationships. In this respect, considering that prioritizing intervention in the supply-demand relationships of the umbrella ESs determined from the network analysis could benefit multiple services’ supply-demand relationships, the supply-demand ratios of umbrella ESs may be complementary with the CESDR. Therefore, we developed different GAMs with Gaussian distributions to explore the variations in both the CESDR and the supply-demand ratios of umbrella ESs along the social-ecological driver gradients in different bundles. Based on the specific sets of social-ecological drivers identified for each bundle type in the redundancy analysis, forward-step selection processes were performed to select several factors that mainly influenced the overall state of the supply-demand relationships of multiple services or the supply-demand relationships of umbrella ESs for use in the GAM of each bundle. Specifically, in these processes, the social-ecological drivers that met three criteria were selected (Girardin et al., 2021): the p values were less than 0.05, the Akaike information criterion was smallest in each step, and the increase in R<sup>2</sup> was greater than or equal to 0.01 if an additional driver was included in the GAM. The smooth terms applied to different social-ecological drivers were modelled using thin plate regression splines, and the basis dimension of these smooth terms was set to five (Laiolo et al., 2018; Peters et al., 2016, 2019). To account for potential spatial autocorrelation, Gaussian process splines of latitudes and longitudes were applied in different GAMs, and the dimension of the basis was set to thirty to allow considerable flexibility (Ziter et al., 2019). The response curves of the CESDR or the supply-demand ratios of umbrella ESs along the gradients of individual drivers in each bundle were predicted by fixing all the other explanatory variables to their median values (Cazalis et al., 2020; Millette et al., 2020). In addition, with the aim of assessing the extent to which the variations in the CESDR and the supply-demand ratios of umbrella ESs were explained by the corresponding major drivers in each bundle type, the full models were compared with sets of reduced models by calculating the deviance reductions (Kemppinen et al., 2021; Strindberg et al., 2018). To develop these reduced models, one social-ecological driver was excluded at a time from the full models, and the same smoothing parameters as those applied in the full models were maintained to truly reflect the deviance reductions (Kemppinen et al., 2021; Strindberg et al., 2018).

## Results

### Spatial patterns of the supply-demand relationships of ecosystem services

The spatial patterns of the supply-demand relationships of different ESs varied diversely and heterogeneously across the landscape (Fig. 1 and A.7; Table A.4). In general, greater surplus areas of different regulating services and cultural service were mostly concentrated in the north-western mountainous areas, whereas shortages in the supply of multiple services gradually increased from the outer suburbs to the urban centres in the southeast part of the study area (Fig. 1 and A.6). The areas where the supply of food production met or exceeded the demand occupied 69.6% of the landscape, and these regions were distinguished from the urban and surrounding areas that showed high unmet demand for this provisioning service. Soil conservation deficits appeared in approximately half of the landscape, within which the relative degree of imbalance between the supply and demand was apparently high in the western mountains, while the northern oversupplied areas with high vegetation coverage displayed much higher supplies than demands for soil conservation. Excess supplies of carbon sequestration were located not only in some southern croplands but also in the north-western forests and grasslands where more obvious surpluses of this service were observed. In contrast, the carbon sequestration deficit areas were more clustered and accounted for 23.3% of the landscape, and the relative degree of deficiency of this service increased from the outskirts to the city centre. The areas in which there was an overdemand for biodiversity protection were most widely distributed in the southeast and comprised 64.7% of the landscape. In addition, a significantly higher supply of the biodiversity protection relative to the lower demand could be seen in the north-western mountains. The flood mitigation supply fell short of its demand in only a few areas that were primarily located along waterscapes. Strong deficits in recreation service were merely circumscribed to urban areas, as this cultural service was oversupplied in most of the landscape, especially in the northwest region.

### Spatial distributions of bundles of supply-demand relationships of ecosystem services

The four BSDRESs were spatially clustered in different locations and were unequivocally associated with different types of landscapes consisting of various land-use types (Fig. 2, Table A.5). Mostly located in the urban areas and peri-urban farmlands, BSDRES1 represented the key source of multiple ES demands, coldspots of the provision of several regulating services, and the main ES-deficit bundle that involved shortages of the most types of ESs. Spreading across the riverine landscapes made up of water bodies and other various land use types, BSDRES2 was revealed by serious flood mitigation supply-demand mismatches and the second-largest biodiversity protection deficits among the bundle types. Widely distributed in the agroecosystems in the south-eastern plains and western mountains, BSDRES3 contained the most evident excess food production supply and showed relatively high carbon sequestration, flood mitigation and recreation service surpluses. Spatially concentrated on the north-western mountainous landscapes of forests and grasslands, BSDRES4 was especially recognized as having an oversupply of all six ESs, among which the high supply abundances of all the regulating services and cultural services coincided with low demands of these services, resulting in massive ES surpluses.

### Trade-offs and synergies among the supply-demand relationships of bundled ecosystem services

The trade-offs and synergies in the four bundle types exhibited differences in the numbers, intensities and types of involved services’ supply-demand relationships (Figs. 3 and 4). The significance, direction and intensity of the correlations among the same pair of ESs supply-demand relationships could be dissimilar or resemble in different bundles. In terms of the trade-offs among the supply-demand relationships of bundled ESs, the main ES supply-demand relationship types involved trade-offs with varying strengths, including that of the soil conservation ES in BSDRES1, flood mitigation in BSDRES2, both food production and soil conservation in BSDRES3, and both food production and carbon sequestration in BSDRES4. The most trade-offs among the supply-demand relationships of ESs were observed in BSDRES3, and the most severe trade-off between the supply-demand relationships of food production and soil conservation was presented in BSDRES1.

The preponderance of synergies over trade-offs required more profound investigation into how the supply-demand relationships of multiple ESs were synergistically bundled. Therefore, a synergy network was built for each bundle and the network metrics were calculated to unveil the topological differences among the different bundle types and the ES supply-demand relationships (Figs. 3 and 4, Table A.3). BSDRES1 and BSDRES2 showed higher weighted network densities, node degrees and weighted node degrees than the other two bundles, indicating that the ES supply-demand relationships appeared to be more highly and synergistically correlated in these two bundles. Except for the two-hub network of BSDRES4, in which the supply-demand relationships of both biodiversity protection and soil conservation were identified as umbrella ES supply-demand relationships, the supply-demand relationship of biodiversity protection was consistently the sole hub of the synergy networks for the other three bundles (Table A.6). Among these well-connected hubs, the supply-demand relationship of soil conservation in BSDRES4 was distinguished by the lowest maximum adjacency ratio among all bundles and formed many weak synergies, whereas hubs with higher maximum adjacency ratios, such as the supply-demand relationship of biodiversity protection in BSDRES3, tended to show strong synergies with a few ES supply-demand relationships. Compared with BSDRES2 and BSDRES3, the weighted clustering coefficients of most ES supply-demand relationships in both BSDRES1 and BSDRES4 were equal to one, demonstrating that there were high clustering structures in the respective synergy networks. Nevertheless, distinctive features were also discovered in the degree correlations of the synergy networks for BSDRES1 and BSDRES4. All the nodes in the synergy network of BSDRES1 displayed neutral assortativity, while most of the nodes in the BSDRES4 network were characterized by disassortativity. Regarding BSDRES2 and BSDRES3, the supply-demand relationships of both biodiversity protection and recreation service exhibited similar assortative behaviours, which was particularly reflected by the stronger synergy observed between the two high-degree nodes.

### Impacts of social-ecological drivers on supply-demand relationships of bundled ecosystem services

The supply-demand relationships of the ESs in each bundle were simultaneously driven by the significant impacts of the diverse numbers and types of social-ecological factors (p value < 0.001). Different sets of social-ecological drivers explained different proportions of the variances in the supply-demand relationships of bundled ESs (adjusted R<sup>2</sup>, Figs. 5 and 6, Table A.7). The same driver could either impact the supply-demand ratios of certain ESs in the four bundles with the same impact direction or have effects with opposing directions on the same type of ES supply-demand relationship among different bundle types (Fig. 5). Social factors such as the population density and socioeconomic activity intensity played more important roles in severely affecting the balances of ESs in BSDRES1 and BSDRES2, while biophysical factors such as the normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI) were more critical for maintaining or improving surpluses of multiple services in BSDRES3 and BSDRES4.

The overall states of the supply-demand relationships of multiple ESs and the supply-demand relationships of umbrella services were similarly affected by restricted sets of major social-ecological drivers in different bundles (Fig. 6, Table A.7). In each bundle, the responses of both the CESDR and the supply-demand ratios of umbrella ESs to the individual drivers exhibited nonlinear trajectories with varying magnitudes and revealed potential threshold impacts that could either change the general trends of the response curves or alter the relative differences between the supply and demand of ESs. The CESDR produced similar decreasing curves to the population density and socioeconomic activity intensity in different bundles. An increase in either of these two drivers caused nonlinear decreases in the magnitudes of the overall ES surpluses in BSDRES3 and BSDRES4 but also changed the overall status of the ES supply-demand relationships from surpluses to deficits in BSDRES1 and BSDRES2. In all four bundles, the CESDR responded positively and nonlinearly to the NDVI, and the overall status of the ES supply-demand relationships gradually improved as this driver increased. In addition, positive covariations were found between the magnitudes of the overall ES surplus and elevation in BSDRES4. After identifying the supply-demand relationships of umbrella ESs in all bundles, the supply-demand ratios of biodiversity protection displayed three types of response curves along the population density gradient: a unimodal curve for BSDRES1, negative nonlinear curves for both BSDRES2 and BSDRES3, and a U-shaped curve for BSDRES4. The nonlinear effects of the socioeconomic activity intensity induced a sharp decline in the biodiversity protection supply-demand ratio and produced a weak surplus of this umbrella service in BSDRES4 and strong deficits of this service in the other bundles. In particular, the supply-demand relationships of the two umbrella ESs in BSDRES4 were particularly associated with several major drivers, including the NDVI, elevation, topsoil clay fraction and distance to urban areas. The supply-demand ratios of soil conservation and biodiversity protection generally conveyed ascending patterns along the gradients of these drivers, as reflected by the nonlinear increases in the magnitudes of the two corresponding ES surpluses.

## Discussion

### Heterogeneous mismatches between the supply and demand of multiple ecosystem services

Depicting the heterogeneity of the ES supply-demand relations that emerged from the spatial concordances and differences in either the supply or demand of ESs could deepen our understanding of how nature contributes to people and is a prerequisite for addressing human-nature relationships in social-ecological systems (Wei et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022). The heterogeneous variations in the degrees of spatial congruency of the supply-demand ratios of different ESs promoted the formation of distinct BSDRESs in different areas of the landscape (Cui et al., 2019). The delineation of these BSDRESs determined the partitions among spatial units, the differentiation among social-ecological subsystems, and the division of social-ecological driver gradients, thus making it possible to pinpoint the sustainability challenges faced in specific locations (Saidi and Spray, 2018). Supply-demand imbalances (i.e., in BSDRES1) that exacerbate along the urban-rural gradients might warn the increasing risk of compromising the continuous provision of these ESs and could have serious implications for the long-term fulfilment of human needs (Washbourne et al., 2020). The strong flood mitigation deficits in BSDRES2 suggested the vulnerability of the concentrated population to the high flooding risk in these riverine landscapes (Cao et al., 2021). Agricultural extension and intensification ensured an explicit surplus of food production but caused soil conservation and biodiversity protection shortages in BSDRES3 (Tamburini et al., 2020). The coincidence of ES multifunctionality with ES demand coldspots in BSDRES4 emphasized the important role of forests and grasslands in oversupplying multiple regulating and cultural services simultaneously and may indicate less threats to ESs needing amelioration to benefit people’s quality of life in these mountainous areas (Maron et al., 2017).

### Varying trade-offs and diverse synergies in different bundles of supply-demand relationships of ecosystem services

Uncovering the existence of a diverse range of trade-offs and synergies in the supply-demand relationships of bundled ESs shed new light on the limited knowledge regarding whether the overdemand or oversupply of one service can induce changes in the states of other related services and paved a promising avenue towards reducing unaware trade-offs and strengthening desirable synergies (Schröter et al., 2014). Among the bundle-specific trade-offs and synergies that exhibited varying intensities and involved the supply-demand relationships of different ESs, the shifts between the trade-offs and synergies of the same pairs of services’ supply-demand relationships demonstrated the spatial specificity of the mutually antagonistic or synergistic impacts of the supply-demand relationships of these involved ESs that were inherent to the corresponding social-ecological subsystems.

Comparing the supply-demand relationships of the major ESs having trade-offs with multiple other services’ supply-demand relationships in BSDRES3 and BSDRES4 illustrated that favouring the oversupply of provisioning service could decrease the surplus or worsen the deficit of cultural and regulating services (Felipe-Lucia et al., 2014; Raudsepp-Hearne et al., 2010). The excess supply of carbon sequestration was antagonistic with the biodiversity protection, flood mitigation and recreation service surpluses in BSDRES4, implying that the social-ecological constraints limited the exactly concurrent oversupplies of these bundled services in the same mountainous areas, and the brunt of focusing on maximizing the relative magnitudes of the carbon sequestration surplus could be borne by jeopardizing the supply-demand balances of the other three ESs (Costanza et al., 2017; Ferreira et al., 2018; Perino et al., 2019; Villarreal-Rosas et al., 2020). In addition, the pronounced trade-offs involving undersupply of soil conservation or flood mitigation in three bundles (i.e., BSDRES1, BSDRES2 and BSDRES3) imposed critical challenges regarding the reconciliation of these relevant trade-offs to reduce the two ES deficits while minimizing the detrimental consequences on other services.

The general lack of trade-offs offered a joint improvement opportunity regarding the supply-demand relationships of diverse bundled ESs in different social-ecological subsystems. Distinguishing the topological differences of the synergy networks in different bundle types revealed the synergistic interdependence of diverse bundled services’ supply-demand relationships. Depending on how the supply-demand relationships of the ESs were interconnected, optimizing the supply-demand relationships of one service could have varying, positive, cascading impacts that propagate within the synergy network and affect the other ESs’ supply-demand relationships (Dee et al., 2017). On the one hand, the dense interconnections among the nodes in the high clustering network of BSDRES4 might indicate that raising the surplus of certain services could not only contribute to positive variations in the excess supplies of the other two ESs through interlinkages in a closed triplet but could also spark positive cascading changes in the oversupply of additional ESs in the bundle. On the other hand, the downside of the synergetic interdependence was observed by the nodes that were also densely connected but were more highly correlated in the BSDRES1 network. The failure to prevent further deterioration of the deficit of one ES would likely evoke greater repercussions that could propagate through the numerous triplets in this synergy network and cause more serious supply-demand imbalances in the other services. In particular, the supply-demand relationships of umbrella ESs lied at the heart of managing the bundles by their strong or various synergies with other services’ supply-demand relationships (Boesing et al., 2020). Based on the variety in the topological properties of these well-connected hubs, prioritizing the overdemand for biodiversity protection, which was prone to having strong synergies with a few high-degree services’ (e.g., recreation service) supply-demand relationships in both BSDRES2 and BSDRES3, or promoting the oversupply of soil conservation, which tended to have weak synergies with the supply-demand relationships of many high-degree ESs in BSDRES4, could deliver different benefits to the respective social-ecological subsystems.

### Contrasting and nonlinear impacts of social-ecological drivers on the supply-demand relationships of bundled ecosystem services

Clarifying the contributions of different groups of social-ecological drivers to the supply-demand relationships of ESs in different bundles was helpful for determining where and how the manipulations of which social-ecological drivers could be effective for rebalancing the mismatches between the supplies and demands of ESs and obtaining cobenefits from the synergies among ES supply-demand relationships (Maron et al., 2017; Mastrángelo et al., 2019). The spatial heterogeneity of the social-ecological drivers resulted in variations in the nature and extent of their impacts on the oversupply or overdemand of ESs according to the corresponding location and bundle type (Jiang et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2021; Spake et al., 2019). In each bundle, the dependence of the supply-demand relationship of a particular ES on multiple social-ecological drivers meant that various options were available to boost adequate supplies of these individual services, whereas the opposing effects of one common driver on the supply-demand relationships of different ESs might bring about trade-offs and underscore the need for coordination across a variety of shared social-ecological drivers to enhance complementarities (Almenar et al., 2021; Cortinovis and Geneletti, 2019; Hanisch et al., 2020; Mitchell et al., 2014). The identified primary drivers were useful for explaining the different bundled ES supply-demand relationships, paramount for determining the equilibrium between ES supply and demand and important for explaining the formation of bundles, trade-offs and synergies.

The resemblance in the response curves of the CESDR and the supply-demand ratios of umbrella ESs along their common major driver gradients in most bundles could support that alterations in the supply-demand relationships of umbrella ESs might have cascading impacts on the supply-demand relationships of the services that were similarly influenced by the shared drivers and, consequently, were accompanied by variations in the overall states of the supply-demand relationships of multiple ESs (Boesing et al., 2020). In different bundles, the nonlinearity of the responses of the CESDR and the supply-demand ratios of umbrella ESs not only demonstrated how the positive impacts of biophysical factors disproportionately improved the supply-demand relationships of ESs but also illustrated how the increasing harmful pressures resulting from social factors caused potential threshold effects that accelerated the exacerbation of ES supply-demand tensions (Baró et al., 2016; Vigl et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2021). Moreover, either the inability to convert the overall supply-demand statuses of multiple ESs from deficits to surpluses by increasing the NDVI or the narrow changes of the supply-demand ratios of umbrella ESs along the population density gradient might suggest the limited effectiveness of focusing on individual dominant drivers when addressing ES deficits in BSDRES1.

### Implications for the spatially targeted management of social-ecological systems

Recognizing the spatial disparities in the supply and demand of different ESs, the spatial differentiations of ES trade-offs and synergies and the spatially nonlinear impacts of determinants on ES supply-demand relationships in different bundles are essential for spatially targeted management that seeks to enable the sustainability of social-ecological systems. Tackling site-specific sustainability challenges corresponding to each bundle should not only entail balanced management actions that focus on the supply-demand relationships of umbrella ESs and on ensuring that the supply of multiple ESs meets or exceeds the corresponding demand for these services, but also require transformative governance approaches that target a particular series of social-ecological drivers.

Formulating tailored management policies on the determining factors of BSDRES1 in a concerted manner could be useful to convert the disadvantages of densely connected network regarding the serious shortages of several ESs into advantages. In view of the negative effects of the population density and socioeconomic activity intensity on the supply-demand ratios of multiple services in urban areas, promoting urban smart growth to alleviate population pressure and regulate human activities would be conducive to nonlinearly reducing the high deficits of umbrella service and reconciling other ES supplies and demands (Wang et al., 2021). In the meantime, fully utilizing urban renewal opportunities on the basis of the NDVI’s positive impacts on the supply-demand ratios of different ESs and applying nature-based solutions could assist in fulfilling multiple services’ demands and preventing the paradox in which denser settlements appear in compact cities along with stronger deficits of various ESs (Dang et al., 2021; Keeler et al., 2019; Larondelle and Lauf, 2016; Ma et al., 2022).

When facing the dilemma of satisfying the excess demand of flood mitigation and lessening the related trade-offs that are likely associated with climate conditions in BSDRES2, it is more urgent to adaptively plan blue-green infrastructure networks to rehabilitate degraded riverine habitats with great flood mitigation deficits in the north-western upper reaches where the temperature and precipitation are lower (Delibas et al., 2021). Considering that the NDVI is positively related to the supply-demand ratios of both flood mitigation and umbrella service and that the other ESs involved in trade-offs were oversupplied, better compromises might be achieved by selecting workable measures with a particular emphasis on revegetation (e.g., riparian buffer strips) for the ecological restoration of the abovementioned priority areas (Qiu and Turner, 2013).

In the south-eastern agroecosystems with higher temperatures, population densities and socioeconomic activity intensities, more severe biodiversity protection shortfalls were caused by trade-off with food production oversupply in BSDRES3, which was inconsistent with the Convention on Biological Diversity’s 2050 vision to provide adequate food while conserving biodiversity. This social-ecological subsystem is thus in dire need of the win–win outcomes that could be realized by applying agricultural diversification practices to minimize the negative influences on the environment and adopting climate-smart agriculture strategies to cope with variability in temperature patterns and maintain food security (Bommarco et al., 2013; Delabre et al., 2021; Lipper et al., 2014; Santos-Martín et al., 2019; Tamburini et al., 2020).

By harnessing the synergistic interdependences among different oversupplied services and the general nonlinear growth of the surpluses in the two umbrella ESs along the gradients of several dominant drivers in BSDRES4, the demarcation of ecological redlines to proactively conserve forests and grasslands and restrict urban expansion in the north-western mountains might disproportionately contribute to safeguarding multiple ESs and advancing a broad range of interlinked sustainable development goals underpinned by regulating and cultural services (Bai et al., 2018; Wood et al., 2018). Furthermore, to protect or improve the great carbon sequestration surpluses in densely vegetated locations with higher precipitation and temperature conditions, more cost-effective policies of achieving carbon neutrality could provide or increase ecological compensation for the establishment of ecological redlines through carbon-trading platforms, fiscal transfers or lateral payments (Ferreira et al., 2018; Schirpke et al., 2021; Villarreal-Rosas et al., 2020), thus offsetting the reductions in the surpluses of the other ESs that would result from their inevitable trade-offs with oversupplied carbon sequestration in these north-western sites.

### Limitations

Although our study presented a novel analysis of the spatial relationships and driving factors of ES mismatches that contribute to the formation of spatially targeted strategies for improving ES supply-demand relationships and promoting the sustainable development of social-ecological systems, some limitations still must be acknowledged when interpreting our results. The prudent selection of the different indicators reflecting the supply and demand of ESs and the potential drivers remained preconditioned by the availability of existing models, methods and data. Therefore, only the supply and demand data of six representative ESs and eleven social-ecological variables were analysed in this study. Refining these ES indicators, improving the data quality, and updating spatially explicit models for quantifying ES supplies and demands could be conducive to reducing potential uncertainties in the mapping results obtained in the study and may lead to additional policy and planning implications. Both the CESDR and the supply-demand ratios of individual ESs were relative indexes that had the potential to serve as alternative depictions of how nature contributes to people’s well-being. Therefore, these index results must be interpreted in terms of relative rather than absolute values of the spatial differences between ES supplies and demands. The characteristics of different BSDRESs might be impacted by applying different cluster analysis methods or by including different ES indicators. Spearman correlations, as nonmechanistic approaches that detect trade-offs and synergies among the supply-demand relationships of ESs, were incapable of reflecting how these services interacted with each other in nonlinear ways. In view of the preponderance of synergies over trade-offs among the supply-demand relationships of ESs in different bundles, we merely built a synergy network for each bundle and calculated a series of network metrics. Constructing one-mode networks such as trade-off networks specifically for ESs and introducing more meaningful network metrics from network science to ES science might offer more insights for coordinating trade-offs among services. In addition, since the supply-demand relationships of umbrella ESs were determined by the aggregated rankings of two centrality measures (i.e., the node degree and weighted node degree), selecting more centrality measures or using other network methods to identify the important network nodes could provide complementary information for our results and help identify the prioritization of ESs. Eventually, considering how ES supply-demand mismatches and social-ecological drivers evolve over time and change across scales, which might allow more dynamic and spatial approaches for ES governance to be identified, was beyond the scope of our study but could be a priority in future investigations.

## Conclusion

Our work presented a comprehensive panorama of the spatial patterns, bundles, trade-offs, synergies and driving forces of ES supply-demand relationships in the Beijing-Tianjin-Hebei region, which could better inform sustainable ES governance and promote the supply-demand balance of ESs. We provided spatially explicit information regarding the full spectrum of multiple services’ supplies and demands. The greater regulating and cultural service surpluses in mountainous regions and stronger multiple ES deficits in urban areas demonstrated the spatial heterogeneity of the supply-demand imbalances of different ESs. The four identified BSDRESs exhibited different ES supply-and-demand mismatch types in different locations and emphasized the importance of bundling ESs to pinpoint the specific sustainability challenges that were faced by different social-ecological systems and should be dealt with tailored interventions. The spatial variabilities in trade-offs and synergies involving the supply-demand relations of ESs in different bundles offered valuable insights for designing bundle-specific policies to advance multiple goals simultaneously. Applying network science approach revealed the differences in the centrality, cohesiveness and assortativity of diverse ES supply-demand relationships in synergy networks and was clearly a promising step towards understanding the intricacies of the emergent properties of these services. As the supply-demand relationships of umbrella services in all the bundles, the supply-demand balance of biodiversity protection appeared to be critical for managing the synergistic interdependence among the supply-demand mismatches of different ESs. A better understanding of the contrasting impacts of different social-ecological driver sets on the bundled ES supply-demand relations provided scientific guidance on alleviating threats to sustainable supplies of ESs in each bundle spatially and precisely, and the depictions of how both the CESDR and the supply-demand ratios of umbrella ESs responded nonlinearly to social-ecological drivers were helpful for identifying potential intervention points where small changes in drivers might yield substantial ES benefits. Ultimately, our findings suggested that achieving and maintaining sustainable social-ecological systems would require spatially targeted management that could be flexibly adjusted according to the spatial characteristics of the ES supply-demand relationships and driving factors.
