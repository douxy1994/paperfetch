# Abundance versus vital rates: The extent and predictors of inconsistent conclusions on avian population responses to forest loss and degradation

## Abstract

Understanding how forest loss and degradation impact species populations is a key conservation inquiry. To date, our knowledge of such impacts is predominantly based on measuring “snapshot” abundance responses, which may not reflect long-term population trajectories and risk spurious conclusions. Averting this risk necessitates understanding to what extent and under what conditions abundance metrics may draw inconsistent conclusions with population vital rates, yet such an assessment remains lacking. In this study, we fill this knowledge gap using a systematic literature review, focusing on birds as the study taxon. We searched for published empirical studies that simultaneously measured species' abundance and vital rate responses to forest loss or degradation. We then assessed the level of consistency in conclusions between these two metric types and possible predictors. We identified 48 studies that together reported 163 paired findings based on abundance and vital rate metrics (“cases”), involving 59 species from 12 countries, mostly in North America. We found that in 36.8 % of these cases, findings were inconsistent between the two metric types. We further found that the more recent forest loss or degradation was relative to the time of study, the more likely findings were inconsistent between abundance and vital rates. Our study highlights the caution needed in using abundance to measure population responses to forest loss and degradation, and the necessity to directly measure vital rates whenever possible, particularly when forest loss and degradation are recent.

## Introduction

Forest loss and degradation are leading threats to global biodiversity (Maxwell et al., 2016; Wilson et al., 2016). As human activities intensify, forest habitats are increasingly being lost or degraded by land uses linked to farming, grazing, logging, etc. (Potapov et al., 2017; Tyukavina et al., 2017). A central conservation question is how species populations respond to these changes, particularly over the long run. Aside from population monitoring over time (LPI, 2022), a key approach to generating such understanding is to assess population vital rates, i.e. demographic parameters that measure various components of population dynamics including survival, reproduction, immigration, and emigration (Table 1; applying to birds as an example taxon; Dugger et al., 2015; Heppell et al., 2000). By describing the processes underlying how populations change, including their stability and viability, these parameters provide direct information on the state and long-term trajectories of populations (Cosset et al., 2019), including under forest loss and degradation. For example, reproductive rates reflect the ability of a population to generate new individuals with which to sustain its future persistence, thus a declining reproductive rate would indicate compromised population persistence over the long term (Vance et al., 2003). However, primarily because of the time and effort needed for assessing vital rates, existing studies have overwhelmingly used the snapshot measure of population abundance to approximate long-term population responses to forest loss and degradation (e.g. Edwards et al., 2010; Gibson et al., 2011; Sandström et al., 2019).

Note: whereas per-capita reproductive rate directly reflects population growth on a per-individual basis, per-unit-area reproductive rate is more relevant to conservation decision-making, because habitats capable of producing more offspring should be considered of higher conservation value (Bock and Jones, 2004; Kohyama et al., 2018).

The use of snapshot abundance metrics may generate spurious conclusions on long-term population responses to forest loss and degradation – and habitat change in general – for several reasons (Van Horne, 1983). First, where source-sink dynamics exist, populations in deforested or degraded habitats may be replenished by individuals from (more) intact habitats nearby; this “rescue effect” may detach apparent population abundance from the actual population-supporting capacity of forest habitats (Mosser et al., 2009; Van Horne, 1983). Similarly, behavioral dominance among competing individuals may displace and potentially crowd subordinate individuals into low-quality habitats, again rendering population abundance to misrepresent the population-supporting capacity of a habitat (Haché et al., 2013; Vickery et al., 1992). Finally, where ecological relaxation applies after disturbance, population abundance may take time to exhibit the demographic changes already set in motion by habitat loss and degradation (Halley et al., 2016; Hylander and Ehrlén, 2013). Until relaxation fully occurs, abundance metrics would almost certainly produce misleading conclusions on actual population responses.

Despite the above caution against using snapshot abundance metrics, they underlie the vast majority of our current understanding of population responses to forest loss and degradation (Dislich et al., 2017; Gibson et al., 2011). It is high time we assess the robustness of such understanding, by asking to what extent and under what conditions abundance metrics produce inconsistent conclusions with vital rates under this important conservation context. However, such an assessment has remained missing to date. Even for the more generic comparison of population abundance versus vital rates in representing habitat quality, there has only been one previous assessment from almost two decades ago (Bock and Jones, 2004). But this study was not specific to the context of forest loss and degradation, nor did it sufficiently explore the conditions that rendered abundance to produce inconsistent conclusions from vital rates (Bock and Jones, 2004). Notably, however, that study did suggest population abundance to be particularly unreliable as a measure of habitat quality in anthropogenically disturbed habitats (Bock and Jones, 2004), indicating that its use in measuring population responses to forest loss and degradation could be particularly problematic.

Here, we provide a formal assessment of the extent to which research conclusions drawn by snapshot abundance and vital rates may differ on population responses to forest loss and degradation. We also test the conditions that may render the two metric types to reach inconsistent conclusions. Using birds as a representative biodiversity group, we compiled an extensive global database of field studies that simultaneously used snapshot abundance and vital rates to assess avian population responses to forest loss and degradation. We focused on birds because data on vital rates – the metric type with more limited data – were less available for other taxa. Within each study, we compared the conclusions reached by the two metric types. We then tested whether their conclusion inconsistency was linked to the three issues noted above that underlie possible spurious conclusions by population abundance: rescue effect, behavioral dominance, and ecological relaxation. The premise of our study is that by directly measuring key aspects of demographics, vital rates provide the most direct, accurate measure of population responses to forest loss and degradation, therefore serving as the benchmark against which the conclusions from snapshot abundance can be assessed. We should acknowledge that because population dynamics are the combined outcome of multiple demographic components, they cannot be adequately represented by any individual vital rate. Therefore, our assessment should ideally combine multiple vital rates into a “whole-picture metric” as benchmark. However, studies reporting multiple vital rates to inform population dynamics are exceedingly rare. This paucity limited our assessment to individual vital rates, and our findings should be interpreted with this caution in mind. In all, our study aims to assess and help ensure the validity of knowledge pertaining to a critical conservation inquiry.

## Material and methods

### Study design

We conducted a systematic literature search to identify empirical studies that simultaneously reported avian population responses to forest loss or degradation using snapshot abundance and at least one vital rate metric. For each species in each primary study, we compiled conclusions drawn respectively from snapshot abundance (“abundance” hereafter) and vital rates on the population impacts of forest loss or degradation, expressed with categorical information on both statistical significance (significant versus non-significant) and effect direction (positive, negative, or no effect). We assessed the consistency of these qualitative – rather than quantitative – conclusions because all vital rate metrics we assessed measured only one aspect of demographic dynamics; we therefore did not expect them to have a one-to-one relationship with long-term population abundance. To test the conditions underlying inconsistent conclusions between abundance and vital rate metrics, we compiled a set of meta-data to represent the extent of rescue effect, behavioral dominance, and ecological relaxation in each study, which we related to the conclusion inconsistency in each study using a weighted linear mixed modeling framework. We represented the extent of rescue effect with the presence of connectivity between deforested/degraded habitats and their reference forests, behavioral dominance with the strength of species territoriality, and ecological relaxation with longer time length of forest loss or degradation relative to species' generation length.

### Literature search and data compilation

We systematically searched for published empirical studies that simultaneously measured birds' abundance and vital rate responses to forest loss or degradation. For vital rates, we considered three metric types that had reasonable availability of empirical data based on our preliminary search: survival, per-capita reproductive, or per-unit-area reproductive rates; we required that qualified studies report at least one metric type, using any of the specific metrics listed in Table 1. We further required qualified studies to meet two more conditions. (1) The study compared a degraded native forest (i.e. a forest disturbed by human activities such as logging, grazing, burning, etc.) with a matching undisturbed forest, or a deforested habitat (i.e. a previous forest that had been converted to non-forest habitat such as farmland, plantation, etc.) with a matching undisturbed or slightly disturbed native forest in measuring species population responses (hereafter we refer to these habitats as “degraded habitat” and “baseline forest”, respectively). (2) For a given species, the study provided conclusions on abundance and vital rate responses, in the categorical form of significantly positive, significantly negative, non-significantly positive, non-significantly negative, or non-significantly neutral (for cases where the degraded habitat and baseline forest had exactly the same abundance or vital rate).

We searched for qualified primary studies in Web of Science on December 24th 2021, using the following search terms in “Topic”: (“survival” OR “survivorship” OR “reproductive success” OR “reproductive performance*” OR “breeding success” OR “breeding performance*” OR “vital rate*” OR recruitment OR “population growth rate*”) AND (densit* OR abundance) AND forest* AND (clear-cutting* OR logg* OR deforestation* OR fire* OR agricultur* OR disturb* OR degrad* OR “secondary forest*” OR plantation* OR fragment*) AND (bird* OR avian). Our search returned 1575 studies. We screened all of them to retain qualified ones, and we confirmed that this collection included all of the 9 qualified studies included in the above-noted 2004 synthesis (Bock and Jones, 2004).

We used the word “case” to represent a pair of conclusions from abundance and vital rates for a given bird species at a given point in time; a primary study may contribute one or more cases. From each qualified study and the case(s) it provided, we extracted data on the location of the study site, the nature of degraded habitat and baseline forest, the identity of bird species studied, species abundance and vital rates in the degraded habitat and baseline forest, sample size for the vital rate data, and the categorical conclusions of population responses to forest loss or degradation based on each of the abundance and vital rate metrics (i.e. significantly positive, significantly negative, non-significantly positive, non-significantly negative, or non-significantly neutral). We assessed the sample size for the vital rate data to use as weight for each case in subsequent analyses, with the rationale that vital rate metrics – because of the intensive sampling efforts needed – typically entailed lower sample sizes compared with abundance metrics. We assessed sample size as the summed number of the finest independent sampling unit across the degraded habitat and baseline forest. We considered sampling units (studying sites, plots, transects, etc.) as independent if they were reported as independent by the primary studies, or if they were spaced ≥200 m apart. If the above information was unclear, we defaulted to the coarsest sampling units as independent sampling units. In total, our databases included 48 primary studies and 163 cases, involving 59 bird species (Table S1).

For each case concerning resident bird species (listed obtained from Runge et al. (2015)), we represented the presence of rescue effect by the presence of connectivity between the degraded habitat and baseline forest. We focused on resident species because the migration of non-resident species should render their abundance in degraded habitats less amenable to the influence of incoming individuals from (more) intact forests. Because most primary studies did not provide exact measures of the distance between the degraded habitat and baseline forest, we were not able to calculate a quantitative measure of functional connectivity (e.g. the ratio between this distance and species' hand-wing index as a measure of dispersal ability; Claramunt, 2021; Tobias et al., 2022). We therefore resorted to a qualitative measure of structural connectivity, differentiating between two categories (“connected” versus “not connected”) based on the following criteria. First, if a primary study clearly stated that the two habitats were “connected”/“adjacent”, or had “connectivity”/“corridors” etc., we considered them as “connected”; correspondingly, if it stated that the two habitats were “isolated”, or had “no connectivity” etc., we considered them as “not connected”. Second, in situations where the primary study did not provide text statement but provided a map of the study area that can inform the connectivity status of the two habitats, we considered the two habitats as “connected” if the map showed contiguous forest cover abutting the two habitats. Applying this procedure yielded 34 cases concerning resident species that provided connectivity information.

We represented the extent of behavioral dominance by the territoriality of the species concerned, with stronger territoriality indicating stronger behavioral dominance. Because of the lack of existing database on species' territoriality in the breeding season, the study season of most of the primary studies compiled, we used the temporal territoriality database by Tobias and Pigot (2019) instead, assuming that species' propensity to defend territories throughout the year was correlated with their strength of territoriality during the breeding season. According to this database, we differentiated among three territoriality levels for all 59 species and 163 cases: strong territoriality (territories maintained throughout the year), weak territoriality (weak or seasonal territoriality, including species with broadly overlapping home ranges or habitually joining mixed species flocks), and no territoriality (never territorial or at most defending very small areas around nest sites; Tobias and Pigot, 2019).

Finally, for each case, we measured the extent of ecological relaxation by the number of years since forest loss or degradation – up until the time of the primary study – relative to species' generation length (“relative degradation time” hereafter), with longer relative degradation time indicating more thorough ecological relaxation. We obtained information on the year in which forest loss or degradation occurred directly from the primary studies. We then divided the time length of forest loss or degradation by species' generation length (a measure of a species' reproductive speed, defined as the average age of parents of the current cohort in y; IUCN, 2021), because the same time length is ecologically different for species with different life history speeds (Bommarco et al., 2014; Hutchings, 2021). In a number of situations, the year in which the event of forest loss or degradation occurred could take multiple possible values, given the uncertainty regarding when ecological relaxation could start. First, for events that lasted a long and known duration (e.g. decades of grazing, as opposed to pulse or short-duration events), we used both their beginning and end years as the year of the event. Second, for cases reported to have involved several recurring events, we used both the beginning year of the first event and the end year of the last event as the year of the event. Third, for cases that indicated a range of years – instead of the exact year – for when the event occurred, we used the median and both ends of the range as the year of the event. We accommodated all possible values for the year of the event in one set of main analysis and five sets of sensitivity analyses. For the first two situations, we used the later year in our main analysis, and the earlier year in a sensitivity analysis; for the third situation, we used the median year in our main analysis, and the other years in sensitivity analyses (Table S2). We were able to derive relative degradation time for 155 cases for our main analysis, and for 123–155 cases for our sensitivity analyses (Table S2).

### Data analysis

For each case, we compared the consistency between the categorical conclusions of population responses to forest loss or degradation based on abundance versus vital rate metrics, using a coding system of four categories in descending order of consistency. (1) Strongly consistent: if the two conclusions had the same significance level and effect direction. (2) Weakly consistent: if both conclusions were non-significant but differed in effect direction. (3) Weakly inconsistent: if the two conclusions differed in significance level but had the same effect direction. (4) Strongly inconsistent: if the two conclusions differed in effect direction and at least one of them was significant. This coding system in effect prioritized statistical significance over effect direction in gauging the consistency between conclusions, with the rationale that the former provided the “weight of evidence” and thus legitimacy for the latter. We assigned ordinal scores of 4, 3, 2, and 1 to the above categories in the same order for subsequent analysis, then calculated the proportion of cases falling within each category.

We used weighted linear mixed models to separately test if the level of consistency in conclusions (in the form of ordinal scores) were predictable from rescue effect, behavioral dominance, or ecological relaxation; we were not able to simultaneously assess all three factors because there were too few cases with data available for all factors (*n* = 26). We used the sample size for the vital rate data on a natural log scale as the weight for each case. For models on behavioral dominance, we modeled territoriality as an ordinal variable (0, 1, and 2 respectively for no territoriality, weak territoriality, and strong territoriality). For models on ecological relaxation, we modeled the relative degradation time on a natural log scale (after adding a value of 1 to avoid negative log values), considering that its possible predictive power of conclusion consistency was likely to saturate beyond a certain time length. For random effects, we included the identity of primary studies and species as separate random intercepts to account for possible data non-independence, although we were forced to drop primary study identity for the model on rescue effect because of small sample size. We also were not able to include the identity of vital rate metrics as a random effect because of model singular fit. To avoid potential spurious results from not differentiating vital rate metrics, we additionally repeated the above models for per-capita reproductive rates alone (the metric with the most amount of data) in another set of sensitivity analyses. We conducted all analyses and visualization in program R (version 4.2.1; R Core Team, 2022), using the function “lmer” in package “lmerTest” (version 3.1-3; Kuznetsova et al., 2017) for linear mixed models and the function “rsquared” in package “piecewiseSEM” (version 2.3.0; Lefcheck, 2016) for calculating marginal R<sup>2</sup>.

## Results

We identified 48 qualified studies conducted in 12 countries, most in North America and none from Asia, Africa, or Oceania (Fig. 1). These studies contributed a total of 163 cases involving 59 bird species (Table S1). For 60 (36.8 %) of these cases, abundance and vital rate metrics produced inconsistent conclusions on population responses to forest loss or degradation, of which 32 were of the category strongly inconsistent and 28 weakly inconsistent (Fig. 2a). The incidence of inconsistent conclusions differed somewhat when abundance was measured against different types of vital rate metrics: whereas over half (seven strongly and four weakly) of the 18 cases involving survival rates produced inconsistent conclusions between abundance and vital rate metrics (Fig. 2b), the incidence of inconsistent conclusions was 33.6 % (23 strongly and 21 weakly) for the 131 cases involving per-capita reproductive rates (Fig. 2c), and 35.7 % (two strongly and three weakly) for the 14 cases involving per-unit-area reproductive rates (Fig. 2d).

We did not find evidence that the inconsistent conclusions between abundance and vital rate metrics were related to rescue effect (Fig. 3a) or behavioral dominance (Fig. 3b). However, we found that conclusions tended to be more inconsistent when forest loss or degradation was more recent relative to the time of study (i.e. when relative degradation time was shorter; Fig. 3c), indicating the relevance of ecological relaxation in abundance metrics generating spurious conclusions on population responses to forest loss or degradation. This relevance was overall robust to a suite of sensitivity analyses involving different calculations of relative degradation time (Table S2). However, it was not supported by the additional set of analyses on per-capita reproductive rates alone (Table S3).

## Discussion

Our systematic literature review found that in existing empirical studies assessing avian population responses to forest loss or degradation, there was a 36.8 % incidence rate of inconsistency between conclusions drawn from abundance and vital rate metrics. We also found that this inconsistency was predictable by the extent to which populations had undergone ecological relaxation: conclusions tended to be more inconsistent when forest loss or degradation occurred more recently. These findings provide the first evidence for considerable risks of abundance metrics to generate spurious conclusions on the population impacts of forest loss and degradation. They also provide a much-needed empirical assessment of theoretical predictions that abundance measures may misrepresent long-term population responses to anthropogenic habitat change (Bock and Jones, 2004): the extent to which such misrepresentation occurs has not been assessed with synthesized evidence before.

Filling in the above gaps has clear conservation significance. Given the severe threats forest loss and degradation pose to global biodiversity (Maxwell et al., 2016), accurately quantifying their impacts on species populations over the long run is an important conservation inquiry (Newmark, 2006). Although abundance metrics are known to potentially generate misleading conclusions on the long-term population consequences of habitat change (Bock and Jones, 2004; Vickery et al., 1992), they constitute the dominant metric upon which the vast majority of our current understanding is based. This leaves the question open that our understanding may not be robust. In the only relevant previous test of this question based on synthesized evidence, Bock and Jones (2004) compared findings on birds from abundance and vital rate metrics under the wider context of “different habitats” (i.e. not limited to forest loss and degradation or anthropogenic habitat change at large), and found a ~15–28 % incidence rate of inconsistency between the two metric types. They also preliminarily suggested the incidence of inconsistency to be higher in anthropogenically disturbed habitats than undisturbed habitats. Our synthesis directly tests the above question under the explicit context of forest loss and degradation, and highlights the considerable – and higher than reported by Bock and Jones (2004) – risk of spurious understanding based on abundance metrics. Future studies on population responses to forest loss and degradation (and habitat change in general) should strive to directly measure vital rates whenever possible.

This necessity to directly measure vital rates is all the stronger under more recent events of forest loss or degradation, when abundance can generate more inconsistent conclusions with vital rate metrics, as indicated by our analyses. This tendency is predicted by ecological theory pertaining to ecological relaxation: whereas disturbance events may immediately change demographic processes, time lags may exist for responses in population abundance to manifest, particularly for long-lived, slow-reproducing species (Hylander and Ehrlén, 2013). Such time lags relative to habitat disturbance events have been empirically well supported (e.g. Brooks et al., 1999; Metzger et al., 2009; Uezu and Metzger, 2016), and its length (also called “relaxation time”) is considered to depend on a range of factors including species' life history pace and dispersal distance, the nature and intensity of habitat change, and the difference of abundance level between the current time and the new equilibrium (Bommarco et al., 2014; Hylander and Ehrlén, 2013; Lalechère et al., 2019). In theory, population abundance should be an honest representation of vital rates and long-term population trajectories once ecological relaxation has occurred. In practice, however, the influence of multiple factors on relaxation time makes it extremely difficult to predict (Hylander and Ehrlén, 2013). This difficulty again argues for directly measuring vital rates rather than the more convenient abundance metrics whenever possible. Notably, ecological relaxation provides a window for conservation interventions to reverse population declines and even extinctions (Kuussaari et al., 2009; Wearn et al., 2012), and it may be too costly a price if seemingly positive conclusions from abundance metrics misled us to miss this window.

The extent to which abundance generated inconsistent conclusions compared with vital rate metrics appeared to depend on the type of vital rates, with survival rates exhibiting the highest incidence of inconsistencies at over 50 % of the cases. Relatedly, the fact that results from our sensitivity analysis conducted on data from a single vital rate (per-capita reproductive rate) did not support those from our main analysis (Table S3) also indicated difference in conclusions among vital rates. On the one hand, this difference may have been an artefact of the small number of cases involving survival rates and per-unit-area reproductive rates (18 and 14, respectively). On the other hand, it is also conceivable that some vital rates may themselves involve ecological relaxation under environmental change (Layton-Matthews et al., 2021; Walker et al., 2013) and therefore exhibit more muted sensitivity to forest loss and degradation compared with others. Finally, different vital rates may make different demographic contributions (Coluccy et al., 2008; Manlik et al., 2018), and are therefore not expected to have equivalent relationships (or lack thereof) with population abundance or with each other.

The last possibility above echoes the demographic buffering hypothesis, which on the basis of life-history theory stipulates that under natural selection, vital rates that have larger contributions to population change should be more buffered against environmental fluctuations (Bjørkvoll et al., 2016; Hilde et al., 2020). In the context of our study, this means more muted responses of certain vital rates to forest loss and degradation compared with others. According to this hypothesis, vital rates more important in determining population change should – via their reduced sensitivity to forest loss and degradation – be more likely to exhibit consistent responses as abundance metrics, given that the spurious conclusions of the latter typically stem from their muted responses (as discussed in the Introduction). Our finding that survival rates had the highest incidence of inconsistencies with abundance thus may be linked to their likely low levels of contribution to population change in many species – which is possible given that most studies in our database came from colder climates where the avifauna tends to have more fecund, less long-living “fast” life-history strategies (Martin, 2015). In sum, the difference among vital rates suggests that individual vital rates only provide incomplete representation of population responses to forest loss and degradation, and that truly assessing populations' long-term trajectories would necessitate measuring demographic dynamics based on multiple types of vital rates (Camfield et al., 2011).

Three issues of our study are worth noting in interpreting our findings. First, the lack of support we found for rescue effect and behavioral dominance may be statistical artefacts: the number of cases for the analyses on rescue effect was only 33, while the dataset for the analyses on behavioral dominance was dominated by species of weak territoriality. The latter analyses were potentially also undermined by our use of temporal territoriality data (Tobias and Pigot, 2019) because data on species' territoriality intensity during the breeding season were not available. Second, our representation of the extent of ecological relaxation should have considered factors other than species' life history pace that also influence relaxation time. As noted above, factors such as the nature, intensity, and demographic impacts of forest loss and degradation should also affect the “functional” length of relaxation time, but data availability prevented us from also considering these other factors. Finally, our study has a clear spatial bias, with most primary studies conducted in North America and Europe while none from Asia, Africa, or Oceania. It thus remains unclear to what extent our results are applicable to these data-deficient regions – which also happen to be where most of the world's ongoing forest loss and degradation occurs (Jha and Bawa, 2006; Potapov et al., 2017). Future studies aimed at assessing the biodiversity impacts of forest loss and degradation in these regions should go beyond the traditional abundance metrics to assess impacts on species' vital rates.

Based on synthesized evidence, our study provides a much-needed assessment of the extent to which abundance metrics may generate spurious conclusions about the population impacts of forest loss and degradation. Our findings highlight the caution needed in using these metrics – particularly when forest loss and degradation are recent. While we limited our assessment to birds, conclusions from our study are likely broadly applicable to other taxa. Researchers have reported similar cases where population abundance mis-represents forest quality for insects (Govindan et al., 2012), amphibians and reptiles (Ujvari et al., 2011), and mammals (Henry et al., 2007; Weldy et al., 2020), indicating similar potential for abundance metrics to mis-represent the vital rate – and long-term population – responses to forest loss and degradation in these taxa. As with birds, it would be prudent to directly measure vital rates in studying population responses of these taxa whenever possible. When it is impractical to measure vital rates – e.g. because of cryptic life stages or very small populations (Rose et al., 2021; Tremblay and McCarthy, 2014) – other indicators could be used to complement abundance metrics, including body condition, vegetation structure and food resources (Johnson, 2007).
