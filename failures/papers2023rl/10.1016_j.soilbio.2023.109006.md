# Priming effects induced by degradable microplastics in agricultural soils

## Abstract

Microplastics (MPs) in farmland soils have the potential to cause environmental and human health risks due to their ubiquitous and persistent nature. Degradable MPs may significantly alter the decomposition of soil organic carbon by the so-called priming effect (PE). However, the amplitude and direction of the PE induced by degradable MPs remain uncertain in farmland soils. In this study, a 56-d laboratory incubation experiment was conducted by adding three types of degradable MPs, i.e., poly-hydroxyalkanoates (PHA), polybutylene succinate (PBS), and polylactic acid (PLA) to three farmland soils planted with strawberry, blueberry, and paddy-rape rotation, respectively. The differences in isotopic values (δ<sup>13</sup> C) between soils (−25.30−-27.46‰) and MPs (−11.94−-9.99‰) were used to assess the PE and net carbon balance by the natural<sup>13</sup> C isotopic tracing method. We found that the stronger the degradability of MPs, the greater the PE induced. Subsequently, PHA-induced PE was the highest, followed by PBS- and PLA-induced PE corresponding to their biodegradability. Moreover, PHA-induced PE reached an impressive 552−1744%, while PBS- and PLA-induced PE achieved 44−179%, and −29−43% across the above three soils, respectively. Overall, PHA resulted in the highest cumulative PE and the lowest net carbon balance relative to PBS and PLA in all three soils. This study, for the first time, demonstrates that the amplitude of PE is dependent on the degradability of MPs, emphasizing that microbial nitrogen mining may play an important role in driving the PE in the long term due to the nitrogen deficiency of MPs.

## Introduction

Soil is a heterogeneous habitat built by a complex mixture of organic carbon (C) and minerals (Joos and De Tender, 2022). Slight variations in the soil C pool may greatly influence terrestrial C dynamics, therefore, impacting future climate change (Davidson and Janssens, 2006). Priming effect (PE) refers to the change in decomposition rate of native soil organic carbon (SOC) after fresh organic matter (OM) input (Kuzyakov et al., 2000; El-Naggar et al., 2019). The fresh C input can stimulate or inhibit native SOC decomposition by PE, thereby mediating terrestrial C balance (Kuzyakov et al., 2000). Generally, PE is closely related to the chemical composition and amount of exogenous substances, and physio-chemical properties of soil (Feng and Zhu, 2021). The overall positive mean PE of about +59% was found across dead fine roots, root exudates, or single compounds, e.g. glucose and amino acid (Huo et al., 2017; Feng and Zhu, 2021), while the overall negative mean PE of about −4% was found in biochar application (Wang et al., 2016). Thus, the predicted increase of fresh OM input may significantly influence the feedback relationship between climate change and terrestrial C cycle in a warming world.

Microplastics (MPs) are less than 5 mm, mainly from the breaking of agricultural films through prolonged exposure to ultraviolet and weathering (He et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022). The debris of MPs can exist in soils or further enter into rivers and oceans through precipitation, infiltration, and surface runoff. Most of the studies in the literature initially focused on the toxicological effects of MPs on biological and ecological functions of marine ecosystems (Carpenter and Smith, 1972; Woodall et al., 2014; Lenz et al., 2016). It is also recognized that MPs may result in a degeneration of aquatic ecosystem functions (Cole et al., 2011; Carbery et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2022). In contrast with conventional plastics, most of biodegradable plastics are produced from plant materials to alleviate the “white pollution” of the environment (Qi et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021b). Thus, the particles or fragments of biodegradable plastics entering soil pores may be used as potential carbon sources by soil microorganisms in the plastisphere to produce microbial biomass and respiration (Green et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2021a), therefore disordering the soil microbial community (Liu et al., 2023) and biogeochemical cycles in terrestrial ecosystems (Awet et al., 2018).

Soils are a more extensive reservoir of MPs than oceans (Nizzetto et al., 2016). The abundance of MPs in the soil is found to be 4−23 fold of that in the ocean (Horton et al., 2017). Recent studies showed that MPs in the soil might be potential microbial C substrates and primers affecting SOC decomposition by altering soil structure or microbial function (Rillig, 2018; Rillig et al., 2019). Subsequently, it was found that MPs can also negatively affect plant-microbial metabolic functioning by decreasing soil microbial activity and altering bacterial community structure (Brown et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). Rillig et al. (2021) suggested a probable PE mechanism via MPs by paralleling pyrogenic C and proposed two major hypotheses. Firstly, a negative PE is caused by the organo-organo persistence of the dissolved organic carbon (DOC) adsorbed on MPs surfaces or preferential metabolization of the easily mineralized organic C in MPs. Secondly, a positive PE could be caused by the microbial co-metabolism of SOC and biodegradable MPs. However, these hypotheses have not been experimentally verified yet.

In this study, farmland soils planted with strawberry, blueberry, and rape-paddy rotation were incubated in the lab for 56 days with three types of biodegradable MPs, i.e., poly-hydroxyalkanoates (PHA), polylactic-acid (PLA) and polybutylene succinate (PBS). Soil- and microplastic-derived CO2–C were distinguished by the natural<sup>13</sup> C isotope tracer technique. The objectives of this study were to (i) explore the direction and range of the PE induced by degradable MPs; (ii) compare the PE induced by three types of degradable MPs; and (iii) assess the effect of degradable MPs on soil net C balance.

## Materials and methods

### Soil samples

In this study, plow layer soils (0−20 cm) were collected from farmlands planted with blueberry, strawberry, and paddy-rape rotation in Wanzhou, Chongqing, southwest China (30° 40′ N, 108° 17′ E, 520 m above sea level). The soils were air-dried and passed through a 2 mm mesh after manually removing visible plant roots and residues. The soils were homogenized before analysis and incubation. The isotopic values (δ<sup>13</sup> C) of the soils under blueberry, strawberry and paddy-rape rotation were −25.30‰, −25.48‰, and −27.46‰, respectively (Table 1).

### Microplastics used

In this study, poly-hydroxyalkanoates (PHA), polylactic-acid (PLA) and polybutylene succinate (PBS) were purchased from plastic chemical raw materials Co., Ltd (Dongguan, China). Before using MPs for an experiment, the purchased plastics were cleaned with 75% ethanol and passed through a 0.15−0.18 mm mesh without milling. This MPs size is typically found in contaminated soils (Sun et al., 2022). The δ<sup>13</sup> C of PHA, PBS, and PLA were measured as −11.94‰, −11.78‰, and −9.99‰, respectively (Table 2). The δ<sup>13</sup> C differences (>10‰) between MPs and studied soils were used to partition the contribution of MPs to total carbon dioxide (CO2) emission.

### Soil incubation

In this incubation experiment, 40.0 g of dry soil mixed with the MPs of PHA, PBS or PLA, corresponding to 15 mg C g<sup>−1</sup> soil (about 3% w/w), was added to a 50 mL triangular flask by considering the field concentrations of plastic debris in agricultural soils and the doses used in similar incubation studies in the literature (X.J.A. Liu et al., 2017; Fei et al., 2020; Song et al., 2023). The flask and 20 mL vial with 1.0 M NaOH were sealed into a 600 mL jar for CO2 trapping. The soil was pre-incubated for 7 d at 50% water holding capacity (WHC) and 22 °C. Then soil moisture was kept at 60% WHC during the following 56-d incubation by weighing (Blagodatskaya and Anderson, 1999; Chen et al., 2022b). Three flasks without soil were treated as the control to account for ambient CO2. The vials were replaced on days 7, 14, 21, 35, and 56 for CO2 and isotopic (δ<sup>13</sup> C) analysis. The experiment treatments (total 180 units) included 3 soils (strawberry vs. blueberry vs. rape-paddy) × 4 inputs (control vs. PHA vs. PBS vs. PLA) × 1 temperature (22 °C) × 3 replicates × 5 samplings. After 56-d incubation, the soils were destructively sampled for chemical analysis.

### CO2 analysis

The δ<sup>13</sup> C (‰) of the inorganic C in NaOH solution was measured using an isotope ratio mass spectrometer coupled to a GasBench II (Delta V Advantage, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Germany). The inorganic C content in the NaOH solution was measured using a Lotix combustion TOC analyzer (Teledyne Tekmar, USA).

### Chemical analysis

The total organic carbon (TOC) and total nitrogen (TN) in the studied soils and MPs were measured with an elemental analyzer (EA 3000 Euro Vector, Milan, Italy). Soil pH was determined at a soil: water ratio of 1: 2.5 using a pH electrode. The soil particle size distribution was determined by adopting the hydrometer method (Bouycous, 1962). The WHC was measured by filling air-dried soil in a 100 cm<sup>3</sup> cutting ring, soaking it for 24 h, and then putting it on another air-dried soil to drain for 8 h. Microbial biomass carbon (MBC), microbial biomass nitrogen (MBN), DOC, and dissolved total nitrogen (DTN) were measured by fumigation-extraction with a correction value of 0.45 (Vance et al., 1987; Wu et al., 1990). Briefly, 5 g of fresh soil after incubation with or without ethanol-free chloroform fumigation was extracted with 25 mL of 0.5 M K2SO4 for 1 h. Then the extracts were measured with Shimadzu TOC-VCPH (Shimadzu Corp., Tokyo, Japan). The non-fumigated samples were used to assess DOC and DTN. Soil ammonium (NH4<sup>+</sup>-N) and nitrate (NO3<sup>−</sup>-N) were analyzed using standard soil test procedures of the Chinese Ecosystem Research Network (CERN, 1996).

### Calculations

Absolute PE and relative PE were calculated as follows (H. Liu et al., 2017; Feng et al., 2021):

(1)

$$
Absolute PE = CMPs-amended − Ccontrol
$$

(2)

$$
Relative PE = [(CMPs-amended − Ccontrol) / Ccontrol] × 100
$$

(3)

$$
CMPs-amended = Ctotal × (δtotal − δMPs) / (δcontrol − δMPs)
$$

Where CMPs-amended is SOC-derived CO2–C from MPs-amended soil (mg C g<sup>−1</sup> SOC d<sup>−1</sup>); Ccontrol is SOC-derived CO2–C from the control (mg C g<sup>−1</sup> SOC d<sup>−1</sup>); Ctotal is total CO2–C from MPs amended soil; δtotal is the δ<sup>13</sup> C of total CO2–C respired from MPs amended soil, ‰; δMPs is the δ<sup>13</sup> C of MPs-C, ‰; δcontrol is the δ<sup>13</sup> C of SOC-derived CO2–C from the control, ‰.

Net soil C balance (mg C g<sup>−1</sup> SOC) was calculated as the difference between retention of added MPs-C and primed soil C loss (Qiao et al., 2014):

(4)

$$
Net C balance = Cremained – Cprimed
$$

Where Cremained indicates the retention of added MPs-C in the soil (mg C g<sup>−1</sup> SOC), which is obtained by subtracting MPs-C respired from MPs-C initially added; Cprimed is the primed soil C loss from the soil caused by degradable MPs (mg C g<sup>−1</sup> SOC).

### Statistical analyses

The one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was applied to test the significant differences among different treatments in each soil. The linear mixed model was used to assess the effects of soils, MPs, and their interaction on cumulative CO2 emission, relative PE, absolute PE, MBC, DOC, MBN, DTN, NH4<sup>+</sup>-N and NO3<sup>−</sup>-N. A statistical probability of *P* < 0.05 was considered significant. All statistical analyses were performed using IBM SPSS Statistics 20.0 (IBM SPSS Inc.). Graphical illustrations were produced using Origin 2018 (OriginLab, Northampton, MA, USA).

## Results

### Impact of microplastic types on CO2 emission

PHA and PBS significantly enhanced total cumulative CO2 emission (Figs. 1 and 2). On the contrary, there were no significant differences in total cumulative CO2 emission between PLA and control (Fig. 2). The δ<sup>13</sup> C of total CO2–C with PHA and PBS addition was generally more enriched than that of PLA and control treatments in the three soils (Fig. S1), indicating that PHA-C and PBS-C contributed more to the total CO2–C respired than PLA-C during the 56-d incubation. Specifically, PHA, PBS and PLA contributed 74−87%, 51−73%, and 14−23% of the total cumulative CO2 emission across the soils of strawberry, blueberry and paddy-rape rotation during the 56-d incubation, respectively (Fig. 3).

### Priming effects vs. microplastic types

The PE was strongly dependent on MPs stypes. The relative and absolute PE were higher in PHA and PBS than in PLA during the incubation regardless of soils (Figs. 4 and 5). Specifically, for strawberry soil, the relative PE in PHA, PBS and PLA were 1086–1744%, 93−179%, and −26−5%, respectively. For blueberry soil, the relative PE in PHA, PBS, and PLA were 552–1012%, 44−104%, and −29−43%, respectively. For paddy-rape soil, the relative PE in PHA, PBS and PLA were 992–1097%, 74−102%, and −4−21%, respectively.

### Impact of microplastics types on soil net carbon balance

The soil C gained from all three types of MPs was larger than the soil C loss by PE at the end of the incubation, causing a positive net C balance, which is conducive to soil C sequestration. The net C balance was lower in strawberry soil than in blueberry soil and paddy-rape soil regardless of MPs types. Moreover, the net C balance in PHA was lower than that in PBS and PLA (Table 3).

## Discussion

### MPs induced PE and traditional PE

To the best of our knowledge, this is the first experimental verification of PE induced by degradable MPs in farmland soils by the natural<sup>13</sup> C isotopic tracer technology. The PE is usually evaluated by plant residues, root exudates, and plant pyrolysis products (such as biochar). It was found that the range of PE was −20−12% in pyrolysis-C (Wang et al., 2016) and 17−63% in root exudate fractions (e.g. glucose, glycine, oxalic acid, etc.) in farmland soils (Zhou et al., 2022). It was also observed that maize straw addition resulted in a positive PE of 254%, while biochar addition caused a negative PE of −254% in a paddy soil during 180-day incubation (Su et al., 2017). In contrast with the traditional PE, PHA resulted in a staggering positive PE of 552–1744% across farmland soils of strawberry, blueberry and paddy-rape rotation (Fig. 5). However, PBS- and PLA-induced PE were only 44−179% and −29−43% (Fig. 5), which is similar with the range of the traditional PE induced by plant residues, root exudates, etc. Thus, degradable MPs (except PHA) do not necessarily lead to much stronger PE than traditional substrates.

### Effect of MPs degradability on PE

The magnitude of PE strongly depends on the degradability of MPs. PE was the highest in PHA, followed by PBS and PLA (Figs. 4 and 5). Accordingly, the degradability of PHA was much higher than that of PBS and PLA. A simulated compost experiment found that PHA only takes 60 days, while PLA needs almost 150 days to degrade into fragments in a cropland soil at 15−25 °C (Weng et al., 2013). Moreover, PBS only degraded 14% after 80 days at 30 °C soil compost (Luo and Netravali, 2003). Besides, the biodegradation of degradable MPs negatively correlated with their melting temperature (Tokiwa and Suzuki, 1977; Marten et al., 2005). The melting temperature is ranked as PHA < PBS < PLA (Table 2) (Sudesh et al., 2000; Xu et al., 2014; Lv et al., 2017). Lower melting temperature means lower crystallinity of the polymer (Gray, 1970), but when the crystallinity of MPs is lower, enzymes can mainly attack the loose amorphous domain regions of MPs molecules, thus MPs are faster hydrolyzed into smaller molecules due to random chain scission (Tokiwa et al., 2009). The hydrolyzed MPs molecules are more susceptible and more available for microbial utilization as a faster nutrient source (Kale et al., 2007). Furthermore, unlike PBS and PLA, PHA is known as a type of natural polymer, i.e., bacterial polyester. The natural polyesters are produced by bacteria as C storage in microbial biomass (Suyama et al., 1998), thus can be easily hydrolyzed by extracellular depolymerase that can cleave the ester bonds in the polymer structure (Amass et al., 1998). Altogether, PHA has the greatest degradability, inducing stronger PE and soil C loss via cometabolism compared to PBS and PLA. Thus, the strength of PE is closely related to the degradability of degradable MPs.

Compared with traditional substrates, MPs do not contain nitrogen in their molecular structure. Thus, the nitrogen used for microbial metabolism mainly comes from soil rather than MPs. In a short period, massive, easily accessible, labile organic C (LOC) in more degradable MPs, such as PHA, can lead to the proliferation of fast-growing r-strategists by cometabolism, thus enhancing a positive PE (Blagodatskaya et al., 2007). In this study, the MBC and DOC were ranked as PHA > PBS > PLA at the end of the 56-day incubation (Fig. 6), which also verified that more degradable MPs can simultaneously accelerate microbial assimilation and the transformation of soil organic matter (SOM) into dissolved organic substrates (Blagodatskaya et al., 2010). On a longer time scale, K-strategists grow actively with r-strategists microbial necromass under severe nitrogen deficiency and LOC exhaustion (Cui et al., 2020). More resistant SOM would be decomposed due to the microbial mining effect for nitrogen acquirement (Blagodatskaya et al., 2010; Zhang et al., 2023). This well explains why PE tends to be stable rather than shows a significant decline at the late stage of incubation.

### Soil net C balance

In general, soil microbes can utilize biodegradable MPs to conduct basal metabolism (Chen et al., 2022a). In this study, soil net C balances were positive in all soil treatments (Table 3). Our results are in agreement with the recent works of maize straw addition (Qiao et al., 2014) and fresh C inputs data synthesis (Liang et al., 2018), which consistently show more soil C replenishment with substrate input than soil C loss by priming effect. Despite the SOC loss accelerated by PE, the positive net C accumulation was still found because most of the degradable MPs and their residues were retained in the soil during the limited incubation duration (56 days). Furthermore, if the experiment lasts much longer, the net C balance may decline because most of the residues of degradable MPs may be further broken down and utilized by microorganisms (Phillips et al., 2011). Moreover, the activated microorganisms via PE will continuously decompose more complex SOM for nitrogen requirement due to the nitrogen deficiency of degradable MPs with the incubation prolonged. Thus, the retained C from MPs may be difficult to compensate for the primed SOC loss considering the potential decomposition of MPs and the more complex SOM on a longer scale.

## Conclusion

In the present study, the PE induced by three types of biodegradable MPs (PHA, PBS and PLA) was investigated in three farmland soils (planted with blueberry, strawberry, and paddy-rape). Results found that PHA-induced PE was the highest, followed by PBS and PLA. PHA-induced PE reached an impressive 552−1744% across the above three soils, while PBS- and PLA-induced PE achieved 44−179% and −29−43% across the above three soils, respectively. The magnitude of PE was closely related to the degradability of MPs. The greater the degradability of the MPs, the greater PE induced by the MPs. Moreover, a positive net soil C balance was found in the three MPs treatments regardless of soils. The microbial N mining may play an important role in causing the PE in a long term due to the N deficiency in MPs. Overall, our results, for the first time, demonstrate the magnitude and direction of MPs-induced PE in farmland soils by a natural<sup>13</sup> C isotopic tracing technology. These findings improve the understanding of the effects of degradable MPs on soil biogeochemical cycles and are helpful for more accurately predicting regional and/or global C budgets in cropland ecosystems.
