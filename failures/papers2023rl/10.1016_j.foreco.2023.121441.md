# Advantages and limitations of tree mixtures in enhancing avian diversity in commercial timber plantations: Early insights from southern China

## Abstract

Commercial timber plantations, particularly monocultures, have limited capacity to support biodiversity. A widely promoted approach to enhance this capacity is the diversification of monocultures into tree mixtures, by adding “secondary” timber species while retaining the production focus on the monoculture species. However, the presumed biodiversity benefits of tree mixtures are yet to be robustly tested, because current evidence predominantly comes from mixed plantations in the general sense, which are often not under active production or differ from monocultures in other important ways. To address this gap, we assess the effectiveness of early-stage tree mixtures in enhancing the avian diversity of *Eucalyptus* monocultures in southern China, which is the country's most important timber production region and has been pioneering tree mixtures in large trials over the past decade. During the avian breeding and non-breeding seasons in 2021, we conducted rigorous surveys to compare the avian diversity of *Eucalyptus* monocultures and their tree mixtures involving slow-growing native species, against the reference baseline of mature native forests. We found that tree mixtures exhibited modest advantages over monocultures during the breeding season, and both plantation types had notable shortfalls relative to mature native forests. We additionally found that tree mixtures tended to benefit species that foraged in the medium–low strata, consumed diets of higher trophic levels, and depended more on mature forest habitat. We conclude that the biodiversity benefits of early-stage commercial tree mixtures were rather limited and more modest than typically assumed, highlighting the importance of studying tree mixtures in real-world production settings. Our study also underscores the conservation importance of protecting existing mature native forests.

## Introduction

Commercial timber plantations are a major source for global timber production, accounting for approximately 45% of industrial roundwood produced in 2020 (FAO, 2020). The increasing demand for timber products (FAO, 2022) has driven a substantial expansion of plantations: global total area increased by 3 million hectares per year between 2010 and 2020 to occupy 7% of global forest area as of 2020 (FAO, 2020; United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs and UNFF., 2021). The large and expanding land footprint of commercial timber plantations is of major conservation concern, because of the limited capacity of these plantations to support native species populations and assemblages (Bremer and Farley, 2010; Kriegel et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Hua et al., 2022), and the real threats they pose to a large number of the world’s imperiled species (Maxwell et al., 2016). Given the fact that commercial timber plantations will account for a significant component of the world’s terrestrial land surface for the foreseeable future (Brockerhoff et al., 2013; FAO, 2022), enhancing their capacity to support native biodiversity is an important avenue to ameliorate their negative impacts on biodiversity.

A prominent and widely promoted approach toward this purpose is diversifying mono-specific timber plantations (“monocultures” hereafter) by adding “secondary” tree species without compromising the production focus on the original “primary” monoculture species (“tree mixtures” hereafter, to distinguish from the more general-sense “mixed-species plantations” in the context of our study (Hartley, 2002; Pawson et al., 2013; Puettmann et al., 2015). Monocultures constitute a dominant share in the world’s commercial timber plantations and timber production (EUROPE, 2015; Liu et al., 2018a), yet their capacity to support biodiversity is known to be particularly limited and notably lower than mixed-species plantations (Castaño-Villa et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Hua et al., 2022). The widely acknowledged biodiversity premium of mixed-species plantations is the basis for the expectation that tree mixtures can enhance the biodiversity of commercial monocultures (Hartley, 2002; Lopez-Sampson et al., 2021). However, the effectiveness of this approach has not yet been established, for the important reason that the vast majority of existing studies have not adequately accounted for the confounding effects of various factors that can influence biodiversity, including tree species composition (*e.g.* exotic versus native species, Muhamad et al., 2013), management intensity, and rotation stage (Nichols et al., 2006; Stephens and Wagner, 2007; Liu et al., 2018a). For example, it is not uncommon that studies compare actively managed monocultures with mixed-species plantations that consist of entirely different sets of native species (Ohno and Ishida., 1997; Muhamad et al., 2013; Bennett et al., 2018), that are older (Farwig et al., 2008), or that have quit management and production altogether (Castaño-Villa et al., 2019). In sum, it remains largely unclear to what extent tree mixtures can effectively enhance the biodiversity of commercial timber monocultures.

China has arguably the world’s largest commercial timber plantation estate (Williams, 2015; Niu et al., 2023), and in recent years has been actively promoting the diversification of monocultures using tree mixtures, with the intention of improving the ecological integrity of the plantation estate including its biodiversity profile (State Forestry Administration of China, 2016, 2019). This effort is in particularly high momentum for the state-owned plantations in southern China, the most important timber production region in the country (National Forestry and Grassland Administration of China, 2019), with large trials being carried out on mainstream timber species that are traditionally grown in monocultures, notably *Eucalyptus* spp.(Liu et al., 2018b; Gong et al., 2021). Given the large spatial scope of these trials and their significance for informing the future management of China’s extensive timber plantations, it is of high conservation significance to understand to what extent tree mixtures are delivering their assumed biodiversity benefits in these real-world applications. Such understanding can also provide highly relevant insights for the management of *Eucalyptus* and other timber plantations in other regions across the globe (Brancalion et al., 2019).

Focusing on birds as a representative taxon, we assess the effectiveness of early-stage tree mixtures in enhancing the biodiversity of *Eucalyptus* monocultures in Guangxi Zhuang Autonomous Region (“Guangxi” hereafter), an important forestry province in China that is responsible for almost half of the country’s timber production (State Forestry Administration of China, 2019), and also on the frontline of large-scale tree-mixture trials (Department of Forestry of Guangxi, 2018). While these trials are still in their early stages (≤10 years in progress), they represent the most advanced applications of tree mixtures in active commercial timber plantations in China. Insights on how well they are delivering the intended biodiversity benefits are therefore highly valuable. Based on rigorous surveys of avian communities, we compared the avian diversity between *Eucalyptus* monocultures and their tree mixtures involving slow-growing, precious-wood native species, in the context of the reference baseline of mature native forests in the region. We further assessed which subset of the avian community benefited from the tree mixtures, by assessing variations in a set of key functional traits among groups of species that were associated with different tree cover types (specifically, species that occurred in tree mixtures but not in monocultures, species that occurred in monocultures but not in tree mixtures, and species exclusively found in mature native forests). We predicted that tree mixtures would exhibit enhanced avian diversity compared with monocultures, but only up to a point in comparison with mature native forests. We also predicted that they would preferentially benefit species that forage in the medium–low strata, consume diets of higher trophic levels, and more strongly depend on mature forest habitat, given the more complex understory structure formed by the added native trees that may render tree mixtures to more closely resemble mature native forests (Dagan and Izhaki, 2019; Jakobsson et al., 2020; Atikah et al., 2021).

## Materials and methods

### Study area

We conducted our study in the Nanning and Chongzuo municipalities in Guangxi. Guangxi has set an ambitious target of establishing 1.48 million hectares of timber plantations by 2035, notably of the fast-growing *Eucalyptus* spp., *Pinus* spp., and Chinese Fir (*Cunninghamia lanceolata*), but also of the slower-growing precious-wood species as tree mixtures in what would have been fast-growing monocultures (Department of Forestry of Guangxi, 2018). Guangxi has a subtropical monsoon climate with a mean annual temperature of 21.8 °C and a mean annual precipitation ranging from 1200 to 1500 mm (Weather China, https://www.cma.gov.cn/). We collected field data on monocultures and tree mixtures in three state-owned forest farms that had been actively implementing tree-mixture trials: Gaofeng (22.90° N, 108.25° E) and Qipo (22.63° N, 108.093° E) near Nanning, and Dongmen near the Dongmen Township in Chongzuo (22.34° N, 107.84° E; Fig. 1). We additionally collected data on mature native forests in two nature reserves in the region: Damingshan National Nature Reserve (23.53° N, 108.36° E) and Xidamingshan Provincial Nature Reserve (22.85° N, 107.76° E; Fig. 1).

### Study design

We compared avian diversity among three tree cover types based on rigorous community-level surveys: *Eucalyptus* monocultures, *Eucalyptus* tree mixtures, and mature native forests. We selected plantation stands that were matched in biophysical conditions (*i.e.,* elevation, slope, and soil conditions), age, and management regime except for tree species composition (monospecific versus using species mixtures). Monocultures consisted of closed-canopy monospecific stands of *Eucalyptus* spp., and ranged between 2 and 12 (6.7 ± 8.6) years in age (Fig. 1). Tree mixtures were similarly of closed canopy, but exhibited a distinct stratified vegetation structure, with *Eucalyptus* trees – of similar age as the monocultures (2–14 years, 7.2 ± 3.6) – dominating the upper layer and the secondary native trees in tall shrub form in the understory (Fig. 1). Secondary trees were of the same age as the *Eucalyptus* trees, and consisted of two to four native broadleaf species of typically slow growth, including *Castanopsis hystrix*, *Mytilaria laosensis*, and *Erythrophleum fordii*. They were planted in tree-level mixtures with the *Eucalyptus*. Both plantations were subjected to intensive management practices, including biannual clear-cutting of grass and understory vegetation. Mature native forests were subtropical evergreen broadleaf forests characteristic of the region (Fig. 1). While they had been subject to historical disturbances of mostly selective logging and grazing up until around the 1970s, they had not been clear-cut in the past century, and represented the most advanced state of native forest vegetation currently available in the region.

We conducted repeated point count surveys to quantify avian diversity on the community level during the avian breeding and non-breeding seasons. For plantations, we used a tiered sampling design where we placed one or more point-count stations ≥ 300 m apart in multiple stands, with the consideration that stands constituted the management unit of plantations. We required all stands to be ≥ 5 ha, and we selected larger stands whenever possible, capping the number of point count stations in each stand at three. We selected a total of 19 stands and 41 point-count stations for monocultures, and 20 stands and 60 point-count stations for tree mixtures. For mature native forests, we selected 10 transects of approximately 2 km in length, on which we placed point count stations ≥ 300 m apart, resulting in a total of 66 point-count stations. To mitigate the influence of edge effects, we required all point count stations to be ≥ 80 m from the edge of stands (for plantations) or native forest (for mature native forests).

To identify the subset of the avian community that benefited from tree mixtures, we assessed variations in three functional traits among groups of species that were associated with different tree cover types (*i.e.* species that occurred in tree mixtures but not in monocultures, species that occurred in monocultures but not in tree mixtures, and species exclusively found in mature native forests): foraging height, diet trophic level, and habitat association guild reflecting species’ dependence on forest habitat. We selected these traits in light of the most salient difference between monocultures and tree mixtures in our study system in vegetation features (*i.e.* the added tall shrub vegetation by secondary tree species in tree mixtures), and of the potential relevance of these traits in mediating birds’ relationship with these features (Dagan and Izhaki, 2019; Cardoso et al., 2023). Birds that forage in medium–low strata of the forest habitat may have an inherent reliance on shrub vegetation (Goodale et al., 2009). The added tree species and associated greater structural complexity in tree mixtures may also benefit species that feed on food of higher trophic levels and those with stronger reliance on mature forest conditions – as opposed to those associated with open habitat or early-successional forests (Barlow et al., 2007).

### Data collection

We conducted three rounds of bird surveys at each point count station in each of the breeding (April 2021) and non-breeding (December 2020) seasons, during the first three hours after sunrise and the last three hours before sunset on days without heavy rain or strong winds (Bibby et al., 1992). At each point count station, we recorded all bird species observed or heard within a 50-m radius (distance measured using a range finder) during a 12-min period. Four teams of observers, each led by a principal observer familiar with the regional avifauna and comprising one or, on rare occasions, two other members, conducted the surveys. We minimized potential bias in data quality that may result from observer capability by ensuring that within each field season, each point count station was surveyed by different teams at different times of the day across the three survey rounds, and that by the end of each season, each of the four teams had surveyed all four tree cover types in roughly equal proportions. For a given point count station, survey rounds were spaced 3–5 days apart.

For each species we recorded, we obtained data on the three functional traits from published databases. For foraging height, we used data from the Elton Traits 1.0 database (Wilman et al., 2014) on the proportion of time a given species spends foraging at each of four vegetation strata (expressed in ordinal scores of 0–3 for below-water-surface/water surface/ground, understory, mid-height, and canopy/aerial strata, respectively) to calculate a weighted average. Data range of foraging height was thus 0–3, with 0 indicating a species that foraged entirely on the ground level, and 3 a species that foraged entirely in the canopy. For diet type with regard to trophic levels, we also used data from the Elton Traits 1.0 database, which quantified the reliance of each species on each of five diet categories in percentages (Wilman et al., 2014). We classified each species into one of four diet types in increasing order of trophic level, based on the diet category(ies) with the highest percentage(s): FruiNectSeed-consumer (consuming fruits, nectar, plants, and seeds), omnivore, invertebrate-consumer, and VertFishScav-consumer (consuming vertebrates [including fish] and carrion). Finally, we derived data on species’ habitat association guild from the habitat descriptions of each species provided in the Birds of the World database (https://birdsoftheworld.org/bow/home) and BirdLife International (https://www.birdlife.org). We followed a set of criteria (Supplementary Table 1) to classify each species into four habitat association guilds reflecting increasing degree of dependence on mature forest habitat: open country, generalist, early-successional forest, and late-successional forest.

### Statistical analysis

We compared avian diversity among the three types of tree cover in terms of species richness and community composition, separately for the breeding and non-breeding seasons. For species richness, we used the “*iNEXT*” function of package “iNEXT” (version 3.0.0; Hsieh et al., 2016) in program R (version 4.2.3, R Core Team, 2017) to extrapolate expected richness for each tree cover type, using individual-based rarefaction of the total abundance recorded for each species across all three survey rounds within each study season (Calviño-Cancela, 2013). For community composition, we applied nonmetric multidimensional scaling (“NMDS” hereafter) based on the Bray-Curtis dissimilarity index, on a processed community dataset where for each species at a given point count station, we took its highest count across the three survey rounds as its recorded abundance. We conducted this analysis using the “*metaMDS*” function of package “vegan” (version 2.5–7 Oksanen et al., 2007). We then conducted multilevel pairwise comparisons of community composition using the “*pairwise.adonis*” function of package “pairwiseAdonis” (version 0.4.1, Arbizu, 2020) and adjusted p-values with Bonferroni correction.

We assessed variations in the three key functional traits among four groups of species: 1) species recorded exclusively in mature native forests (NF-unique); and for species recorded in any of the two-plantation tree covers (note that these species could also have been recorded in mature native forests), those recorded 2) in monocultures but not in tree mixtures (MX-excluded); 3) in tree mixtures but not in monocultures (MN-excluded); and 4) in both monocultures and tree mixtures (MN-MX-shared). We applied ANOVA and multiple comparison tests (using the “*glht*” function in package “multcomp”; version 1.4–23, Bretz. et al., 2010) to assess the difference in species’ foraging height among the four species groups, and we visualized the among-group differences in species’ diet type and habitat association guild.

## Results

Across all tree cover types studied, we recorded a total of 126 species during the breeding season and 109 species during the non-breeding season (Appendix 1). Individual-based rarefaction suggested that while tree mixtures supported higher species richness than monocultures during the breeding season (Fig. 2 A), they did not have a species richness advantage over monocultures during the non-breeding season (Fig. 2B). In both study seasons, mature native forests had clear advantages of species richness over both types of plantations (Fig. 2).

**Comparisons of species richness among the tree cover types studied.** Species accumulation curves in the breeding season (A) and the non-breeding season (B) for *Eucalyptus* monocultures, *Eucalyptus* tree mixtures, and mature native forests.

We found significant overall differences in avian community composition among the tree covers studied during both study seasons (p < 0.001; Fig. 3). In accordance with findings on species richness, pairwise comparisons revealed significant differences in community composition between monocultures and tree mixtures for the breeding season (adjusted p-value = 0.003; Fig. 3A) but not the non-breeding season (adjusted p-value = 0.086; Fig. 3B). For both study seasons, both monocultures and tree mixtures showed significant differences in community composition from mature native forests (adjusted p-value < 0.003; Fig. 3).

![Figure 3](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S0378112723006758-gr3_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

**NMDS ordination biplots for bird community dissimilarities among tree cover types studied in the breeding (A) and non-breeding seasons (B)**. Each point represents a point count station. Results of pairwise comparisons between monocultures and tree mixtures are shown on the top right.

Given the findings above that tree mixtures exhibited biodiversity advantages over monocultures only during the breeding season, we focused on this season in assessing the subset of avian community that benefited from tree mixtures based on species' key functional traits. As predicted, we found that tree mixtures tended to benefit species that foraged in the medium–low strata, consumed diets of higher trophic levels, and depended more on mature forest habitat (Fig. 4). Specifically, compared to species recorded in plantations that were exclusively associated with monocultures (MX-excluded species), tree mixtures “brought in” species of marginally higher foraging height that centered around the medium–low strata (z-value = 2.3, p = 0.094, as demonstrated by MN-excluded species) and that were in greater resemblance to species uniquely recorded in mature native forests (Fig. 4A). Similarly, compared with MX-excluded species, MN-excluded species resembled NF-unique species more in having a greater proportion of species that consumed the VertFishScav diet (*i.e.* the diet type of the highest trophic level; Fig. 4B) and that depended on late-successional forest – as opposed to open country – as habitat (Fig. 4C).

For species recorded in any of the two plantation types, MX-excluded represents species recorded in monocultures but not in tree mixtures, MN-excluded represents those recorded in tree mixtures but not in monocultures, and MN-MX-shared those recorded in both monocultures and tree mixtures. NF-unique represents species recorded exclusively in mature native forests. For MX-excluded, MN-excluded, and MN-MX-shared species, the symbol “*” indicates species that were also recorded in mature native forests.

## Discussion

Focusing on commercial *Eucalyptus* plantations in a flagship timber production region in China that has been actively pioneering the diversification of monocultures, we found modest benefits of early-stage tree mixtures in enhancing the avian diversity of monocultures. Nevertheless, both plantation types had notable shortfalls relative to mature native forests in the region, highlighting their limitations in supporting native biodiversity. As predicted from the most salient differences between tree mixtures and monocultures in habitat features, we also found these early-stage tree mixtures to generally benefit bird species that foraged in the medium–low strata, consumed diets of higher trophic levels, and depended more on mature forest habitat. Our study provided a much-needed empirical test of the biodiversity benefits – and limitations – of tree mixtures in real-world commercial applications, at a time when they are being actively promoted in China and elsewhere to ameliorate the negative biodiversity impacts of commercial timber plantations.

The greater avian diversity we observed of tree mixtures over monocultures during the breeding season was consistent with prevailing views about mixed plantations (Hua et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2019b) but noticeably more modest, particularly in light of the shortfalls of both plantation types relative to mature native forests. This suggests that while the higher tree diversity and more complex vegetation structures in the tree mixtures do benefit some species – likely by providing a greater abundance of various resources (Hilmers et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019a) – their effectiveness in enhancing the avian diversity of commercial timber monocultures is rather limited (Castaño-Villa et al., 2019), at least during the early stages (*e.g.* the first ten years). This limited effectiveness likely reflects the realities of tree mixtures in real-world applications, which can render important differences with the more general-sense “mixed plantations” typically studied for biodiversity advantages over monocultures (Castaño-Villa et al., 2019). Notably, commercial tree mixtures are highly likely to be subject to intensive management practices (Scheper et al., 2023), as is the case with our study: the tree mixtures we studied were subject to biannual removal of non-target trees and understory vegetation, with the purpose of providing a competitive advantage for target tree species (Department of Forestry of Guangxi, 2018 and Fig. 1). These practices inevitably reduce the compositional and structural complexities of tree mixtures (Cardoso et al., 2023), and can diminish their potential biodiversity benefits.

The lack of biodiversity enhancement effects of tree mixtures during the non-breeding season was possibly linked to seasonal variations in avian community composition and species ecology, notably habitat requirements. In our study system, the avian community exhibited a considerable difference in the pool of constituent species between the breeding and non-breeding seasons, and this difference could underlie seasonal differences in the extent to which tree mixtures may enhance avian diversity. In addition, food availability is typically lower during the non-breeding season (You et al., 1982; Basset et al., 2015), and this can cause many species to exhibit less stringent habitat requirements and adopt behavioral adaptations, such as forming mixed-species flocks (Goodale et al., 2017; Gentry et al., 2019). These adaptive changes may allow more species to utilize both monocultures and tree mixtures, thereby diminishing the potential advantages of the latter over the former in supporting species.

The profile of the species that benefited from tree mixtures was consistent with predictions from the most salient differences in vegetation features between the mixtures and monocultures. The greater tree diversity and more complex vegetation structure of tree mixtures, particularly in the mid-lower story, were expected to benefit species that forage in such strata (Dagan and Izhaki, 2019; Atikah et al., 2021), and that rely on food resources of higher trophic levels and habitat conditions more resembling mature forests (Jakobsson et al., 2020). The lack of species relying on fruits, nectar and seeds for diet in both monocultures and tree mixtures was most likely because of the lack of flowering and fruiting resources in these plantations, given that their timber trees typically take around 10 years to flower and fruit (Calviño-Cancela, 2013).

The avian diversity shortfall of both monocultures and tree mixtures relative to mature native forests highlights the limited capacity for these commercial timber plantations to support the avifauna of our study system. Notably, a large number of species were simply unable to use either plantation type as habitat, and these “loser” species seem disproportionately reliant on mature forest conditions, as seen from their tendency to forage at greater heights (Fig. 4A), feed on flower-based food resources such as fruits, nectar and seeds (Fig. 4B), and rely on early and late successional forests for habitat (Fig. 4C). These findings are consistent with prevailing understanding of the limitations of plantations in supporting native biodiversity (Calviño-Cancela, 2013; Law et al., 2014; Goded et al., 2019), particularly species with stronger associations with mature forest conditions and sensitive to disturbance (Barlow et al., 2007; Brockerhoff et al., 2008). They also underscore the critical importance of protecting existing mature native forests to provide habitat and refuges – particularly in regions where such forests have mostly disappeared, as is the case with our study region – for those species that rely on these forests (Barlow et al., 2007). Nevertheless, it is worth noting that as tree mixtures in our study system age, as they are intended to by design (their harvest cycle is 30 years, in comparison with the 5–7 years typical of *Eucalyptus* monocultures; Department of Forestry of Guangxi, 2018), it is possible that they may be able to deliver greater biodiversity benefits (Calviño-Cancela, 2013; Wang et al., 2021).

In light of the large and expanding land use of commercial timber plantations (FAO, 2022) – linked to the world’s growing demand for timber resources – enhancing their capacity to support biodiversity is of high conservation significance. The tree mixtures in our study represent a mainstream approach toward this purpose (Liu et al., 2018b; Gong et al., 2021), yet their effectiveness in enhancing biodiversity relative to monocultures has remained largely assumed, primarily based on insights from mixed plantations in the general sense that may have important differences from commercial tree mixtures (Castaño-Villa et al., 2019). By studying tree mixtures under active management in a commercial production setting, our study found that the biodiversity benefits of these mixtures were rather limited and more modest than typically assumed, most likely linked to the intensive management they were subject to. Reducing management intensity in tree mixtures may help to improve such benefits – such as decreasing the frequency of clear-cutting for shrubs and other resource vegetation, especially trees that provide flowers, fruits, seeds, and shelter (Goodale et al., 2009; Castaño-Villa et al., 2019). However, it remains unclear to what extent these practices are realistic for commercial plantations geared toward production, or what implications they may have for the production yield of these plantations. These factors are important determinants for the biodiversity-enhancing potential of commercial tree mixtures, and should be assessed by future research and field trials.
