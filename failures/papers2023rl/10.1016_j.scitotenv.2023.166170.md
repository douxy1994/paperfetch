# Warming inhibits the priming effect of soil organic carbon mineralization: A meta-analysis

## Abstract

Fresh organic carbon (C) input will accelerate or inhibit the mineralization of native soil organic carbon (SOC), which is called positive or negative priming effect (PE), respectively. However, little is known about how warming affects the PE. Here, we adopted a widely-used ratio of SOC mineralization between substrate-added and unadded-control treatments to represent PE intensity and used the PE difference between ambient-control temperature and elevated temperature to indicate the effect of warming on PE (ΔPE). By conducting a meta-analysis of 146 observations from 57 independent soils worldwide, we found that experimental warming significantly decreased the PE by 0.26 (unitless). Among ecosystems, warming significantly suppressed the PE of cropland and grassland soils by 0.43 and 0.21 respectively, but did not change the PE of forest soils. Moreover, we found significant positive correlations of ΔPE with the initial soil C/N ratio and the effect size of warming on microbial biomass. Between substrate types (i.e., containing N or not), warming significantly decreased the PE induced by N-containing substrates. These results suggested that the response of PE to warming is likely regulated by soil N availability and warming-induced changes in microbial biomass. As such, we proposed a conceptual framework—the microbial N mining hypothesis dominates in soils with low C/N ratio where warming inhibits PE by promoting N mineralization, while the stoichiometric decomposition hypothesis dominates in soils with high C/N ratio where warming stimulates PE by promoting N mineralization. Collectively, these findings provide important insights into how warming affects SOC dynamics via inhibiting PE, which may weaken the positive feedback between soil C emission and climate warming.

## Introduction

The priming effect (PE) of soil organic carbon (SOC), increasing or decreasing the rate of SOC mineralization in response to fresh C inputs, is one of the most complex and poorly understood processes associated with soil C cycling (Kuzyakov et al., 2000; Bernard et al., 2022). Previous studies suggested that PE significantly stimulates SOC decomposition by 50 % (Sun et al., 2019). Atmospheric temperature has increased by >1 °C since the 1900s and is expected to increase by another 2.7 °C by 2100 (IPCC, 2021). The stimulation or inhibition of warming on PE may be one of the important factors that contribute to whether warming increases or decreases the positive feedback between soil C emission and climate warming (Guenet et al., 2018). Thus, understanding the response of PE to warming is of great significance for accurately predicting SOC dynamics under future climate warming.

In the past decades, several studies have explored the effects of warming on PE, but there are still two unsolved problems. On one hand, the response of PE to warming is consistent among studies. Some studies reported that warming decreased PE (Frøseth and Bleken, 2015; Verbrigghe et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022). However, other studies found that warming increased (Thiessen et al., 2013; Yanni et al., 2018), or had no significant effect on PE (Ghee et al., 2013). On the other hand, the mechanism regulating PE's response to warming remains unclear. Previous case studies have identified microbial properties (i.e., biomass and enzyme activity), soil properties (i.e., N availability and SOC content), and substrate quality as potential controlling factors regulating the effect of warming on PE (Ghee et al., 2013; Thiessen et al., 2013; Verbrigghe et al., 2022). For example, warming may increase microbial activity and metabolism (Koch et al., 2007; Wang et al., 2021a), promote native SOC mineralization, and then increase PE under warming (Creamer et al., 2015). By contrast, it was reported that the effect of warming on native SOC mineralization may be limited by the depletion of microbial-accessible C pools or the reduction of microbial biomass (Melillo et al., 2017), which leads to the negative response of PE to warming (Wei et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021). Additionally, warming may stimulate soil N mineralization, thus reducing the microbial mining of N from native SOM mineralization (Bai et al., 2013; Grant, 2014), and inducing the negative response of PE to warming (Li et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2022). Overall, warming has the potential to alter PE via changing soil and microbial properties (e.g., C and N availability, microbial biomass) (Thiessen et al., 2013; Guttières et al., 2021; Dong et al., 2022). A recent meta-analysis study found that warming on average suppressed PE among ecosystems, but the effect size of warming on PE was more negative in forest than in cropland and grassland (Dong et al., 2022). This shows that the response of PE to warming may be inconsistent in different ecosystems, and the factors controlling the impact of warming on PE need to be further explored.

Here, we conducted a global-scale meta-analysis of case studies on PE with warming treatment. We aimed to 1) elucidate the effects of warming on PE within and across ecosystems; and 2) investigate how soil, climatic and experimental variables and changes in microbial biomass regulate the responses of PE to warming. We hypothesized that 1) given that warming can stimulate N mineralization and reduce microbial biomass, warming would decrease the PE, and 2) the magnitude of soil PE response to warming would vary among ecosystems, due to the difference of soil, climatic and microbial properties in different ecosystems.

## Materials and methods

### Data collection

We searched proper case studies using the Web of Science and China National Knowledge Infrastructure (CNKI, https://www.cnki.net/) with the search terms: (priming OR isotope OR “<sup>14</sup> C label*” OR “<sup>13</sup> C label*”) AND (warming OR “increase* temperature” OR “temperature increase*” OR “temperature sensitivity”) AND (enrichment OR addition OR application OR input OR amendment) AND (decomposition OR respiration OR mineralization) AND (terrestrial OR soil OR land). To increase the comparability of data, all found studies were further screened based on the following criteria. (i) The soils used for laboratory incubation were collected from terrestrial ecosystems. (ii) The study must include the four treatments of untreated control (without substrate addition or warming), substrate addition, warming, and substrate addition plus warming. (iii) The study must clearly report the cumulative SOC-derived CO2-C over the whole incubation period using<sup>13</sup> C or<sup>14</sup> C isotope-tracing methods, or they could be calculated from the provided data. Following these criteria, a total of 32 studies were included in our dataset and used for subsequent analysis (Fig. S1). In addition, data on matched site information, including climatic factors (mean annual temperature (MAT) and mean annual precipitation (MAP)), ecosystem types, soil properties (soil types in USDA Soil Taxonomy, SOC, total nitrogen, C/N ratio, pH, and sand, silt and clay content), and experimental settings (types and magnitudes of substrates, ambient control temperature, experimental duration, warming intensity [the temperature difference between ambient temperature treatment and warming treatment]) were also collected from original studies.

To construct our dataset, studies with experimental duration of <48 h or under anaerobic incubation conditions were excluded. For studies including multiple soil layers, we only used data from the uppermost mineral soil layer to meet the requirement of independence in meta-analysis. If there were multiple incubation temperature gradients in the study, we used the lowest temperature as the ambient control temperature treatment and the other temperatures as the warming treatments. If more than one soil sampling site contributed to the same study, then all the different soils were extracted and treated independently. In total, 146 observations from 57 independent soils were collected from 32 studies (Fig. 1). When variables were not reported, we used site location information to extract meteorological data (i.e., MAT and MAP) from the WorldClim database (http://www.worldclim.org/) and soil properties (i.e., SOC, TN, soil pH, and sand, silt, and clay content) from the SoilGrids250m 2.0 database (http://soilgrids.org). We also collected soil microbial biomass data (measured by phospholipid fatty acids or chloroform fumigation-extraction method) under different treatments after incubation. In this study, ecosystem types were grouped into forest, grassland, cropland, and others (only including a few soils from tundra and wetland) (Fig. 1). We grouped the substrates into substrate type I and substrate type II according to the substrate quality and whether the substrates contain N, respectively. As for substrate type I, the substrates were grouped into labile C (e.g., glucose, sucrose, and glycine) and complex C (e.g., leaf, straw, litter residues, lignocellulose, and biochar). As for substrate type II, substrates were grouped into two groups, depending on whether the substrates contain N. Specifically, one group includes substrates that contain N (e.g., plant litter or biochar), and the other group includes substrates that do not contain N (e.g., glucose, sucrose or cellulose). In addition, the substrate C addition rate was calculated and unified as the C addition amount per unit SOC.

### Data analysis

We used the difference in PE between ambient control temperature (PEcontrol) and warming (PEwarming) to indicate the effect of warming on PE (ΔPE) (Meyer et al., 2018; Feng and Zhu, 2021a; Fig. S2):

(1)

$$
PE_{control}(unitless) = C_{addition} / C_{control}
$$

(2)

$$
PE_{warming}(unitless) = C_{addition - warming /}C_{warming}
$$

(3)

$$
ΔPE = PE_{warming}–PE_{control}
$$

where *C*<sub>contro</sub> l, *C*<sub>additi</sub> on, *C*<sub>warmin</sub> g, and *C*<sub>additi</sub> on-warming are SOC-derived CO2-C in untreated control, substrate addition, warming, and substrate addition plus warming treatments, respectively. A similar method was used to quantify the effect of warming on microbial biomass (ΔRRMB) between ambient control temperature and warming treatments. The weight (*W*r) of each observation (i.e., ΔPE, ΔRRMB) was calculated based on the number of replicates (Hedges et al., 1999; Wang et al., 2021b).

(4)

$$
Wr = (N_{addition} \times N_{addition - warming}) / (N_{addition} + N_{addition - warming})
$$

where *N*<sub>additi</sub> on and *N*<sub>additi</sub> on-warming are the numbers of replicates for each observed variable in the ambient control temperature and warming treatments, respectively.

To test whether warming had significant effects on PE, we applied linear mixed-effect models (LMM) with ‘soil’ as a random factor to calculate the mean effect size and its 95 % confidence interval (CI) (Wang et al., 2021b). The LMM was fitted with restricted maximum likelihood using the “*lme4*” package (Bates et al., 2015). The relationships were performed to analyze the effects of warming on PE (ΔPE) with initial soil C/N ratio, the response ratio of soil microbial biomass (ΔRRMB), MAT, MAP, pH, SOC, total nitrogen, silt + clay, warming intensity, substrate C addition rate, or experimental duration. Our analyses with Egger's test and Begg's rank test suggested that all of the variables were without publication bias (Table S1). All statistical analyses were performed using the software R version 4.2.0 (R Core Team, 2022). The figures were plotted using the R software with the “*ggplot2*” package and OriginPro 2020b (OriginLab Corporation, Northampton, Massachusetts).

## Results

Our results showed that warming significantly inhibited PE by 0.26 (unitless) across all studies (95 % CI: −0.43 to −0.09, *P* < 0.05; Fig. 2). The effect of warming on PE (ΔPE) varied among ecosystems, with the largest negative effect occurring in cropland soils (average value = −0.43, *P* < 0.05), followed by grassland soils (−0.21, *P* < 0.05). However, such warming-effect on PE was non-significant in forest soils (*P* ≥ 0.05). In addition, we found that with the addition of complex C and N-containing substrates, warming had a significant negative effect on PE (average value = −0.29 and − 0.27, respectively, *P* < 0.05; Fig. 2). By contrast, with the addition of labile C or substates not containing N, warming did not significantly change PE. Our results showed that the effect of warming on PE was independent of soil types (Fig. 2). Warming had a negative effect on soil microbial biomass measured by phospholipid fatty acids or chloroform fumigation-extraction method, but the effect was both insignificant (Fig. S3).

## Discussion

### Relationships of the effect of warming on priming with soil N availability and microbial biomass

Our results showed that warming significantly inhibited PE across all studies (*P* < 0.05; Fig. 2), which was consistent with our first hypothesis and previous observation (Dong et al., 2022). Dong et al. (2022) suggested that warming suppressed PE by decreasing microbial N mining and slowing down the growth of microorganisms. However, we found that the response of PE to warming was not only related to microbial N mining and microbial biomass reduction, but also related to stoichiometric decomposition hypothesis. Specifically, we found a significant positive correlation between ΔPE (used to indicate the effect of warming on PE) and initial soil C/N ratio (Fig. 3a). Given that soil C/N ratio is often used as an indicator to represent soil N availability across sites (e.g., Demoling et al., 2007; Terrer et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2023), this result suggests that the effect of warming on PE might be regulated by the soil N availability.

To explain the relationship between the warming-effect on PE and the initial soil C/N ratio, we proposed a conceptual framework (Fig. 6). Specifically, “stoichiometric decomposition" theory suggests that microbial activity is highest and their decomposition rates are maximum when C and N inputs match microbial stoichiometric C and N ratios (Hessen et al., 2004; Craine et al., 2007; Feng and Zhu, 2021b). We expected that warming increases PE in soils with low C/N. However, in the present study, warming inhibited the PE in soils with low C/N, suggesting that the “stoichiometric decomposition" theory is not applicable to explain the PE response to warming in low C/N soils. Conversely, low soil C/N generally indicates high soil N availability. After the addition of C substrate, microorganisms are likely to directly use soil available N to meet their demands for growth, and decrease mining of N from recalcitrant organic compounds of soil organic matter, leading to a reduction in PE under warming (microbial N mining hypothesis) (Moorhead and Sinsabaugh, 2006; Blagodatskaya and Kuzyakov, 2008; Wang et al., 2021a). By contrast, warming promoted PE in soils with high C/N (Fig. 6), which can be explained by the “stoichiometric decomposition" theory. In soils with higher C/N ratios, microorganisms are more likely to be limited by soil N availability. It was reported that warming increases soil available N concentration via promoting N mineralization (Bai et al., 2013; Grant, 2014). The input of exogenous C and the increase in N availability induced by warming may well match microbial demands for C and N and stimulate microbial activity and its decomposition of soil organic matter, thus leading to an increase in PE under warming (Chen et al., 2014; Feng and Zhu, 2021b). Given the overall negative effect of warming on PE, the microbial N mining hypothesis may dominate the effect of warming on PE globally or at least in our dataset with 57 soils.

![Figure 6](https://api.elsevier.com/content/object/eid/1-s2.0-S0048969723047952-gr6_lrg.jpg?httpAccept=%2A%2F%2A)

Conceptual diagram representing the overall effects of warming on priming effect (PE) and how warming affects PE as soil nitrogen (N) availability changes. Under warming, with increasing N limitation of microbial respiration, the effect of warming on PE increases, switching from negative effects to positive effects. Specifically, in soils with less N limitation of microbial respiration (or high N availability), warming suppresses PE (N mining hypothesis dominates); on the contrary, in soils with relatively high N limitation of microbial respiration (or low N availability), warming stimulates PE (stoichiometric decomposition hypothesis dominates).

Another potential mechanism for this phenomenon is warming-induced reductions in microbial biomass (Wei et al., 2021). Our results showed that warming tended to decrease microbial biomass, particularly in cropland soils (Fig. S3). This phenomenon may be related to the limited sample size in other ecosystems (i.e., *n*<sub>forest</sub> = 8, *n*<sub>grassl</sub> and = 2) and the large variation among different studies. Previous studies have reported that warming decreases microbial biomass (Gao and Yan, 2019; Zhang et al., 2022). The negative effect of warming on microbial biomass could be attributed to the fact that warming accelerates the depletion of microbial-accessible C pools (e.g., particulate organic carbon) or/and decreases soil C availability (e.g., dissolved organic carbon), which in turn leads to a reduction of PE under warming (Melillo et al., 2017; Wei et al., 2021).

### Effects of warming on priming across and within ecosystems

Among ecosystems, warming significantly suppressed the PE of cropland and grassland soils, but did not change the PE of forest soils. The distinct effects of warming on PE among ecosystems are likely associated with the differences in their soil C/N ratios. In our dataset, the mean soil C/N ratios of cropland and grassland were lower than that of forest (Fig. 3b), which is consistent with the global database (Xu et al., 2013). As mentioned above, for cropland and grassland soils with low C/N ratios, warming could inhibit PE by reducing microbial N mining, which could explain the significant negative effect of PE in cropland and grassland soils. Moreover, a positive relationship between ΔPE and ΔRRMB was observed in cropland (Fig. 4), suggesting that the warming-induced reduction in PE is related to the changes in microbial biomass. By contrast, for forest soils with higher C/N ratios, warming tended to stimulate microorganisms to break down N-containing soil organic matter and induce a positive effect or only have a minor effect on PE (Feng et al., 2021a, 2021b).

### Regulations of substrate types on the warming effect on priming

For the added C substrate, we found that warming inhibited PE under the addition of N-containing substrates, but there was no significant change in PE when adding N-free substrates (Fig. 2). When adding N-containing substrates, warming is likely to stimulate the mineralization of these substrates and promote the release of N from them (except biochar), which thus can increase soil N availability. According to the microbial N mining hypothesis, in the face of N sufficiency, microorganisms may decrease the mining of N from soil organic matter, resulting in a reduction in PE (Blagodatskaya and Kuzyakov, 2008; Feng and Zhu, 2021b).

In addition, we observed that with the addition of complex C (e.g., plant residues and biochar), warming had a significant negative effect on PE, while with the addition of labile C (e.g., glucose, sucrose, and glycine), warming had minimal effects on PE (Fig. 2). Most complex C substrates (e.g., plant residues) contain N. According to the enzymatic kinetic theory, the temperature sensitivity of complex C decomposition is higher than that of labile C (Fierer et al., 2005; Conant et al., 2008). The N released from complex substrates decomposition induced by warming is likely to be used by microorganisms, which will reduce the mining of N from native soil organic matter. This may be the reason for the decrease in PE under warming with the addition of complex C. Overall, these results further support the hypothesis that the warming effect on PE is regulated by soil N availability.

### Implications, uncertainties, and future study

In this study, we revealed the global-scale patterns of how warming affects PE and explored how environmental and experimental factors accounted for the variations in the warming effects on PE. We found that warming inhibited PE overall, and the effect of warming on PE was regulated by soil N availability and warming-induced changes in microbial biomass. These findings imply that a warming-induced decrease in PE would weaken the positive feedback between soil C loss and climate warming. The proposed conceptual framework provides a new perspective for understanding the warming effect on PE from the perspective of soil N availability and can help to solve the debate on the contrasting effects of warming on the PE. Despite these significant implications, the results in our present meta-analysis may still have some uncertainties.

First, the location of soil samples collected in case studies in our database was not evenly distributed geographically (Fig. 1), and observational data within grassland ecosystems was very limited. As such, the results for the effects of warming on PE in grassland soils are more likely to have significant uncertainty, which should be further explored by future studies. In addition, the observations were mainly from temperate regions, with fewer observations from boreal and tropical regions. Given the large differences in soil C/N ratios among different climatic regions (Xu et al., 2013), the insufficient observations from boreal and tropical regions would limit our understanding of how climatic warming affects PE in these regions. As such, we call for more attention to be paid to the response of soil PE to warming in grasslands and tropical and boreal regions in the future.

Second, previous studies used different experimental designs and neglected to collect data on soil and microbial properties after post-incubation. The large heterogeneity in ambient control temperature, experimental duration, and the form and rate of adding substrate C across studies may confound the key factors controlling the effects of warming on PE identified in this study. The lack of measurement of post-incubation variables would limit our understanding of how soil processes (e.g., N mineralization) and microbial properties (e.g., enzyme activity, carbon use efficiency, community composition) change during incubation, as well as their regulatory role in the response of PE to warming. To solve these issues, we suggest that future studies collect soil from multiple sites and conduct experiments with a consistent design (e.g., ambient control temperature, experimental duration, warming intensity, and form and rate of the substrate), with additional testing of soil properties after incubation. Such attempts, combined with necessary measurements of soil and microbial properties, will be very helpful for solving uncertainties that meta-analysis technique cannot solve and validate the results found in this study.

## Conclusion

Overall, our results showed that warming inhibited soil PE, which would weaken the positive feedback between soil CO2 emission and climate warming. The warming-effect on PE was positively correlated with soil C/N ratio and warming-induced changes in microbial biomass. Based on this relationship, we proposed a conceptual framework linking the PE, warming, and soil nutrient availability, which can help to explain the contrasting effects of warming on PE. Additionally, although caution should be exercised when interpolating results from laboratory incubation to ecosystem level, these findings provide key insights for understanding soil C dynamics in a warmer world and help to improve modeling of soil C stocks under future climate warming.
